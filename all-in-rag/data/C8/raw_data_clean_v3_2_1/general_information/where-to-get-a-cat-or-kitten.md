# Where to get a cat or kitten

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/where-to-get-a-cat-or-kitten

---

There are several places to get a cat from. You can buy a pedigree cat from a breeder, choose a kitten or adult cat from a rehoming/rescue centre or get a cat from friends or family, for example. Where you get your cat from, the experiences they’ve had, and the care they’ve received, can all have a lasting effect on their health and behaviour. So, it’s important to know what to look for, and the questions you need to ask. Be prepared to walk away if you’re not happy with what you see or with the answers to your questions. If not, you face taking on a cat which may not be healthy or happy in your home.

## Where should I get a cat from?

Some people get a kitten from friends or family, whose cat has had an ‘accidental’ litter (cats can beneuteredat 4 months old to make sure this doesn’t happen). As these kittens are often born into busy households and they are the only cats there, they’re usually well-handled and involved in plenty of daily activities that prepare them to be sociable, confident adult cats.

You can also go to a breeder for a pedigree kitten or visit a rehoming/rescue centre for a kitten or adult cat which needs a new home.

Wherever you decide to get your cat or kitten from, this article provides you with details on what to look for and the questions you should ask, to choose wisely.

The Kitten Checklisthas been put together by 20 animal welfare and veterinary organisations, to help you make an informed decision when choosing a kitten.

## What should I look for in a rescue organisation?

Although all rehoming/rescue organisations may mean well, not all do well for cats. Many organisations are highly professional and have taken on board the mental and physical health needs of their cats in the way they’re cared for. Sadly, however, there are some ‘rescues’ or ‘sanctuaries’ where cats are kept in inappropriate conditions.

#### A rehoming/rescue organisation should

Only take in the number of cats it can care for properly

Make sure that sick or injured cats have appropriate veterinary care

Make sure cats aren’t at risk of catching diseases while they’re in the centre

Make sure that cats aren’t stressed by the conditions they’re kept in, or by the proximity of other cats or dogs

Make sure cats are kept in such a way that they can relax and display normal behaviour so that the right homes can be found for them

Aim to match cats to owners with the aim of long-term harmonious relationships

Make sure that every effort is made to find suitable homes as quickly as possible for the cats in their care

The way cats are housed within the rehoming/rescue centre is very important. The more cats you have in an enclosure, the more risk there is of passing on disease. Viruses and other infectious agents thrive where there are lots of cats, bad hygiene and stressed animals. In addition to this, many cats are solitary animals and don’t enjoy the company of other cats. While some may be sociable, others will be terrified if put in a communal run with cats they don’t know. They may not fight, but they’ll often be very subdued, and each cat will sit as far away from the others as possible, wherever they can find a small amount of space of their own.

Many charities have good facilities and resources and keep cats in individual units with a warm sleeping place and a run where they can stretch their legs and see what’s going on. Cats that come in together and seem to get on very well may be kept together. In other centres, there may not be the facilities available, and cats may be kept in communal runs. Here, cats must be chosen very carefully for compatibility and numbers kept to a minimum. High hygiene standards must be in place and cats should be given areas to climb and hide if they want to get away from one another.

If you go to a poor-quality homing/rescue centre and see cats being kept in poor conditions, it’s natural to feel sorry for them and want to take them away to save them from their current situation. The problem with this is that you may take on a cat that’s been made susceptible to infection by being kept in a stressful situation and that’s potentially been exposed to a lot of disease. There are not only financial and emotional impacts when taking on a sick cat, but also, the risk of taking disease home to cats you may already have.

In good homing/rescues, health and wellbeing are understood and staff house the cats in a way that means they can assess an individual cat’s behaviour and personality as well as health. This allows them to match a new owner to a cat as successfully as possible, and this is not always easy. Cats will also be checked carefully for health problems and will ideally beneutered, treated for fleas, andwormsand any other conditions they may have developed.

That’s not to say you shouldn’t take on a cat with an illness or disability. If the homing/rescue centre can tell you about any potential health problems, and the needs of the cat moving forward, you can make an informed choice on whether you’re able to provide the cat with all it needs for a healthy, happy life.

## How do I choose a breeder to buy a pedigree kitten from?

If you decide you want a pedigree cat, again, there are different routes to explore, depending on whether you want an adult cat or a kitten. Many breed clubs have welfare groups that take in and rehome cats of a particular breed. They’ll have in-depth knowledge of the breed and what’s required to care for them properly, along with any potential health problems recognised in the breed.

If you want a pedigree kitten, you’ll need to choose the breed you want and a breeder who can provide a kitten that’s in the best health and, equally importantly, that will make a good pet. Read our A–Z of cat breeds and article onpedigree cats – things to considerfor further help.

There are good breeders and bad breeders. Good are those with the knowledge, experience and desire to make their cats’ health and welfare their top priority, whilst there are others who don’t understand cats at all and don’t care enough to do what’s necessary to produce a healthy, well-rounded cat. As a cat’s character and behaviour towards people are formed almost entirely during the time they’re with the breeder, this is a significant responsibility for the breeder. If they’re knowledgeable about cat health, have a small number of breeding cats and ensure their kittens have lots of handling and care, they’re likely to provide new owners with a healthy, happy kitten. Kittens that aren’t socialised well, and those living in crowded homes, are more likely to be fearful as adult cats, as well as being susceptible to disease.

‘Breeder’ is not a professional qualification. The description can be used by anyone who has accidentally or deliberately bred a litter of kittens, and the quality of breeders varies. Some breeders are dedicated to their cats and are very knowledgeable, not just about that particular breed, but also about cat health and the need for socialisation, and are dedicated to finding excellent homes for the kittens they produce.

If the breeder has a range of litter ages and breeds to choose from, or has different rooms full of cats, look elsewhere. What you should be looking for is a home similar to your own, with a clean environment, a healthy-looking mother cat that’s confident with people and healthy interactive kittens.

If possible, get your kitten from a good breeder, from a good rehoming/rescue centre or from a friend or family member.

## Should I get a kitten from an advertisement?

There are many advertisements in newspapers, local stores or on the internet that offer kittens for sale. The same amount of care should be taken in vetting these sources as any other in choosing a kitten.

## What about dealers?

Avoid kittens being advertised through dealers. The breeder should care enough about the kittens they’ve bred to want to talk to potential owners, not just hand their kittens over to a third party or dealer to sell.
