# The different needs of domestic cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/the-different-needs-of-different-cats

---

Feral, street, and pet cats are all the same species - the domestic cat (Felis catus) - but they don’t share the same needs or comfort levels when living closely with people. Understanding these differences is vital when deciding how best to help them in the long term.

Cats live in many different ways. At one end of the spectrum is the feral cat - independent, free-roaming, and generally avoiding contact with people. At the other end is the pet cat - happy to share our homes and our company. Between these two are cats with varying levels of human contact and comfort around people, whose reactions to being near us can range from relaxed to distressed.

## What influences where cats sit within this range?

#### We often think of domestic cats as pets, but in reality, pet cats aremade - not born that way! When we describe a pet cat, we’re talking about a mutually enjoyable relationship between a cat and a human. How this relationship develops, and how a cat lives, is influenced by several key factors

Temperament

A cat’s temperament is its natural, innate disposition - the foundation of their personality – and is shaped by genetics and early experiences. Temperament determines how a cat perceives and responds to the world around them. While a cat’s behaviour will vary depending on the situation, their underlying temperament is fixed and remains consistent throughout their lives. Recognising and respecting this helps us understand what each cat finds comfortable and what may cause stress.

Early life experience

Between two and seven weeks old, kittens learn what feels safe and familiar. Gentle handling and positive experiences with people and home environments during this period help them grow into confident, sociable cats.

Understanding both temperament and early experience helps us identify a cat’s lifestyle, their comfort around people, and what support they need.

Types of cats

Feral Cats

Feral catsare usually born outdoors and come from generations of other feral cats. Growing up in areas with little or no exposure to people, they learn to survive independently and naturally view humans as a potential threat. They live solitary lives, except when finding a mate to reproduce, hunting for food and seeking out safe places to rest and raise their young. Because they have not experienced confinement or positive interaction with people during their early development, feral cats find close proximity to humans - or confinement in a pen or home - highly distressing.

Street Cats

Street cats are similar to feral cats, likely living and being born outdoors, but they tend to grow up in more populated areas such as cities, towns, or villages, where people and human activity are part of their everyday surroundings. They often gather in groups, or colonies, around reliable food sources - sometimes scavenged, sometimes provided by humans - and may show some tolerance towards familiar people. However, despite this tolerance, street cats that haven’t been socialised as kittens, and most haven’t been, aren’t adapted to home life and will find confinement in a pen or home distressing.

Breeding within these groups can be prolific due to the easy availability of mates and plentiful sources of food. Other cats, such as strays, may be drawn to these groups by the abundance of resources. With increased numbers and a steady supply of nutrition, street cat populations can grow rapidly. Neutering intervention is required to control the population, and in some countries, designated people provide them with food, shelter, and monitor their welfare.

Pet cats

Most people are familiar with pet cats - they are seen as part of the family, living alongside people as companions. Pet cats are well adapted to living with humans.

Having been socialised as kittens, they have grown up in regular contact with people and learned that handling and home environments are safe. As a result, they are comfortable interacting with humans and living in confined spaces, such as a house or flat, whether they have outdoor access or live entirely indoors

Pet cats can, however, become strays. This happens when they leave or lose their familiar territory by becoming lost, choosing not to return home, or being intentionally abandoned by their owner. These cats may find themselves among street cat colonies or brought into a homing centre (also known as a shelter). But irrespective of where and how a pet cat is currently living, it is still a pet cat - its early experiences mean it likely has the capacity to live comfortably with people. However, time without human contact or negative experiences may influence how it behaves around humans in its current situation.

The Inbetweener

Finally, we have theinbetweener. This term, recognised and named by International Cat Care, describes cats that have lived as pets - sharing homes and human companionship - but unsuccessfully. This is because they experience chronic fear, anxiety, or frustration when living in a conventional pet setting, such as within a home or in close proximity to people. This may be due to limited positive interaction with people as kittens, or to temperament traits that make them more sensitive or fearful.

As a result, many in-betweeners end up in homing centres and unfortunately may remain in care for long periods. When it comes to finding them homes, they require suitable alternative living arrangements that ensure their welfare.

## Key points

When deciding how to help a cat in the long term, it’s essential to understand and recognise that their needs will vary depending on their capacity to live alongside people, their early socialisation experiences, and their overall temperament. Understanding these different types of cats, and how their experiences shape their behaviour, helps us decide the most appropriate and humane ways to support them. Each cat - feral, street, pet, or inbetweener - will require their own unique solution.
