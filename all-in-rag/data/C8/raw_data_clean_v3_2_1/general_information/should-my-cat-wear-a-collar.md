# Should my cat wear a collar?

SubjectHazards & Risks

Walk into any pet shop or supermarket, and you’ll see a variety of cat collars – from reflective to diamante, leather to fabric and, of course, some flea collars too. It can be hard to know if your cat needs a collar and if so, the type of collar you should get.

## What are the reasons for putting a collar on my cat?

#### Owners put collars on their cats for a variety of reasons

Identification

Some owners use collars and tags to easily show that a cat has an owner. ID Tags list the owner’s details, giving a quick and easy way to contact them. However, microchippingis the safest and most permanent method ofidentification (and is a legal requirement for all cats over the age of 20 weeks in England).

Cat flap activation

Until recently, the only way to make a cat flap selective (so that only your cat could open it and no intruder cats could enter) was to use a magnetic or electronic key attached to their collar. Now, there are cat flaps which are activated by your cat’s microchip, allowing selective entry without your cat needing to wear a collar.

To reduce hunting

Some owners also want to minimise the number of birds and wildlife their cats catch, and work carried out by the RSPB (Royal Society for the Protection of Birds) has shown that attaching a bell to a cat’s collar can reduce the number of birds caught. Using a bell may not entirely stop cats from catching wildlife, but it can be a helpful option. To learn more about how to stop your cat from hunting read our separate articlehere.

Flea control

Many flea collars available through pet shops or supermarkets containpermethrinor organophosphates, which aretoxic to cats. Although the concentration of these chemicals is low and the collars are licensed for use on cats, we do not recommend putting permethrin or organophosphates on a cat. There are safer, more effective alternatives forflea controland if a collar is preferred, we only recommend collars available from your veterinary clinic (which contain safe and effective products).

To increase visibility

Collars with reflective strips can help cats that are out in the dark to be seen, especially if they are crossing a road.

## What injuries can collars cause?

#### Collars put your cat at risk of injuries, such as

Minor problems, such as hair loss due to rubbing or a reaction to a chemical in a flea collar

Injuries caused by your cat getting the collar caught around their jaw

Injuries caused by your cat getting one front leg stuck through the collar (if left, the collar cuts into their skin, resulting in a serious and painful injury)

Strangulation by becoming hooked on something and the collar not coming off or breaking open

Problems caused by attachments (bells, discs etc) becoming caught or poor stitching (being swallowed or caught on the tongue)

## Why do injuries happen?

#### Injuries with cats and collars happen for a variety of reasons

The collar is fitted poorly

A collar that is too loose can allow your cat to get their leg or jaw stuck. Collars that are too tight can also cause injury, so owners should be aware of how to fit a collar correctly.

Collars with elastic inserts

Collars that have a length of elastic as part of the design (allowing them to expand) can also cause serious injuries. We would never recommend putting a collar with an elastic section on your cat.

Poor-quality collars

The stitching on poor-quality collars can loosen and can then be swallowed or wrapped around your cat’s tongue.

Bells and bits

Bells can be beneficial in certain situations, for example, for blind owners. Bells, discs and other bits hanging from the collar can also be hazardous – your cat can either become caught on something by one of these attachments or get claws caught in the bell. If used, the bell should be completely sealed and not have slits that the claws can get caught in.

## What can owners do to help keep cats safe?

Choose the right collar

If you’ve decided that you want or need your cat to wear a collar, pick a collar with a ‘snap-open’ mechanism– a plastic buckle which comes apart and releases your cat if it becomes trapped. Some snap-open fastenings open more easily than others, so try them out and choose one that doesn’t require excessive force to open.

Fit the collar properly

Knowing how to fit a collar correctly is important. Collars need to be quite firmly fitted – you should only be able to get 1-2 fingers underneath. When you first fit the collar, your cat may tense their neck muscles, so always re-check the fit after a few minutes and adjust if necessary.

Get your cat used to a collar

As with many things, getting your cat used to wearing a collar as a kitten is much easier than putting one on an adult cat for the first time. Regardless of your cat’s age, associating the collar with a good experience will be key. Providing positive distractions, such as play, food, or treats, when you first put the collar on your cat will help prevent reluctance to wear it.
