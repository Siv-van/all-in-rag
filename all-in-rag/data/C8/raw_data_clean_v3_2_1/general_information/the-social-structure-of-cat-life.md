# The social structure of cat life

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/the-social-structure-of-cat-life

---

The Felidae or cat family are solitary predators and don’t live in socially structured groups (except for lions).

However, while the wildcat ancestors of domestic cats are solitary animals, the social behaviour of domestic cats varies, depending mainly on the number of cats in an area (the density of cats), and the availability of food sources. Proving to be remarkably adaptable as a species, while retaining its roots as a solitary hunter, in some situations, the domestic cat adapts to group living through the development of social structures.

In general, free-living domestic cats have two main living arrangements. Some females form small groups (similar to a pride of lions), while others prefer to live alone with their own territories, which is more common among wild felids.

Understanding the social structure of cats

Understanding the social structure of cats can be beneficial for owners who have multi-cat households. Let’s look at this in more detail.

The importance of territory

Cats are solitary hunters by nature, so it’s crucial for them to establish their own territory for hunting. This helps them avoid conflict with other cats and ensures the survival of their species. Cats mark their territories using scents from their facial glands, urine, faeces, interdigital glands (on their paws from scratching) and anal glands. This, combined with their keen sense of smell, allowscats to communicateeffectively and helps prevent direct conflict.

A cat’s territory is made up of a core area (or den) where they feel safe enough to sleep, eat, play, and potentially enjoy social interaction. This joins the hub of the territory, which is the region beyond the core area that the cat actively defends against invasion from other cats. Beyond this lies the home or hunting range, over which the cat will roam.

In the wild, cats may have territories that overlap, with ‘neutral areas’ where they can greet and interact with each other. If an unfamiliar cat enters another cat’s territory, this may provoke behavioural responses aimed at deterring the cat and driving them away - starting with staring, hissing, and growling, and if that doesn’t work, chasing them and possibly a short, noisy, violent attack.

Colony living

Feral cats, which are cats that live without any help from humans, often form small colonies around sources of food. While some cats will continue to live alone, it’s not uncommon for groups of related queens (mother cats) and their kittens to develop and form what’s known as matrilinear colonies. These groups may have a loose dominance hierarchy, but it’s not as interdependent as you might see in dogs. The relationships between cats in these groups are complicated – some cats are closer to each other than others, possibly based on factors like relatedness, sex or age. Despite forming these groups, feral cats don’t develop a pack mentality; they still hunt alone and don’t rely on each other for survival. So, even though they can adapt to living in social groups, cats are not ‘pack’ animals.

Social groups of cats tend to work best when the members are familiar with each other and there is no competition for food or resources. Cats can form strong bonds with familiar individuals, especially between kittens in the same litter, and between kittens and their mother. In feral cat colonies, kittens may be nursed by more than one lactating queen, which helps them form social bonds at a young age. Kittens may even seek attention and try to suck from non-lactating queens, which also helps them form social bonds. Group-living females also have the advantage of greater protection from intruding males and predators.

There may be a larger, ‘central’ colony of females living close to the major food source, and smaller, ‘peripheral’ groups that form around the central colony. While these smaller groups have access to the food source, it’s less than that of the central colony, and they tend to suffer poorer health and reproductive performance.

Male cats

Male cats don’t commonly form part of the small colonies, instead, they usually live on the periphery with large territories that may overlap several groups of females. Female groups, on the other hand, tend to have smaller territories that are more discrete. The size of these territories can vary significantly, depending on how much food is available and how many females are in the group. Some studies have shown that the size of a cat's territory can vary by up to 1000 times in different places - from 0.27 hectares for a city cat in Jerusalem to 170 hectares for a cat in the Australian bush. Male territories might overlap with each other; in general, there’s considerable overlap (more so than between female groups) and their size is dependent on the availability of food and the number of females present. Dominant males may have larger territories, which can be up to 10 times larger than those of female groups (on average, about three times larger). In areas where there are lots of small groups of females close together, larger male territories may exist.

Conflict between cats

Conflict isn’t common within female colonies, likely because they’re so familiar with each other and often related. When it does occur, it’s usually when male kittens reach sexual maturity and are excluded from the group, or between males and females as the tom (entire male cat) ‘patrols’ his territory. Toms don’t tend to have conflict with females, but females will often try to repel toms who get too close unless they’re sexually receptive. That said, friendly behaviours, such as grooming each other (allo grooming) and ‘greeting’ behaviour (tail up, sniff noses, rub heads) can be seen, particularly between males and females who are familiar with each other.

Positive greeting behaviours between two female cats include tails up and head rubbing

Mature males show distinct behaviours when interacting with one another, typically characterised by agonistic encounters, avoidance, or tolerance rather than friendly interactions.

Cats have evolved as largely solitary hunters. They don’t rely on complex social interactions in the same way other animals do, leading to a limited capacity for complex visual signalling compared to animals that live in social groups. Most of the body language, postures and facial expressions are meant to be read from a distance, usually to increase or decrease distance from another cat. As a result, they struggle to show appeasement to other cats, which can lead to more fighting in situations of conflict.

Some cats can find multi-cat living challenging as the social structure is chosen by the owner and not the cats

Additional considerations

It’s worth considering this social structure when bringing more than one cat into your home, as living in a group, often chosen by the owner, can be challenging for some cats. If you are already having issues with conflict, or if any of your cats are suffering from a health issue, it is not advisable to bring another cat into the home.

If you’d like further advice before making the decision to offer a home to another cat, speak to your veterinary team who will be happy to help. You may also benefit from seeking advice from anABTC registeredanimal behaviour technician or clinical animal behaviourist.

If you do introduce another cat, ensure that you have enough resources (food and water bowls, litter trays, scratching posts, beds etc) for each cat to have their own, plus at least one extra, spaced out around the house so there’s no competition for resources. It’s also important to have areas where cats can be by themselves and avoid other cats, should they choose.
