# Identifying your cat

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/identifying-your-cat

---

It’s not unusual for cats to go missing – some just for a few hours, some for days and some permanently.

Losing your cat can be very traumatic, and it can happen even if your cat only goes outdoors occasionally or is an indoor cat – all it takes is a door or window left ajar for them to escape. Accurate, up-to-date and permanent identification for your cat is vital to ensure that, should your cat ever stray, escape, or become lost, you have the best possible chance of being reunited.

## What are the different forms of identification for cats?

There are different ways to identify your cat: visual identification with a collar and tag, by tattooing, or by using a microchip.

Microchips

A microchip is a tiny chip that’s about the size of a grain of rice, which is easily and safely inserted under your cat’s skin (usually in the scruff of the neck) via an injection. The microchip contains a unique number that’s linked to a central database containing the owner’s name and contact details. Should your cat become lost, a microchip scanner can be used to identify your cat by providing the unique number on your cat’s microchip.

As of June 2024, microchipping cats in England became a legal requirement. This means that all cats must be microchipped before they reach the age of 20 weeks – indoor cats included. As part of the new rules, your contact details must also be kept up to date. Without doing so, there’s no guarantee that you will be reunited with your cat, should they become lost. Other countries may also have their own legislation regarding microchipping in place, so make sure you are aware of your legal responsibilities as a cat owner.

Collars

Collarswith tags can also be used to identify cats and carry contact information via a tag or a small barrel attachment with information inside.

Some cats wear collars to carry ‘keys’ to a cat flap (some cat flaps can also be controlled by your cat’s microchip), so identification information can be attached to this.

In an ideal world, a cat would not wear a collar because there are safety risks. Cats, being active and inquisitive, often get into small or dangerous places and can catch their collar on a branch, fence or even something indoors, and could potentially choke or be injured. They can also get their leg caught through the collar if it is too loose, and this can cause serious injuries if not treated immediately.

Choosing the right collar (a quick-release snap-open safety collar which comes undone if it gets caught) and fitting the collar properly helps reduce the risk of injury from your cat’s collar.

## The benefits of collars are that it’s immediately obvious that a cat has some identification; the disadvantages are that a collar can be easily removed or, if not chosen and fitted properly, could be uncomfortable or even dangerous.
