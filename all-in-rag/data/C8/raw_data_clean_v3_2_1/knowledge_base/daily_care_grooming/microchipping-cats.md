Microchipping your cat gives you the best chance of being reunited with them should they become lost. It’s a permanent method of identification, which we strongly recommend.

Additionally, as of 10 June 2024, all pet cats in England must be microchipped before they reach the age of 20 weeks, by law. This includes indoor-only cats. As part of the legislation, your contact details must also be kept up to date on a Defra-approved database. Failure to have your cat microchipped as per the legislation in England could result in a fine. Other countries may also have their own legislation regarding microchipping in place, so make sure you are aware of your legal responsibilities as a cat owner.

## What is a microchip?

A microchip is a tiny chip that’s about the size of a grain of rice, which is easily and safely inserted under your cat’s skin (usually in the scruff of the neck) via an injection. The microchip contains a unique number that’s linked to a central database containing the owner’s name and contact details. Should your cat become lost, a microchip scanner can be used to identify your cat by providing the unique number on your cat’s microchip.

## When should cats be microchipped?

Many owners have their cats microchipped at the same time as vaccination or when they are under anaesthetic for neutering. A cat can be microchipped as a kitten or as an adult. Microchipping can be carried out by your veterinary team or other trained animal care professionals.

## What is a microchip cat flap?

A microchip cat flap can be a useful addition to your home if you want to give your cat access to the outdoors but prevent other unfamiliar cats from entering your home. Many can also be set to lock during certain hours. Microchip cat flaps scan your cat’s microchip and will only unlock for the microchip number that’s been programmed into the cat flap. Most microchip cat flaps allow multiple microchip numbers to be programmed, which is helpful for multi-cat homes.

## Are your contact details up to date?

A microchip won’t help bring your cat home if your details are wrong. If you move house, change your phone number, or change any other details, it is important to update the microchip database as soon as possible.

Updating your details

The first thing you’ll need, and something useful to have a record of anyway, is your cat’s microchip number.

#### You might find this

On the paperwork given to you when your cat was microchipped

On your pet passport or vaccination booklet if your cat has one

On pet insurance documents

On your cat’s veterinary records

If you can’t find the number or haven’t previously been provided with the details, you can call your veterinary team or the professional who implanted your cat’s microchip to see if they have a record of the number. Failing this, you can take your cat to your veterinary clinic or rehoming centre where they’ll be able to use a microchip scanner and find the number for you.

You will also need to know the database company that holds the registered information of your cat’s microchip. This can be found by entering the microchip number into thecheck-a chip, PetlogorPETMAXXwebsites, which scan UK and international microchip databases.

Once you have the microchip number and the database that it’s attached to, the process of changing the details is straightforward and can be done online.

There is generally a fee for changing your details. Some companies offer membership for unlimited changes, and some charge each time.

Details should be updated whenever you change your address or telephone number. If you can’t be reached using the information in the database, then the microchip is rendered useless.

## FAQs

## Can implanting a microchip harm my cat?

International Cat Care agrees with the World Small Animal Veterinary Association and the American Veterinary Medical Association that microchipping cats is safe and very rarely associated with any significant problems. The microchip is the size of a grain of rice and is implanted in a similar way to giving a vaccination, causing very minimal, temporary discomfort.

## Why not use a collar and tag?

Collarscan be removed or become lost, and while they do help to visibly show that a cat is owned, they’re not without risk and can cause significant injury to cats (if using a collar, always ensure it has a ‘snap open’ mechanism). Microchipping is a safer option as it is permanent with minimal risk involved.
