# Hand-rearing kittens

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/hand-rearing-kittens

---

Raising a kitten can be a rewarding experience. However, kittens are very vulnerable, and raising them can be difficult, time-consuming, and not always successful. For a short period of your life, the kittens will demand a lot of your time, energy and attention, so it’s important to be prepared.

Note: before attempting to hand-rear any kitten, ask your veterinary team for advice on all aspects of care including feeding, toileting, handling, health and behaviour.

## What causes a kitten to need hand-rearing?

Kittens usually have their needs met by their mother. She’ll keep them clean, warm, well-fed and help them to pass urine and faeces. But in some situations, she may not be able to care for them – for example, if she’s ill, if the litter is too large for her to cope with, if she dies or rejects her kittens for some reason. If her illness is short-lived, you may only need to care for the kittens for a few days, or if she has a large litter, the kittens will likely only need supplemental feeding. However, be prepared that in some situations, kittens may need to be hand-fed until they are weaned (fully transitioned onto solid food).

## Do hand-reared kittens develop normally?

Hand-rearing is carried out in many ways but those that remain closest to the ways a mother rears her kittens are likely to cause the least negative impact on development. Research shows that separation from the mother has lasting effects on behaviour, with hand-reared kittens appearing more nervous than mother-reared kittens. Weaning is also crucially important, as mothers would wean slowly and gradually and if this is not replicated during hand-rearing, kittens can show increased aggressive behaviours and may also experience digestive problems and weight loss. Having siblings and interactions with sociable adult cats may also help encourage normal development in terms of social behaviours, but caution should be given mixing unvaccinated kittens with adult cats that are not their mothers.

## What are the essential things to think about when hand-rearing kittens?

#### The key things you’ll need to be able to provide are

Appropriate food fed in a manner that mimics mother feeding as much as possible

Toileting support, as young kittens need stimulation to urinate and defecate

General care

Attention to general health

A plan for socialising the kitten and introducing them to the regular experiences and events of living as a pet cat to ensure they find this way of living enjoyable

Total dedication and commitment are always required by the carer.

Newborn kittens need up to 10 feeds in each 24-hour period

The carer’s lifestyle will need to be flexible. You will need to be on hand to attend the kitten’s needs at all times, wherever you may be

Carers need to be able to maintain strict feeding and toileting times as energy supplies in a kitten can deplete very quickly and young kittens cannot toilet without stimulation

Providing a safe, clean, warm home environment

If the kittens are being fed by their mother, even partially, they should be kept with her. If no mother is around, for the first 3 weeks of life it’s useful to use a small cat carrier with lots of cosy bedding, plus a soft toy to snuggle up to. As they grow and become mobile, use a kitten pen, large dog crate or convert a baby’s travel cot/playpen. Use a Velcro safety net to stop young kittens from climbing and falling out.

Warmth is essential for newborn kittens, as they can’t react to cold by shivering and can’t control their body temperature. In normal circumstances, warmth is provided by direct body contact with the mother and conserved by the enclosed bedding. Hand-reared kittens can be kept warm by providing a warm, well–covered hot water bottle, an electric heat pad, or a microwave heat pad. Ensure the heat source isn’t too hot, otherwise it may cause contact burns.

The temperature in the kitten box with no mother present should initially be maintained at 29.4–32.2°C (85–90°F), but the box should be large enough for the kittens to move away from the heat if they become too hot. The temperature can be gradually reduced to 26.7°C (80°F) by 7–10 days and to 22.2°C (72°F) (by the end of the first month.

Feeding the kittens

Never use cow or goat’s milk to feed kittens as protein and fat levels are too low. Only use a properly formulated replacement queen’s milk formula, available from veterinary clinics, pet shops or online. Always discussed feeding with your veterinary team before starting to hand-rear. Demonstrations on how to feed by a trained/experienced person can be invaluable.

Make up the milk replacement solution as per the instructions on the packaging. The amount on the label is usually given as ‘per 24 hours’, so the overall quantity can be divided into several feeds. The milk should be warmed to 35-37.8°C (95–100°F) and the temperature checked on the back of your hand before feeding.

Baby bottlescan be bought which are specially designed for kittens. The size of the hole in the nipple is critical – too large, and you risk drowning the kitten, too small, and the kitten may struggle to get milk from it, become discouraged and refuse to feed. The correct size hole allows the milk to drip from the nipple with minimal or no squeezing of the bottle. As the kittens grow, the size of the hole in the nipple can be gradually enlarged. Each kitten will naturally stop sucking when they’re full and will pull away from the teat. Never force milk into the kitten, as this can mean they inhale the milk into their lungs causing severe illness.

Spoon, dropper, syringe and tube feeding can all be used, if a bottle cannot be sourced, but such methods do carry risks such as milk inhalation into the lungs. Kittens should always be fed with their four paws touching a surface in the standing or lying down position. They should never be fed on their backs, and the risk of choking/milk inhalation is greater in this position.

Suggested feeding times

0–2 weeks

10 feeds in 24 hours at 2–2.5 hour intervals

2–4 weeks

7 feeds in 24 hours at 2.5–3.5 hour intervals

4–5 weeks

5 feeds in 24 hours at 3.5–5 hour intervals

Hygiene is vital, both in terms of all the kittens’ feeding and measuring equipment and the carer’s hygiene in preparing feeds and toileting kittens. Orphaned kittens are very prone to infections, so they must always be kept clean, and utensils used for preparing or giving the milk must be sterile. A steriliser designed for human babies is suitable.

Weaning

Weaning (moving the kittens gradually from milk to solid food) should begin at 3 to 4 weeks of age. Initially, the kittens should be offered their milk replacement, in a flat shallow dish. At 3 weeks, mix wet (tins or sachets) or moistened drykitten foodwith a small amount of milk solution, so it becomes soft and paste-like. Offer this to the kitten from a small, shallow bowl or saucer, placed nearby and at ground level. Continue this until the kittens are eating just solid food. Never force the kitten to eat as this can risk them inhaling the food into their lungs.

Monitor the kittens’ growth rates by weighing them daily (at the same time). This can initially be done with clean kitchen scales and a large bowl to safely place the kitten in and by keeping a record. They should double their birth weight in the first 7 to 10 days, then continue to gain weight steadily.

General care of the kittens

Kittens under 2 weeks of age need to be stimulated to pass urine and faeces. The ‘voiding reflex’ (by which urine and faeces are released) is normally initiated by the mother licking the kitten’s anogenital region (the area under the tail). In the absence of the mother, you’ll need to help your kittens pass urine and faeces, until the kitten is independent, by gently rubbing under their tail with soft tissue, damp cotton wool or fragrance-free wet wipes. This should be done before and after feeding, as kittens feed better with empty bladders/bowels.

From 3 weeks of age, the reflex should be triggered while the kitten is placed on the litter tray. Leaving a small amount of soiled litter within the tray will remind the kittens where they can go to the toilet.

For more advice, read our article onhow to choose and use a litter tray.

Health of the kittens

Normal kittens should eat or sleep for 90% of the time for the first 2 weeks of life. If they cry excessively or fail to suck, they are usually ill and/or not receiving enough milk. Since kittens can die very quickly, they (and their mother, if still present, and other litter siblings) should be examined by a vet as soon as possible if you’re in any way worried about their health, or if they’re crying a lot or not feeding. If visiting a vet, take the entire litter (and mother if present) rather than separating the unwell kitten, as separation from the nest can be highly stressful.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If the kittens or mother are showing any signs of ill health, are crying excessively, not feeding, or not gaining weight as expected, contact your veterinary team for advice.

## FAQs

## Should kittens be wormed regularly?

Since worms are common in kittens, all kittens should be treated from about 3 weeks of age. Before each dosing, the kittens should be accurately weighed – if too little wormer is given it may not be effective, and if too much is given it may make the kittens ill. Speak to your veterinary team for advice on the most suitable wormer for your kittens and read our article onworming your catfor further information.

## When should kittens be vaccinated?

Kittens gain some protection from disease from maternal antibodies passed in the mother’s colostrum (the milk the kittens receive in the first few hours after birth).

The protective effect of maternal antibodies lasts for only a few weeks, so the kittens’ vaccination programme should start at around 8-9 weeks of age. If the kittens were orphaned, and never received colostrum, speak to your veterinary team about the best time to start vaccinations. For more information, read our article onvaccinating your cat.

## When do kittens’ eyes usually open?

At birth the kittens’ eyes are closed – they usually open within 1–2 weeks. If the closed eyelids become swollen or matted with pus, contact your veterinary team. In some breeds, eg, SiameseandOrientals, the eyes may be partially open at birth and open completely within a few days.

## Do I need to socialise the kittens?

Socialisation is extremely important for kittens to be well–adjusted pets and used to living in households alongside people or other animals. If kittens are not well socialised, they can develop behavioural problems later in life, which may also lead to stress-related health issues. The socialisation period for cats is approximately 2-7 weeks, so positive social exposure to people needs to begin early. There is very little documentation on how the hand-rearing process affects the behavioural development of kittens. However, many who have extensive experience of hand-rearing kittens find that the behaviour of the kitten (and also when they are an adult cat) is different to cats that have been mother-raised. For example, some report that hand-reared cats have difficulty reading other cats’ behaviours, often not knowing when to stop playing or appear to have an inability to know when to leave another cat alone. Such reports tend to be more frequent when a single kitten has been hand-reared as opposed to a litter – most likely because the kitten has neither the social interactions with its mother or siblings.

For these reasons, Introducing the kittens to a variety of people and other animals in a safe, calm and positive environment as soon as possible is recommended. Any handling of the kittens should be done gently and in short bouts over multiple short sessions each day.
