#### Did you know that cats reach sexual maturity from around the age of 4 months? If your female cat isn’t neutered before this age, and you let her outside, an unneutered male cat is highly likely to find her and before you know it, she’ll be pregnant. It’s not always that noticeable, until she starts to get quite round. So, what can you expect, and how does this happen from such a young age? Read on to find out

## What are the basics of cat reproduction?

As we’ve mentioned, cats reach sexual maturity (and so can breed) from around 4 months of age, which is why the current advice is to have your cat neutered around 4 months old to prevent unwanted pregnancies. You can learn more about spaying (in females) or castrating (in males) in our separate article, neutering your cat.

There’s reason to believe that the timing of any reproductive activity is triggered by an increase in day length. Why? In the wild, better weather brings new life, meaning there’s more food available for the mother and her kittens after her 9-week pregnancy.

So, in the northern hemisphere, cat pregnancies and births increase from February and decrease from October to January. In the southern hemisphere, the reverse is true. And cats on the equator probably don’t experience a great deal of change in their reproductivity throughout the year.

An entire female cat rolling and calling on the floor for a mate

This time of ‘heat’ or coming into season isn’t one long period, but lots of short periods with each cycle being around 14 days long. During these periods, your cat will behave quite differently - rubbing and rolling on the floor, marking, and making a demanding, rising and falling noise known as ‘calling’. If you’ve never experienced this, your first instinct may be that your cat is in pain - but in fact, these behaviours are perfectly normal for a female cat in search of a mate.

Unneutered male cats are constantly on the search for females, picking up their scent, sound and body language. She may even attract several males, which is where fighting can break out. Female cats won’t accept the advances of a male until she’s ready, at which time, she will exhibit what’s known as the lordosis position - rump in the air, front end on the ground, and waving her tail to one side.

Cats don’t ovulate or release eggs into the fallopian tubes and into the horns of the uterus for fertilisation until they have mated, unlike other animals which already have the egg in place when mating occurs. In order to become fertilised, the egg must be released, and the stimulus is mating. It can take several matings to stimulate ovulation, so a female cat may mate several times, and potentially with several males, over a period of four to six days. This long period of receptiveness gives the cat a chance to ovulate and to choose the best male; one which is healthy and in his prime.

It takes a few days for the eggs to move down the fallopian tubes and reach the uterus. As sperm survives for several days, the resultant litter may actually have several different fathers.

If the female cat doesn’t mate, the eggs aren’t released, and the cycle is repeated about two weeks later.

## How long does pregnancy last in cats and what are the signs?

After the female cat becomes pregnant, her body slowly changes over the next 63-65 days as the foetuses grow. There’s very little to see in terms of outward change in the first few weeks of pregnancy, and one of the first signs you may see is the nipples becoming more visible and pinker in colour. She’ll then gradually put on weight, and as she gets closer to the birth, her mammary glands will start to fill.

Behavioural changes include looking for a good nest site where she can hide her kittens. In the wild, this site would be dry and well hidden, and several sites may even be selected so that there are safe alternatives should the original site become unsafe for any reason. In a home environment, as an owner you may notice ‘nesting’ behaviour (this usually happens in the final two weeks of pregnancy). Your cat will look for a quiet, safe, and often dark place to have her kittens. You may want to provide her with a cardboard box with bedding or a cat bed if you become aware of a site she’s chosen to have her kittens - just make sure the bedding is suitable for snuggling into, but won’t wrap the kittens within pockets or folds.

## What happens during a normal cat birth?

Just before the birth, your cat may become restless or meow, purr or pant. There are several different stages of labour - you can find out more by reading our article oncat birth. Each kitten is born in a sac of amniotic fluid which their mother licks and nibbles to free her kitten. She bites through the umbilical cord, and using her rough tongue, she cleans the kitten and stimulates it to breathe. Most cats (especially non-pedigree ones) will give birth without a problem and will need no human intervention.

## How do I care for newborn kittens?

If all goes to plan, you won’t have to do much for the first few weeks except make sure that the mother is well fed and has all the facilities she needs, as she should take care of her kittens herself. She’ll protect her kittens from disease in their early weeks of life with colostrum, the first type of milk she produces, and will keep her kittens warm by lying on her side, encircling them, and encouraging them to suck. While kittens can’t hear when they’re first born, their mother will purr when they’re suckling as they can follow the vibrations to move towards her. They have a built-in rooting or nuzzling behaviour, so they’re able to find the nipple, latch on and stimulate the milk to flow.

At birth, kittens weigh around 100g. This doubles in a week and triples in three weeks. Cat milk is high in protein and fat – necessary for this rapid growth period. They initially feed for several hours per day and keep the milk flowing by kneading their mother’s stomach with their paws (the same behaviour they may show on our laps or on a fluffy blanket). For the first two to three weeks, the kittens remain highly dependent on their mother for feeding, cleaning, defaecation and keeping warm. By four weeks, they’re using litter and copying their mother’s use of the tray, and at six weeks, they’re grooming themselves and each other, and forming bonds. At around four weeks old, the mother cat will start to wean them off her milk and onto solid food - in the wild, she would be teaching them about prey and hunting so they can be self-sufficient as soon as possible.

The only time you may need to intervene and help with the kittens is if the mother becomes ill or can’t feed the kittens for some reason. If this happens, you may consider hand-rearing - but be prepared, this is quite an undertaking. Kittens are very fragile, and raising them can be difficult, time-consuming (and in most cases, will mean getting up throughout the night), and it’s not always successful. Read our information onhand-rearing kittensto learn more about what’s involved.

## When should I contact my vet?

You can contact your vet for advice if your cat gets pregnant, as they’ll be able to answer any questions you have and give you specific advice on how to keep her happy and healthy. If you’re worried about your cat at any point during her pregnancy, contact your vet straight away.

## FAQs

## What should I feed my pregnant cat?

As soon as you’re aware of your cat’s pregnancy, we recommend feeding your cat a growth type diet. This can continue to be fed after the birth of the kittens and until they’re weaned. If you’re not sure which food is best for your cat, chat with your vet who will be happy to help.

## If I don’t want my cat to fall pregnant, when should I spay her?

We recommend neutering from around 4 months of age - read our advice on neutering your cathere.

## How do I find homes for the kittens?

Once your cat is pregnant, it’s not just her you’ll need to think about - you’ll also have the responsibility of caring for her kittens and then finding them suitable homes. Providing the kittens are happy and healthy, they can be rehomed from around 8 weeks, once they’re fully weaned.
