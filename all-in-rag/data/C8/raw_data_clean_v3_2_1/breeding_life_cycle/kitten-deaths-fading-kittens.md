# Fading Kittens

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/kitten-deaths-fading-kittens

---

Kitten deaths are, understandably, very distressing for both breeders and owners.

The causes and prevention of kitten death are often the same, regardless of whether the situation occurs at the breeder’s home, in the kitten’s new home, or at a homing centre or shelter.

When breeding cats, a low level of loss must be expected. Generally, pedigree cats have higher levels of kitten deaths than non-pedigrees.

## What causes kitten deaths?

Most kitten deaths occur before birth (known as stillborn kittens) and during the first week of life. After the first week, mortality rates drop dramatically. Most early deaths are from non-infectious causes, as kittens receive protection against many infectious diseases during the early weeks of their life from their mother (also known as the queen) - this is known as maternal-derived immunity. Infectious diseases become more common as this wears off, often in and around weaning, and at this point, mortality rates can rise a little again. Kittens dying between birth and weaning are often called ‘fading kittens’.

Newborn kittens can die suddenly or ‘fade’ within a few days. Unfortunately, many of the diseases affecting newborn kittens have very similar, vague signs. While normal, healthy kittens tend to cuddle together and sleep contentedly between feeds, sick kittens often lie separately, will generally be more restless, aren’t keen to suck, and cry frequently (if they’re still strong enough to do so).

## What are the major causes of kitten deaths before weaning?

Low body temperature (hypothermia)– Newborn kittens can’t regulate their temperature and rely on their mother (and their home) for warmth. In addition to causing kittens to fail to suck milk effectively, hypothermia can also result in decreased heart and breathing rates, which can lead to heart (cardiovascular) failure. The rectal temperature of kittens should be 35–37°C in the first week, 36–38°C in the second and third weeks and reach normal adult levels of 38–39°C by the fourth week

Low blood sugar (hypoglycaemia)– Newborn kittens have high energy requirements but no energy reserves, so they’re highly dependent on the milk from their mother. Any kitten who is ill, stressed or has inadequate milk intake may rapidly develop low blood sugar. This may be seen as weakness, hypothermia, crying, difficulty breathing and ultimately seizures, coma and death

Dehydration– Young kittens are at high risk of dehydration because their bodies have a higher water content, and they’re much less able to regulate water loss compared with adult cats. The normal fluid requirements of a newborn kitten are around 130–220 ml/kg/24h compared with just 50–65 ml/kg/24h for an adult cat. Without adequate milk intake, or if the kitten suffers an excessive fluid loss (eg, diarrhoea), dehydration can occur quickly

Common causes of ‘fading kittens’

Birth and queen-related factors

Kittens born after difficult birthing have a higher risk of death usually due to a lack of oxygen (hypoxia) and/or trauma. Studies have shown that cats with extreme body conformation such as ‘flat-faced’ Persians experience more birth difficulties, probably as a combined result of the kittens being relatively large (with large heads) and the pelvis of these cats being narrow. Lack of oxygen during birth can result in stillbirth, or the birth of weak kittens that fail to suck

Kitten deaths also tend to be higher in a queen’s first litter, possibly due to inexperience, trauma and possibly cannibalism. Older queens (after their fifth litter) may also experience more problems

Kittens who don’t receive maternal-derived immunity, either because of a lack of colostrum intake or a lack of production from their mothers are at risk of death from infectious disease at an early age

Kitten deaths also increase with maternal obesity, and with other queen-related causes such as a lack of milk, mastitis (inflammation of the mammary glands), or maternal neglect

Congenital abnormalities

#### A congenital abnormality is a physical defect that’s present from birth. Such defects may be inherited or simply part of the kitten’s development. Common congenital defects include

- Cleft palate

- Umbilical hernia

- Skeletal defects

Severe defects usually result in stillbirth or early death. Milder disorders may cause fading kittens, or only become obvious later in life. Because inbreeding increases the risk of genetic disease, congenital disorders are seen more frequently in pedigree cats.

Low birth weight

Underweight kittens are more susceptible to hypothermia, dehydration, respiratory failure and infections. Ill health, congenital disease, infections, or a queen who’s not receiving adequate nutrition herself can all lead to kittens being born underweight. The average birth weight for most breeds is around 90–100g. Some breeds, such as Orientals, have smaller kittens, while some, such as Maine Coons, have larger kittens. Generally, kittens weighing less than 75g at birth are at a higher risk of death.

Inappropriate home environment

#### Factors around the kitten’s home can be important causes of fading kittens. These include

Extremes of temperature– providing kittens with a suitable environmental temperature is essential and a kitten that has stopped sucking milk will quickly become cold and develop low blood sugar (hypoglycaemia). Extra heating may be needed (and will always be needed if kittens are hand-reared) to maintain an environmental temperature of 29–32°C in the kitten box, although it’s important that the kittens can move away to cooler areas if needed. The temperature can be gradually reduced to around 27°C by 7–10 days and can go down to 22-25°C by the end of the first month

- Poor hygiene– will increase the risks of infectious diseases

- Overcrowding– will lead to increased risks of infectious diseases and malnutrition resulting from competition for milk/food

- Overhandling– this may limit the kitten’s feeding time, and with nervous queens, may result in cannibalism of her kittens

- Inappropriate nutrition

The nutritional demands of a queen producing milk for her kittens are extremely high. Without adequate nutrition, the quality of her milk can be affected so she must be fed high-quality commercial cat food to meet these needs. If you’re not sure of the best diet for your cat, speak to your veterinary team, who will be happy to help.

#### Inadequate milk production may be caused by

- Old queens

- Sick or malnourished queens

- Difficulty giving birth

- Mastitis

- Illness elsewhere in the body

It may sometimes be a family trait

#### Inadequate milk intake by the kitten can also result from

Anything causing ill health or weakness

Competition or bullying from other kittens

Environmental factors or over-handling that interfere with the queen-kitten bond

Neonatal isoerythrolysis (NI)

In certain breeds, neonatal isoerythrolysis (NI) is a relatively common cause of fading kittens, caused by incompatibility between the blood type of the queen and the kitten. For more information, read our article onblood groups and incompatibility.

Infections (viral, bacterial, parasitic)

Under normal circumstances, kittens should suck for the first time within 2 hours of being born. They absorb antibodies fromcolostrum, the first milk produced by the queen, during the first 16–24 hours, and they must suck well during this period to protect them from infections. Depending on the number of antibodies present and the amount absorbed, the effect of colostrum usually begins to fade when the kittens are around 3–4 weeks old. At this point, their natural immunity is still developing, and as a kitten’s initialvaccinationcourse is usually started around 8–9 weeks of age, there’s a period when the kittens are at a higher risk from infectious diseases.

Bacterial infections are often secondary to viral infections (such as feline herpesvirus [FHV], feline calicivirus [FCV], feline leukaemia virus [FeLV], feline immunodeficiency virus [FIV], feline panleukopenia virus [FPV], andfeline infectious peritonitis [FIP]) in kittens, but can be the primary cause.

If the queen hasn’t beentreated for worms, or if hygiene is poor, intestinal parasites (including roundworms and coccidia) can cause a problem in kittens. Heavy infestations of worms causing bloody faeces, lack of appetite, a pot-bellied appearance and weight loss can be life-threatening in some kittens.

## How do I care for a queen and her newborn kittens?

Generally, you’ll need to ensure the queen and her kittens have a clean, warm, safe and comfortable place to rest, as the queen will take care of her kittens, providing them with nutrition and warmth, and helping them to pass urine and faeces. You must take care of the queen’s nutritional needs and preventative healthcare, such as worming - speak to your veterinary team if you’re not sure what the queen needs and when. If, for any reason, the queen can’t take care of her kittens, or if she rejects them, you’ll need to take over their care.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you notice any signs of ill health with either the queen or her kittens, if the kittens aren’t sucking, or if they’re losing weight/not gaining weight as expected, contact your veterinary team for advice. They’ll also be happy to guide you on the most appropriate diet for the queen, discuss worming requirements, advise on what to expect in the first few weeks and when and how to begin the weaning process.

Additional considerations

#### It’s important to check that the kittens are getting enough food and growing as expected. For this reason, we recommend monitoring their weight regularly

Weigh your kittens at birth, daily for the first week, then at least twice weekly until weaning

Up to 10% of weight may be lost in the first 24 hours

After this, their daily weight gain should be around 10–15g/day (5–10%).

Kittens should double their birth weight by 1–2 weeks of age

Any weight loss (or lack of weight gain) should be investigated, so if you’re concerned, contact your veterinary team

If the kittens aren’t getting enough milk, you may need to consider supplemental feeding. This requires a lot of time and dedication - read our article onhand-rearing kittensfor more information.

## Glossary of Terms

- Queen– adult female cat that hasn’t been spayed (neutered)

- Weaning- transitioning the kittens from feeding on their mother’s milk to solid food

- Hypothermia- low body temperature

- Hypoglycaemia- low blood sugar

- Hypoxia- lack of oxygen

- Respiratory- of or relating to breathing, or the organs involved in breathing

- Malnutrition- lack of proper nutrition/poor nutrition

- Mastitis- inflammation of the mammary glands, often caused by a bacterial infection
