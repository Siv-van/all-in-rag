# Neutering your cat

If you’ve welcomed a kitten into your home, for the first few months your thoughts will be focused on their preventative healthcare needs and giving them the best start to life. It may surprise you to learn that kittens reach sexual maturity from around the age of four months, so unless you want to breed, neutering your cat is something you’ll need to consider early on to prevent unwanted pregnancies.

#### Homing centres (e.g. shelters, rescue organisations) are bursting at the seams with cats and kittens looking for their forever homes – neutering not only prevents adding to this situation, but it also curbs unwanted behaviours and reduces the risk of certain diseases. Let’s take a closer look at the reasons for neutering both male and female cats

## Why should I neuter my cat?

Protection from preventable diseases and health issues

Unneutered female cats can develop pyometra (infection of the uterus) and are more likely to suffer from mammary tumours (cancer) later in life. There’s also a risk associated with pregnancy and birth, and queens (unneutered female cats) with infectious diseases may pass these on to their kittens. Unneutered male cats (toms) are likely to suffer from fight injuries, such as cat bite abscesses, and are more likely to spread diseases such asFIV(feline immunodeficiency virus) andFeLV(feline leukaemia virus), both of which are transmitted through cat bites. Because they wander over a large area in search of a mate, they’re also at greater risk of being hit by a car.

## Control of unwanted behaviours

During sexually active times of the year, a female cat will ‘call’ regularly – usually around every three weeks if she doesn’t get pregnant. Unneutered male cats will roam across a large area away from home and may not return, marking their territory with a very pungent urine spray (sometimes, in the house!) and are much more likely to fight

Population control

To avoid adding to the number of unwanted cats and kittens looking for homes, it’s important to neuter a female cat before she can have kittens. The first season is usually around six months of age but can be earlier, and queens can have up to three litters per year. For male cats, it only takes one male in an area to make a lot of cats pregnant! Unwanted kittens may not be cared for and can be at risk of infectious diseases such ascat flu. It's also important to remember that males and females from the same litter (siblings) can and will breed with each other once they reach sexual maturity.

## What is neutering?

Neutering is the general term given to the surgical procedure to stop cats from reproducing. In female cats, it’s called spaying and in male cats, it’s called castration.

It can be hard to tell the sex of a kitten, especially when young. Read our article onhow to sex a kittenfor more information. If you’re still unsure if you have a male or female kitten, speak to your vet – this will always be checked by your veterinary team before neutering.

Spaying

The surgical removal of the ovaries and sometimes the uterus under general anaesthetic. You’ll need to withhold food for a period of time advised by your veterinary team before the operation and keep your cat indoors. Spays are usually performed through an incision on the belly or sometimes, the flank (side of the body - most common in the UK), and your cat will have a shaved patch of fur around the incision site. Your cat should be able to return home the same day and may have stitches that need removal after seven to ten days.

Castration

The surgical removal of the testes under general anaesthetic, through a small incision into the scrotum. As with the spay operation, you’ll need to withhold food for a period of time advised by your veterinary team before and keep your cat indoors. Your cat should be able to return home the same day, and as the skin incisions for castration are so small, stitches aren’t required.

Surgical neutering of a male cat, or castration.

Darker hair colouring is visible on the chest of this Siamese (left) after regrowing

In certain breeds, such as Siamese, the skin temperature determines the hair colour. When a patch of fur is shaved, as it will be for a spay, the new hair may grow back darker in colour. However, this is only temporary and, as further hair growth occurs, the dark hairs are replaced by normal, lighter-coloured hairs.

Cats usually recover from the neutering operation quickly. You may notice your cat is a little drowsy when they first come home, but in most cases, they’ll be back to their normal self by the following day. This is when it can be hard to keep them quiet, but we recommend trying to reduce how much they can do to allow internal wounds time to heal. Your veterinary team will advise you on how long you should keep your cat quiet and reduce activity.

## When should I neuter my cat?

Neutering should routinely take place at around four months of age, before cats reach sexual maturity.

## What are the benefits of early neutering?

It’s less stressful for a young female cat to be spayed than to have a litter of kittens early in life

It reduces the risk of certain cancers in female cats if they’re neutered before six months of age

Both male and female cats are less likely to pick up infectious diseases such as cat flu, FIV, and FeLV due to mating and fighting with other cats

Male cats are less likely to be injured from fighting or roaming

Younger cats are likely to recover more quickly and experience less pain after neutering in comparison to older cats

It can prevent sexual-related urine marking

It plays an important role in cat population control – nearly 50% of cat litters in the UK in 2023 were unplanned

It costs less to neuter your cat compared to the costs associated with caring for and feeding a litter of kittens

It prevents the nuisance of excessive noise when a female cat comes into ‘call’ (which is very loud!)

If your cat is a very large breed, e.g. Maine Coon, you may want to discuss the timing of neutering with your vet.

The Science: Debunking the myths of neutering

Historically, there have been concerns surrounding the early neutering of cats. However, recent evidence has shown that there are no disadvantages to neutering cats at around four months old. Early neutering doesn’t cause problems with a cat’s development, behaviour or health. And, thanks to improved surgical techniques, anaesthetic drugs, equipment and patient care, the risks associated with anaesthesia of kittens is lower now than it was several years ago.

Additional Considerations

It’s important to remember that once your cat has been neutered, they’re more prone to weight gain. Monitor your cat, and adjust their diet accordingly if you’re worried they’re putting on weight. If you’re not sure, speak to your veterinary team who will be happy to guide you on your cat’s ideal weight.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your kitten seems unusually quiet after neutering, contact your veterinary team. You’ll also need to monitor their wound and speak to your veterinary team if they lick or scratch excessively at the wound or if it appears red or swollen.

## Glossary of Terms

- Pyometra- a potentially life-threatening infection of the uterus (womb).

- Spay– removal of the ovaries +/- the uterus (womb).

- Castration- removal of the testes.
