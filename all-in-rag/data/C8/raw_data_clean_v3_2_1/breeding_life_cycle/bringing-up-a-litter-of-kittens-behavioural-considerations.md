# Bringing up a litter of kittens

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/bringing-up-a-litter-of-kittens-behavioural-considerations

---

Bringing a litter of kittens into the world carries great responsibility – the health and emotional wellbeing of your kittens as adults will largely depend on how carefully and sensitively you care for them in the first few weeks after birth.

Kittens are not only growing physically from birth, but they are also learning about the world and developing their own unique personalities. Raising your kittens in the right kind of environment with careful handling can help them adjust to living with humans and all the experiences this normally entails.

## What influences a kitten’s behaviour in early life?

The development of behaviour depends both on genetic and environmental factors. Genes “programme” a kitten with the potential to react in a certain way in certain circumstances. The kitten’s life experiences then influence whether that behaviour is ever actually expressed and to what level. There’s often a significant difference in friendliness and general responses between littermates that can only be explained by a difference in genetic programming since environmental influences are new and relatively constant for all the kittens. The behavioural characteristics of both parents will also influence the behaviour of their offspring.

The effectiveness of the socialisation you provide for your kittens will depend on their underlying personality types. While timid kittens still benefit from socialisation, the overall effect in terms of interaction with people is less when compared to more confident kittens.

As your kittens grow, they will learn a great deal from their mother by watching her behaviour. They will also learn social and hunting skills by playing with their siblings. There is, however, a big role for you, and it’s important to give your kittens positive experiences of humans so that they enjoy their lives as pet cats.

## How do I socialise my kitten and help them prepare for life in a new home?

The ‘sensitive period’ for socialisation

The sensitive period of a cat’s life is when certain events are more likely to have long-term effects on their development. The key socialisation period for kittens is between two and seven weeks of age. Relationships that depend on familiarity are formed at this time, so humans and other species, such as dogs, may be brought into the cat’s social group and responded to with affection.

It’s also important that kittens learn to accept non-threatening objects and activities during this period. Without new things to explore and new experiences, kittens can find it hard to cope with new things later in life. They can be at risk of developing behavioural problems (such asurine marking) relating toanxietyand fear of things which are normal in a new home. The aim of anyone bringing up a litter of kittens is to ensure they can take new things in their stride. This is achieved by gradually introducing them to a variety of things from the home environment and allowing them to investigate with confidence.

It is important kittens are socialised with different people

Getting kittens ready for life as a pet in a home with people

Between the ages of two and seven weeks, get your kitten used to being handled, lifted and gently restrained. This helps prepare them for the type of handling expected by most owners and reduces the potential stress of living with people. Handling and interactions should also feel positive for your kitten – so should be gentle, sensitive and at a time when your kitten appears receptive.

Meeting lots of different people is important to avoid your kitten accepting just one or two individuals, rather than people in general. Research has shown that kittens who are handled by four or five different handlers (male, female, adults and children) before they are seven weeks of age will be more sociable to people as adult cats and more inclined to initiate social interaction with them. In comparison, cats that are only handled by one individual will be particularly affectionate towards them but will be suspicious of other people and may avoid them.

The amount of time kittens spend in social interaction with people is important, and human handling for a total of 30-60 minutes per day, in multiple short sessions, will help your kittens feel more confident to approach and more inclined to remain in close physical contact. Handling in the presence of their mother and littermates will also help this to be a positive experience for your kitten. However, if the mother cat has been poorly socialised and reacts negatively towards people this will adversely influence the socialisation process, and it would be better to move her from the room while her kittens are being handled.

During the socialisation period kittens should be gently introduced to novel objects in the home, such as their cat carrier

#### Part of the socialisation process also includes exposing your kittens to as many sights, sounds, tastes, textures and smells as possible that they may come across in a normal home. For example

- Vacuum cleaners, washing machines, tumble dryers

- Cat carriers, baskets

- Carpets, wood floors, linoleum

- Litter materials (wood litter, recycled paper, corn-based, etc)

- Music, television

- Wet and dry cat food

- Car journeys

All of these novel objects and experiences should be introduced gradually and gently to avoid overloading your kittens with too much stimulation at once. Choosing a time when your kitten is active and responsive, and building up the experiences slowly will have a more positive outcome. Remember, this is an investment in your kittens’ futures, as the effort you make at this stage will help them cope well with the challenges of everyday life.
