# Preparing for your new cat or kitten

After deciding to welcome a cat or kitten into your home, preparation for your new arrival is key.

By fully researching and understanding their needs, you can help your new cat or kitten live a happy, healthy life.

## What can I do to make my cat feel safe at home?

As owners, we need to ensure that it’s somewhere they feel safe and secure.

Safe territory

Cats should never be disturbed when they are hidden away in a safe space that provides them with perception of safety

Though we may think of cats as domesticated, we need to remember that they are often solitary animals. Naturally, cats are territorial. A wild cat or a feral cat (a domestic cat which lives a wild lifestyle without the direct help of humans) will have a large territory to roam in, a smaller area that they will defend vigorously, and a small den where they feel safe. That instinct still influences cats today.

Think about the ‘den’ we call our homes, where we aim to give our cats safety. We think they’re safe inside and they may feel that way too. However, we may be unaware that another cat is ‘visiting’ through the cat flap or an open window or causing anxiety by looking through the windows near to your cat’s litter tray. Perhaps the neighbour’s cat waits outside the cat flap to pounce on your cat as it emerges. All of these can make your cat feel threatened and insecure.

If you’re aware of the importance of these aspects, you can take measures to ensure your cat feels secure, for example, positioning the litter tray in a safe place and preventing other cats from coming in. If your cat uses a cat flap, ensure that it only unlocks for them (e.g. by using a microchip cat flap).

For more information and ideas, read our article onmaking your home cat friendlyso you can consider all areas of your home when it comes to your cat.

## What facilities will my cat or kitten need?

We all know that a cat needs food and water and, if it stays indoors or is fearful of going out, a litter tray. However, there’s more to it than that. Often, we line up the food, water and litter tray so that they’re all close together and convenient for our cats to find and use. However, given the choice, your cat will drink well away from where they eat and will eat and drink far away from where they go to the toilet. Understanding your cat means understanding the need to separate these important resources.

A cat flap? A cat flap allows your cat free access to the outdoors without having to leave a door or window open, so the house is still secure and your cat doesn’t have to stay outdoors until they’re let in. There are a wide range of cat flaps available, simple flaps that are also lockable in or out, flaps that are locked until opened by a magnet or an electronic key on the cat’s collar, and cat flaps that are triggered by the cat’s microchip (or can be programmed for several microchips if you have multiple cats). In areas where there’s a high population of cats, we recommend choosing a cat flap which stops strange cats from coming into the house. Additional considerations

## Keeping your cat safe

If you haven’t had a cat or kitten before, or if it’s a long time since you had one, do a quick check of the house to highlight any potential dangers. There are things which are poisonous to cats, products which will burn paws or skin, and situations to avoid. To prepare for a safe, hazard-free home for your cat, read our articles onhousehold hazards, poisons, andpoisonous plants.

Multi-cat households

In homes with more than one cat, it’s important to provide enough key resources so your cats don’t have to share if they don’t want to. As a minimum, for all key resources (food and water bowls, litter trays, beds, scratching posts etc.), you should provide one per cat, plus one extra.
