# Liver and gall bladder disease in cats

---

The liver is a vital organ found in the belly, just behind the diaphragm. It has a very wide-ranging role in maintaining normal health and metabolism in cats through a variety of functions. The liver has a good blood supply and receives blood from the gut (intestines), so can be affected by toxins and poisons, and diseases of other organs (eg, hyperthyroidism, intestines) can also affect the liver.

The gall bladder is a sac sitting within the liver, and contains bile, a substance needed for digestion. The gall bladder is connected to the intestines via a tube called the bile duct. The gall bladder and bile duct can be affected by various problems and cause disease in cats.

Liver and gall bladder disease explained

Cats can suffer from a wide range of liver and gall bladder diseases, which is why further tests can be vital in finding the underlying cause and the most appropriate treatment for your cat.

#### Some conditions include

- Neutrophilic cholangitis

This condition is caused by a bacterial infection within the bile duct and gall bladder spreading to the liver which causes inflammation.

Extra-hepatic bile duct obstructions

In some cases of neutrophilic cholangitis, and with other causes such as pancreatitis and tumours, the bile duct connecting the gall bladder to the intestine can become inflamed or even blocked.

Lymphocytic cholangitis

This is a non-infectious inflammatory liver disease.

Hepatic lipidosis

This is a condition where large amounts of fat build up within liver cells, causing widespread swelling and damage to the liver.

Toxic liver damage

Cats are very prone to liver damage caused by a wide range of drugs or toxins that may be safe in other species, as they lack the important pathways that break these down into harmless chemicals. Because of this sensitivity, you must always consult your veterinary team before giving your cat any medications.

Liver tumours

Many different tumours can occur within the liver – some just affecting the liver (primary liver tumours), some that have spread to the liver from elsewhere (secondary liver tumours).

Amyloidosis and hepatic peliosis

Amyloidosis is a disease where a type of protein is deposited within the liver, making the cat vulnerable to liver rupture and bleeding into the belly. Some cats are genetically predisposed to this condition.

Hepatic peliosis is an unusual condition where multiple blood-filled spaces develop within the liver. As with amyloidosis, the liver may become very fragile and can spontaneously rupture, causing bleeding into the belly.

Other liver diseases

Many other diseases can affect the liver in cats, including portosystemic shunts (abnormal blood vessels the cat is born with), toxoplasmosis, andfeline infectious peritonitis, to name a few.

Present from birth, a Portosystemic shuntis an abnormal blood vessel from the intestine, bypassing the liver and causing illness.

## What causes liver or gall bladder disease in cats?

A variety of causes can lead to liver disease in cats. As mentioned previously, it could be due to bacterial, viral, or fungal infections, inflammatory conditions, toxins, cancers, a portosystemic shunt, or other diseases and disorders.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team for an appointment if your cat is showing any of the signs listed above, or if you’re worried about their health in general.

How your vet will diagnose liver or gall bladder disease

As the clinical signs of liver and gall bladder disease are often non-specific, blood and urine tests are usually needed to identify that liver disease is the underlying cause of the cat's illness.

Veterinary physical examination of the eye looking for signs of jaundice (yellowing of the eye)

While results of blood tests may help confirm the presence of liver or gall bladder disease, it can be difficult to assess the severity or extent of disease from these tests alone, and they won’t reveal the underlying cause. Some of the changes on blood tests can also occur with other diseases, so further tests may be needed to rule these out.

An ultrasound scan (and/or occasionally an x-ray) of the liver can also help to narrow down the possible causes of liver or gall bladder disease. Sometimes, to determine the exact cause and find the most appropriate treatment, a liver biopsy or sample of bile from the gall bladder is needed. Before taking a sample, blood clotting function should be checked, and sedation or anaesthesia will be needed. Samples can be taken at surgery or with a needle guided by ultrasound.

Cat having a liver ultrasound scan under sedation

## How is liver and gall bladder disease treated?

Treatment of liver and gall bladder disease depends very much on the cause, which is why additional tests such as a liver biopsy or bile sample are often needed. In addition to specific treatments, supportive treatment may help, including a drip when cats are dehydrated, nutritional support with medications to encourage appetite and treat nausea or even a feeding tube, and drugs that help to support liver function or blood clotting. Occasionally, surgery is needed to relieve blockages in the bile duct or to remove the gall bladder.

Cats with portosystemic shunts may need medication and sometimes surgery to tie off the abnormal vessel.

## FAQ

## What’s the outlook for cats with liver or gall bladder disease?

The outlook depends on the cause of liver or gall bladder disease. If caught early and treated quickly, some forms of disease have a good outlook, and your cat can go on to live a happy, healthy life. More severe or advanced cases, however, may have a poor outlook. Your veterinary team will guide you through all potential outcomes.

## Glossary of Terms

- Gall bladder– a sac sitting in the liver holding bile for digestion.

- Bile duct– a tube connecting the gall bladder to the intestine

- Cholangitis– inflammation of the gall bladder

- Portosystemic shunt– an abnormal blood vessel present from birth from the intestine bypassing the liver and causing illness
