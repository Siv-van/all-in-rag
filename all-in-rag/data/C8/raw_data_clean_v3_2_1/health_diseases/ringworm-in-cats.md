# Ringworm in Cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/ringworm-in-cats

---

Despite its name, ringworm actually has nothing to do with worms. It’s an infectious fungal disease that can affect many animals, including humans. So, what should you do if you think your cat may have ringworm? Read on to find out.

Ringworm explained

Ringworm (also known as dermatophytosis) is an infection caused by a type of fungus. This fungus grows on the skin and feeds on the outer layers of the skin, hair or claws. The species of fungus that most commonly causes infections in cats (Microsporum canisorM. canis) can also infect dogs and humans.

## What are the signs of ringworm in cats?

#### Signs can vary – some cats will have severe skin disease, and others may only have mild signs or even no changes to their skin and coat at all. Some of the more common signs of ringworm include

- Roughly circular areas of hair loss, particularly on the head, ears or legs

- Hairs surrounding the affected area may be broken

- Scaly and inflamed patches of skin

- Ringworm can look very similar to many other skin diseases of cats, including flea allergy dermatitis. Some hair loss is usually involved, but the amount of inflammation, scaling and itchiness can vary.

## What causes ringworm in cats?

Severe ringworm affecting the face

Ringworm is a contagious fungal infection. During infection, large numbers of microscopic spores are produced around infected hairs. These spores are shed into the environment, where they can live for up to two years. Cats can become infected with ringworm either through direct contact with an infected animal, or by exposure to a contaminated environment or object (for example, bedding or brushes).

Spores stick to the skin, which is how a new infection starts. Intact healthy skin is quite resistant to infection, but any graze or break to the skin allows infection to develop more quickly. Ringworm is more commonly seen in younger cats (less than one year old), who may have poorer natural skin defences and less well-developed immune systems. Ringworm is also more common in longhaired cats, where spores become trapped more easily in their fur and are harder to remove. Some breeds, such as Persians, also have a genetic predisposition.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat is showing any of the signs we mentioned above, or if you notice any changes to their skin, hair or claws, make an appointment with your veterinary team.

How your vet will diagnose ringworm

If your vet suspects ringworm, there are three commonly used tests they may perform to confirm a diagnosis and rule out other diseases.

Examination under a Wood’s lamp: A Wood’s lamp emits ultraviolet (UV) light. When a cat is infected withM. canis, the infected area can fluoresce an apple-green colour under a Wood’s lamp. Contaminants in the hair and on the skin can also cause fluorescence, as can some medications, so further investigation may be needed

Examination of hairs under a microscope: Your vet may collect hairs and skin scrapings to examine under the microscope, and this can be really valuable. Fungal elements and spores can often be seen around infected hairs – these samples are typically sent to a laboratory for examination, as this requires specialist experience

Fungal culture: A sample of hair and skin scrapings can be placed into a special fungal culture test kit. This is the most accurate way of diagnosing ringworm, and also allows the species of fungus to be identified. The only disadvantage to this test is that it can take up to three weeks to get a fungal culture result.

If one cat in a household is diagnosed with ringworm, all other animals will need to be examined, and in most cases, all cats will be positive and need treatment.

## How is ringworm treated?

Treatment can either be given by mouth (anti-fungal tablets or liquids) or applied to the skin – in most cases, a combination of both is used.

Anti-fungal drugs

Anti-fungal tablets or liquids are usually given for a minimum of six weeks.

Topical therapy

#### Topical therapy can play a really important role in reducing environmental contamination and speeding up recovery in your cat. Treatments include

- Anti-fungal creams

- Anti-fungal shampoos

- Anti-fungal dips

- Ongoing care

- Decontamination of the environment and objects

It’s important to limit the chances of other animals and humans becoming infected with ringworm by decontaminating the infected cat’s home environment. If possible, restrict the infected cat to one easily cleaned room. While all areas of the house that the infected cat had access to will need decontaminating, ongoing efforts can then just be focused on the room where the cat is kept.

Objects such as collars, baskets, bedding, toys and grooming tools need to be disinfected (or disposed of if this isn’t possible). Cardboard boxes can be used as disposable beds for cats receiving treatment, and should be disposed of at least once a week.

Managing infections in multi-cat households

In multi-cat households, it’s usually easiest to assume that all cats are infected and need treatment. For those that aren’t infected, the treatment can help prevent infection developing.

## FAQ

## How can ringworm be prevented?

Preventing ringworm is especially important for cat breeders, and while it’s impossible to completely prevent the risk, some precautions can be taken. For example, when introducing a new cat to the household, they can be tested using a fungal culture and kept separately from the other cats until a negative result is confirmed. We recommend keeping new cats separate before beginning to introduce them to resident cats anyway – so this would not affect that process.

## What is the outlook for a cat with ringworm?

While it can take several weeks, treatment is effective in most cases, and the cat will recover.

## Can I catch ringworm from my cat?

Yes – ringworm is a zoonotic disease, which means it can be passed between humans and animals. If you’re treating a cat infected with ringworm, wear protective clothing such as disposable gloves and aprons, practice excellent hygiene and don’t let children interact with infected cats. If you notice any skin changes after contact with an infected cat, seek medical advice as soon as possible.

## The Science

Collectively, the fungi that cause ringworm are called ‘dermatophytes’.

There are around forty different species of dermatophyte fungi. In cats, most cases of ringworm are caused by infection with Microsporum canis (M. canis), which can also cause infection in other animals, including dogs and humans.

Glossary of terms

Zoonoses– diseases which can pass between animals and people.
