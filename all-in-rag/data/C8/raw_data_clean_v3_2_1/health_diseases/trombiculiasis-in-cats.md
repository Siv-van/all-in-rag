# Trombiculiasis in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/trombiculiasis-in-cats

---

Trombiculid mites, commonly known as harvest mites, chiggers, red bugs or berry bugs are red-orange in colour and are barely visible to the naked eye – though the larvae swell to about three times their original size when they’ve finished feeding. The eight-legged adult mite lives in vegetation, but the six-legged larvae are parasitic and live on mammals (including people and cats) and birds. The larvae feed on tissue fluid and often cause intense skin itching and discomfort to cats in the late summer and autumn.

## What is the mite’s life cycle?

The first active stage in the life cycle of Neotrombicula autumnalisis the six-legged larval period, the only stage that affects animals. These larvae are present on plants and are active during the day, especially when it’s dry and sunny. When they meet any warm-blooded animal, they swarm and congregate in areas with little hair and thin skin e.g. armpit, groin, ears.

The larva cause irritation by feeding on animals before dropping off and burrowing into the soil, where they mature, and the cycle begins again.

## How do I know if my cat has harvest mites?

Harvest mites on the top of a cat's head and ear. The irritation often causes the skin to become red with papules (a small swelling or pimple) and crusted areas

When larvae inject fluid into the skin, it can cause a severe skin reaction. In cats, the irritation often causes the skin to become red with papules (a small swelling or pimple) and crusted areas. Areas commonly affected are the base of the ears (Henry’s pocket – the little pocket of skin on the side of the ear), the face (especially the chin and around the mouth), the neck and shoulders, under the front legs, between the toes, on the belly and around the nipples, vulva and scrotum.

Scratching, which may dislodge the mites (until more attach), can cause your cat’s skin to become sore and in severe cases, raw areas can develop, which can then become infected with bacteria.

If you notice any of these signs with your cat, make an appointment at your veterinary clinic. Your vet will take a sample from your cat (a skin scraping) to look at under the microscope, to confirm the presence of larvae.

## How do I treat my cat?

Spot on parasite treatment being applied to the back of a cat’s neck, parting the hair and applying close to the skin

Some flea treatments available from your vet will be effective, but treatment should always be carried out under the direction of your vet.

If your cat seems hypersensitive to the mites, they may also need medication for a short period to relieve the itching, until the mites have reduced in number.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Book an appointment with your vet as soon as possible if you notice any of the signs mentioned above, as the larvae can drop off quickly, making diagnosis more difficult. Take a picture of the affected area to show the vet the distinctive orange mites.
