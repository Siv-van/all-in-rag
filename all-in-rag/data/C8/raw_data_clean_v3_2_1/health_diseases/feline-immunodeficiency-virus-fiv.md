# Feline immunodeficiency virus (FIV)

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/feline-immunodeficiency-virus-fiv

---

Commonly transmitted through bite wounds, once infected, your cat will remain infected with the virus for life. After a period (that could last several years), the virus can damage your cat’s immune system, making them more susceptible to infection that they struggle to recover from (similar to HIV in people). FIV only affects cats - it can’t be passed to humans or other animals.

Feline immunodeficiency virus (FIV) explained

Bite wounds are a common transmission route of FIV and other infections

FIV is a virus that infects cells of the immune system, damaging them, or compromising their normal function. This may eventually cause a gradual decline in your cat’s immune system, meaning they struggle to fight off infections that a healthy cat can. In the first few weeks after infection, the virus replicates and may cause mild signs of disease, such as a mild temperature and swollen lymph nodes (glands) - these signs are so mild, they often go unnoticed.

After a period of time, viral replication increases again in some cats which is when they typically develop signs of disease. This is often around 2-5 years after first becoming infected.

## How common is FIV infection?

FIV tends to be more common where cats live in crowded conditions, or there are a lot of unneutered cats mixing together and fighting. Infection is much more common in outdoor cats and is about twice as common in male cats compared with female cats. Although cats of all ages can be infected, middle-aged cats (5-10 years of age) are more commonly diagnosed.

## What are the signs of FIV infection?

Inflammation of the tissue in the upper jaw of the mouth (gingivostomatitis)

There are no specific signs associated with FIV, but infected cats often develop recurrent bouts of infections or diseases that gradually get worse over time, and infections may not respond to treatment as well as would normally be expected.

#### Some of the most common signs seen in FIV-infected cats are

Weight loss

Recurrent high temperature (fever)

Lethargy/tiredness

Enlarged lymph nodes (glands)

Gingivitis and stomatitis (inflammation of the gums and mouth)

Chronic or recurrent respiratory, ocular, and intestinal disease

Chronic skin disease

Neurological (nervous system) disease (in some cats the virus can affect the brain)

Cancers

Other diseases may also develop such as tumours (for example, lymphoma) and other infections may cause more of a problem in FIV-infected cats (such astoxoplasmosis).

## What causes FIV and how is it spread?

FIV belongs to the retrovirus family of viruses in a group called lentiviruses. Lentiviruses typically cause disease slowly, so if your cat becomes infected with FIV they may remain healthy for many years.

Once a cat has been infected with FIV, the infection is virtually always permanent, and the virus will be present in the saliva of an infected cat. The most common way for the virus to be spread from one cat to another is via a cat bite. The virus doesn’t live long in the environment and is easily killed by common disinfectants. On rare occasions, the virus may be spread by non-aggressive contact between cats (for example, mutual grooming), from a pregnant queen to her kittens, and can also be spread through blood transfusions.

It’s not known whether blood-sucking parasites (e.g. fleas) can also spread infection. However, keeping up with regularflea controlfor your cat is important.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if your cat is showing any of the signs listed above, or if you’re worried your cat has been in contact with an FIV-positive cat.

How your vet will diagnose FIV

FIV can be diagnosed through a simple blood test at your local veterinary clinic that detects antibodies to the virus in the blood. This test is generally highly reliable, but if there’s any doubt, your vet may also want to send a blood sample off to an external laboratory for additional testing.

FIV positive blood test result

Kittens born to FIV-infected queens will test positive early in life as they will receive antibodies from the queen via her milk, but they may not be infected. For kittens with a positive test result, retesting when they’re 5-6 months old is recommended.

In countries where the FIV vaccine is available, vaccinated cats will also test positive on routine antibody tests, so alternative tests may be needed.

## How is FIV managed?

Many FIV-infected cats can live happily with the virus for a long time, and in some cats, the virus may never cause illness. Whether disease develops depends on several factors including the strain of FIV a cat is infected with, the cat’s immune response, and the presence or absence of other infections.

The main aims of managing an FIV infection are to prevent further spread of infection to other cats and to maintain a good quality of life for the infected cat. Some antiviral medications used in human patients with HIV infection have also been shown to help some cats with FIV infection but are complicated by side effects.

#### General and supportive treatment includes

- Neuteringall FIV-infected cats to reduce the risk of fighting and the spread of infection

Keeping FIV-positive (andFeLV-positive) cats indoors and keeping them away from non-infected cats. This helps prevent the spread of infection to other cats and reduces exposure of the FIV-infected cat to other infections. You may want to create a cat-proof enclosure in your garden to allow your cat outdoor access to enjoy some fresh air without coming into contact with other cats

In stable homes where cats don’t fight, the risk of spread of infection is low, but discuss with your veterinary team if cats should be separated and separate food bowls used for FIV-positive cats with regular disinfection of food and water bowls and litter trays

- Feeding good quality, commercial cat food and avoiding raw meat, eggs and unpasteurised dairy products

- Maintaining routine preventive healthcare (regular flea and worm control, routine vaccinations etc.)

- Veterinary health checks twice yearly. Your vet may recommend certain blood tests periodically to monitor your cat’s health

- Prompt diagnosis and appropriate treatment of any secondary or concurrent diseases

- Specific Advice

- For breeders

To minimise the risk of FIV, breeders should prevent their cats from having free access outdoors or having contact with cats allowed outdoors. Annual testing of breeding cats is recommended, and any new cats should be tested before being introduced. If any cats test positive for FIV, they should be removed from the breeding colony, and the remaining cats should be isolated and retested after 3-6 months.

For homing centres and organisations

Ideally, routine screening should be performed on all cats before homing. If this isn’t possible, priority should be given to testing high-risk cats (for example, cats showing clinical signs consistent with FIV or cats that are likely to fight). Neutering all cats before rehoming will also help reduce the transmission of FIV.

Advice for boarding catteries

Boarding catteries should be built and managed so that cats from different households can never come into contact with each other or use common exercise areas. In this situation there is no risk to other cats, and no reason not to board an FIV-positive cat.

## FAQs

## How can FIV be prevented?

A vaccine against FIV has been licensed and is available in some countries, though it’s not expected to give complete protection as there are several different strains of FIV. As mentioned above, a vaccinated cat will test positive on routinely used antibody tests.

## What’s the outlook for cats with FIV?

The prognosis for FIV-infected cats is guarded but depends on the stage of disease. Diagnosed early, there may be a long period where your cat is free of signs related to FIV, and not all infected cats go on to develop an immunodeficiency syndrome.

## Glossary of Terms

- Lymph nodes- small organs located in various parts of the body that help fight infection. Often referred to as ‘glands’.

- Respiratory disease- a disease affecting the lungs and other parts of the respiratory system.

- Ocular disease- a disease affecting the eyes.

- Intestinal disease- a disease affecting the cat’s intestines.

- Neurological disease- a disease affecting the brain, spinal cord and nerves.
