# Toxoplasmosis and cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/toxoplasmosis-and-cats

---

The risk of getting toxoplasmosis from a cat is extremely small, and most people are infected through other routes. Living with a cat doesn’t put you at increased risk, and with some simple, everyday hygiene measures in place, you can reduce the risk of infection (from cats and other sources).

Toxoplasmosis is caused by a microscopic parasite calledToxoplasma gondii (T. gondii). Found worldwide, T. gondiican infect virtually any warm-blooded animal, but cats are needed to complete its lifecycle. While cats are important inT. gondii’slifecycle, human infections commonly have nothing to do with the cat itself and often result from eating undercooked meat containing the parasite.

Infection withT. gondiimay not cause significant disease, but in some circumstances, can cause illness in people, cats and other animals.

## What are the signs of toxoplasmosis in cats?

#### Although infection withT. gondiirarely causes disease in cats, signs of illness can sometimes be seen

High temperature, eating less, weight loss, lethargy/tiredness

Breathing difficulties

Inflammatory eye problems (uveitis and retinitis)

Liver disease (hepatitis)

Neurological (nervous system) signs (eg, tremors or seizures/fits)

Less common signs of illness include enlarged lymph nodes (glands), vomiting and diarrhoea, and muscle pain

Toxoplasmosis in humans

#### In humans with a normally functioning immune system, signs of toxoplasmosis often pass undetected or only cause mild flu-like symptoms. It’s most severe in ‘high-risk’ groups of people with lower immunity, for example

Unborn babies

Babies and young children

Very elderly people

Pregnant women (because of the risk to their baby)

Immunosuppressed people – for example, those receiving chemotherapy

## What causes toxoplasmosis?

Toxoplasmosis is caused by infection with Toxoplasma gondii (T. gondii), a protozoal parasite that can be transmitted between animals and people.

The lifecycle ofT. gondiiis quite complex and involves two types of ‘host’: a definitive host (the cat, including wild cat species and domestic cats) and intermediate hosts (other animals).

Definitive host for T. gondii

Cats are the definitive hosts, meaning they’re the only animals whereT. gondiican replicate and produce eggs (oocysts) which are then shed in the faeces.

Most cats become infected by eating meat containingT. gondiicysts – this can include raw or inadequately cooked meat (eg, beef, lamb, pork) or, more commonly, prey species (eg, rodents such as mice or voles). A few days after being infected for the first time, the cat starts to shed millions of eggs in its faeces – this can last for up to 14 days. AsT. gondiiinfects the cat’s intestines, it also spreads through the body with a quickly dividing form of the organism called tachyzoites. The cat’s immune system will stop this, and the organism then changes into bradyzoites, which remain ‘hidden’ from the immune system in tissue cysts (most commonly in muscles). If a cat doesn’t produce an effective immune response, they may show signs of disease.

Intermediate hosts for T. gondii

Other warm-blooded mammals, including people, can act as intermediate hosts forT. gondii. Intermediate hosts can become infected but never produce eggs (oocysts).

When intermediate hosts are infected, the organism initially spreads through the body as tachyzoites (as with infection in cats), and then an immune response will result in tissue cysts forming (containing bradyzoites). Again, in rare cases where the immune response isn’t sufficient, disease may develop.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if your cat is showing any signs of ill health, particularly those listed above.

How your vet will diagnose toxoplasmosis

Making a diagnosis can be difficult, as the eggs are rarely seen in samples of the cat’s faeces. Blood tests may be used to look for toxoplasmosis antibodies and this, along with taking a detailed history and a physical examination of your cat, can help with a diagnosis. Sometimes your vet will need to take samples from the affected cat to look for evidence of the parasite.

## How is toxoplasmosis in cats treated?

Toxoplasmosis is treated with a course of antibiotics. If your cat is very sick, they may need further care, depending on their signs.

## FAQs

## How common is toxoplasmosis?

Infection withT. gondiiis much more common in outdoor cats that are active hunters and in cats that are fed undercooked or raw meat. In general, depending on their lifestyle, between 20-60% of cats will be infected withT. gondii, but very few will ever show signs of illness.

## How do people become infected?

#### In most cases, people become infected by

Eating eggs (oocysts) from the environment (eg, through eating contaminated fruit or vegetables or from gardening in contaminated soil)

Eating meat containing tissue cysts. Fresh meat is the highest risk, as freezing meat for several days kills most tissue cysts

#### Less common routes of infection include

- Contaminated water

- Raw (unpasteurised) goats' milk

- Dust particles (extremely rare)

- T. gondii and human pregnancy

If a pregnant woman is infected withT. gondiiduring pregnancy, there’s a risk that the infection may spread to the unborn foetus and cause severe damage. This will only happen if the woman is infected during pregnancy, and prior infections carry no risk.

## Does contact with a cat increase the risk of infection?

#### Research shows that contact with cats (or owning a cat) does not increase the riskofT. gondiiinfection in people. Studies have shown

It’s rare to find cats shedding eggs in their faeces

Faeces containing eggs are not a risk to people until 24 hours later

Stroking a cat will not spread infection from cats to people

The risk of infection from cats is very low, except in young children playing in soil or sand contaminated with eggs

Most people are infected from eating undercooked meat – especially goat, mutton and pork

Reducing the risk ofT. gondiipassing from a cat to their owner

#### Although the risk is very low, this can be reduced further

People in ‘high-risk’ groups (eg, pregnant women, immunosuppressed people, young children) should not have contact with or handle the cat’s litter tray

Empty litter trays daily, so that eggs don’t have time to sporulate (become infective)

Wear gloves when handling cat litter and wash hands thoroughly after cleaning the litter tray

Use litter tray liners if possible and periodically clean the litter tray with detergent and scalding water (which kills eggs)

Dispose of cat litter safely. For example, seal it in a bag before putting it with other household waste

Cover children’s sandpits when not in use to prevent cats from using them as litter trays

Feed only properly cooked food or commercial cooked cat food to your cat

Gloves should be worn when gardening and hands washed thoroughly after contact with soil which may contain infectious eggs

Fruit and vegetables should be washed before eating
