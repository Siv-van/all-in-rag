# Feline leukaemia virus (FeLV)

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/feline-leukaemia-virus-felv

---

Feline leukaemia virus (FeLV) is a viral infection affecting cats worldwide.

A cat permanently infected with FeLV is highly likely to develop disease related to the virus, which can cause suffering and death in cats. However, the development of effective vaccines and readily accessible tests (to identify infected cats) have significantly reduced the frequency of infection with this virus.

FeLV explained

Feline leukaemia virus (FeLV) belongs to the retrovirus family of viruses, in a group known as oncornaviruses. Oncornaviruses can cause the development of cancers, among other diseases (in both people and animals). FeLV only causes disease in cats and was first discovered in 1964.

For a cat permanently infected with the FeLV virus, there is a significant risk of developing many severe illnesses such asanaemia (reduced numbers of red blood cells), immune system suppression (reduced ability to fight infections and other diseases) and cancer. It’s been estimated that 80-90% of infected cats die within 3-4 years of FeLV diagnosis.

## What are the signs of FeLV infection?

Signs of FeLV are extremely diverse but can include pale gums

#### Signs of FeLV are extremely diverse but include

- High temperature

- Lethargy/tiredness

- Eating less than normal

- Weight loss

- Persistent or recurrent breathing, skin, and intestinal problems

- Pale gums

- Anaemia and cancer are also seen commonly with FeLV, with a variety of signs arising from these conditions, too.

## What causes FeLV/how is FeLV spread?

Bite wounds are a common transmission route of FIV and other infections

The virus is mainly spread in the saliva and potentially the faeces, urine and milk of infected cats. Infection is mostly spread through prolonged social contact (mutual grooming, sharing food bowls and litter trays where the virus may be ingested). However, the virus can also be transmitted through bites and if an unneutered (non-spayed) female (also called a queen) is infected with FeLV, any kittens she produces can also be infected.

When a cat encounters the FeLV virus, there are different potential outcomes of infection according to the cat’s immune response. Some cats will fight the infection and recover (abortive infection), some cats will become permanently infected (progressive infection), and others can partially deal with infection (regressive infection). Cats with regressive infections could suffer a relapse and develop a progressive infection if they encounter stress or other illnesses.

Young cats are more likely to become progressively infected, and cats become more resistant to FeLV as they get older.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if your cat is showing any of the signs listed above, if they’ve come into contact with a FeLV-positive cat, or if you’re worried they may have FeLV.

If your cat has tested positive for FeLV, contact your veterinary team for an appointment.

How your vet will diagnose FeLV

Simple blood tests are used by most vets to diagnose FeLV. These tests are quick, relatively cheap, and generally very reliable.

Occasionally, false positive and negative results happen, so if an unexpected result occurs, your vet may need to send a blood sample to a specialist laboratory for testing.

Sometimes repeat testing is needed after several weeks to establish if the cat is infected.

## How is FeLV treated?

#### There is no cure for FeLV, and management is largely aimed at supporting your cat and treating any signs. This includes

- Quick diagnosis and treatment of secondary infections

## Keeping your cat on a high-quality diet and avoiding raw foods that may carry a health risk

Keeping up with good preventative healthcare - routine vet visits at least twice a year and regular worming, flea treatments and vaccination

Keeping infected cats indoors to prevent the spread of infection to other cats and to reduce exposure to other viruses or infectious diseases

In some cases, supportive therapy may include the use of blood transfusions and drugs to manageanaemia

Chemotherapy – may be used to manage FeLV-associatedlymphomas

Occasionally, antiviral drugs are used but these often have side effects.

#### Efforts should be made to prevent cats being exposed to FeLV. These include

- Where possible, the FeLV andFIVstatus of any cat should be known

- Ideally, FeLV (or FIV) positive cats are kept separately in a home, but other strategies, including vaccinated uninfected cats, can be carried out, and should always be combined with methods to reduce stress in multi-cat homes

- Any FeLV (or FIV) positive cat should be kept indoors to prevent the spread of infection to other cats. Garden fencing and pens can be used to facilitate some outdoor access

- FeLVvaccinationhas been proven to be successful. Several vaccines are available that provide a valuable level of protection against infection

## FAQs

## How can FeLV be prevented?

Vaccinating your cat can help to protect them from FeLV. In addition, cats that are infected with FeLV should be kept indoors and away from other cats.

## What’s the outlook for cats with FeLV?

Infected cats may still have a good quality of life for a period, but due to the many health problems associated with FeLV, cats with progressive infection have a poor prognosis.
