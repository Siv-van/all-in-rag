# Hyperthyroidism in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/hyperthyroidism-in-cats

---

Hyperthyroidism is caused by an increase in the production of thyroid hormones from the thyroid glands, which are situated in your cat’s neck.

Thyroid hormones are responsible for regulating many body processes and when too much hormone is produced, cats can show severe signs and become seriously ill. Thyroid hormones also help control the body’s metabolic rate and cats with hyperthyroidism tend to burn up energy too rapidly and often suffer weight loss, despite having an increased appetite and eating more than normal.

Complications of hyperthyroidism

Uncontrolled hyperthyroidism can affect the heart, causing an increased heart rate and changes in the muscular wall of the heart that will eventually cause heart failure if untreated.

High blood pressure (hypertension) is another potential complication of hyperthyroidism, although seen less commonly, and this can cause damage to several organs including the eyes, kidneys, heart, and brain. If high blood pressure is diagnosed along with hyperthyroidism, drugs will be needed to control your cat’s blood pressure.

Chronic kidney diseasedoesn’t tend to be a direct result of hyperthyroidism, but the two diseases often occur together, because they are both common in older cats and untreated hyperthyroidism can cause or worsen kidney damage. Extra care is needed where both these conditions are present.

## What are the signs of hyperthyroidism?

Hyperthyroidism is usually seen in middle-aged and older cats, rarely in cats under 7 years old. Male and female cats are affected equally.

The signs are usually subtle at first and become more severe over time as the underlying disease gets worse. Also, as it is mostly older cats that are affected, some cats will have other diseases that can complicate and even mask some of the signs.

#### The ‘classic’ signs of hyperthyroidism are

Weight loss

Increased thirst (polydipsia)

Increased activity, restlessness, or irritability

Mild to moderate diarrhoea and/or vomiting can be seen in some cats, and some will be noticeably intolerant of heat and seek out cooler places to sit. Some (especially in advanced cases) may also pant when they’re stressed, which is very unusual in healthy cats.

Although most hyperthyroid cats show a good or increased appetite and restlessness, in some cats there may be generalised weakness, lethargy/tiredness and loss of appetite.

## What causes hyperthyroidism?

In most cats, hyperthyroidism is caused by a benign (non-cancerous) change. Cats have two thyroid glands in the neck, and in more than 70% of cases, both are involved in the disease and become enlarged. The underlying cause of this change is currently unknown, but it is like one of the two main causes of hyperthyroidism in humans called ‘toxic nodular goitre’.

In less than 1-2% of cases, a malignant (cancerous) tumour (thyroid adenocarcinoma) can be the underlying cause of the disease.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat is showing any of the signs listed above, or if you are worried about their health in general, contact your veterinary team for advice.

How your vet will diagnose hyperthyroidism

Although the thyroid glands usually enlarge with hyperthyroidism, this is not usually visible. Your vet will be able to tell the glands are enlarged by gently feeling them, although in some cats, there is no obvious enlargement.

To confirm a diagnosis, a blood test is needed to measure the level of thyroid hormones in the blood. Usually, measuring the thyroxine (T4) concentration is all that is required, but sometimes additional tests may be needed (such as measurement of ‘free T4’ in the blood).

Other changes may be seen on your cat’s blood sample – for example, liver enzymes are commonly increased with hyperthyroidism. Your vet may want to perform more blood and urine tests to help rule out concurrent problems, such as chronic kidney disease, and blood pressure should also be checked. If heart disease is also suspected, an electrocardiogram (ECG – chest x-ray or ultrasound (echocardiogram) may also be helpful.

Technetium scan

A technetium scan, available at some specialist centres, is a simple, safe, and easy procedure that can help confirm a diagnosis of hyperthyroidism - but importantly, will locate exactly where the abnormal tissue is in your cat’s body. This can be helpful if surgery is being considered but where there is no clearly identifiable enlarged thyroid on examination.

The rare cases ofthyroid adenocarcinoma (malignant tumour of the thyroid) are more difficult to treat but can sometimes be successfully managed using higher doses of radioactive iodine.

## How is hyperthyroidism treated?

#### There are four main options for treatment, each with advantages and disadvantages

Radioactive iodine therapy

Radioactive iodine (I-131) is a very safe and effective treatment for hyperthyroidism. It has the advantage of being curative in most cats (up to 95%) with no ongoing treatment. The radioactive iodine is administered as a single injection, usually simply given under the skin. The iodine is taken up by the abnormal thyroid tissue, but not by any other tissues, resulting in a selective local accumulation of radioactive material in the abnormal tissues. The radiation destroys the affected abnormal thyroid tissue but does not damage surrounding tissues or the parathyroid glands.

There are no significant side effects with this treatment, but because cats are temporarily radioactive, they must be hospitalised for a brief period after treatment as a precaution. Because this treatment requires the handling of radioactive drugs, it is only available at certain veterinary clinics.

Following treatment, occasional blood tests are recommended to ensure normal thyroid hormone levels are maintained.

Occasionally, cats will develop a low thyroid level after radioactive iodine treatment which needs to be treated.

Medical management (anti-thyroid drug therapy)

Anti-thyroid drugs are available in tablet and liquid form and reduce both the production and release of hormones from the thyroid gland. They won’t cure your cat’s hyperthyroidism but will control it either short-term or long-term. To keep the disease under control, medication will need to be given at least daily, and often twice daily.

Your cat will be monitored regularly by your veterinary team and the medication will be adjusted to the lowest effective dose. Side effects are uncommon, but can include a poor appetite, vomiting and lethargy/tiredness. This tends to resolve after the first few weeks of treatment. More serious problems (bone marrow suppression, liver disorders, or skin irritation) are rarely seen.

If surgery or radioactive iodine treatments are being considered, drug therapy is often used initially.

Surgical thyroidectomy

Surgical removal of the affected tissues (thyroidectomy) can produce a permanent cure and is a common treatment for many hyperthyroid cats. Occasionally, signs of hyperthyroidism develop again if previously unaffected thyroid tissue becomes diseased.

To reduce anaesthetic and surgical complications, cats should initially be stabilised with anti-thyroid drug therapy before surgery is performed. Any significant heart disease should also be managed. The major risk associated with the surgery itself is inadvertent damage to the parathyroid glands – these are small glands that lie close to, or within, the thyroid glands themselves, and have a crucial role in maintaining stable blood calcium levels. Because there is a small risk that temporary interference with calcium regulation may occur, cats are often hospitalised for a few days after surgery so that your veterinary team can monitor your cat’s blood calcium levels.

After surgery, your veterinary team are likely to recommend occasional blood tests to ensure normal thyroid hormone levels are being maintained.

Dietary treatment

Another option for managing hyperthyroid cats is to feed them a special diet that has strictly controlled levels of iodine. As iodine is used by the thyroid gland to make thyroid hormones, if there is only sufficient iodine in the diet to make normal levels of these hormones, this can help to control the disease.

This is an interesting form of therapy that is successful in some cats. It requires feeding a special therapeutic diet that cats need to eat exclusively to ensure effective control. This means they must not hunt or eat other foods or treats. Many cats can have their disease effectively controlled in this way and it is an alternative form of therapy.

Ongoing Care

Your cat will need to be closely monitored and, depending on the treatment, will need periodic blood samples to check thyroid hormone levels. If you notice your cat developing a recurrence of, or new signs of ill health, contact your veterinary team for advice.

## FAQs

## What is the outlook for cats with hyperthyroidism?

Successfully treated cats, irrespective of what treatment is used, usually have complete reversal of all signs of hyperthyroidism. Further evaluation will be needed to check kidney function, monitor blood pressure, and ensure long-term goals of thyroid hormone levels are being achieved, but most cats do well when treatment is given.
