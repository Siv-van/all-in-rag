# Cancer in Cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/cancer-in-cats

---

Cancer is a term used to describe a disease caused by a tumour – a collection of abnormal cells within the body that continue to grow and divide uncontrollably. This usually results in the development of growths or lumps.

Some tumours don’t spread to other parts of the body or to surrounding tissues – these are called ‘benign’ tumours.

Other types of cancer are described as ‘malignant’ tumours, which often spread to surrounding normal healthy tissue, and may also spread to other sites in the body (or ‘metastasise’).

Because of their more aggressive nature, malignant tumours are generally more serious than benign tumours and are generally more difficult to treat.

The most common sites of cancer in cats are the skin, white blood cells, the mouth, stomach, intestines, and mammary glands.

## What types of cancer affects cats?

#### There are many different types of cancer, some of the most common types that affect cats include

- Lymphoma

- Squamous cell carcinoma

- Basal cell tumour

- Mast cell tumour

- Mammary carcinoma

- Fibrosarcoma

- Carcinoma/adenocarcinoma

- Osteosarcoma

## What are the signs of cancer?

As cancers can affect any tissue in the body, signs can vary and there are no specific signs that automatically suggest a cat has cancer.

Generally, older cats are more often affected than younger cats. As many cancers grow over a long time, initially, your cat may show vague signs such as a reduced appetite, lack of energy and weight loss. In some cases, signs can also include lumps in or under the skin, vomiting, diarrhoea, and wounds that don’t heal.

Additional complications may develop as the disease progresses, which tend to relate more to the tissues or organs affected. It’s important that cancer is caught early, so, if you notice anything unusual with your cat, make an appointment with your veterinary clinic.

## What causes cancer?

In many cats, the underlying cause of cancer is unknown. During a cat’s life, they may be exposed to different things that can trigger abnormalities within cells that may lead to the development of cancer. For example, cats with white or thin fur on their ears tend to be at a greater risk of skin cancer if they’re not well protected from the sun. We also know that some viral infections in cats, such as feline leukaemia virus (FeLV) andfeline immunodeficiency virus (FIV), can make a cat more likely to develop cancer.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you notice anything unusual with your cat or if they’re showing signs of ill health, contact your veterinary team so they can guide you on when your cat needs to be seen.

How your vet will diagnose cancer

Your cat will need to undergo tests for your vet to make a diagnosis. Blood tests, x-rays or an ultrasound scan can help identify the location and/or spread of a tumour and any other disease. Your vet may also need to take a biopsy, either using a needle or via surgery, to send a sample to an external laboratory for testing.

With some cancers, magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) or computed tomography (CT) scans may be needed to either make or confirm a diagnosis. This type of imaging can be particularly useful, for example, in diagnosing brain tumours and assessing the extent of the tumour.

## How is cancer in cats treated?

Before starting cancer treatment, your vet may ‘stage’ the cancer. This is done through x-rays/ultrasounds, taking samples from local lymph nodes (glands), and blood tests, to find out how far the tumour has spread and what (if any) complications have arisen.

Some treatments are widely available in primary care practice, while others are only available at specialist centres. Depending on the type of cancer, your vet may suggest referring your cat to a specialist with expert knowledge and a greater range of treatment options.

#### There are three main forms of treatment for cancer

Surgery

Chemotherapy (anti-cancer drugs)

Radiotherapy (radiation therapy)

#### Which treatment is used will depend on factors such as

The type of cancer

Where it is in the body

Spread of the cancer to another site within the body (metastases)

What is appropriate for your cat

What is available/accessible to you

Surgery for cats with cancer

Surgery is the most common cancer treatment, and the one most likely to result in a cure. That said, complete surgical removal of a tumour may not always be possible, depending on where it is in the body, the type of cancer and whether it has spread.

Sometimes, surgery is used to remove some (but not all) of a tumour to improve your cat’s quality of life or to help with other treatments, such as chemotherapy and radiotherapy.

Radiation therapy (radiotherapy)

Radiation therapy usually involves what’s known as ‘external beam radiation’, which is similar to x-rays, but the radiation is much more intense. With your cat under anaesthetic/sedation, a beam of radiation is focused on the tumour. Ideally, the outcome is to kill off the cancer cells while causing minimal damage to surrounding tissues. In general, several treatments are given over a few weeks, with each session lasting only a few minutes.

Another form of radiation therapy called brachytherapy is sometimes used, where sources of radiation are placed within or on the surface of the body using a probe. This provides a more localised form of radiation therapy.

Radiation therapy is mainly used to reduce the size of tumours and may be used alongside surgery or chemotherapy. This type of treatment is not widely available and will require travel to a specialist centre.

Chemotherapy (anti-cancer drug therapy)

Most chemotherapy drugs work by interfering with the ability of cells to divide. Side effects generally only happen in 1 in 5 cats, but your vet will advise you of any expected side effects you may see before treatment starts. Some chemotherapy drugs are given as tablets, others are given as injections by your vet.

Cat being prepared for chemotherapy treatment

For many cancers, a combination of drugs is used to attack the tumour in different ways.

Unlike human medicine, chemotherapy generally does not result in hair loss in cats, although they may lose whiskers and the coat may change. Other common side effects involve the bone marrow, and blood tests will be needed before each treatment.

Precautions in cats receiving chemotherapy

#### Because anti-cancer drugs can affect healthy as well as cancerous cells, unnecessary exposure to these drugs should be avoided wherever possible. This includes unnecessary handling of the drugs, but also exposure to the drugs in urine and faeces produced by the cat being treated (and also other body fluids like saliva and vomit). With some simple precautions, this exposure and any risks can be reduced

Giving tablets at home– your vet will warn you if they are prescribing tablets for you to give at home that could be harmful. If this is the case, these tablets (or capsules) must not be split or crushed – they will have a protective coating designed to prevent you from coming into direct contact with the drug itself. Ideally, the tablets should be handled and given while wearing disposable gloves. If your cat spits out a tablet, this can be picked up (wearing gloves), wrapped in kitchen paper and then put into a specific waste bin provided by the clinic.

Dealing with urine and faeces– most drugs are eliminated from the body in the urine and/or faeces. Although the levels of drug being passed are very low, it’s safest to wear disposable gloves when cleaning your cat’s litter tray and place soiled litter in a sealed bag in the dustbin.

Dealing with soiled bedding– bedding soiled with urine or faeces should be washed separately from any other routine washing; food and water bowls should also be washed separately from your other bowls and utensils.

General and palliative care for cats with cancer

It’s useful to keep a diary of your cat’s behaviour, appetite, and any abnormalities you see, as well as a note of when (day and time) you give medication. This will help you and your veterinary team determine if any additional treatments or investigations are necessary.

Ensuring your cat is eating well is an important part of the care you provide. Read our article onencouraging your cat to eatfor some helpful tips on how to stimulate your cat’s appetite, and if your cat still doesn’t want to eat, speak to your veterinary team for further advice. In some cases, an appetite stimulant or feeding tube may be required.

#### Ensuring a good quality of life that is free from pain is the main goal in managing cats with cancer. Supportive therapy can be an important part of this, and treatments may include the use of

- Pain relief

- Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)– anti-inflammatory drugs which can also help to relieve pain

- Anti-emetic drugs– drugs that reduce nausea (feeling sick) and vomiting

- Antibiotics– if your cat develops a secondary bacterial infection or a very low white cell count (leaving them vulnerable to infection), antibiotics may be prescribed

- Never be afraid to ask questions and find out as much information as you can about your cat’s cancer and treatment options - if there is anything you’re concerned about, always contact your veterinary team immediately.

## FAQs

## Will my cat be radioactive if they receive radiation therapy?

Although radiation therapy is used to kill cancer cells, this does not mean your cat becomes ‘radioactive’ and there is no risk to people in contact with your cat.

## What’s the outlook for cats with cancer?

The outlook depends on the type of cancer, and the treatment options available. When treating cancer, everyone involved must have the same goals in mind; an improved and good quality of life for your cat without side effects. For cancers that aren’t curable, there may come a day when you have to consider euthanasia – this can be a difficult and distressing time, and having the support and help of your veterinary team, and also friends and family, can be invaluable.

## Glossary of Terms

- Lymphoma- cancer affecting the lymphocytes (a type of white blood cell)

- Squamous cell carcinoma- cancer commonly affecting the mouth and skin

- Basal cell tumour- cancer affecting the skin

- Mast cell tumour- cancer that commonly affects the skin, spleen or intestines

- Mammary carcinoma- cancer affecting the mammary glands

- Fibrosarcoma- a type of soft tissue cancer

- Carcinoma/adenocarcinoma- cancer affecting the intestines, but can also be found in other parts of the body such as the lungs

- Osteosarcoma- cancer affecting the bones

- Metastases– when a cancer spreads to other sites within the body

- Biopsy- surgical removal of a small piece of tissue

- Fine needle aspirate- where a small needle is inserted into a mass to remove or ‘suck out’ a few cells that can be smeared on a slide for examination

- Surgical biopsy– a larger portion of the tumour is removed at surgery and sent to the laboratory.
