# Urethral obstruction in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/urethral-obstruction-in-cats

---

Urethral obstruction – otherwise known as a blocked bladder - is a problem that occurs almost exclusively in male cats and can be life-threatening. Urgent veterinary treatment is needed to ‘unblock’ the urethra and allow urine to pass. Commonly, the condition results from feline idiopathic cystitis or urinary stones (urolithiasis) and stress can play a role in its development.

## What is the urethra?

The urinary system of the cat

The urethra is the tube between a cat’s bladder and the outside, and it can become blocked with material or spasm, preventing the flow of urine. Urethral obstruction must be treated as an emergency. Without treatment, it can cause kidney failure and can be fatal within 2-3 days. It is also incredibly painful, and your cat will make repeated attempts to urinate without being able to pass anything.

Contact your vet immediately if you think your cat may have an obstructed urethra, as this is an emergency.

## What are the signs of urethral obstruction?

#### Common signs of urethral obstruction include

- Repeated attempts to urinate without passing anything

- Crying or discomfort when straining to urinate

- Urinating outside the litter tray

- Increased agitation

- Licking around the genital area

- Refusing food and seeming depressed

- Vomiting

- In the days prior to these signs, you may have noticed your cat urinating more frequently, straining, appearing uncomfortable and possibly passing blood in their urine - the signs will depend on the underlying cause.

## What causes urethral obstruction?

#### Several underlying conditions can cause obstruction of the narrow urethra of a male cat, including

- A ‘plug’ in the urethra – this is usually a build-up of proteins, cells, crystals, and debris in the bladder that forms a plug that lodges in the urethra

- Urinary stones surgically removed from the cat’s bladder

- Stones (uroliths) – these form in the bladder but may become lodged in the urethra

Swelling and spasm of the urethra – inflammation of the bladder and urethra may cause swelling, and in some cases, the inflammation, pain, and irritation can cause the muscles around the urethra to go into spasm. This can block the urethra if your cat is not able to relax the muscles

## When to contact your veterinary team

Urethral obstruction is life-threatening, so contact your veterinary team immediately if your cat is showing the signs listed above.

If your cat can pass urine but has any other signs associated withfeline lower urinary tract disease (FLUTD), contact your veterinary team as soon as possible for an appointment.

How your vet will diagnose urethral obstruction in your cat

Collected urine sample with blood - a common symptom of urethral obstruction

Your vet will make a diagnosis based on your cat’s clinical signs and from a physical examination (in particular, feeling a very full and painful bladder when the abdomen is palpated). X-rays or ultrasound scans of the bladder, along with blood and urine tests, may also be required.

## How is urethral obstruction treated?

If your cat’s urethra is blocked, your vet will need to run some blood tests and relieve the obstruction quickly. To do this, your cat will need to be heavily sedated or anaesthetised so that your vet can locate the obstruction - sometimes, careful massage will relieve it, but more commonly a catheter (tube) needs to be passed into the urethra so that the obstruction can be cleared, and the bladder emptied.

## What happens after the obstruction is relieved?

Following removal of the blockage, your vet will remove the urine in the bladder. They will then either remove the urinary catheter, or, in severe cases, leave it in place for a few days to ensure your cat continues to pass urine while treatment for the underlying cause is started. Further tests may be needed, for example to look for other stones that could cause a repeat obstruction.

## What other treatments are given?

Further treatment depends on the underlying cause, the severity, and what (if any) complications have arisen. Your cat may need to be placed on a drip if their kidneys are affected, and medication may include anti-inflammatories, pain relief, and drugs to relieve any muscle spasms.

Ongoing Care

Preventing any further episodes of obstruction is the long-term goal. Depending on the underlying cause, this may include a special diet to prevent the formation of bladder stones and crystals, increasing your cat’s water intakeand encouraging frequent urination, and minimisingstress. Your vet will advise you on how best to manage your cat’s care moving forward.

## FAQs

## What is the outlook for cats with urethral obstruction?

Providing your cat receives treatment quickly and measures are put in place to prevent them from blocking again, the outlook is good. Left untreated, urethral obstruction is life-threatening. It is important to consider the underlying cause to prevent recurrence.

Following an episode of urethral obstruction, your vet may ask you to collect a urine sample to take in for analysis when they go for check-ups. Follow our recommended steps below to collect a urine sample.

## How to collect a urine sample from your cat

#### You will need

Non-absorbable litter (can be purchased online or from your veterinary clinic)

A urine pot (if you’ve bought non-absorbable litter, it may come with one - if not, pick one up from your vet)

#### How to collect the sample

Fill your cat’s litter tray with non-absorbable litter

Once your cat has used the tray, tip the litter tray so that the urine collects in one corner and use the pipette to collect a sample

Put the sample into the urine pot and seal the lid tightly

Write your cat’s name, the date, and the time the sample was collected on the label
