# Dental Disease in Cats

---

Just as with humans, dental disease becomes more common, and severe, as cats get older. In cats, it’s often associated with the build-up of dental plaque and tartar formation, leading to what’s known as ‘periodontal disease’ (affecting the teeth and surrounding structures). Other types of dental disease can also affect cats, often causing pain.

Dental disease explained

Plaque

Plaque is a soft film of bacteria, saliva and food that develops on the surface of the teeth. Left undisturbed, it hardens. Hard, calcified plaque is known as dental ‘tartar’ or ‘calculus’.

Tartar is clearly visible and looks like a cream/yellow or brown hard deposit on the tooth surface. Because it’s hard, it can’t be removed by brushing your cat’s teeth - a dental scale and polish (performed by your vet under an anaesthetic) is usually required to remove it.

A build-up of plaque and then tartar can lead to more advanced periodontal disease, with the most common types listed below.

Gingivitis

Gingivitis is inflammation of the gums. It’s extremely common, found in cats of all ages, and varies widely in severity.

Mild gingivitis– very common in cats of all ages. Mild gingivitis doesn’t affect the tooth root and dental home care, such as toothbrushing, can easily reverse most cases

Moderate gingivitis– also very common. Sometimes gum recession can be seen at this stage, as can gingival “pockets” - this is where the gum has started to separate from the tooth, providing a perfect site for food, bacteria, plaque, and tartar to accumulate. If tartar and gingival pockets haven’t already formed, again, it may be possible to reverse moderate gingivitis with dental home care

Severe gingivitis– this can be very painful for your cat and is common in cats with a lot of plaque and tartar on their teeth. Gum recession and gingival pockets may occur, and at this stage, toothbrushing won’t help. Your cat will likely require a general anaesthetic so that your vet can scale and polish your cat’s teeth – in cases where the tooth roots have been exposed, teeth may need to be removed

Cats around five months of age quite commonly develop juvenile gingivitis, and you may notice an obvious smell to your cat’s breath. This is usually due to permanent teeth breaking through the gums and loss of deciduous teeth (‘baby teeth’ or ‘milk teeth’), causing gum disturbance and inflammation. You may even find a tooth lying on the floor at home! This is completely normal and will usually take 4-6 weeks to settle down. However, check with your veterinary team if you notice any abnormalities.

Periodontitis

Periodontitis is a very advanced gum disease, and it’s more commonly seen in older cats. The gums are usually very inflamed and often recessed. Large amounts of calculus are usually present on the teeth. The ligaments surrounding and supporting the tooth are also diseased and have often begun to break down, exposing the tooth root and causing the tooth to be very unstable. Bacterial infection is common, and pus may be seen surrounding the tooth. At this stage, the tooth is so diseased that extraction is the only treatment option.

Feline chronic gingivostomatitis (FCGS)

Stomatitis means inflammation inside the mouth. Cats can suffer from a condition called Feline chronic gingivostomatitis (FCGS) where inflammation spreads from the gums to other areas of the mouth, particularly the corners.

The exact cause of this disease is still unknown, though it seems to happen when a cat’s immune system responds too aggressively to the presence of bacteria or other infectious agents in the mouth. Some cases are associated with persistent FCV infection, orFIV infection.

This is an extremely painful disease and cats often have difficulty eating, drool, paw at their mouth and show other signs of mouth pain – but remember some cats with significant dental disease may show very few signs.

Various treatments may be used, but in very severely affected cats, extraction of all of the cheek teeth may be the only option.

Feline Resorptive Lesions (FRLs)

Feline resorptive lesions (FRLs) are common in both young and old cats, with more than 70% of cats over the age of five years estimated to have at least one FRL.

A feline resorptive lesion is an erosion in the tooth, commonly formed around the gum line, but can also be found below the gum line in some cats. When examining your cat’s mouth, FRLs can be difficult to identify, but they appear as a small amount of gum growing out of the tooth. FRLs can be diagnosed using dental X-rays or by probing the teeth under general anaesthetic.

FRLs are extremely sensitive, and cats will often show signs of pain. Left untreated, they cause gradual erosion of the tooth to the point where the crown will fracture off, leaving the root behind.

Fractures

Fractured teeth need to be assessed individually before deciding if removing them is necessary. As a general rule, teeth that have fractured through to the dentine or pulp cavity (affecting the nerve and blood supply) are likely to need extraction, as the tooth will be painful and at risk of developing an infection and tooth root abscess. If only the tip of a crown is fractured, and the dentine or pulp cavity aren’t exposed, the tooth may not need to be removed.

## What are the signs of dental disease in cats?

#### Signs can vary depending on the problem but commonly include

- Bad breath (halitosis)

- Red, sore, and bleeding gums

- Reduced appetite

- Weight loss

- Mouth pain

- Pawing at the mouth

- Drooling

- Difficulty eating

- Tartar build-up

- Loose teeth

- Reduced grooming

Cats will often hide signs of pain, so it’s also not uncommon for them to show no signs yet have severe dental disease. This is just one of the reasons why regular health checks with your veterinary team are so important.

## What causes dental disease in cats?

#### Several factors may affect the development of dental disease including

- Tooth alignment

- Diet

- Infectious diseases

- Trauma

- Lack of any home dental care

- Chemistry in the mouth – the bacteria and other local changes in the mouth will have an important effect

- Genetics – some cats are likely more genetically predisposed to developing dental disease than others

- Tooth Alignment

#### Cats with misaligned teeth are more likely to accumulate plaque and tartar than cats with correctly positioned teeth. This is because the teeth aren’t cleaned by the natural abrasion that should occur when food is eaten and chewed. Tooth alignment is a common problem with

Certain breeds – very short-nosed breeds (for example, Persians, Chinchillas, British and Exotic Shorthairs) due to overcrowding in their mouth

Deciduous tooth retention – in some cats, deciduous teeth can be retained after the permanent teeth have grown through. If the adult tooth doesn’t push the deciduous tooth out, the adult tooth may grow at an abnormal angle, resulting in permanent misalignment

Trauma or congenital abnormalities – sometimes the jaw of a cat may have an abnormal shape, either because the cat has a congenital abnormality (an abnormality present from birth, such as an undershot or overshot jaw), or as a result of trauma (for example, a fractured jaw)

Diet

Diet may play a role in dental disease in cats, but the relationship is not clear. Some special diets are designed to remove plaque when chewed and may be recommended by your veterinary team.

Infectious disease

Some infectious diseases are associated with gingivitis and your vet may recommend testing forfeline immunodeficiency virus (FIV), feline leukaemia virus (FeLV) or feline calicivirus (FCV).

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat is showing any signs of dental disease, contact your veterinary team for an appointment.

Cats should ideally have their teeth examined by a vet at least once every 12 months, and cats that have had dental problems should be examined once every 3-6 months, depending on their condition. Generally, the sooner the problem is identified, the easier and quicker it is to treat.

How your vet will diagnose dental disease

Diagnosis of the exact problem can be made in several ways, including a thorough examination of the mouth (which may require sedation or anaesthesia for some cats), dental x-rays, and probing the teeth. Other tests, such as blood tests, may also be required.

## How is dental disease treated?

#### Treatment will depend on the problem but often includes

- Pain relief, to keep your cat comfortable

- Antibiotics, to treat infection

- A dental scale and polish under general anaesthetic to remove plaque and tartar build-up

- Tooth removal, if severe dental disease is discovered

Other medications for FCGS

- Ongoing Care

Looking after your cat’s teeth is just as important as looking after your own! If you can, regular toothbrushingis the best way to keep your cat’s teeth and mouth healthy. If your cat won’t tolerate having their teeth brushed, speak to your veterinary team about the other options available for home dental care.

## FAQs

## What’s the outlook for a cat with dental disease?

The earlier dental disease is detected, the better - not just for the teeth, but also to prevent infection affecting other body systems. It may be possible to reverse early-stage dental disease, but cats that have teeth removed (even if this includes all teeth) adapt very well.
