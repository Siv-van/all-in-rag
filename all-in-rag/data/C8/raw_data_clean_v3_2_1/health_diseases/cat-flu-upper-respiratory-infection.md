# Cat flu – upper respiratory infection

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/cat-flu-upper-respiratory-infection

---

Cat flu, or upper respiratory infection (URI) is a very common disease that can vary considerably in severity, and on occasion can even be life-threatening.

In most cases, the disease results from infection with feline calicivirus (FCV) or feline herpesvirus (FHV, or FHV-1) plus other secondary bacterial infections.

#### Cats more commonly at risk include

- Cats kept in large groups or colonies, such as breeding catteries, rescue centres and feral cat colonies

- Unvaccinated cats

- Kittens

- Elderly and immunosuppressed cats (for example, cats withFeLVorFIVinfection, or cats receiving immunosuppressive therapy) are more vulnerable to developing severe disease

- Cats under stress, which can affect their immune system

## What are the signs of URIs?

#### Typical signs of URIs include

- Sneezing

- Discharge from the nose

- Discharge from the eyes

- Redness of the eyes and blinking frequently

- Lethargy

- Loss of appetite

- High temperature

- The severity of these varies – in some cats the signs are very mild, in others, they may be severe.

FHV infectiontends to be more severe, often causing marked conjunctivitis and ulceration of the cornea (eye). FHV may also cause severe pharyngitis (throat inflammation) leading to loss of appetite; inflammation in the trachea (airway); and coughing. FHV infection can occasionally cause skin problems on the face

FCV infectiontends to be milder, with less severely affected eyes, but often causes tongue ulceration (and sometimes the palate or the lips) and pharyngitis (sore throat). FCV may cause transient arthritis (‘limping syndrome’), especially in young kittens, and in very young kittens, it can cause severe viral pneumonia

Although FCV and FHV are viral infections, secondary bacterial infections are common and can contribute to signs of illness, and even lung infections. While most cats will recover from URIs, on occasion they can be life-threatening, and with severe infection, the recovery may take several weeks. Some cats may also be left with permanent damage within the nose and may have persistent or recurrent discharge from the nose (known as ‘chronic rhinitis’).

In rare cases, a much more severe and often fatal form of FCV infection may occur. Fortunately, such infections are very rare.

## What causes URIs in cats?

More than 90% of URIs in cats are thought to be caused by infection with one or both of the cat flu viruses, feline herpesvirus (FHV or FHV-1) and feline calicivirus (FCV).

Some cases of URIs may involveBordetella bronchisepticaorChlamydia felis.

## How are URIs spread?

#### The viruses associated with URIs are spread in three ways

Direct contact with an infected cat showing URI signs (large amounts of virus are found in secretions from sneezing, for example)

Direct contact with a carrier cat shedding the virus

Contact with the virus carried on clothing, food bowls and other objects - the viruses may be able to survive in the environment and on objects for up to 2 (FHV) to 10 (FCV) days

## When to contact your vet

If your cat is showing any of the signs mentioned above, or if you’re worried about their health in general, make an appointment with your vet.

How your vet will diagnose an upper respiratory infection

Your vet will usually make a diagnosis based on the signs your cat is showing and after ruling out other causes. While not always necessary, confirming which viruses are involved is possible by taking a throat or eye swab from your cat for testing at an external laboratory.

## How is an upper respiratory infection treated?

Treatment of URIs tends to be about keeping the cat comfortable rather than treating the viruses. However, severe cases may benefit from antiviral medications.

Antibiotics may be used to treat secondary bacterial infections, and if your cat has severe nasal congestion and is struggling to breathe, steam inhalation or nebulisation may be used.

Discharge should be cleaned away and specific eye drops may be needed. Cats with URI should receive pain relief as they likely have sore throats and eyes.

Affected cats are often reluctant to eat – they will have a poor sense of smell and eating may be uncomfortable. Using soft, highly aromatic foods (for example, kitten foods or fish in oil) that are gently warmed will help totempt a cat that won’t eat.

If your cat won’t eat, they may need to be hospitalised and fed via a feeding tube, as poor nutrition will slow down recovery. A drip may also be needed if your cat isn’t drinking properly to avoid dehydration. Nursing can play a key part in keeping your cat clean, warm and comfortable.

Ongoing care

Most cats that recover from URI viruses will become ‘carriers’. Carrier cats usually show no sign of illness but may shed virus in saliva, tears and nasal discharge, and can be a source of infection to other cats.

Although almost all cats infected with FHV will remain long-term carriers, many of these will never shed significant amounts of virus. Others may shed the virus intermittently, especially during times ofstressor if treated with immunosuppressive medications. Some cats may show mild signs of URI again when they shed the virus, but most do not. Carrier cats in a breeding colony are a source of risk to their kittens, as the stress of kittening may induce shedding of FHV.

Most cats infected with FCV remain carriers of the virus and continue to shed the virus for weeks or months after infection, but the majority will eventually eliminate the virus within a few months.

## How can URIs be prevented?

Vaccination: The risk of URIs can be dramatically reduced byvaccinationagainst FHV and FCV. Although vaccination usually prevents severe disease from developing, it can’t always prevent infection from occurring so mild disease may still occur in some cats

Preventing spread of infection from an unwell cat is important and includes barrier nursing and disinfection. If you have more than one cat, it’s important to minimise the risk of infection being spread to the other cats. A cat showing clinical signs should be kept isolated from the other cats (for example, confined to one room). Separate food bowls and litter trays should be used, and ideally, your cat should be kept in a room that has very easy-to-disinfect surfaces (not soft furnishings and carpet) - check with your vet which disinfectant is best to use, and safest for your cat

Breeders and those working with unowned cats may need additional measures to prevent cat flu becoming a problem such as isolating new arrivals, testing breeding cats and early vaccination. Protocols should be discussed with the veterinary team.

## Glossary of Terms

- Conjunctivitis- inflammation of the conjunctiva (also known as ‘pink eye’)

- Pharyngitis- inflammation of the pharynx (also known as a ‘sore throat’)

- Anorexia- a sustained loss of appetite

- Rhinitis- inflammation and infection in the nose

- Pneumonia- inflammation of the lungs and airways
