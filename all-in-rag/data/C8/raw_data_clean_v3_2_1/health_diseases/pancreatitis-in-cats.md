# Pancreatitis in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/pancreatitis-in-cats

---

The pancreas is an organ in the belly that has several important roles in the body. It sits next to the stomach and small intestine, and is involved in making enzymes for digestion and insulin to control blood sugars. Sadly, ‘pancreatitis’ (inflammation of the pancreas) is quite common in cats.

Pancreatitis explained

Pancreatitis is when the pancreas becomes inflamed. It’s most common in middle-aged to older cats but is occasionally seen in younger cats. When the pancreas becomes inflamed, the digestive enzymes it makes can be released into the pancreas and surrounding tissues, causing damage and pain. In very serious cases this can be life-threatening, as it will affect other organs and blood pressure.

Location of the pancreas in the domestic cat

## What are the signs of pancreatitis in cats?

#### Signs may vary but the most common are

Eating less or refusing to eat at all

Vomiting

Lethargy/tiredness

Diarrhoea

#### Pancreatitis can be very painful, but as cats often hide signs of pain, the only signs you may notice are

Interacting less with your family

Sleeping more

Resting and sleeping in different places than normal

Grooming less

Affected cats may also lose weight and become dehydrated, and their coat may be dull and flaky. Occasionally cats with long-standing pancreatitis can developdiabetesand show signs of weight loss, drinking more and passing urine more often. Uncommonly, long-standing pancreatitis can result in failure to produce enough digestive enzymes resulting in weight loss and diarrhoea.

## What causes pancreatitis in cats?

In most cases, we don’t know what causes pancreatitis, but it can be related to inflammation of the liver and/or intestines, or secondary to other diseases. Pancreatitis can also occur after an accident, for example, if a cat is hit by a car and the pancreas is affected.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat is showing any of the signs of pancreatitis listed above, or if you’re worried about their health in general, contact your veterinary team for advice.

How your vet will diagnose pancreatitis

Your vet will make a diagnosis of pancreatitis based on the signs you describe, a physical examination of your cat, blood tests and further tests such as an ultrasound scan. Mild pancreatitis can be difficult to diagnose, so treatment may be given on the suspicion that this is the cause of your cat’s illness. Occasionally, a sample of tissue (biopsy) will be taken from the pancreas, often at surgery, and sent to the laboratory for testing.

## How is pancreatitis treated in cats?

There’s no specific treatment for pancreatitis. Your vet will treat the signs of pancreatitis and the underlying cause if known, and most cats improve with time.

If your cat has a severe case of pancreatitis, they’ll need to be hospitalised at your veterinary clinic and placed on a drip. Many cats with pancreatitis are reluctant to eat, and some may need afeeding tubeplaced to allow them to recover or treatment with drugs to improve their appetite. Cats should never be forced to eat, as this can have a very negative effect on their appetite and will not meet their needs.

Cats with pancreatitis may feel sick and in pain, and medication may be prescribed to help them feel more comfortable.

As cats with pancreatitis may have other diseases affecting the intestine or liver, specific treatment may be needed. Cats with intestinal disease may need to have their diet changed, but this should only be done on veterinary advice.

Ongoing Care

Your veterinary team will be able to recommend the most suitable diet for your cat, based on their individual needs. Cats with diabetes will need ongoing medications.

## Can pancreatitis be prevented?

We can’t specifically prevent cats from developing pancreatitis. As with all illnesses, maintaining a healthy weight, feeding a good quality diet, preventative healthcare (vaccinations, worming, flea control), and regular check-ups with your veterinary team are the best we can do to keep our cats healthy.

For cats with recurrent (repeated) episodes of pancreatitis, further investigations and treatment may be recommended by the veterinary team.
