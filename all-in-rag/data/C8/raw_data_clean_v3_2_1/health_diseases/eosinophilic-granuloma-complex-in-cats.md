#### Eosinophils are a type of white blood cell often involved in allergic responses. Granulomas are areas of inflammation (lumps or nodules), and a complex is a group of similar diseases. The three different forms of EGC are

Eosinophilic ulcer, or indolent ulcer

Eosinophilic plaque

Eosinophilic granuloma

## What are the signs of eosinophilic granuloma complex in cats?

#### The three different forms of eosinophilic granuloma complex have slightly different signs

Eosinophilic ulcer, or indolent ulcer

These lesions appear on the edge of the cat’s upper lip (on one or both sides, usually towards the front of the lip). The lip develops a distinct ulcer that may have a raised border. The lip can be significantly swollen around the ulcerated area and in severe cases, the ulcer can be large.

Eosinophilic plaque

These lesions can occur anywhere on the body but are commonly seen on the belly. They are often distinct, flat, red skin swellings. Hair loss and ulceration are commonly seen, and the lesions are often very itchy

Eosinophilic granuloma

These lesions can again occur anywhere on the body, and common sites include the mouth (on the tongue or palate), hind legs and foot pads. The lesions are often long, thin patches of raised, thickened skin that may be red. Hair loss and ulceration are, again, common, though they may not be as itchy

Eosinophilic granuloma on a cat's foot

## What causes eosinophilic granuloma complex in cats?

While EGC may be caused by several underlying disorders, in most cats, it is caused by an allergy. Common allergies include flea bite allergy, mosquito bite allergy and food allergy, although in some cats, the skin reaction can be caused by something in your cat’s environment (e.g., pollen, mould, or dust mites).

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if you notice any signs of a skin problem in your cat, including any of the signs listed above.

How your vet will diagnose eosinophilic granuloma complex

Your vet will take a detailed history from you and examine your cat. To diagnose EGC, they will need to rule out other causes such as fungal, bacterial, or viral infections, skin or mouth tumours, abscesses, and other causes of skin swellings. To do this, your vet will need to take a skin sample (biopsy), either using a needle, or via surgery, to send to an external laboratory for testing.

## How is eosinophilic granuloma complex treated?

Treatment depends on the cause – for example, cats with a flea allergy will need to be treated regularly for fleas, along with the house being treated, whereas a cat with a food allergy will need a food trial. Trials and treatments can take time (6-8 weeks in some cases) but are important to avoid unnecessary or continued use of drugs such as steroids.

Treatment of EGC lesions

Although there is no evidence that bacteria cause EGC lesions in cats, it is common to have secondary bacterial infection present. Your vet may prescribe antibiotics for your cat, and you may see a dramatic improvement – although it won’t resolve the lesion itself.

If no underlying cause is found, or if an underlying cause is found but can’t be controlled, the lesions alone may need to be treated. Small single lesions which don’t bother your cat may be left and will sometimes heal by themselves. If the disease is more extensive, treatment will be needed.

Steroids are often given, and, in many cats, the lesions respond well, and your cat will quickly become less itchy. The duration and dose of steroids needed varies between cats – in some, recurrent treatment or continuous therapy may be needed to control the disease.

#### If long-term (or high) doses of steroids are needed, additional or alternative therapies may be tried to help reduce the steroid dose and minimise the risk of long-term side effects. Other treatments include

- Antihistamines

- Immunosuppressive drugs

- Topical steroids such as sprays or creams (that should have minimal side effects)

- Whilst most cats respond well to treatment, some cases can be challenging and come back once treatment is withdrawn. Your vet can consult a veterinary dermatology specialist for advice if needed.

- Ongoing Care

If you can find out what your cat is allergic to, you can prevent flare-ups of EGC by avoiding what triggers a reaction. This isn’t always possible, but depending on the cause, speak to your veterinary team about the long-term management of your cat’s condition.

## FAQs

## What is the outlook for cats with eosinophilic granuloma complex?

Most cats respond well to treatment and if an allergic cause can be found, it can make preventing future problems easier. When flare-ups occur, your cat may need to be managed with medication as and when they need it.
