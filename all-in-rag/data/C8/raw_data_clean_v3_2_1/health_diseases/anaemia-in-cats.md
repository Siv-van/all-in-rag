# Anaemia in Cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/anaemia-in-cats

---

Red blood cells carry oxygen from the lungs to tissues around the body.

Untreated, anaemia can make cats very ill, and in severe cases, it can be life-threatening. Unfortunately, cats are prone to anaemia. This is partly because their RBCs have a shorter lifespan (around 70 days), and also cats can suffer from several diseases and infections that cause anaemia.

Anaemia can usually be broadly divided into regenerative and non-regenerative forms. Regenerative anaemias are where the bone marrow responds to the anaemia by trying to produce new RBCs to replace those that have been lost. In non-regenerative diseases, anaemia develops because the bone marrow fails to produce or release new RBCs (or produces too few RBCs) to replace those that are being lost.

## What are the signs of anaemia in cats?

#### The signs of anaemia include

- Paleness (pallor)– Usually seen as pale mucous membranes (eg, gums and membranes lining the mouth and around the eyes)

- Weakness– severe anaemia can cause weakness, and cats will often be lethargic/tired

- Heart and breathing– anaemia, especially when severe, is likely to cause an increased heart rate (known as tachycardia), and fast breathing (increased respiratory rate or tachypnoea), cats can also develop heart murmurs

- Pica– anaemic cats can develop pica, where they eat non-edible materials, such as litter, or lick non-food materials

- Jaundice– occasionally anaemic cats will also become jaundiced (yellow discolouration of the mucous membranes)

In addition to the above signs, cats may also show signs of an underlying disease (such as chronic kidney disease) that may be causing the anaemia. Cats that develop anaemia gradually over a long period often adapt and show fewer signs (until the anaemia is really severe), compared with cats that develop anaemia suddenly.

## What causes anaemia in cats?

Knowing whether the anaemia is regenerative (or not) will help narrow down the potential underlying cause. Regenerative anaemias are usually caused by either an increased breakdown of RBCs (haemolytic anaemia) or by blood loss (eg, from bleeding excessively). Non-regenerative anaemias are usually caused by an underlying problem in the bone marrow preventing or interfering with the normal production of RBCs.

#### Specific causes include

- Haemolytic anaemia

- Feline leukaemia virus (FeLV) infection

- Mycoplasma haemofelisinfection (feline infectious anaemia)

- Immune-mediated haemolytic anaemia (where the cat’s immune system attacks its own RBCs)

- Toxins, such as onions or paracetamol (acetaminophen)

- Increased fragility of RBCs, for example, with pyruvate kinase deficiency, a disease seen particularly inAbyssinianandSomalicats

- Low blood phosphate levels

- Incompatibleblood transfusions

- Neonatal isoerythrolysis– a condition seen in very young kittens where there is an incompatibility between the blood type of the kitten and the mother

#### Blood loss anaemia

Trauma

Bleeding from an ulcerated mass or tumour

Bleeding due to poor blood clotting

Bleeding into the intestines/stomach

Excessive bleeding may sometimes be very obvious, but can also be within the body or in the intestines (internal bleeding), where it may be more difficult to detect.

#### Non-regenerative anaemia

Feline leukaemia virus (FeLV) infection

Feline immunodeficiency virus (FIV) infection

Bone marrow failure

Red cell aplasia (failure to produce RBCs)

Leukaemia (cancer of the white blood cells that may affect the bone marrow)

Chronic kidney disease

Iron deficiency

Chronic (long-standing) inflammatory diseases (mild anaemia)

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if your cat is showing any of the signs listed above, or if you’re worried about their health or behaviour in general.

How your vet will diagnose anaemia

Your vet will diagnose anaemia by performing a blood test.

As there are several potential underlying causes of both regenerative and non-regenerative anaemia, further tests are often needed to find the specific cause. These may include additional blood tests, x-rays and/or ultrasound scans, or collecting a sample of bone marrow (under anaesthetic).

## How is anaemia treated?

Treatment depends on the underlying cause but can include antibiotics for some infectious conditions, immunosuppressive drugs (such as corticosteroids) for cats with immune-mediated haemolytic anaemia and iron supplements for iron deficiency anaemia. In severe cases of anaemia, ablood transfusionmay be required, and your cat may need to be hospitalised for close monitoring and care.

## FAQs

## What’s the outlook for anaemic cats?

The outlook for cats with anaemia depends on the cause. Many cats can respond well to treatment, but in some cases (especially severe non-regenerative anaemias caused by bone marrow disease, or cancers) the long-term outlook may be poor.
