Roundworms, and particularlyToxocara cati, are the most common intestinal parasites in cats and occur in cats of all ages throughout the world.

Toxocara catiexplained

Toxocara cati (roundworm)

Toxocara cati is a parasitic roundworm (ascarid) that infects cats. Not all infected cats show signs of having worms, but they can cause serious illness in cats and can be the cause of human disease. As the eggs can remain alive in the environment for several years, routine treatment for worms through your cat’s life is important.

Toxocara leoninais another roundworm that can infect cats, though it has less disease-causing potential compared to Toxocara cati and can’t be passed to humans.

## What are the signs of Toxocara cati?

Kittens infected with intestinal worms may have a typical ‘potbelly’ appearance, and, although some affected cats may not show many signs of illness, the presence of the worms is likely to affect their health. Worms may be up to 10cm long and live in the small intestine where they can affect digestion and a kitten’s growth.

#### Severe infections in kittens/cats may cause

Variable or complete lack of appetite

Vomiting (especially after feeding)

Diarrhoea, alternating with constipation

Poor growth and thin body condition

Passing a large number of (usually live) worms in vomit is also quite common. While adult cats often show milder signs, severe infections can cause signs of gastrointestinal irritation such as vomiting, diarrhoea and weight loss.

## What causesToxocara catiin cats?

Toxocara catican be passed to kittens from mother cats (queens) through milk. Cats can also become infected from eggs in the environment – either through ingesting the eggs directly from the environment, or from eating an infected ‘intermediate host’ (e.g. a rodent or bird).

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if you’re worried your cat may have worms, if they’re showing any of the signs listed above, or if you’d like to discuss a routine preventative care plan for your cat.

How your vet will diagnoseToxocara cati

Your vet will suspectToxocara catibased on your cat’s signs and worming status. To confirm a diagnosis, they’ll collect a sample of your cat’s faeces to examine under the microscope for the presence of worm eggs. Occasionally, worms can be identified in vomit or faeces.

As part of a routine preventative healthcare plan, you will need to medicate your cat regularly throughout the year – read our article onhow to give your cat medicationfor tips on how to make this a stress-free experience for your cat.

## How isToxocara catitreated and can it be prevented?

A variety of products are available to treat and prevent worm infections in different formulations (e.g. spot-on, tablet, liquid) in cats. If your cat is diagnosed with Toxocara cati, your vet will prescribe an appropriate treatment.

Ongoing Care

Routine faecal tests or treatment (when necessary) for roundworms are recommended throughout a cat’s life according to their risk of infection

## Are people at risk from Toxocara cati?

WhileToxocara canis (the roundworm species found in dogs) causes most cases of infections in people, Toxocara catialso has the potential to infect people and may be an underestimated source of human disease.

#### People are usually infected from the ingestion of eggs present in soil, sand or in contaminated food. Other sources of human infection are raw/undercooked meat from intermediate hosts (e.g. chickens or cattle). Visceral Larva Migrans (VLM)

Children, and in particular, toddlers, are at greater risk of VLM due to low hygiene and frequent exposure to areas (e.g. sandpits, sandboxes, gardens, playgrounds) potentially contaminated by the eggs of Toxocaraspecies.

#### Signs of VLM in children include

- Fever/high temperature

- Generally feeling unwell and lethargic/tired

- Abdominal pain

- Infection can affect the liver, lungs, heart, and central nervous system with a variety of signs relating to each.

- Ocular Larva Migrans (OLM)

OLM occurs at any age but more frequently in children aged 5 to 10 years. The most important sign is impaired vision up to a total loss of sight, followed by complaints of ‘seeing lights’, squinting and glaucoma (increased pressure in the eye).

Prevention of human infection involves covering sandpits to prevent cats from using them as a litter tray, hand hygiene, washing vegetables and good food hygiene, including cooking food appropriately.
