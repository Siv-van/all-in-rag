# Feline infectious peritonitis (FIP)

Despite FIP belonging to the same family of viruses as COVID-19, there is no risk to human health - feline coronaviruses only infect cats and can’t be passed to humans. Coronavirus is a common virus, and many cats will be exposed to the infection without developing FIP. A small number of cats will go on to develop FIP, due to mutations in the virus and the way their immune system reacts.

Signs of FIP

Signs of FIP depend on which parts of the body are affected and how the cat’s immune system reacts to infection with coronavirus. Young cats and pedigrees are more likely to be affected, but the disease can develop in any cat at any age.

#### Early signs of FIP are usually non-specific and can include

- Lethargy/tiredness

- Reduced appetite

Further signs can develop, sometimes days, weeks or months later. FIP can be divided into ‘wet’ and ‘dry’ forms, but cats are likely to have both at different times or a mixture of both. One of the difficulties in diagnosing FIP is that the signs are not specific to FIP, with other illnesses causing similar signs.

Wet or effusive disease

In this form, there is an accumulation of fluid within the belly and/or the chest. This can cause a swollen belly and breathing difficulties. The fluid accumulates because of damage to, and inflammation of, blood vessels, which causes fluid to leak from the blood into the belly or chest. The fluid tends to be thick and bright yellow in colour and contains a lot of protein. However, other diseases (including some liver diseases and cancers) can also cause a similar fluid accumulation.

Other forms of FIP

FIP can affect any organ of the body, so other forms of FIP without fluid accumulation (often called ‘dry’ FIP) can occur. The signs will vary depending on which organ is affected. This inflammation affects the eyes in around 30% of cases and the brain in around 30% of cases but can also affect almost any tissues in the body including the liver, kidneys, intestines, lungs, and skin.

#### Because of this, a wide range of signs can be seen, which can include

- Lethargy/tiredness

- Bleeding or cloudiness in the eyes

- Vomiting and/or diarrhoea

## What causes FIP in cats?

FIP is caused by an infection known as feline coronavirus. Coronaviruses are a common group of viruses that often infect the upper respiratory tract (nose and throat) or intestines in different animals.

Feline coronavirus is found in the faeces of many cats. In most cats, infection causes no signs or mild diarrhoea that settles without treatment. However sometimes, the virus changes (mutates) inside a cat, and if their immune system reacts in a certain way this can lead to the development of the disease known as FIP. A positive test for feline coronavirus doesn’t mean a cat has the disease FIP, just that they have been exposed to the virus.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if your cat is showing any of the signs mentioned above, or if you are worried about a change in their behaviour or health in any way.

How your vet will diagnose FIP

#### As there are no signs specific to FIP, and no simple blood test to confirm a diagnosis, it is a difficult disease to diagnose. Your vet may suspect FIP if

Your cat is showing signs associated with FIP (such as fluid in the abdomen or chest, fits or changes in the eyes)

Your cat is in a higher risk category (for example, younger cats, pedigree cats, and cats living in multi-cat households)

Your cat has recently gone through a stressful situation such as neutering/moving house/rehoming, which may increase the chance of FIP developing

Certain changes are seen on routine blood tests (such as high protein levels and markers of inflammation)

Your vet may perform several routine tests including blood tests, an eye exam, and imaging (x-rays and ultrasound) of the chest and belly. If any fluid has developed, taking a sample, and testing it can help with a diagnosis. Your vet may also want to take samples of any abnormal organs – for example, a needle sample of the liver, kidneys, or lymph nodes (glands) in the belly. Occasionally, a surgical procedure is needed to remove a sample of tissue for testing (biopsy).

More advanced tests such as brain scans (MRI) and further fluid sampling may also be necessary if your cat has neurological signs (affecting the nervous system, such as fits or a wobbly walk). As these samples require a general anaesthetic and advanced equipment to collect, vets try to be as confident as possible that your cat has FIP based on more routine tests.

FIP effusion fluid, collected in a syringe

If a sample of fluid is taken, your vet will look to see if it has the appearance expected with FIP (most often straw yellow in colour, thick and sticky) before sending it to a laboratory for testing. This is important to help rule out other serious conditions such as liver disease, other infections, and cancers that can mimic FIP.

Treatment Options

FIP was once fatal for most cats. However, in the last 3 years, significant developments in treatment have been made, with studies showing that antivirals can be effective in curing FIP in most cats (>85%).

Since 2021, legal antivirals have been available in the UK and Australia and are available for export to many other countries. You can find out more about these drugs in ourCaregiver FIP handout. Your vet is the best person to decide on the most appropriate treatment options for your cat.

Your vet needs to be confident that FIP is likely before starting antivirals as the treatment course is several weeks, so it should be reserved for cats with highly likely/confirmed FIP. In addition to this, inappropriate use of antivirals in cats without FIP could lead to coronavirus resistance problems, meaning that cats with FIP may no longer be able to respond to these novel antiviral treatments.

Cats with FIP may also require treatment in hospital if they are very unwell and may benefit from medications to encourage appetite and help with pain.

## FAQs

## How can FIP be prevented?

A vaccine is available in a few countries to protect against FIP, but it can only be given to kittens over 16 weeks of age. The vaccine is used for breeding households (especially those with a history of FIP), but by the time the kitten can be vaccinated, it is likely to have been exposed to FCoV infection - so the vaccine may have little value.

## Which cats are most affected with FIP?

Although FIP can occur in cats of any age, it is more commonly seen in young cats. Around 80% of cases diagnosed are in cats under 2 years old, and many cases are seen in kittens around 4-12 months old. The disease is seen more frequently in pedigree cats such as British Shorthairs, Bengals, Birmans, Ragdolls and Maine Coons. FIP is also more common in cats kept in groups or colonies (especially breeding households) as this is an environment where feline coronavirus infections are spread easily. Many cats that develop FIP are now in single-cat homes, despite coming from multi-cat environments.

Reducing the risk in breeding households

FIP is less common in household pets, so the risk can be reduced by getting cats from somewhere with fewer cats and by keeping cats in small groups (less than five cats in a household).

#### In breeding catteries, getting rid of coronavirus completely is extremely difficult. Because of this, it is better to take steps to reduce the risk of FIP occurring in the first place. Good practices to reduce the risk of FIP include

- Avoid keeping large groups of cats and having multiple litters of kittens at any one time

- Keep cats in small, isolated groups

- Have at least one litter tray for each cat, plus one extra, located in easy-to-clean and disinfect areas

- Keep litter trays away from food and water bowls, and clean/disinfectant them regularly (at least daily)

- Avoid stress and maintain good hygiene, nutrition, and preventive healthcare for all cats

#### Wherever FIP occurs in a group of breeding cats

Consider breeding from older cats, as these are less likely to be shedding FCoV

Think about isolating queens (breeding females) just before they give birth and keeping the queen and kittens isolated from all other cats until the kittens are rehomed

Stop breeding from any queens or tom cats (breeding males) that produce litters of kittens that develop FIP as there may be a genetic link to the risk of developing the disease

Carefully review management and hygiene policies

If faced with an outbreak of FIP, stop all breeding for several months

Note that the routine use of antiviral medications to prevent coronavirus shedding in cats without FIP is not recommended.

Reducing the risk in rehoming facilities

Good hygiene and avoiding overcrowding are essential for minimising the risk of FIP. Cats should ideally be housed individually or kept in small groups if this isn’t possible. Litter trays and cleaning/disinfection should be managed as in breeding households.

## The Science

Following the human COVID-19 pandemic, antiviral medications became available for the legal treatment of cats with FIP. Initially, a drug called remdesivir (given by injection) followed by GS-441524 as a tablet or liquid. Research in 2022 and 2023 demonstrated that treatment of cats with FIP with these drugs resulted in >85% of cats being cured. Following anoutbreak in Cyprus, other drugs have been used successfully. In many countries there are legally available drugs of known content and safety, and research is ongoing to look at the best dosages and duration of treatment.
