Feline acne is a skin condition in cats that’s probably more common than realised, as most cases are mild and pass unnoticed. However, more severe cases are obvious in appearance, and may respond slowly to treatment.

Acne and stud tail explained

A cat’s skin has two main types of glands – sweat glands and sebaceous glands. Most of the sebaceous glands are attached to hair follicles and produce sebum, an oily secretion, that waterproofs the hair and keeps the skin supple. There’s a collection of much larger sebaceous glands on the chin, lips, the top surface of the base of the tail, and around the eyelids, prepuce (skin covering the penis) and scrotum.

This cat has some common signs of chin acne including yellow greasy discolouration and flecks of black, greasy material on the fur and chin

The oily secretion in these larger sebaceous glands is important when it comes to scent marking, and cats will repeatedly rub their chin, lips, head (temporal area) and the base of the tail over objects. After some time, these secretions can build up on objects, often seen as black/brown greasy patches.

Chin acne and stud tail develop due to overactivity of the sebaceous glands. Stud tail is more commonly seen in entire (unneutered) males but can occasionally be seen in both neutered male and female cats.

As the hair follicles produce too much sebum (and possibly also too much keratin, the major protein in skin and hair), they become plugged with black sebaceous material, forming blackheads also called ‘comedones’. Secondary bacterial infection can lead to inflammation of the hair follicles, and pimple-like swellings to form, which pus may ooze from.

Problems in Persians – idiopathic facial pruritus

A severe form of this condition can be seen in Persians and is known as ‘idiopathic Persian facial dermatitis’. This can be very difficult to manage, with the skin showing a black waxy material on the hairs in a symmetrical pattern on the face, particularly the chin and around the eyes.

## What causes acne and stud tail?

Acne and stud tail are caused by too much sebum and keratin being produced, leading to a greasy coat and skin, blackheads and pustules.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if you notice any greasy or discoloured patches, sores, blackheads or pustules on your cat’s chin or at the base of their tail.

How your vet will diagnose acne and stud tail

Your vet will diagnose acne and stud tail based on what they find when they examine your cat. They may want to perform skin tests to rule out other skin conditions.

## How is acne and stud tail treated?

Treating feline acne and stud tail involves removing excess sebum, preventing blackhead formation and secondary infection.

An antibacterial wash, such as chlorhexidine, can be used for this, initially two or three times daily. In mild cases, no further treatment is necessary, but in cases showing extensive secondary infection, antibiotics will be needed. Severe cases may also need short–term treatment with steroids to reduce inflammation.
