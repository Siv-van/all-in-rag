# Feline haemoplasma infections

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/feline-haemoplasma-infections

---

Feline infectious anaemia (FIA) is a disease caused by a group of specialised bacteria, calledMycoplasmas, that infect red blood cells (RBCs). These bacteria or Mycoplasmasare collectively known as ‘Haemoplasmas’.

FIA explained

Haemoplasmaslive on the surface of RBCs and can sometimes invade the cell itself. By attaching to the surface of the RBC, they damage it, which can result in its destruction (a process known as haemolysis, or red cell death). If enough RBCs are destroyed, anaemiadevelops (low red blood cell count). The cat’s immune system may be partly responsible for destroying the infected RBCs as it tries to eliminate the bacteria attached to them.

Cats infected with Haemoplasmasmay remain carriers for long periods, possibly for life.

Bite wounds are a common transmission route of FIV and other infections

HowHaemoplasmasspread between cats is not fully understood, but blood transfusions, fighting, biting insects (eg, fleas), and from a queen (mother cat) to her kittens are the most likely route of spread.

## What are the signs of FIA?

There are several types of Haemoplasma, and not all cause severe disease. Infections, particularly due to a type ofHaemoplasmacalledMycoplasma haemofelis (M. haemofelis), may cause anaemia – which can be accompanied by a high temperature (fever) in the early stages.

Signs of FeLV are extremely diverse but can include pale gums

#### Signs of anaemia include

- Lethargy/tiredness

- Weakness

- Reduced appetite

- Pale gums (pallor)

- Some cats will also lose weight and breathe more quickly than normal (increased respiratory rate or tachypnoea) and have an increased heart rate (tachycardia).

These signs can be seen with several diseases that cause anaemia and are not specific for Haemoplasmainfections.

## What causes FIA?

#### At least three differentHaemoplasmaspecies have been found in cats

Mycoplasma haemofelis

Candidatus Mycoplasma haemominutum

Candidatus Mycoplasma turicensis

Of the three, M. haemofelisinfection is the most likely to cause severe anaemia, whileM. haemominutumandM. turicensiscause mild anaemia or no anaemia at all. Even withM. haemofelis, many infected cats won’t necessarily develop anaemia – but it may be more likely if the cat’s immune system is compromised in any way (eg, infection withfeline leukaemia virus (FeLV) orfeline immunodeficiency virus (FIV).

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if your cat is showing any of the signs listed above or if you’re worried about their health in general.

How your vet will diagnose FIA

Your vet will examine your cat and take a detailed history from you. A blood sample will be taken to confirm a diagnosis of FIA.

Infected cats should be checked for underlying illnesses, including FeLV and FIV infection, which may worsenHaemoplasmainfections.

## How is FIA treated?

Your cat will be treated with antibiotics. Occasionally, corticosteroids (glucocorticoids, steroids) are used along with antibiotics to prevent immune-mediated destruction of red blood cells.

In cats with severe anaemia, blood transfusionsmay be needed.

## Which cats are at risk of FIA?

Candidatus Mycoplasma haemominutumis seen more commonly than the other haemoplasmas but is mostly found in otherwise healthy cats. Mycoplasma haemofelisis less common but is more likely to cause anaemia to develop.

Adult male cats with outdoor access are more likely to be infected, and it can be spread through fighting. Fleas may also be able to spread infection between cats, but while fleas can carryHaemoplasmas, it’s not known how easily infection is transmitted this way.

Carriers

Although antibiotics can help treat anaemia with Haemoplasmainfections, they may not eliminate the bacteria. Many cats remain chronic (long-term) carriers for months or years. Carrier cats often appear healthy without any signs of illness, but relapses (recurrent anaemia) may occur if the cat becomes stressed.
