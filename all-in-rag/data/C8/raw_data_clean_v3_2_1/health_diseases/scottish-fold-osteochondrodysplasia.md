# Scottish Fold osteochondrodysplasia

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/scottish-fold-osteochondrodysplasia

---

The Scottish Fold cat breed has a genetic mutation that affects the development of cartilage. This mutation makes a Scottish Fold’s ear cartilage fold, so the ears bend forward, giving the cat its characteristic appearance, but also causes painful joint problems.

Unfortunately, the genetic defect that causes the abnormality of the ear cartilage characteristic of the Scottish Fold breed, also causes severe abnormalities of the cartilage of bones. This is known asosteochondrodysplasia– a general term for a disorder of the development of bone and cartilage. These bone and cartilage abnormalities lead to severe and painful arthritis. How severe the disease is will vary between one cat and another.

## What are the signs of Scottish Fold osteochondrodysplasia?

The signs vary depending on the amount of bone and joint damage. Some cats may show no signs of pain, others may have minor signs, which may worsen over time.

#### Your cat may show signs which include

- Being reluctant to move

- Abnormal posture and gait (walking stiffly and not curling up comfortably)

- Lameness (limping)

- Difficulty jumping up or down from furniture

- Short, misshapen limbs

- The above signs are a result of the varying degrees of painful degenerative joint disease your cat is suffering from - which can result in fusing of the tail, ankles, and knees.

#### Pain may also cause your cat to

Hide away

Have a reduced appetite

Have accidents outside the litter tray (if climbing into the tray is painful)

Groom less than normal

## What causes Scottish Fold osteochondrodysplasia?

Scottish Fold disease is an inherited condition, meaning it has been passed on by one or both of your cat’s parents.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if you’re worried your cat may be affected by this disease.

How your vet will diagnose Scottish Fold osteochondrodysplasia

Diagnosis will be based on your cat’s breed and by a physical examination and x-rays, which will help to identify the extent of the problem.

## How is Scottish Fold osteochondrodysplasia treated?

While there is no treatment for the disease itself, your vet can help you manage your cat’s pain with appropriate medication and may be able to recommend things you can do at home to make your cat more comfortable.

Ongoing care

#### There are some measures you can take at home which may help to make your cat’s life easier and more comfortable. These include

- Providing comfortable beds in accessible (low-level) locations or with steps up to access them

- Raising food and water bowls to prevent your cat from having to bend sore joints

- Using a litter tray with low sides, so that it is easier for your cat to get in and out

- Providing ramps to access favourite sleeping spots and placing rugs on slippery floors

## The Science

International Cat Care (iCatCare) believe that it is unethical to continue to breed cats with a genetic mutation that is known to cause significant painful disease – the rationale for breeding these cats was purely human pleasure, as some people believe the folded ears look appealing. This is an unnatural appearance for a cat, and the underlying genetic defect has far-reaching and severe consequences for the health of the cat.

iCatCare has joined forces with the British Veterinary Association (BVA) to raise awareness of the suffering of Scottish Fold cats because of the way they have been bred to look.

More information is needed about other breeds of cats with folded or curly ears before we can be certain that they, too, do not have defects that may cause other problems.

## FAQs

## How can Scottish Fold osteochondrodysplasia be prevented?

Ethical breeding is the only way to prevent Scottish Fold osteochondrodysplasia. It is unethical to continue to breed cats with a genetic mutation that is known to cause significant, painful disease.
