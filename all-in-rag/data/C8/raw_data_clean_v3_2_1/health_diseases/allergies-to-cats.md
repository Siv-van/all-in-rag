# Allergies to cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/allergies-to-cats

---

People are allergic to many things, including cats, and these allergies can show in different ways. Some people will be mildly affected, feeling itchy, sniffly, or sneezing more than usual, while others will have severe skin reactions or may even experience a serious asthma attack.

These reactions happen because a person’s immune system reacts to proteins (antigens) from animals or plants to produce antibodies and/or other chemicals to protect the body. Antibodies and other immune reactions are part of the body’s normal defence mechanism designed to protect against foreign organisms, such as viruses, bacteria and parasites. However, with allergies, this immune response is excessive and often misdirected (targeting proteins or antigens that aren’t genuinely harmful). During an allergic response, histamine may be released unnecessarily from cells upon exposure to the antigen, which can irritate the lungs, nose, skin and other tissues – hence the common reactions of sneezing, coughing, wheezing or itching.

## What triggers an allergic response to cats?

Many people think an allergic response to cats is due to their fur, but the main trigger for an allergic reaction to cats are proteins which are released in saliva (Fel d 4) and in the sebaceous glands in the skin of all cats (Fel d 1). The allergy is not to cat hairs themselves. Proteins are found on the coat of a cat because they groom themselves, leaving saliva (containingFel d 4) on their hair. In addition to this, secretions from the sebaceous glands (containingFel d 1) also surround their hair. Fel d 4is also shed in a cat’s urine and faeces.

## Do all cats provoke an allergic response?

You may see signs of an allergic reaction in people if the allergen is inhaled, if the person strokes a cat, cleans their litter tray or even sits where a cat has been sitting.

As with most things, people react differently. Some people who are allergic to cats can’t tolerate being in a room where cats have been, while others may only have a mild reaction.

All cat breeds produce allergens, but some may produce more than others. It can be a case of trial and error to find out which cats you react to (or which you react worst to). Although the reason isn’t clear, many people seem to react more to long-haired cats. It could be because more allergens build up on their fur, or more hair is shed around the house. But even cats with very little hair, such as Sphynx cats, can still cause allergic reactions, as the allergy is not to the cat hair itself.

## What can I do if I’m allergic to cats?

#### If you have a cat and are mildly allergic to them, there are steps you can take to reduce your exposure to Fel d 1 and the severity of your allergy

Keep your cat off your bed and out of your bedroom

Try to avoid allowing your cat access to rooms where there is carpet (which can trap hair and dander)

Where possible, choose laminate or tiled floors with washable rugs, as they are much easier to clean than carpets

Try to avoid using soft furnishings (cushions, etc) where your cat is allowed, other than those that can be easily washed, and wash any that you use frequently

Vacuum the house frequently and thoroughly with a cleaner fitted with a HEPA filter, and make sure the house is well ventilated

Consider using a cat flap to allow your cat to spend time outdoors if it is safe to do so. Confining them to the house could increase indoor antigens, and safe controlled outdoor access can be achieved with the use of fencing if you have access to a cat friendly garden space

Use air purifiers

Make sure you wash your hands well after handling your cat

With severe cat allergies, the only solution may be to avoid having a cat in your house at all. For most people, though, combining different strategies to reduce the amount of antigen in the environment will help control allergic reactions.

Additional Considerations

Only your doctor can diagnose whether you are allergic to cats, and in severe cases, medication is available to help ease the severity of your reactions. Antihistamines are usually the mainstay of treatment for sneezing and itching, while an inhaler and even steroids are sometimes required for more severe reactions. Always seek advice from your doctor before using any medications.
