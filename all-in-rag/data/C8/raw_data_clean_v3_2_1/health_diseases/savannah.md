# Savannah

## Source

---

Savannah cats are the result of crossing a domestic cat and a captive-housed Serval — a medium-sized, large-eared wild African cat. This hybrid cat is larger and often leaner than the domestic cat and has a spotted coat and large ears. Due to the retention of wild genes, hybrid cats can have strong predatory instincts and can be territorial in their interactions with people and other pets.

Savannah cats are reported to be highly active and exploratory and have unique behavioural needs in comparison to domestic cats. Due to their strong predatory instincts and territorial behaviour, Savannah cats are often kept indoors but meeting their needs in an indoor environment is extremely difficult and thus many are likely to experience compromised welfare.

Welfare issues in the breeding of hybrid cats

Hybrid cats are developed by crossing the domestic cat, with other wild breeds. While this crossing may produce a large and/or attractive looking cat, there are many concerns and unknowns about the temperament, behaviour and safety of these cats in a home setting and the effect on wildlife (and other cats) if they go outdoors.

The breeding process also raises significant welfare and ethical concerns as female domestic cats are bred with much larger and stronger captive wild male cats and run the risk of injury in the process. Male wild cats live entirely captive lives for this purpose, as do the early crosses, which many countries require licenses to own. The length of pregnancy for the two species often differs which may lead to premature or difficult births as well as increased risk of health problems in young kittens.

We have many beautiful cats already; we do not need to breed hybrids. In fact, the legality of owning Savannah cats varies from country to country as recognition of the welfare and safety concerns increases.

International Cat Care does not support the creation of hybrid breeds for these reasons.
