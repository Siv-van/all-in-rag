# Tritrichomonas foetus infection in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/tritrichomonas-foetus-infection-in-cats

---

Although cats of all ages can develop diarrhoea withT. foetusinfection, it’s more commonly seen in young cats and kittens, the majority being under 12 months of age. Most affected cats come from rescue shelters, pedigree breeding colonies and multi-cat households.

Young pedigree cats, particularly Siamese and Bengals, are also more likely to be infected in the UK.

## What are the signs of a Tritrichomonas foetusinfection?

Diarrhoea with blood and mucous in litter

#### T. foetusinfection causes diarrhoea in cats. You may also notice

Your cat passing faeces more often than usual (often in small amounts)

Fresh blood or mucous in the faeces

Faecal incontinence (losing control in holding in their faeces)

Although the diarrhoea may be persistent and severe, most affected cats are otherwise well, eat normally and show no significant weight loss.

## What causesTritrichomonas foetusinfection?

T. foetusis passed from cat to cat in the faeces of infected cats, so it’s more commonly seen in colonies and multi-cat households. It can be spread by close and direct contact between cats. It can also survive for a short period (minutes to hours) in the environment, so can be spread via shared litter trays, food and water bowls.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if your cat has diarrhoea or is showing any of the signs listed above.

How your vet will diagnoseTritrichomonas foetusinfection

#### Your vet will need to test a fresh faecal sample to diagnoseT. foetusinfection. There are three tests commonly used

Faecal or rectal smear– your vet will examine your cat’s faeces under a microscope to look for evidence of the parasite.

PCR– this is performed at an external laboratory and is the most sensitive test, identifying the parasite’s genetic material in your cat’s faeces.

Faecal culture– this is performed at an external laboratory and also looks for evidence of the parasite in the faeces.

As diarrhoea can be a sign of many different conditions, your vet may also want to run blood samples or perform x-rays or ultrasound scans to rule out other diseases.

## How isTritrichomonas foetusinfection treated?

Since the diarrhoea usually resolves over time and is often more of an inconvenience rather than a cause of significant illness, it may not be necessary to treat all cats. Using a highly digestible or high-fibre diet and/or probiotics or synbiotics may result in more formed faeces and may be enough to control the signs in some cats.

Severely affected cats may need specific treatment. T.foetusis not susceptible to normal antiparasitic or antibiotic drugs. Your vet will advise you on the best treatment or management plan for your cat.

FAQ’s

## What’s the outlook for cats with Tritrichomonas foetusinfection?

The long-term outlook for infected cats is good and they will eventually overcome the infection. However, this is a slow process – in one study of infected cats, it took an average of nine months for the diarrhoea to resolve, with some cats having diarrhoea for more than two years.

## CanTritrichomonas foetusinfect people?

Very rare cases ofT. foetusinfection have been reported in immunosuppressed people. As a precaution against infection, people in contact with infected cats are advised to take basic hygiene precautions to avoid ingesting the parasite. These precautions will also help to prevent the spread of the infection to other cats and prevent humans from being infected with other infections that the cat may carry.

#### Hygiene precautions include

- Washing hands thoroughly after handling cat faeces

- Washing hands thoroughly after cleaning cat litter trays, whether the cat has diarrhoea or not

- Cat scratches or bites should always be washed immediately with soap and water. Seek medical attention as soon as possible if signs of infection appear, such as redness, pain or swelling

- People with a weakened immune system should not handle their cat’s faeces or litter tray, must wash their hands after handling their cat
