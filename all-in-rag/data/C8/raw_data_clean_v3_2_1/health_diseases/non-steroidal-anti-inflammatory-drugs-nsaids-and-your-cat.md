# Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) and your cat

## What are NSAIDs used for in cats?

NSAIDs are commonly used in people and animals to help relieve pain, fever and inflammation. They may be used for short-term pain control (eg, for a few days following a surgical operation) or longer-term pain control in cats with chronic (long-lasting) conditions, such asdegenerative joint disease and arthritis.

Controlling your cat’s pain is crucial for their welfare. Many cats greatly benefit from NSAIDs, having better mobility, less pain, increased appetite and an improved quality of life.

## Are NSAIDs safe to give cats?

NSAIDs can make a vital difference to your cat’s pain management and wellbeing, but should only ever be used if they’ve been prescribed for your cat by your vet. Many human drugs such as aspirin, ibuprofen and paracetamol/acetaminophen can behighly toxic to catsand should never be given as doing so can be life-threatening.

Adverse effects can be seen with NSAIDs, just as with all drugs. Some cats may be at increased risk of adverse effects (eg, older cats and cats with certain diseases). If this is the case, your vet may recommend increased monitoring to find the lowest effective dose of the drug for your cat.

## What adverse effects should I look out for?

Various NSAIDs are available for cats depending on the country and local medicine rules. Some are recommended for short-term pain management, and others for longer-term use.

#### Most adverse effects are mild, but some can be serious, involving the stomach and intestines, kidneys, heart or liver. NSAIDs should not be given, and your vet consulted if your cat shows the following signs

- Eating less

- Nausea or vomiting

- Lethargy/tiredness and dullness/depression

- Altered thirst and/or urination

- Diarrhoea and/or black-coloured faeces

- Yellowing of the skin, gums, or whites of the eyes (jaundice)

## What do I need to know if my cat has been prescribed NSAIDs?

Make sure you understand how much of the drug to give, how frequently, and for how long. NSAIDs come in different forms (liquid or tablets), so make sure you are clear on how to administer the correct amounts (eg, on the dosing syringe)

If you are unsure, always ask your vet

Most NSAIDs must be given with or after food. This also allows you to be sure your cat has a normal appetite

Maintaining a goodfluid intakewhile on medication is important

If your cat doesn’t eat, DO NOT give the medication – contact your veterinary team

Talk to your vet about what level of monitoring is needed while on the medication. For example, how frequently your cat should be examined, what blood and urine tests are needed, and how frequently these should be done

Never give your cat any other medication at the same time without speaking to your vet first

Discuss with your veterinary team how you will know the medication is helping your cat, there are various ways to monitor your cat’s mobility, for example

If you’re worried, or see any potential adverse effects, STOP giving the medication and contact your veterinary team immediately

Safety first

If you are ever in doubt, stop the medication and talk to your vet. Following these simple steps will ensure your cat gets the treatment they need but with the lowest risk of any adverse effects.
