# Cardiomyopathy (heart disease) in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/cardiomyopathy-heart-disease-in-cats

---

Cardiomyopathy is the most common form of heart disease seen in cats, and the most common cause of heart failure.

#### The disease can be divided into

Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM)– this is the most common form of heart disease in cats. It happens when there’s an increase in the thickness of the muscular wall of the heart. This reduces the volume of blood within the heart and prevents the heart muscle from relaxing properly between contractions. A staging system may be used by your vet to describe HCM from stage A (cats prone to develop HCM) to stage C (cats with HCM and heart failure).

Dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM)– this is where the muscular wall of the heart generally becomes thinner than usual, the heart enlarges, and the heart muscle can’t contract effectively.

Restrictive cardiomyopathy (RCM)– here, there’s fibrosis (thickening or scarring) of the wall of the heart, making it stiff and inelastic, which prevents the heart chambers from filling normally.

Arrhythmogenic cardiomyopathy (AC) - also called right ventricular cardiomyopathy, this uncommon condition affects only the right side of the heart and can cause abnormalities of the heart rhythm.

Nonspecific (unclassified) cardiomyopathy– this is where there are changes consistent with more than one type of disease – for example, a mixture of both hypertrophy and dilatation present.

There are rare cases of temporary thickening of the heart wall that can be reversed and may be seen in young cats subjected to severe stress.

## What happens in cardiomyopathy?

#### In cardiomyopathy, the underlying abnormality of the heart muscle leads to a compromise in the heart’s function. How the heart’s function is affected depends on the type of cardiomyopathy

With HCM and RCM– the disease mainly interferes with the ability of the heart muscle to relax properly between contractions. The relaxation phase between each cardiac contraction is calleddiastole, and if this doesn’t occur properly, the heart can’t fill with blood as effectively. If severe, this will lead to heart failure - known as ‘diastolic heart failure’

With DCM– the disease mainly affects the ability of the heart muscle to contract (called ‘systole’). This will compromise the ability of the heart to pump (and eject) blood. In severe cases, this also leads to heart failure – known as ‘systolic heart failure’

## What are the signs of heart disease in cats?

In the early stages of the disease, cats may show no signs at all and seem completely normal. That said, while in some cats the underlying disease progresses slowly, in others, it can be quite rapid.

#### Some of the early signs of heart disease may be picked up by your vet during an examination, which is one of the reasons why your cat should be checked at least once a year by a vet, and ideally, more often in older cats. Early signs that your vet may pick up include

- A heart murmur– this is an abnormal noise due to turbulent blood flow that your vet can detect when listening to your cat’s heart with a stethoscope

A gallop rhythm– normally, your vet can hear two sounds when they listen to the heart with a stethoscope. With significant heart disease, a third sound is sometimes heard, and this is referred to as a ‘gallop sound’ or ‘gallop rhythm’

- Abnormalities in heart rate– with heart disease, the heart rate can sometimes significantly increase or decrease

- Cardiac rhythm disturbances– these are also referred to ascardiac dysrhythmias or arrhythmias. With heart disease, there can be interference in the normal electrical impulses that control heart contractions, which can lead to disturbances to the normal rhythm

Many cats, especially those in the early stages of the disease, may only have changes in the cardiac muscle that are detected during an ultrasound examination of the heart. These cats are referred to asasymptomaticor clinically silent, as they’re showing no signs of disease.

Heart failure

If heart function is significantly affected by cardiomyopathy, this leads to heart failure (often called congestive heart failure), where there’s a compromise in blood flow through the heart and blood output from the heart. Cats may seem to develop signs very suddenly as they often don’t show signs of illness until it is very severe. Before this, cats may have less energy and be more tired, which can be hard to detect.

The most common sign of heart failure is difficulty breathing and/or more rapid breathing due to fluid inside the lungs or between the lungs and the chest wall. Along with breathing difficulties, cats may have cold extremities (eg, ears and paws), and may have pale gums and eyes due to poor circulation. Very occasionally cats may seem weak or faint.

Feline Aortic Thromboembolism (FATE)

Another sign which can happen in cats, and may be the first indicator of underlying heart disease, is the development of ‘feline aortic thromboembolism’ orFATE. A blood clot may develop within one of the heart chambers in a cat with cardiomyopathy. The clot can travel around the body, lodge in a blood vessel and obstruct the flow of blood to regions of the body. Although this can happen in different areas of the body, it more commonly affects blood vessels to the legs. This is seen most with HCM and will cause a sudden onset of paralysis to one or both back (occasionally front) legs, with severe pain and distress.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat is showing any of the signs listed above, or you’re worried about their health or behaviour in general, contact your veterinary team for advice. As most cats don’t tend to show signs of heart disease until it’s advanced, getting your cat checked over as quickly as possible is important.

How your vet will diagnose cardiomyopathy

#### Various tests can be performed to help in the diagnosis of heart disease in cats

Electrocardiogram (ECG)– an electrical trace of the heart activity. It can be very useful for the detection of rhythm disturbances

Heart ultrasound (echocardiography)– allows a view of the internal dimensions of the heart, the wall thickness, and the contractility of the heart to be assessed. It can also show where a heart murmur originates from. This is the only test which can identify different types of heart disease in cats. Although a small area of skin usually needs to be shaved to perform an ultrasound scan, the procedure is not uncomfortable or painful and can be performed in most cats without any sedation or anaesthetic. Ultrasound can also identify fluid in the chest

Ultrasound examination on a conscious cat receiving supplementary oxygen therapy

Radiography (X-rays)– help show changes in the overall shape and size of the heart, and can show a build-up of fluid

Blood tests– some blood tests are used to detect heart problems

Tests for underlying disease– may need to be performed in some cases, including blood tests and blood pressure measurement

## How is heart disease in cats treated?

Treatment will depend on the cause. If cardiomyopathy develops secondary to taurine deficiency in the diet (which can be a cause of DCM), or to conditions such ashypertension (high blood pressure) orhyperthyroidism (overactive thyroid gland), then treating the underlying disease may improve your cat’s heart function.

Where heart failure develops, various drug treatments may also be available to help improve and manage the condition. Your veterinary team will be able to discuss all available treatment options with you and decide on a plan based on your cat’s individual needs.

## What’s the outlook for cats with heart disease?

Depending on the type of heart disease, some cats can live for many years without developing heart failure. If it develops into heart failure, treatments may improve the signs of the disease and make cats comfortable for variable periods of time before the disease progresses.

## How can heart disease be prevented?

#### Certain breeds of cat are more at risk of heart disease, for example, Maine Coons and Ragdolls are at greater risk of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM). Screening tests are available in these breeds, which involve an ultrasound examination of the heart (echocardiogram) and having a blood sample/cheek swab taken. Samples are sent to a specialist veterinary laboratory to determine the genetic status of the cat. It’s recommended that

Cats affected with HCM based on an echocardiogram should not be bred from

Any cat with a homozygous positive gene test (two abnormal genes) for the defect should not be bred as they will always pass on the abnormality to their kittens

Any cat with a heterozygous positive on the gene test (one normal gene and one mutated gene) will inevitably pass on the defect to a proportion of their kittens, so breeding should be considered carefully

Breeding cats should have an annual echocardiogram during their breeding years. In some countries, registers of breeding cats have been established. This information allows careful selection of suitable breeding cats.

In addition to this, regular check-ups with your veterinary team can help to detect problems early, before signs develop.
