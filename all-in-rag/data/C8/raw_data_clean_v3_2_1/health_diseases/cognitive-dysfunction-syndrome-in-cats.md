# Cognitive dysfunction syndrome

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/cognitive-dysfunction-syndrome-in-cats

---

Thanks to advancements in nutrition and veterinary medicine, our cats are living longer lives. We all want as much time as possible with our cats, so it’s useful to know the health issues that could affect our cats as they reach their senior years.

One problem facing senior cats is a decline in cognitive abilities (eg, learning, memory, attention, spatial abilities) because of changes in the brain. In humans, this is referred to as being senile (senility) or dementia, but in cats tends to be called cognitive dysfunction syndrome (CDS).

Cognitive dysfunction syndrome explained

CDS can be identified through changes in your cat’s behaviour, which may be subtle to start with and worsen over time. However, the behavioural changes seen in CDS can also result from other diseases, such ashyperthyroidism, chronic kidney disease, brain tumours, pain fromarthritisandhigh blood pressureto name a few. CDS can only be diagnosed by your vet once other conditions have been ruled out.

Providing your cat’s quality of life is good, there’s no reason why CDS should shorten their life. Always speak to your veterinary team for advice on the best way to manage your cat’s signs, depending on their individual circumstances.

## What are the signs of CDS?

#### The most common signs of CDS include

- Spatial disorientation (confusion about where they are) eg, becoming trapped in corners, forgetting where their litter tray is (resulting in passing urine/faeces outside the tray)

- Temporal disorientation (confusion about what time it is) eg, forgetting that they’ve just had feeding time

- Changes in sleep-wake cycles eg, your cat is awake when they’d normally be sleeping, and vice versa (pacing or crying in the night)

- Toileting in the house ie, passing urine or faeces in places they normally wouldn’t

- Excessive vocalisation eg, loud crying which can be disruptive at night

- Altered interaction/relationships with owners and/or other pets in the household eg, becoming more attention-seeking, becoming more reactive to people/other animals

- Altered interest in food eg, eating more or (more commonly) eating less

- Grooming less, resulting in a dull, matted coat

- Changes in general behaviour eg, being more irritable or anxious, being less responsive

- Changes in learning and memory eg, forgetting commands, forgetting previous litter tray training

- Changes in activity eg, reduced activity, aimless wandering or pacing

## What causes CDS in cats?

CDS is caused by age-related changes to the brain, which leads to nerve cells (neurons) dying off. Nerve cells receive information, and give commands based on the information received. As they die off, it can affect your cat’s behaviour.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you notice any of the behavioural changes listed above in your senior cat, contact your veterinary team as some conditions can be treated (eg, high blood pressure) and cats with CDS may benefit from medications.

How your vet will diagnose CDS

A diagnosis is usually made once other conditions that can cause similar signs (such as hyperthyroidism, chronic kidney disease and high blood pressure) have been ruled out. Your vet will take a detailed history from you, examine your cat, and will likely run some blood tests and/or test a sample of your cat’s urine.

## How is CDS treated?

Although CDS can’t be cured, the signs of it can be reduced with suitable management. This may include making changes to your cat’s home, changing or supplementing their diet, and there are also some drug therapies available. Your veterinary team will be able to discuss these options with you.

Environmental (home) management

#### Changing things in and around your home can help a cat with CDS. Senior cats are also much more likely to have arthritis and will benefit from changes around the home, such as

Ensuring your cat has easy access to resources eg, food, water and litter trays. This may mean providing more of these facilities (on each floor of the house)

Making sure litter trays are clean and easy to access (eg, with a lower side)

Keeping a consistent routine of feeding and interacting with your cat

Providing a night light to help with navigation and confusion overnight

Increasing the amount of time you spend with them (if your cat enjoys human company)

Spending short periods of time playing with your cat if they enjoy this – but remember older cats often have sore joints, so keep things gentle

Providing hiding places and high-up perches which are easily accessible (eg, using ramps or steps)

Providing puzzle feeders if your cat is interested and can use them

Using timed feeders which can help with cats asking for food in the early morning

Puzzle feedersare objects which hold food and must be manipulated to release this food, providing a fun ‘brainteaser’ for your cat. Puzzle feeders are an example of ‘environmental enrichment’, which can lead to increased mental stimulation, an increase in the growth and survival of nerve cells in the brain, and an increase in cognitive function.

#### Other changes to the home can be made to make life easier for senior cats and those withmuscle/joint pain or mobility problems, to reduce potential frustration and stress

Avoid placing food and water on high surfaces that senior cats might find difficult to reach, or provide a ramp up to the surface

Raise food and water bowls slightly from the floor, to make it easier for your cat to reach them, especially if they’re suffering fromarthritis. Ensure that food and water bowls are separate

Provide several comfortable beds in easy to reach areas; think about providing heated beds for added comfort

Provide large, low-sided litter trays for easy access and place them in easy to reach locations

Provide soft litter eg, sandy types which are gentler on the paws

Senior cats may find it more difficult to usecat flaps– consider letting them in and out via a door

Allow senior cats to have peace and quiet away from other pets and family members when they want it

Introducing a new cat or dog can be very stressful for senior cats, so this should be avoided

Consider the use of synthetic feline pheromones to help reduce anxiety

Introduce any changes to your cat’s environment gradually, so as not to confuse them.

Severe cognitive dysfunction syndrome

Changes within the home can make things worse for cats with severe CDS, as they may become stressed, which could worsen the signs of CDS. For cats with severe CDS, keep changes to a minimum or avoid them altogether. They may even benefit from having their area of the home reduced and having all their key resources within a smaller area, where they feel safe and secure.

Dietary management

A diet rich in antioxidants may help improve cognitive function and there are also dietary supplements available. The combination of stimulation at home and a diet enriched with antioxidants can have a positive effect in improving cognitive function, so try both changes together. Speak to your veterinary team for advice on the most suitable diet for your cat.

Drug therapies

Some drugs have been used to treat certain signs of CDS and can help reduce anxiety. Speak to your veterinary team for more information about potential drug therapies.

As the condition can be worsened by chronic pain or other illnesses, discuss management of conditions like arthritis with your veterinary team.

## What’s the outlook for cats with CDS?

Providing your cat’s quality of life is good, there’s no reason why CDS should shorten their life. Always speak to your veterinary team for advice on the best way to manage your cat’s signs, depending on their individual circumstances.
