Mycobacteria are a specific group of bacteria, capable of causing disease in both animals and people.

Mycobacterial disease in cats explained

#### Mycobacterial infections generally cause one of the following types of disease

- Tuberculosis: the term used to describe disease where granulomas (inflammatory nodules or lumps) form in the body caused by infection with members of the Mycobacterium tuberculosis complex (MTBC)

Non-tuberculous mycobacterial (NTM) infections: these are infections that usually involve the subcutaneous tissues (tissues just below the skin itself) and skin itselfThis group includesleprosy, where infection results in granulomas forming in the skin (seen as skin lumps or nodules) and other types of slow or rapidly growing mycobacteria

## What are the signs of mycobacteriosis in cats?

#### The signs will vary according to the type of Mycobacteriainvolved in the infection and how it is transmitted. If the disease is picked up from eating or drinking infected items (eg, eating infected rodents or drinking unpasteurised milk infected withM. bovis), signs may include

- Diarrhoea

- Vomiting

- Loss of appetite

- Weight loss

#### However, infection via the skin occurs more commonly (eg, from rodent bites or cuts and scratches). In cats infected this way, signs include

- Non-healing sores or wounds

- Nodules (small firm swellings or lumps) in the skin

- Nodules often lose the overlaying hair, and may become ulcerated

- Swollen lymph nodes (glands)

#### As the disease worsens, or if the infectious agents are inhaled, further signs can include

- Coughing

- Difficulty breathing (dyspnoea)

- Lethargy/tiredness

- Occasionally, cats with mycobacteriosis can have other signs, such as neurological (brain and spine) abnormalities or changes in the eyes.

## What causes mycobacteriosis in cats?

Tuberculosis is usually caused by infection with Mycobacterium bovis (M. bovis). Cats may become infected by drinking unpasteurised infected cow’s milk, or from contact with other infected animals or their environment. In very rare cases, cats may become infected withM. tuberculosiswhich is likely caused by people with the disease.

Other tuberculosis-causing mycobacteria can also infect cats, includingM. microti– this type mainly infects rodents (eg, voles) and can infect cats that hunt. Most cat infections have been reported in the UK, but the disease is also seen in other countries.

There are also a number of different types of non-tuberculous mycobacteria species that can cause disease in cats such asM. aviumandM. lepremurium. M. lepremurium (and other species) cause leprosy in cats which affects the skin.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if your cat is showing any of the signs listed above or if you’re concerned about their health in general.

How your vet will diagnose mycobacteriosis

Infection may be suspected based on the signs your cat is showing, but further tests such as blood samples, x-rays, ultrasound and a biopsy (surgical removal of a small piece of tissue) will be needed to confirm a diagnosis. It can be challenging to identify the bacterial species involved in some cases, and samples will need to be sent to specific laboratories.

## How is mycobacteriosis in cats treated?

Treatment of mycobacteriosis is difficult in cats, so your vet will discuss your options in detail with you. Antibiotics may be used, but treatment can take many months and involve a combination of two or three antibiotics. Antibiotic resistance may also develop. Occasionally, surgical removal of affected skin can be helpful.

In some cases when a cat is infected by a species that causes illness in people, treatment is not recommended due to risks to human health.

## FAQs

## What is the risk to people?

All mycobacteria that cause tuberculosis in cats pose a potential threat to people (and other animals too), although the risk appears very low. Nevertheless, asM. tuberculosisandM. boviscause tuberculosis in humans, if cats are infected with these bacteria, treatment may not be recommended.

Other types of non-tuberculous mycobacteria pose a low risk to humans, with the exception of people withreducedimmune systems. In this case, the risks should be discussed with medical professionals.

## What’s the outlook for cats with mycobacteriosis?

The outlook for infected cats depends on the type of mycobacteria, and the extent and severity of the infection. Although some cases may be treated effectively, the prognosis is uncertain or poor, with lengthy courses of treatment needed.
