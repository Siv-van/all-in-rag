# Lymphoma in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/lymphoma-in-cats

---

Lymphoma, also known as lymphosarcoma [LSA], is the single most common cancer that affects cats. It affects the lymphatic system, which is vital in maintaining a cat’s immune system.

Lymphoma explained

Lymphoma is a solid, malignant tumour of lymphocytes (a type of white blood cell involved in immune responses and protecting a cat’s body from infection). Lymphocytes are found in the blood, lymph nodes (glands) and other tissues.

Lymphoma cells under the microscope

Lymphocytes are widely distributed throughout the body, therefore, lymphoma can develop in almost any part of the body and commonly occurs at multiple sites. The most commonly affected areas include the gastrointestinal tract (stomach/intestines), the lymph nodes (which are distributed throughout the body), the chest cavity, the nose, the kidneys and the nervous system.

#### Lymphoma in cats is often classified according to the tissues that are affected – common terms that you may hear used include

- Alimentary lymphoma– affecting the stomach and/or intestines

- Mediastinal lymphoma– affecting lymphoid tissue in the chest cavity

- Multicentric or nodal lymphoma– affecting multiple lymph nodes and possibly other sites in the body

- Extranodal or miscellaneous lymphoma– cases that don’t fall into one of the other categories, eg, lymphoma affecting the kidneys, nose, brain, etc.

## What are the signs of lymphoma in cats?

#### Signs can vary widely, depending on where in the body the lymphoma has developed and the tissues that are affected. Some of the signs your cat may show include

- Vomiting

- Diarrhoea

- Weight loss

- Reduced appetite

- Lethargy/tiredness

- Difficulty breathing

- Coughing

- Increased thirst (polydipsia)

- Increased urination (polyuria)

- Discharge or bleeding from the nose

## What causes lymphoma?

Both infection withfeline leukaemia virus (FeLV) andfeline immunodeficiency virus (FIV) can be underlying or predisposing causes of lymphoma in cats. A lowered immune system, genetic predisposition (Oriental breeds) and environmental toxins, such as cigarette smoke, may also increase a cat’s risk of developing lymphoma.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat is showing any of the signs of lymphoma listed above, or if you’re worried about their health in general, contact your veterinary team for advice. For cats that have been diagnosed with lymphoma, if you see a worsening of their signs or any new signs, speak to your veterinary team as further check-ups/tests may be needed.

How your vet will diagnose lymphoma

Your cat will need to undergo tests for your vet to make a diagnosis. Blood tests, x-rays and/or an ultrasound scan can help identify the location and/or spread of a tumour and any other disease. Your vet may also need to take a biopsy, either using a needle (known as a fine needle aspirate) or via surgery, to send a sample to an external laboratory for testing.

## How is lymphoma treated?

Before starting treatment, your vet may ‘stage’ the lymphoma. This is done through x-rays/ultrasounds, taking samples from local lymph nodes (glands) and blood tests, to find out how far the lymphoma has spread and what (if any) complications have arisen.

Some treatments are widely available in primary care practice, while others are only available at specialist centres. Your vet may suggest referring your cat to a specialist with expert knowledge and a greater range of treatment options, but many cats can be treated at their usual clinic.

#### Treatment options for lymphoma include

- Chemotherapy (anti-cancer drugs)

- Radiotherapy (radiation therapy)

- Surgery (usually combined with chemotherapy)

#### Which treatment is used will depend on factors such as

Where the lymphoma is in the body

What is appropriate for your cat

What is available/accessible to you

In many cats, the response to therapy can be very good and long-lasting. Although uncommon, some cases of lymphoma will go into complete and permanent remission, though it’s worth noting that lymphoma is rarely cured.

Chemotherapy (with or without surgery) is the most common treatment option, but it is difficult to predict which cats will respond well to therapy for lymphoma. It can be helpful, especially with chemotherapy, to use trial therapy. The initial response to chemotherapy (how well the tumour responds in the first few weeks) is often the best predictor of long-term response.

Chemotherapy (anti-cancer drug therapy)

Most chemotherapy drugs work by interfering with the ability of cells to divide. Side effects generally only occur in 1 in 5 cats, but your vet will advise you of any expected side effects that you may see. Unlike in human medicine, chemotherapy generally doesn’t result in hair loss in cats, although they may lose their whiskers, and their coat may change. Other common side effects involve the bone marrow (blood tests will be needed before each treatment), or the gastrointestinal system and anti-sickness medications are provided if needed.

Some chemotherapy drugs are given as tablets, others are given as injections by your vet. A combination of drugs may be used to attack the tumour in different ways.

Precautions in cats receiving chemotherapy

#### Because anti-cancer drugs can affect healthy as well as cancerous cells, unnecessary exposure to these drugs should be avoided wherever possible. This includes unnecessary handling of the drugs, but also exposure to the drugs in urine and faeces produced by a cat being treated (as well as other body fluids like saliva and vomit). With some simple precautions, exposure and any risks can be reduced

Giving tablets at home– your vet will warn you if they are prescribing tablets for you to give at home that could be harmful. If this is the case, these tablets (or capsules) must not be split or crushed – they will have a protective coating designed to prevent you from coming into direct contact with the drug itself. Ideally, the tablets should be handled and given while wearing disposable gloves. If your cat spits out a tablet, it can be picked up (wearing gloves), wrapped in kitchen paper and put into a specific waste bin provided by your veterinary clinic

Dealing with urine and faeces– most drugs are eliminated from the body in the urine and/or faeces. Although the levels of drug being passed are very low, it’s safest to wear disposable gloves when cleaning your cat’s litter tray and place soiled litter in a sealed bag in the bin

- Dealing with soiled bedding– bedding soiled with urine or faeces should be washed separately from any other routine washing; food and water bowls should also be washed separately from your other bowls and utensils

- Radiation therapy (radiotherapy)

Radiation therapy usually involves what’s known as ‘external beam radiation’, which is similar to x-rays but the radiation is much more intense. With your cat under anaesthetic/sedation, a beam of radiation is focused on the tumour. Ideally, the outcome is to kill off the cancer cells while causing minimal damage to surrounding tissues. In general, several treatments are given over a few weeks, with each session lasting only a few minutes.

Radiation therapy is mainly used to reduce the size of tumours (eg, for lymphoma in the nose) and may be used alongside chemotherapy or surgery. This type of treatment is not widely available and will usually require travel to a specialist centre.

Surgery for cats with lymphoma

Your vet may recommend surgery if there is a single mass in an accessible site (eg, removing a single tumour from the intestines). In most cases, follow-up chemotherapy is also needed.

Sometimes, surgery is used to remove some (but not all) of a tumour to improve your cat’s quality of life or to help with other treatments, such as chemotherapy and radiotherapy.

General and palliative care for cats with lymphoma

Ensuring your cat is eating well is an important part of the supportive care you provide. Read our article onencouraging your cat to eatfor some helpful tips on improving your cat’s appetite, and if your cat still doesn’t want to eat, speak to your veterinary team for further advice.

#### Ensuring a good quality of life that is free from pain is the main goal in managing cats with lymphoma. Supportive therapy can be an important part of this, and treatments may include the use of

- Pain relief

- Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)– anti-inflammatory drugs which can also help to relieve pain

- Anti-emetic drugs– drugs that reduce nausea (feeling sick) and vomiting

- Antibiotics– if your cat develops a secondary bacterial infection or a very low white cell count (leaving them vulnerable to infection), antibiotics may be used.

- Never be afraid to ask questions and find out as much information as you can about your cat’s lymphoma and treatment options. If there is anything you’re concerned about, always contact your veterinary team immediately.

## FAQs

## Will my cat be radioactive if they undergo radiation therapy?

Although radiation therapy is used to kill cancer cells, this does not mean your cat becomes ‘radioactive’ and there is no risk whatsoever to people in contact with your cat.

## What’s the outlook for cats with lymphoma?

The outlook depends on the type of lymphoma and the treatment options available. When treating lymphoma, everyone involved must have the same goals in mind; an improved and good quality of life for your cat without side effects that will cause further suffering.

Lymphoma is not generally curable, so inevitably, there will come a day when you have to consider euthanasia. This can be a difficult and distressing time, and having the support and help of your veterinary team, along with friends and family, can be invaluable.

## Glossary of Terms

- Lymphoma - cancer affecting the lymphocytes (a type of white blood cell)

- Malignant - a cancerous tumour which often spreads to surrounding normal healthy tissue, and may also spread to other sites in the body

- Metastases- when a cancer spreads to other sites within the body

- Biopsy - surgical removal of a small piece of tissue

- Fine needle aspirate - where a small needle is inserted into a mass to remove or ‘suck out’ a few cells that can be smeared on a slide for examination

- Surgical biopsy - a larger portion of the tumour is removed at surgery and sent to the laboratory
