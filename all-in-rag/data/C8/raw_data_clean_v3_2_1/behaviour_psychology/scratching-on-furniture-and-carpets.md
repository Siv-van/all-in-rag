# Scratching on furniture and carpets

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/scratching-on-furniture-and-carpets

---

Cats scratch to maintain healthy claws, exercise the muscles in their front legs and spine, and as a form of territorial communication or marking behaviour. Kittens start to scratch from around 5 weeks of age.

Stropping, where cats drag their front claws downwards, either on a horizontal or vertical surface, loosens and removes the outer keratin husk of their claws, revealing sharp new surfaces underneath. Having healthy, sharp claws is important for cats as it helps with climbing, hunting, playing, and defence.

Cat dragging their claws downwards on a scratching post, known as stropping

Along with urine marking and face rubbing, scratching is used as a marking behaviour for communication with other cats. Glands in between your cat’s pads mix to produce a chemical message, a combination of pheromones and odours. When their claws scrape down a surface, the chemical message is deposited. A combination of the mark, discarded claw husks, and the smell provides a strong visual, chemical and scent message to other cats. You may see evidence that your cat has been scratching outdoors - trees, fence posts, sheds and wooden gates, for example, are important locations in a cat-populated area.

## What causes my cat to scratch indoors?

As scratching performs many functions, not just territorial marking, cats will still scratch indoors – to keep their claws in good condition and during exploration and play. While cats may scratch less indoors as a means of territorial communication, the scent deposits made while scratching may provide feelings of enhanced physical safety and security to the scratching cat.

Scratching can also be used an attention-seeking tool if they have learnt this behaviour is positively reinforced. In addition, cats will often scratch vigorously in the presence of their owners or other cats as a sign of territorial confidence.

## How do I know if the amount of scratching my cat does is normal?

If the scratched locations are widespread throughout the home, particularly around doorways and windows, your cat is likely signalling a general sense of insecurity. Whether the scratching represents claw maintenance, marking, or both, depends on the dynamics of your cat household, the pattern of locations and various other factors. Even if the level of scratching is normal for your cat, if attractive scratching posts or areas are not provided indoors, damage will likely occur to furniture, wallpaper or carpet! Each cat will have preferences for the type of surfaces they like to scratch too and whether they prefer to scratch on a vertical or horizontal surface.

## What can I do to stop my cat from scratching my furniture and carpets?

All cats including those who are indoor-only need their sharp claws to explore and climb, for defence against attack and to escape threats

Claw trimming may help prevent immediate damage but doesn’t help meet your cat’s needs, which is important to prevent your cat from scratching in unwanted areas. Remember scratching is a normal and necessary behaviour for cats, so you will not be able to stop it (nor should you – cats need to scratch!) but you can help encourage them to scratch on a surface that is designed for that purpose. If a particular surface or object is being damaged, it’s important to provide an alternative that offers a similar experience for your cat when used. For example, if your cat is scratching textured wallpaper at a certain height, the alternative scratching area should be vertical with similar texture and ridges that allow your cat to stretch to the same level. Kittens or senior cats might prefer scratching softer surfaces such as large angled or horizonal scratchers made from cardboard too as they provide less resistance, so are easier to scratch.

Scratching posts range from a basic single upright structure with a heavy base to an elaborate floor-to-ceiling modular unit that provides many opportunities for play, exercise and rest, as well as a variety of surfaces to scratch. It’s important the posts are as tall as your cat is when standing on their hind legs in a full stretched scratching position to allow optimal scratching. In multi-cat households, we recommend one scratching post per cat (plus an additional one for choice) positioned in different locations.

If space is an issue, scratching panels can be fixed to walls, either using homemade (for example, loop-weave carpet) or commercially available products (for example, panels of sisal twine, bark, or corrugated cardboard).

Use of a synthetic feline pheromone can also help, as this provides a sense of security and reassurance to your cat, making scratching for territorial reasons less likely to occur.

It’s best to offer alternatives for your cat to scratch rather than relying on trimming their claws to prevent damage to furniture. However senior cats may need regular claw trims because they are physically unable to scratch as easily or often as they need. Overgrown claws can become caught on soft furnishings, or even grow into a cat’s pads, which is painful for them. Very overgrown or thickened claws can also be a sign of certain diseases such as osteoarthritis or hyperthyroidism, so veterinary advice should be sought should this be the case with your cat.

## FAQs

## I’ve bought a scratching post, why won’t my cat use it?

Some scratching products are too lightweight to support scratching or can’t be fixed to rigid surfaces - cats may not like these as they don’t provide enough resistance and can move around during use. It’s also important to place the scratching post or panel in an area where your cat spends a lot of time - and, as cats often scratch after sleep, it may help to place the post near their favourite bed. And remember - upright posts need to be tall enough for your cat to scratch at full stretch, so if your adult cat was given a short scratching post as a kitten it’s time to upgrade to a taller post!

Some commercially available posts are impregnated with catnip, which will draw attention without much effort for cats who are sensitive to catnip. In addition to this, to encourage use, play a simple predatory-type game with your cat around the base of the scratching post, for example using a wand toy. Once the claws contact the surface of the post, this is often all that’s needed to encourage further use. You could also try placing treats around the base or on top of the desired scratching surface, or rewarding your cat with treats or gentle praise when they use it. Pre-scratching the post with the tip of a screw also helps provide a visual cue and encourage use. Offering items such as self-grooming arches or wall corner groomers give cats the opportunity to facial mark in addition to scratching opportunities.

Never force your cat to try and scratch a surface by attempting to place their paws on it, as this could be uncomfortable and frighten them, which could put you at risk of injury and damage the bond you have with your cat.

## Some scratching posts are covered in carpet, won’t that encourage my cat even more to damage my stairs?

Although many commercially available scratching posts are covered with carpet, there’s no evidence that your cat’s scratching habits will move to other carpeted areas within your home once the post is used regularly.

I’ve bought a scratching post; my cat uses it but still uses the sofa – what should I do?

#### If your cat’s scratching has damaged furniture, you can try

Covering the affected area with a thick, heavy blanket or one-sided sticky plastic film to cover and protect the surface and make it less attractive to scratch

Using synthetic feline pheromone on the areas where your cat has previously scratched to help them feel more secure and prevent any anxiety-related scratching

## What if my cat is damaging wooden surfaces?

If wooden furniture, doorframes, or bannisters have been damaged, wash them with a solution of unscented soapy water and use synthetic feline pheromone spray on the affected areas to create a sense of security and reduce the likelihood of scratching. Severely scratched surfaces may need to be discarded, and suitable posts or scratching panels (you could try using something made of wood) can be provided near the area that was damaged.

## What if the carpet is damaged?

Many cats target the lower step on staircases and scratch horizontally whilst lying down. Placing sections of old/sample carpet or large objects covered with carpet near the stairs can be offered as an alternative surface for cats that scratch these areas.

## Should I punish my cat for scratching the carpet?

No, your cat should never be punished. If the motivation for scratching is claw maintenance or communication, then you’re punishing a natural behaviour which is very confusing for your cat, and if your cat is scratching excessively due to anxiety and insecurity, then punishment will add to their distress and probably make the situation worse.

## How do I know if my cat’s scratching is anxiety-related?

If the scratching is widespread, you have a multi-cat household and/or you live in a densely cat-populated territory, the scratching may be anxiety-related. Even major building work within your home may trigger intensive scratching as your cat adjusts to its altered territory.

There are often tensions within multi-cat households or territories that aren’t easily picked up by owners. To address any territorial marking behaviour, you’ll first need to understand what’s causing your cat’s stress. Once this has been established, you may be able to decrease your cat’s anxiety by providing additional resources around the home (bowls, litter trays, scratching posts, beds etc.) to prevent competition between your cats. Another way of potentially increasing security is to use synthetic feline pheromones that mimic naturally occurring secretions that all cats produce from glands in their cheeks. Cats use this scent to mark their territory, and the smell gives them a sense of security and reassurance. Research has shown that cats are less likely to scratch or spray urine in areas where this pheromone is deposited. Depending on the country you live in, a synthetic version of the pheromone might be available in both a spray and diffuser form.

If you’re suspicious that the scratching is anxiety-related, contact your vet for general advice or ask for a referral to a feline behaviour specialist.
