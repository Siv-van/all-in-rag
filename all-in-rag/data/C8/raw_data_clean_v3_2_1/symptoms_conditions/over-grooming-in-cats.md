# Over-grooming in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/over-grooming-in-cats

---

Over-grooming describes any behaviour that goes beyond normal grooming for coat maintenance and scent distribution. While it can happen anywhere your cat can reach on their body, the most common areas for over-grooming are the belly, the legs (particularly the insides of the hind legs) and across the flank (side). Patches will often be symmetrical on either side of your cat’s body.

It’s worth noting that over-grooming is not the same as ‘alopecia’ (the partial or complete lack of hair from areas of the body where it normally grows). The difference between the two is that when hair is removed by deliberate grooming, the remaining hair shafts feel spiky and sharp, whereas with alopecia, any remaining hair in the area feels soft and normal to touch. Your veterinary team may look at hairs under the microscope to determine which type of hair - loss is occurring.

## What are the signs of over-grooming?

If your cat is over-grooming, you may notice patches of broken or sparse hair, complete hair loss in some areas and occasionally damage to the underlying skin. You may notice your cat grooming more often, or even biting or chewing at their skin and hair. This behaviour can be difficult to distract the cat from.

## What causes over-grooming in cats?

#### Several diseases may result in over-grooming, and they can generally be broken down into

- Skin problems

- Underlying pain

- Stress

- Skin problems

One of the most common causes is hypersensitivity tofleasaliva, known asflea allergic dermatitis. Cats can also develop skin reactions to something in their diet or many other substances present in the environment – read our article onitchy cats – when it’s not fleas, to learn more about what else can cause intense itchiness in your cat.

Underlying pain

Feline lower urinary tract diseaseis a common problem in cats and this can cause pain and discomfort in the bladder and other parts of the urinary tract. As a result, cats may over-groom in the localised area where the pain is – a common sign would be a cat with a bald patch (or sparse hair) on their lower belly, the inside of their thighs and around their genitals.

Feline hyperaesthesia syndrome may be another possible cause. This is a poorly understood condition which leads to behaviours including skin twitching and sudden bouts of intense grooming and self-mutilation, often of the cat’s back and tail.

Stress

While the majority of cases of over-grooming in cats are initially caused by a skin or pain problem, there’s reason to believe that most cases also have a stress component. For a small number of cats, the problem can be entirely due to stress.

Cats frequently use behaviour such as grooming to make themselves feel better duringstressful situationsor when they are anxious. This can become compulsive if the grooming behaviour is performed out of context and so frequently that it disrupts normal activity. Some Oriental breeds are more likely to develop purely stress-related grooming problems, often plucking the fur, but there is no conclusive evidence to suggest how common this is or why this should be the case.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team as soon as possible if you notice your cat over-grooming or any other signs of ill health or unusual behaviour.

How your vet will diagnose the cause of your cat’s over-grooming

Your vet will first investigate to see if there’s an underlying medical problem causing your cat’s over-grooming. They will do this by taking a detailed history, performing an examination, and possibly further tests such as examining samples of hair or scrapings from the skin, blood tests, urine tests, x-rays or ultrasound scans, depending on what they think the problem may be.

## What is the treatment for over-grooming?

The treatment will depend on the cause. If your vet has found an underlying medical cause, they will treat this accordingly. If they suspect stress is the cause, they can advise you on ways to reduce your cat’s stress and may refer your cat to a local, qualified clinical animal behaviourist, who will identify and address any stressors which may have triggered or exacerbated the problem.

## How can over-grooming be prevented?

Again, it depends on the cause of over-grooming. For example, a cat suffering from flea allergic dermatitis will need regular flea treatments throughout their life to reduce the chances of a recurrence. Arthritic cats who may be over-grooming due to pain will need to have their joint pain managed. If your cat is suffering from stress, once the trigger is identified you can take steps to address the problem, which may include ensuring your cat has a routine, doesn’t need to compete for resources around the home and is mentally stimulated. Where helpful, using synthetic feline pheromones can also make your cat feel safe.

Your veterinary team will be happy to guide you on ways to prevent further episodes of over-grooming, depending on what’s caused the problem.
