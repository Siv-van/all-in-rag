# Seizures in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/seizures-in-cats

---

A seizure isn’t a disease but a sign of an abnormal functioning of the brain. Most seizures in cats happen while they’re relaxed and resting quietly.

While there are many types of seizures in people, dogs and cats, they usually come down to two major categories: partial and generalised.

## Generalised seizures

Generalised seizures tend to be easy to recognise. The signs you can expect include; sudden collapse, loss of awareness, violent shaking or paddling of all four legs, chewing/twitching of the face, drooling, passing urine and faeces. These signs usually last for one to three minutes. Your cat may have multiple seizures with a short recovery in between – these are known as cluster seizures. If there’s no recovery and the seizure continues for more than 5 minutes, this is known as status epilepticus. This is an emergency – contact your veterinary team straight away.

Before the seizure, your cat may show some changes in behaviour, such as drooling, growling or pacing. Recovery from a seizure may take a few seconds to a few hours, during which your cat may appear disorientated, blind and wobbly, or not recognise their surroundings.

## Partial seizures

Partial seizures only affect part of the body, so they can be a lot harder to recognise. Signs may include drooling, eyelid/facial twitching, crying out, chewing motions, growling and unusual head, neck or limb movements. A partial seizure may progress to a generalised seizure.

Although seizures in cats are believed to be less common than in dogs, affected cats tend to experience a higher frequency of seizures, regardless of the type and cause.

## Seizures explained

Seizures, also called fits or convulsions, are caused by a sudden surge in the electrical activity of the brain, leading to the signs we’ve listed below. Seizures can be caused by multiple conditions, both inside and outside the brain.

## What are the signs of seizures in cats?

Signs of seizures can be broken down into stages.

#### Before a seizure, your cat may show the following signs (or may show no signs at all)

- Drooling

- Growling

- Pacing

#### A cat experiencing a generalised seizure will show signs that include

- Sudden collapse

- Loss of awareness

- Violent shaking of all four legs

- Rigid legs, paddling/running movements

- Chewing/twitching of the face

- Drooling

- Passing urine or faeces

#### A cat with a partial seizure may show signs that include

- Drooling

- Eyelid/facial twitching

- Excessive vocalisation (crying out)

- Growling

- Unusual head, neck, and limb movements

#### Following the seizure, your cat may

- Appear disorientated, blind and wobbly

- Seem lethargic/tired

## What causes seizures in cats?

Causes of seizures can be found inside the brain (intracranial causes) or outside the brain (extracranial causes).

Extracranial causes

Extracranial causes include poisons and metabolic diseases. The brain itself is perfectly healthy, but the seizures are a reaction to a toxin that’s either eaten by the cat or applied (for example, in cases ofpermethrin poisoning), or as a result of a metabolic problem (for example, liverorkidney disease, low blood sugar level, low calcium), high blood pressure, or an abnormal heart rhythm. ‘Reactive epileptic seizures’ is a term often used to describe this category of causes.

With toxic causes, recurrent seizures aren’t expected - unless your cat is exposed to the toxin again.

Intracranial causes

Intracranial causes can be divided into primary and secondary epilepsy.

With primary epilepsy (also known as idiopathic epilepsy), there’s no structural problem with the brain and the epileptic seizures are caused by a functional problem with the brain. Cats with primary epilepsy tend to experience their first seizure at a young adult age. This is an uncommon cause of seizures in cats.

Secondary epilepsy means there is a structural disease in the brain - for example, a brain tumour, inflammation or infection of the brain (encephalitis), or a recent or previous stroke or head trauma. In these cases, you may only see seizures, or there may be other signs such as walking in circles, blindness, and being wobbly, restless, or sleepy.

## How are seizures treated?

If an underlying cause for your cat’s seizures is found, treatment will be focused on that primarily, but medications to control seizures may also be needed, at least at first.

Cats with an ‘extracranial’ cause of seizures may need treatment for the underlying disease (eg, management of high blood pressure, treatment of low calcium), and cats with ‘intracranial’ causes such as infections or cancers (tumours) may need specific treatment, as well as medication, to reduce seizures.

Anti-seizure drugs will be used long-term (often for the remainder of your cat’s life) in cases of primary epilepsy, and while this won’t cure the condition, it will help to reduce the frequency and severity of seizures. Successful treatment aims for your cat to be free of seizures, or for the frequency of their seizures to be reduced by at least 50%, with no or minimal side effects from the medication.

Keeping a seizure log or diary, listing the number of seizures your cat has, how long they last, how severe they are and what signs are shown, can be really helpful along with video footage for your veterinarian to tailor your cat’s treatment as required. If your cat isn’t responding to treatment, further investigations can be carried out, and treatment changed as needed.

## When to contact your veterinary clinic

If your cat shows any of the signs listed above, contact your veterinary clinic who will be able to guide you on the next steps. If your cat has been experiencing a seizure, without recovery, for 5 minutes, or has several seizures one after anotherurgent treatment is required - contact your veterinary team immediately.

## FAQs

## What’s the outlook for cats suffering from seizures?

The outlook depends on the cause of the seizures and the severity of the condition. In cats diagnosed with primary epilepsy, your cat can still enjoy a happy, healthy life, provided they receive appropriate treatment and are routinely monitored by your veterinary team.

What to do while your cat is having a seizure

Although a seizure is frightening for any cat owner, it’s important to remain calm and not disturb your cat. Make sure they’re not at risk of injury, for example, by falling down stairs or off furniture. When your cat starts a seizure, make a note of the time. Most seizures will stop on their own within one to three minutes, although it may take a few minutes to a few hours for your cat to completely recover. If a seizure lasts for a very long period (over five minutes for the actual fit) or is followed by other seizures close together (cluster seizures), contact your veterinary team immediately.
