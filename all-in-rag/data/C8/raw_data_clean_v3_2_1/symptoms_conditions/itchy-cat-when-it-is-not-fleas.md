# Itchy cat – when it is not fleas

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/itchy-cat-when-it-is-not-fleas

---

All cats scratch occasionally, but if you notice your cat scratching or licking more than usual, it could indicate there is an underlying problem. In some instances, it can be difficult to differentiate between skin disease due to itchiness, and skin disease caused by something else. Hair loss in cats is often caused by excessive self-grooming due to itchy skin — but cats may be ‘secret groomers’ and we might be unaware that our cat is grooming more frequently or more aggressively.

## What are the signs of itch skin disease in cats?

#### Signs of itchy skin disease in cats include

- Noticeable itching, scratching and self-induced skin damage

- Hair loss (bald patches)

- ‘Miliary’ dermatitis – tiny 2-3 mm diameter crusts throughout the body surface. The skin and coat may also be greasy and have excessive dandruff

- Eosinophilic granuloma complex– this is a variety of skin lesions that are usually associated with allergies

All these signs of itchy skin look completely different, but can all be caused by the same things — in most instances, fleas are the cause, but other parasites and allergies can be involved. Some cats may have more than one sign of disease present simultaneously - for example, miliary dermatitis and symmetrical hair loss.

## What causes cats to itch other than fleas?

#### Causes of pruritus other than fleas include

- Food intolerance/allergy

- Atopy (an environmental allergy, for example, to house dust or pollen)

- Insect bites

Other skin parasites (e.g., mites or lice)

- Bacterial infections

- Food intolerance or allergy

It is difficult to know why certain foods make animals and humans itch. In some cases, the itchiness may be caused by a reaction to the food or to additives or preservatives.

Changing your cat’s diet to a food it hasn’t previously eaten can cure some cases of pruritic skin disease. In these cases, the likely cause is a food allergy, but the terms ‘food intolerance’ or ‘food-responsive’ skin disease are sometimes used as a specific diagnosis is not always made.

Your cat may need to be fed an alternative diet for 6-8 weeks to rule out food-response dermatitis, and the choice of food is important. Your veterinary team will advise you on the most appropriate diet to use – this might be a home-prepared diet, or your vet may suggest a special ‘hydrolysed’ diet for the trial period. Home-prepared diets may not be balanced and miss vital nutrients so should only be fed under veterinary or veterinary nutritionist supervision. Many cats also hunt or may be fed by neighbours, which can complicate the trial - no other food must be eaten during the trial period.

Atopy or non-flea non-food allergic dermatitis

Atopy (atopic dermatitis) is an allergy to something in the environment - for example, pollen or dust mites. It is quite an uncommon condition in cats, and while allergies to pollen and house dust occur in cats, they are difficult to diagnose.

In most cats, atopy is diagnosed by ruling out other potential causes of pruritus, including fleas, other parasites, and food. Allergy testing can be performed on cats (with both skin and blood tests available), but the results can be unreliable.

Atopy is incurable and life-long medication is needed to manage the condition and keep your cat comfortable. Treatment will include avoiding the allergen where possible, and medication to control the inflamed, itchy skin. If an allergy test has successfully identified the offending allergen, it is possible to use a ‘hyposensitisation vaccine’ as a therapy – these rarely resolve the disease, but in some cases, they can reduce the need for medication.

Insect bites

Insects such as wasps and bees can cause stings that lead to painful and swollen skin. However, some other insects including fleas, midges, flies, and mosquitoes may bite, and the reaction to the bite (or the insect saliva) can cause intense irritation and itchiness. Flying insects usually bite hairless areas, such as the bridge of the nose and ears. Mosquitoes have been known to cause a reaction on the bridge of the nose of some cats (this is known as mosquito-bite hypersensitivity).

Ear mites – Otodectes cynotis

Ear mites are well known as the major cause of external ear canal inflammation (otitis externa) in young cats and breeding colonies – read our advice on commonear problems in catsto learn more. It is also possible for the mites to wander onto the skin around the head and neck and cause itchiness around these areas, too. As cats sleep curled up, the infection (and subsequent dermatitis) may spread to their rump and tail, too.

Other skin parasites

Harvest mites (Neotrombicula autumnalis) are a recognised cause of skin disease in cats in some areas, commonly seen in late Summer and Autumn. These tiny, orange, dot-sized mites are visible to the naked eye and are usually found between the toes and in Henry’s pocket of the ear flap (the fold of skin that forms an open pouch at the bottom of your cat’s ears).

In some parts of the world, the mitesNotoedres catiandSarcoptes scabieimay also be found on cats and can cause intense pruritus. Lice are a rare cause of itching, and affected cats often have other underlying diseases and ill-health.

Bacterial skin infections (pyoderma) and fungal infections

Bacterial skin disease in cats is uncommon, but when it occurs, some cats make a fantastic recovery following antibiotic treatment. Bacterial infection can complicate other skin diseases e.g., atopic dermatitis.

Ringwormor dermatophytosis is caused by a fungal infection and is not usually itchy, but skin infection with yeasts (e.g., Malassezia) can be a problem in some cats and is more common in breeds like the Sphynx.

Autoimmune skin disease

Rarely, skin disease can be caused when a cat’s immune system attacks the skin. Conditions such as pemphigus foliaceous fall into this category.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you notice your cat scratching or licking more than normal, contact your vet. Pruritus can make your cat uncomfortable, so it is important to find the underlying cause and start any treatment required as soon as possible.

How your vet will diagnose the cause of pruritus

Your veterinary team may take skin or hair samples (e.g., coat brushings, scrapes or impressions) to look at under the microscope, or they may take blood samples or perform allergy testing.

Treatment options

Again, treatment will depend on the cause. Food intolerance or allergy will require a special diet, other medications may include antibiotics, steroids or hyposensitisation vaccines.

## FAQs

## Can pruritus be prevented?

In some ways, yes. For example, if your cat is allergic to fleas, regular preventative treatments are necessary, or if your cat is sensitive to certain foods, feeding an appropriate diet can keep any flare-ups under control.

## What is the outlook for pruritic cats?

Assuming you follow your veterinary team’s recommended treatment plan, there’s no reason your cat can’t live a comfortable life.

## Glossary of Terms

- Pruritus- itchy skin

- Dermatitis- inflammation of the skin

- Otitis externa- inflammation of the external ear canal

- Pyoderma- bacterial skin infection

- Dermatophytosis (ringworm)- infection with a dermatophyte fungal organism
