## What causes runny eyes in cats?

Runny eyes can be a sign that your cat has an eye infection, most often conjunctivitis. In cats with conjunctivitis, infection leads to inflammation of the pink lining that covers the inner surface of the eyelids (conjunctiva) and some of the eye. In addition to discharge from the eyes (which may dry to form a crust), the areas around the eyes may be red and swollen. Conjunctivitis causes discomfort and pain - and if left untreated, can lead to permanent damage. If you notice a problem with your cat’s eyes, they must receive veterinary treatment.

Eye infections can be caused by injuries to the eye or foreign objects in the eye (for example, dirt). Newborn kittens can develop eye infections due to vaginal infections of their mother at the time of birth and can also develop them if born into a home that’s not clean enough. Viral or bacterial diseases can also lead to eye infections, for example, Chlamydia infection. Usually, a green or yellow discharge indicates there’s a bacterial infection involved.

Runny eyes can also be a sign of an upper respiratory infection, more commonly known as ‘cat flu’. In over 90% of cases, cat flu is caused by infection with feline calicivirus or feline herpesvirus. Both viruses can cause discharge from the eyes and conjunctivitis. Other signs of cat flu include sneezing, discharge from the nose, lethargy, reduced appetite and fever.

An eye ulcer can also cause runny eyes. Other signs of an eye ulcer include red eyes, sensitivity to light, squinting or blinking, rubbing the eyes with the paw, the third eyelid remaining visible or cloudiness of the eye.

In some cases, runny eyes may be a sign that your cat has an allergy. Cats can be allergic to a variety of things, such as pollen, dust, mould, chemicals or certain foods. Other signs that a cat may be suffering from an allergic reaction include sneezing, itchiness and redness of the skin.

Runny eyes can also be caused by an overflow of tears (epiphora). This happens when there’s a problem with the drainage from the eye to the nose via the tear ducts, or too many tears are being produced. The most common reason for this is a blocked tear duct, which may be caused by inflammation of the nose and sinuses. Overproduction of tears can also be caused by the presence of small hairs on the under surface of the eyelid, and in addition to excessively teary eyes, you may see staining on your cat’s face.

Some breeds are prone to runny eyes (epiphora), most commonly, brachycephalic (flat-faced) breeds such as Persians and Exotic Shorthairs. Because of the shortening of the muzzle and other features in these breeds, tears can’t drain normally into the nose, resulting in runny eyes and often staining on the face. These cats also have protruding eyes due to the changes in the shape of the skull, and difficulty closing the eyelids. This can result in increased exposure of the surface of the eye (cornea), leading to pain and inflammation (keratitis).

## How do I know if my cat’s runny eyes need treatment?

If you notice any problem with your cat’s eyes, including that they’re runnier than usual, make an appointment with your veterinary clinic.

Treatment of runny eyes depends on the cause. Your vet will examine your cat’s eyes, make a diagnosis and start treatment. The earlier treatment is given, the better, as your cat may be experiencing discomfort and pain. If left untreated, eye infections can lead to permanent damage to the eye and even blindness. While generally nothing can be done to cure runny eyes in brachycephalic cats (caused by their face shape), they may need treatment for inflammation or infection that may develop as part of this condition.

If you have a cat with a flat face, such as a Persian, you may need to wipe around their eyes regularly if they water constantly. Read our article on caring for your cat's eyes, which includes tips on how to clean your cat’s eyes and how to apply eye drops if prescribed for a medical condition.

## Glossary of Terms

- Cornea– the outer transparent layer of the eye

- Conjunctiva– pink membrane lining the inside of the eyelids and part of the eye

- Conjunctivitis– inflammation of the pink membrane lining the inside of the eyes

- Rhinitis- inflammation of the lining of the nose

- Sinusitis- inflammation of the lining of the sinuses

- Keratitis- inflammation of the cornea
