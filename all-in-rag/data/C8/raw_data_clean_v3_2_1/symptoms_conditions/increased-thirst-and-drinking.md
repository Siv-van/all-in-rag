# Increased thirst and drinking

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/increased-thirst-and-drinking

---

How much water our cats drink under normal circumstances depends on their diet. A cat fed on wet food will get a large proportion of the water they need from their food, whereas cats fed mainly dry food will naturally drink more water. Changes in the amount your cat drinks could indicate a health problem.

## What is polydipsia?

If you notice that your cat is drinking more than usual, this may be a sign that something is wrong. Increased water intake is known as ‘polydipsia’.

## What causes cats to drink more water?

#### The most common causes of increased thirst and drinking are

Kidney disease

Diabetes mellitus

Hyperthyroidism

High blood calcium levels

But there are also other causes, such as liver diseases, that may result in polydipsia.

We can also see cats drinking more to compensate for water loss (or dehydration), for example, after a period of vomiting ordiarrhoea, or in warmer weather.

## How do I know if my cat is drinking too much?

If fed a wet diet, you may rarely, if ever, see your cat drinking. So, if you notice them visiting the water bowl more than normal, monitor their water intake for 24 hours. You can do this by filling their water bowl (s) to the brim, measuring the amount of water left at the end of the 24 hours, and taking this away from the volume in the full water bowl (s).

A cat drinking more than 100ml per kg of their body weight daily is drinking more water than usual.

Additional considerations

It can be tricky to monitor how much water your cat is drinking if they share water bowls with other pets or spend lots of time outside. You may need to consider keeping pets separate for 24 hours to measure how much water just one cat is drinking, providing it doesn’t cause any distress to your pets.

If your cat is drinking more than usual, you may also find they pass urine more often too. Make sure there’s always a clean litter tray available for your cat to avoid accidents in the house.

## When should I contact my veterinary team?

Any cat that is drinking more than usual should be seen by a vet, particularly if your cat is drinking over 100ml per kg of body weight daily. Even if you cannot measure the amount of water drunk, tell your veterinary team if you see your cat at the water bowl more often. If you’ve measured your cat’s water intake at home, let your veterinary team know the amount your cat is drinking.

It can be useful to collect a urine sample to take to your veterinary clinic, if possible - see below on how to collect a sample

## FAQs

## How will my vet find out what’s causing my cat to drink more?

As many conditions cause polydipsia in cats, your vet will usually take a blood and urine sample to determine the underlying cause. Once the cause of polydipsia is diagnosed, then suitable treatment can begin.

Additional resources and Practical tips

## How to collect a urine sample from your cat

#### You will need

Non-absorbable litter (from your veterinary clinic or pet shop)

A urine pot (if you’ve bought non-absorbable litter, it may come with one - if not, pick one up from your veterinary clinic)

#### How to collect the sample

Fill your cat’s litter tray with non-absorbable litter

Once your cat has used the tray, tip the litter tray so that the urine collects in one corner and use the pipette to collect a sample

Put the sample into the urine pot and seal the lid tightly

Write your cat’s name, the date, and the time the sample was collected on the label

## Glossary of Terms

- Polydipsia- increased water intake

- Polyuria- increased urination

- Hyperthyroidism- overactive thyroid
