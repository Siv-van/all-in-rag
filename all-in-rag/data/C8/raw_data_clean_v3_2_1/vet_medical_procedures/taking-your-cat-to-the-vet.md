# Taking your cat to the vet

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/taking-your-cat-to-the-vet

---

All cats should have at least one annual check at the veterinary clinic, but some cats need to visit more often. For many cats, a visit to the vet is a stressful experience. However, we can do things to reduce this stress.

## What makes veterinary clinic visits so stressful for my cat?

By nature, cats are independent, territorial, need to be in control of their surroundings, and are sensitive to different smells – all these things make visits to the vet stressful, for both you and your cat.

#### Things that can potentially cause stress to your cat when visiting the vet clinic include

- Discomfort, pain and sickness

- Lack of control

- Change in routine e.g. being kept indoors before being taken to the vet clinic or possibly having food withheld

- Travelling in the car

- Being unable to escape e.g. from the cat carrier, from being restrained during an examination, from the consultation room

- Previous negative experiences

- Unpleasant or strange social experiences e.g. seeing, hearing or smelling unfamiliar animals such as dogs and other cats

- Unfamiliar handling e.g. being restrained, being examined

- Unpleasant or strange sensations e.g. smell of disinfectant, stethoscope being placed against the chest

## How can I make my cat’s vet visit less stressful?

#### There are things you can do that can make a difference

Never travel with your cat loose in the car – always use a robust carrier

Choose your cat carrier carefully – cardboard, for example, is no match for a determined cat!

The carrier should be easy to clean (preferably plastic or plastic-coated) and not so large that your cat falls about in it

A carrier that opens at the top is much easier to use as your cat can be gently lifted in or out

Cover the carrier with a cloth or towel while travelling to keep your cat calm

Secure the carrier in a footwell or on a seat (with a seatbelt) so it can’t move, placing it on a towel/padding to keep it level in the car and to absorb any ‘accidents’

Drive carefully to avoid your cat being thrown around

Stay calm so your cat doesn’t pick up stress from you. Be reassuring and avoid loud noises (keep the radio low volume)

On arrival at the clinic, avoid rushing. Keep your cat in the carrier and hold it carefully – avoid swinging the carrier or banging it against objects or your legs – hold the carrier underneath rather than by the handle

#### Make sure there are familiar smells for your cat

Your cat will be less stressed if the carrier smells familiar and reassuring

Ideally, the carrier should be ‘part of the furniture’ at home and somewhere your cat chooses to sleep or is fed, so it doesn’t only appear when a visit to the vet is imminent

Put bedding your cat normally sleeps on at home in the carrier, along with an article of clothing belonging to your cat’s favourite person

Wipe a soft cloth around your cat’s face to pick up their scent and rub it around the carrier, especially in the corners, then leave it in the carrier

Spray the carrier with synthetic feline pheromones at least 15 minutes before putting your cat in it

If your cat panics at the sight of the carrier, keep calm. Keep it close, but out of sight. Wrap your cat in a thick towel/blanket that smells familiar and place your cat and the towel into the carrier quickly but gently. A top-opening carrier makes this much easier

Take some spare bedding (smelling of home) in case your cat is sick or soils the carrier

In the waiting room

Check with reception if there’s an area reserved for cats before bringing your cat in from the car. Ideally, look for anaccredited iCatCare Cat Friendly Clinicthat is committed to reducing stress and improving the veterinary experience for cats.

#### Additionally

Look for a completely separate room, or a separate area within the waiting room, for cats

Ask if your vet clinic offers different consulting times for cats

There should be shelves or raised areas where the cat carrier can be placed above floor level while waiting

Choose a quiet location and keep the cat carrier covered to avoid visual contact with others

Wait in the car until you are seen by the vet if your cat is very stressed

Talking in a quiet voice can help to reassure your cat

In the consulting room

#### Look for a clinic and staff that are quiet, gentle and respectful when handling your cat

An accredited Cat Friendly Clinic has promised to make sure all staff treat cats gently, respectfully and with professional skill

Cats usually need time to get used to the clinic and to calm down – this shouldn’t be a problem and staff should take the time needed

If you don’t understand what the vet tells you, ask for further explanation

If you’re unsure about giving your cat medication, ask a vet, vet nurse or assistant to demonstrate and explain how to do this without causing your cat stress. Our advice onhow to give your cat medicationmay also help

## What should I do if my cat has to stay at the clinic?

Occasionally, your cat may need to stay in the clinic. Again, an accredited Cat Friendly Clinic has promised to meet certain standards, and you should not be afraid to ask to see where your cat will be staying.

#### Look out for

A separate, calm, quiet cat ward; or a separate area within the hospitalisation ward for cats

Solid, clean and secure cages that are an adequate size

Comfortable bedding and bedding/boxes to allow cats to hide should be provided

You may be allowed to leave bedding from home for your cat to use (something that smells familiar)

If your cat is being kept in the clinic for a longer period, ask when you can visit

Taking your cat home after a visit to the vet

#### When your cat returns home after a stay at the vet clinic

If they’ve had sedation or an anaesthetic, your cat may still be quiet and unsteady on their feet, but occasionally may be hyperactive and more alert

Cats can be nervous and disorientated on returning home – talk to your cat and stroke or groom them gently, allowing them to initiate closer contact whenever they are ready, and use synthetic feline pheromones to add reassurance and security to your home

Prevent your cat from licking wounds or pulling any stitches. If this happens, contact your veterinary clinic

Call the clinic if you have any concerns such as the wound looking red, swollen or discharging; or if you’re concerned your cat is not recovering well

Ensure medication is given as instructed, and the full course is completed

Pain can be difficult to recognise in cats. Signs include loss of appetite, hiding or being withdrawn. If you’re worried, contact the clinic for advice

Your cat will likely need to stay indoors, at least initially, depending on the reason for their stay. Provide a quiet, warm spot in the house for your cat to recover with food, water and a litter tray (keeping the tray away from the food)

Re-introduction to other cats at home

#### Your cat will have picked up unfamiliar scents whilst at the veterinary clinic. These smells may cause anxiety in other cats in your home because they smell strange and/or mask your cat’s natural scent, making identifying your cat as part of the group difficult. It’s best to reintroduce your returning cat gently

Don’t overwhelm your returning cat with attention from people, other cats or dogs

Keep your cats in separate rooms for a few hours or a day or two and allow some gradual supervised contact. Some cats experience temporary behavioural changes after anaesthesia which could be perceived negatively by other cats in the household. It also gives your returning cat time to smell familiar again through grooming and interacting within the home

Wash bedding from your cat’s carrier, as this will remove any smells from the veterinary clinic

If your cats share bedding, offer bedding from shared resting areas to your returning cat in their separate room. This will help re-establish a familiar smell. Newly washed bedding should only be provided once your returning cat has been reintroduced to your resident cat after the recommended separation time

Keep a close eye on your cats when they are reintroduced to ensure all cats are comfortable

Spray the areas of your home where your cats spend most of their time with synthetic feline pheromones to provide a sense of security and reassurance
