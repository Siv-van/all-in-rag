# How to choose a veterinary clinic for your cat

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/how-to-choose-a-veterinary-clinic-for-your-cat

---

Taking your cat to the veterinary clinic can be stressful, both for your cat and for you, their owner or caregiver. For this reason, it’s important to choose a Cat Friendly Clinic that recognises the unique nature and needs of cats and puts measures in place to reduce stress wherever possible. It can also help to take recommendations from family or friends who use a local clinic they’ve come to know and trust.

## What should you look for in a good veterinary clinic?

#### It’s always best to decide which veterinary clinic to use before any specific need arises, ensuring your cat is already registered when the time comes to make an appointment. Finding a good clinic may be much harder if you need to find a vet urgently, as you won’t have time to look into the differences between clinics. The following points should help you choose

- Have a look around the clinic

Contact the clinic and explain that you are looking for a clinic for your cat and ask if you can go and visit (without your cat) to look around. Most veterinary clinics will be more than happy to show you around the clinic and allow you to see what facilities they have – some may host open days when you can visit. Think of it from your cat’s point of view as well, and whether you think they would be comfortable there. Remember that different clinics may have different facilities and approaches to cats and their owners or caregivers.

Ask if the clinic is an iCatCare Veterinary Society Clinic Member

The International Cat Care Veterinary Society is the veterinary division of International Cat Care. The iCatCare Veterinary Society offers Clinic Membership to veterinary clinics. Members regularly receive high-quality information on the latest developments in the veterinary care of cats.

Ask if the clinic is an iCatCare Veterinary Society Cat Friendly Clinic

The iCatCare Veterinary Society run aCat Friendly Clinicprogramme that specifically encourages clinics and the veterinary staff in clinics to be more ‘cat friendly’ and to have facilities and attitudes that help reduce the stress of a veterinary visit for your cat.

Accredited Cat Friendly Clinics must achieve minimum standards that help ensure your visit is as pleasant as possible for both you and your cat. You can find a list of accredited Cat Friendly Clinicshere. If the clinic belongs to our Cat Friendly Clinic programme, they will also have designated cat advocates who work to improve cat care – you can speak to them and ask them any questions you may have.

Ask if any of the vets are members of the iCatCare Veterinary Society’s Academy of Feline Practitioners

Just like doctors, many vets undertake further studies after graduation to develop deeper knowledge in certain areas. Membership of the iCatCare Veterinary Society’s Academy of Feline Practitioners is awarded to vets with further qualifications in feline practice or medicine, or specialist qualifications in disciplines complementary to the aims of the International Cat Care Veterinary Society (eg, cardiology, surgery). This forward-thinking worldwide group of vets advance developments in feline practice and welfare.

Ask if any of the nurses or technicians have completed the iCatCare Veterinary Society Certificate or Diploma in Feline Nursing

Like vets, nurses and technicians can complete further qualifications to gain deeper knowledge in different areas. Nurses and technicians who have completed the iCatCare Veterinary Society Certificate or Diploma in Feline Nursing will have a much greater understanding of, and knowledge about, cats.

Although there is a lot to take in when choosing a veterinary clinic for your cat, iCatCare Veterinary Society is leading on improvements to both veterinary knowledge and the quality of care for cats and cat owners in the clinic. Looking for a clinic that meets some or all of the above criteria will mean that you have peace of mind when taking your cat to the vet.

## What are the signs of a good Cat Friendly Clinic?

Waiting room

Many clinics offer a separate waiting area for cats, away from the stress of the sight, sound and smell of dogs. This is hugely beneficial, so see if your clinic offers this. Having shelves, benches or chairs so that the cat carrier can be raised off the floor also helps by making cats feel more secure and less vulnerable. Avoiding visual contact between cats, even with just a clean towel or blanket placed over the cat carrier, will also help cats relax. As an alternative to a separate area in the waiting room, some clinics offer cat-only consultation times to avoid cats having to be in a dog-filled waiting room.

Attitude of staff

We hope that all staff in the veterinary clinic know and understand the important differences in dealing with cats. Sadly, this is not always the case, but you can tell a lot by the greeting you receive, the questions asked, and the suggestions given when you first go to the clinic.

Cat information

Look out for good information about cats in the clinic too. Does the clinic provide leaflets and support on things like transporting your cat or giving your cat medications?

If the clinic has a positive attitude towards cats and the staff are proactive in minimising stress, this should help make your cat’s visit much more relaxing.

Using cat friendly pheromones

The clinic may use synthetic feline pheromones (these are chemicals very similar to those produced by cats to communicate with other cats) to help make cats feel more relaxed. You won’t smell these, but your clinic may use a diffuser plugged into a socket, or pre-sprayed blankets and towels in the waiting room, consultation room and cattery.

Getting your cat out of the cat carrier

Your vet will want to examine your cat, but see how they approach this. A good vet who understands cats will usually open the door or lid of the carrier, and, while talking with you, will see if your cat will come out of the carrier by themself. Your vet may even be able to perform some of the examination while your cat is still in their carrier (if they choose not to leave the perceived safety their carrier offers them) – this is usually only possible if the lid or top comes off or opens completely. Sometimes, cats won’t voluntarily come out of a carrier – if your vet needs to lift them out, make sure they do so gently and with respect. It can sometimes be helpful to have plenty of bedding in the carrier so that both the bedding and your cat can be lifted out together – their bedding will provide them with reassurance as it will carry their familiar scent.

Cat friendly handling

Vets and nurses who know and understand cats will adopt the ‘less is more’ approach. Cats try to avoid danger – they often panic if they’re restrained very firmly and realise they can’t escape. The art of cat examination is to provide the cat with choice and control. Taking a less is more approach while getting as much done as you can confidently and gently, without too much restraint, and leaving anything that may upset your cat until the very end. Avoiding direct eye contact with your cat (and doing a lot of the examination with your cat facing away from the vet) also helps to reduce stress, as does taking breaks and letting your cat find where they are most relaxed (even on a windowsill or on the floor, for example). A rubber mat on the table (or your cat’s bedding or blanket) is extremely helpful to avoid your cat having to stand on a slippery surface. Providing treats to lure your cat to desired locations, such as the weighing scales, without having to physically handle them, and to distract them during necessary procedures, such as blood pressure measurements or blood sampling, helps create a positive association.

Cat comfort

Many other aspects which make a veterinary clinic cat friendly will be going on behind the scenes, such as having a hospitalisation ward just for cats (without barking dogs), and comfortable cages (with somewhere to hide and perch, and warm, cosy familiar bedding). Simple things make a big difference; never be afraid to ask your vet if you can see where your cat will be kept if they need to stay in the clinic.

Good hygiene

You should look for a clean waiting room and consultation room, but preferably without an overpowering smell of disinfectant (which could be stressful for your cat). The clinic should be well-ventilated and largely odour-free.
