# How to give subcutaneous fluids to your cat

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/how-to-give-subcutaneous-fluids-to-your-cat

---

Subcutaneous (SQ or SC) fluid administration is the term used to describe giving fluids into the space under your cat’s skin, otherwise known as the subcutaneous tissue. From here, fluids can be slowly absorbed into your cat’s blood and body.

So, why would this be necessary? Quite simply, it’s a very useful way of providing additional fluids to cats and can help to manage and prevent dehydration.

Withchronic kidney disease (CKD or kidney failure), cats often produce more urine than usual. If they don’t drink enough to replace this extra fluid loss, they can become dehydrated, making their kidney disease worse. Regular SQ fluid administration can be a valuable management tool for cats with chronic kidney disease and may be given by your veterinary team, but can often be given at home, by you, with support from your vet.

## What fluids are used for SQ administration?

Only use fluids given to you by your veterinary team specifically for this purpose - the fluids are the same as those used for intravenous (IV) administration and are sterile (free of any bacteria, viruses, or fungi). Your veterinary team will give clear instructions on how much to administer, and how often, and supply you with any additional materials needed to administer the fluids (needles, tubing, etc).

The most common fluid given to cats is a balanced electrolyte solution known as ‘Hartmann’s’ or ‘Lactated Ringer’s’ solution, but your vet will choose the most appropriate solution based on your cat’s needs.

Used needles should be carefully placed in a suitable container and returned to your veterinary clinic for safe disposal.

## How often can SQ fluids be given?

SQ fluids can be given as often as needed - for most cats, they’re given between once a week and once a day (with 2-3 times weekly being the most common).

## What equipment is needed to administer SQ fluids?

#### Your veterinary team will provide you with everything you need, which will include

- A bag of fluids (don’t ever use fluids that appear cloudy or discoloured)

- A ‘giving’ set (tubing to connect the bag of fluids to the needle)

- Needles

- All equipment is sterile and should be supplied to you in sealed wrappers. Keep the equipment stored in the wrappers, only opening the various components when you are ready to use them and only handle with clean hands.

## How is the fluid given?

There are different ways to give SQ fluids, but using a ‘drip bag’ (the bag containing the fluid for administration) and a length of ‘drip tubing’ attached to a needle, which is placed under the skin, is the most common method. Most cats tolerate being given SQ fluids really well.

Watch our video on how to give your cat subcutaneous fluids or read the steps below.

The fluids can be warmed for 5-10 minutes in a bowl of warm water before being given, as this helps to reduce irritation for your cat. Close and attach the giving set (drip tubing) to the bag of fluids, half-fill the chamber with fluid, and then open the pinch clamp to allow a slow flow of fluids through the drip tubing until there’s no air left in the tubing. Close the clamp again, and attach the needle to the giving set (your veterinary team will demonstrate how to do this while keeping all equipment sterile).

The drip bag is suspended above the level of your cat so that the fluid can run into the space under the skin via gravity. Lift your cat’s skin at the scruff of the neck and insert the needle smoothly and gently into the tent that has formed. Fully open the clamp, and the fluids should flow freely. It usually takes several minutes to administer the fluid, and it can help to cuddle, stroke, or pet your cat during this period, or offer some food or a treat to distract them.

## How much fluid is given?

Your veterinary team will tell you how much fluid to give, and whether to give it all in one place or to use more than one area of the body. Generally, around 10-20 ml per kg of bodyweight of fluid can be given at a single SQ injection site (around 60-100 ml for an average-sized cat).

A soft lump will develop under the skin at the site where the fluid has been given. This shouldn’t be painful, and the fluid will gradually be absorbed over several hours. If the fluid is still obvious under the skin when your cat is next due for fluids, check with your veterinary team before giving any more.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Complications from SQ fluid administration are very uncommon, but if there is anything you’re concerned about, speak to your veterinary team without delay. If your cat develops a persistent swelling at the site of injection, and especially if this becomes hard, warm, and painful, this could indicate local infection that will require treatment.
