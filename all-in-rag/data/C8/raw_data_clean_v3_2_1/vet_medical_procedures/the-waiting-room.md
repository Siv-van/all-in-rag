# The Waiting Room

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/the-waiting-room

---

Cats will inevitably be anxious and stressed in the waiting room as they are outside their normal territory and will feel particularly vulnerable.

In this article, we look at ways to make the waiting area in your clinic more cat friendly.

Separation between cats and dogs

Prevent visual contact between cats and dogs by providing areas separated by a sight barrier to prevent cats seeing dogs and vice versa.

Separate waiting areas for dogs, and cats and small animals

This may be a permanent barrier between the dog and cat areas of the waiting room

Some clinics separate the two areas with a temporary or semi-temporary barrier or partition – for example, a wooden or metal barrier, or even a barrier composed of densely potted plants.

In some situations, a clinic may be able to have an entirely separate cat waiting room – this is an ideal situation but still needs additional thought about the layout

If separation is not possible in the waiting room, consider blocks of consultations for cats that are at separate times from those offered to dogs.

Separation from other cats

Cats also need to be protected from each other – they are not naturally social animals so direct eye contact between cats should be avoided. Sneeze barriers help prevent spread of infectious diseases. Provide a supply of clean blankets or towels that an owner can be asked to place over the carrier, ideally sprayed with synthetic feline pheromones.

Provide ‘Cat Parking’

Provide ‘cat parking’ to keep carriers off the floor and make cats feel less vulnerable.

As a minimum, cats in their carriers must be placed on a seat alongside the owner.

Having a strong shelf or unit on which to place carriers, with solid vertical barriers in between, is ideal and this should be 1m or more off the ground.

Make sure there is clear signage and clients are verbally directed

Minimise stress

#### Other ways to minimise stress to cats include

- situationg the cat waiting area in a way that minimises passing by dogs when cats come in and out of the clinic or move into a consulting room.

- Taking measures to keep dogs quiet in the waiting room, and perhaps have a separate room for noisy dogs or ask owners of noisy dogs to wait outside the clinic until they can be seen.

Having an empty consultation room into which a cat and owner can go if the cat is becoming particularly stressed in the waiting room is an advantage, but this should not be the only way of separating cats and dogs in the waiting area.

Having a low-level reception desk – or an area that is at a lower level – to provide an opportunity for cat carriers to be placed on the reception desk rather than on the floor when clients book in/out

Alternatively, a shelf or table can be provided next to the reception desk to place the cat carrier on, make sure cat owners are encouraged to use this!

Synthetic feline pheromone diffusers are a useful addition to the waiting room – if used along with other measures to make a waiting room cat friendly they may help reduce cat stress.

Client Communications

Have a display board in the waiting room with pictures of the staff, who they are and what they do. It is a good idea to also have this information on a clinic website.

It should be clear who the Cat Advocate (s) in the clinic are. Clients should know they can approach them for further information or advice if desired.
