# How to give a cat medication

---

Giving medication to your cat can be a daunting task. But considering the formulation (eg, liquid, tablet) and with support from your veterinary clinic, this can be done with minimal stress to both of you.

## What do I need to consider before giving my cat medication?

#### The key things to think about (and ask your veterinary team, as it may depend on the medication type) are

## Can the medication be given with food or a treat?

## Can I split or crush the tablet?

What’s the simplest way to administer the medication? Think about what’s easiest for both you and your cat

Ask for a demonstration – your veterinary team will be happy to show you how to give the medication

Do you need help? Sometimes having someone else hold the cat while you medicate can be easier

Don’t get hurt – your cat may become upset and unintentionally scratch or bite you, and this can be serious. If you are struggling, please tell your veterinary team as soon as possible

Is it causing stress? If you feel the process of medicating your cat is causing them significant stress, please speak to your veterinary team, as this will not help recovery from illness and can be upsetting for you and your cat

Tablets

## How should I give my cat a tablet?

#### There are two options when it comes to giving your cat a tablet - hiding the tablet in food or administering it directly into your cat’s mouth. Let’s look at both options

Giving the tablet with food

Check with your vet that the tablet can be given with food first. Some medication must be given with food, other tablets should be given on an empty stomach.

#### If it’s safe to give the tablet with food, follow our top tips

Make sure your cat is hungry! Take all food away for 12 hours (if appropriate) to make sure your cat will want to eat

Some tablets are designed to taste good (palatable), so you can try feeding these to your cat by placing them at the tip of your fingers. Most cats won’t eat a tablet voluntarily on its own because the flavour and/or texture is unfamiliar, so don’t be too disappointed if your cat won’t take a tablet in this way

You can try hiding small tablets in a little of your cat’s favourite food. Use a soft food that your cat loves, soft cheese, a small piece of soft meat or fish, or a treat. Some treats can be moulded around the tablet and others are specifically designed for giving medication

When hiding a tablet in food, make sure it’s completely hidden, and only offer a small amount of food initially. If your cat eats, check that the tablet hasn’t been left behind or spat out! For those cats that are clever at finding tablets hidden in food, you can consider crushing the tablet to mix it in more thoroughly - again, just check with your vet that it’s safe to do so and always take note of any precautions for handling the tablet

Crush the tablet (if appropriate) and mix with a liquid treat – some cats like that type of treat and will take the medication this way

Some crushed tablets may be mixed with a little water or oil (for example, from a tin of tuna in spring water). This can then be sucked up into a syringe (without a needle) and dribbled gently into the side of your cat’s mouth – always check with your vet before attempting this; it won’t be suitable for all tablets

Do not mix into your cat’s main meal as it could deter them from eating and make it hard to see that all medication has been consumed

Once your cat has eaten the small amount of food with their tablet in, offer the remainder of their meal as normal. If they need a second dose of medication later in the day, consider removing food once they have eaten to ensure they are hungry

Giving a tablet by hand

If your cat won’t take their tablet voluntarily or in with food, you’ll need to give it by hand. Gentle, safe restraint of your cat is important and, if possible, we recommend having two people for this task - one to give the tablet, and one to hold your cat.

Make sure your cat is on a stable, non-slip surface, such as the floor or a table

Allow your cat to sit upright in front of you but facing away from you

Gently hold each front leg above the elbow with your hands pressed softly against the sides of your cat

This hold prevents your cat from running off, keeps them sitting upright, and controls their front legs, paws, and claws.

#### Alternatively, restrain your cat with a towel

Use a mid-sized soft towel. Put the towel on the floor or a flat stable surface, then sit your cat on top of the towel, facing away from you

Bring up one side of the towel and then the other, around your cat’s neck so that they can’t get their front legs out of the opening

Hold your cat gently but firmly in the towel

Using a towel can be especially useful for very wriggly cats or if you don’t have a second person to help hold your cat.

Never heavily restrain your cat and let go if they are struggling or trying to bite or scratch. Then discuss other options with your veterinary team.

Giving the tablet

#### Having gently restrained your cat using one of the above techniques, you can now administer the tablet. Make sure you have everything ready before you restrain your cat. Try to do this quickly but calmly, so that your cat doesn’t get upset

Hold the tablet between the thumb and forefinger of one hand

Place the other hand on the top of your cat’s head (approach your cat from the side rather than from above – this is less threatening for your cat) and hold your cat’s head gently but firmly between your thumb and fingers. Extend your thumb and forefinger downwards to either side of their jaw

Gently tilt your cat’s head upwards and use the middle finger of the hand holding the tablet to pull the lower jaw down and open their mouth

Keep their head tilted up and quickly place or drop the tablet as far back on your cat’s tongue as you can. Aim for the centre of the tongue as far back as you can see – the further back the tablet goes, the harder it is for your cat to do anything other than swallow it

Hold your cat’s jaw closed for a few seconds and wait for them to swallow. Gently rubbing their throat under the chin may help. If your cat licks their lips or nose, you know they’ve swallowed

If your cat spits the tablet out, as long as they’re not distressed, you can try repeating the procedure - you may find a pill giver helpful (read on for advice on how to use pill givers)

Follow tablets with a little treat or water – some medications can irritate the oesophagus between the mouth and the stomach so check with your vet if this applies to your cat’s medicine.

Remember, if you have problems and/or your cat gets upset, contact your veterinary team for advice on how they may be able to help.

Liquids

Liquid medications can be mixed into a small amount of food (if approved by your vet) or a liquid treat

Do not mix into your cat’s main meal as it could deter them from eating and make it hard to see that all medication has been consumed

If necessary, follow the steps above from restraining your cat, but insert the syringe of liquid into the side of the cat’s mouth and gentle depress the plunger.

Other forms of medication such as powders or capsules can be given in similar ways.

Try to follow giving medication with something positive, this could be a favourite treat, a brush or a short period of play with a wand toy. This may help avoid negative associations with medication.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you’re struggling to medicate your cat with tablets prescribed by your vet, contact your practice for advice. It may be possible for your veterinary team to give the medication or advise on alterative formulations.

Gelatine capsules

Available from your veterinary clinic

An empty gelatine capsule can be used to put two or more small pieces of tablet inside so that they can be administered in one go - especially helpful if your cat needs more than one type of tablet. Always check with your vet before giving more than one type of tablet at the same time – this can sometimes cause problems

The two halves of the gelatine capsule can be pulled apart, the pieces of tablet placed inside, and then the two halves put back together

The shape of the capsule means that it can be easier to administer but lubricating it with a little smear of butter or something similar may also help. The capsules can be administered by hand or with a pill-giver

‘Spot on’ medication

#### Some medication (typically flea and worm preventatives) can be given by dropping liquid onto the back of your cat’s neck. The following tips can help

- Read the manufacturer instructions for how the pipette is undone (varies between products) before holding your cat so you are completely ready

- Wear gloves if recommended and wash hands after application

- Part the fur high on the back of the neck to apply the product. Too low and your cat may be able to reach it to lick it off

- Distract your cat with a treat or favourite food

- Have another person gently hold your cat as described above (with or without a towel) so you can focus on applying the product

- Some cats will immediately run off once the product is applied (some feel cold) so close cat flaps if it is raining to avoid it being washed off immediately

- Try to follow with a positive experience as for other medications, a treat, a play or brush as preferred by your cat.

- Spot on parasite treatment being applied to the back of a cat’s neck, parting the hair and applying close to the skin
