# Blood donor cats

https://icatcare.org/articles/blood-donor-cats

A variety of conditions can cause severeanaemia (reduced numbers of red blood cells) in cats and sometimes this results in the need for a life-savingblood transfusion.

Other blood products such as plasma (blood without the red cells) or platelets (that play a role in blood clotting) may also be needed to manage some conditions.

There may be times when your local veterinary clinic is seeking blood donor cats. This article looks at what you need to consider before volunteering your cat for blood donation.

## What must I consider before volunteering my cat as a blood donor?

If you’re considering volunteering your cat as a blood donor, any potential risks to your cat must be carefully evaluated first. This is especially important in cats where the risks of donating blood may be higher than in people or dogs.

## What are the risks for a cat that donates blood?

Most cats need to be sedated for blood donation, which itself carries a small risk. Tests should be performed to check the donor’s health and for example identify underlying heart or kidney problems.

## What can be done to minimise the risks of blood donation?

A series of checks should be performed to ensure your cat is suitable as a blood donor, as not all cats are.

Suitability to be a blood donor

#### Your cat’s weight, age and health status should be carefully assessed by a vet. Donor cats should

Ideally, weigh more than 4.5 kg (but should not be overweight)

Be aged between 1 and 8 years (but ideally, 1 to 5 years old)

Have a calm temperament and not become overly stressed when visiting the veterinary clinic

Be up-to-date withvaccinations, worm and external parasite control.

Not being treated with any medication

Preferably, live indoors (where there’s less exposure to infectious diseases)

Not have previously received a blood transfusion

Have normal blood test results

Test negative forfeline leukaemia virus (FeLV) andfeline immunodeficiency virus (FIV)

Test negative for other infectious diseases (this may vary depending on geographical location)

#### Health checks your vet will perform should include

- Blood tests to check your cat’s red and white blood cells

- Blood tests to check kidney and liver function

- Blood tests to rule out certain infectious diseases

- Blood tests to determine your cat’sblood group

- Additional tests may include a scan of your cat’s heart and measurement of blood pressure

- Giving your consent

Before volunteering your cat to become a blood donor, you should discuss the risks involved with your veterinary team. You will need to sign a consent form to confirm you understand the procedure and risks, and that you give consent for blood donation to proceed.

Precautions during and after blood donation

Because cats are usually sedated for blood donation, food should be withheld for 4–5 hours before a donation is performed to reduce the risk of vomiting during sedation

All relevant pre-donation checks should be performed

Blood donation involves collecting blood from a large vein (jugular vein) in the neck. Generally, no more than 40-50 ml of blood is collected from any one cat

Cats should always be kept warm and monitored closely after blood donation. They are usually fed as soon as they have recovered from the sedation and are awake enough to eat

When a cat is returned home, keep them separate from other pets for a short period while they recover and allow them to acquire scent of home rather than the veterinary clinic

Additional considerations

Provided your cat is a suitable donor, and has been thoroughly assessed before the procedure, the risks to a donor cat are low. Providing blood for a recipient cat may genuinely be a life-saving procedure. However, with any intervention such as blood donation, small risks remain, despite undertaking all appropriate precautions.

To understand more about blood donation and transfusions in cats, read ourcat caregiver guide on blood transfusions.

Don’t rush your decision

It’s important not to rush into the decision to volunteer your cat to donate blood – you must be comfortable with what will happen, and confident that all the necessary precautions are being taken. Discuss everything with your vet and ask them to explain anything you are unsure about.
