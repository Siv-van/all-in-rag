# Blood transfusions in cats

https://icatcare.org/articles/blood-transfusions-in-cats

As with people, a blood transfusion for a cat can be a life-saving procedure. However, feline blood transfusions need to be undertaken with care – there are practical difficulties associated with the collection and storage of blood, and a risk of complications for both the donor cat and recipient.

## When is a blood transfusion needed?

A blood transfusion provides an immediate supply of red blood cells (RBCs), which are essential for carrying oxygen around the body, meaning it can be life-saving in severelyanaemiccats. However, blood contains many other elements which may also be useful.

#### When blood is transfused, the three major elements that can be provided are

Red blood cells (erythrocytes, RBCs): these are the cells that carry oxygen around the body and are given to treat anaemia

Plasma: this is the fluid component of blood (within which the red blood cells are suspended). It contains many proteins with essential functions

Platelets: these are very small cells in the blood that work alongside clotting factors to allow blood to clot and prevent prolonged bleeding after injury

Blood can be separated into these three components, however, this is less commonly performed in cats as quite a lot of blood is needed to produce enough plasma or platelets.

## FAQs

## Do cats have different blood groups?

There are three major blood groups in cats — A, B and AB. Group A is the most common, while group B is common in some pedigree breeds. Group AB is rare in all breeds.

Cats given a blood transfusion of a different blood group can develop a severe life-threatening transfusion reaction. Cats can have naturally occurring antibodies (a part of the immune system) in their blood against red blood cells of the wrong type. The incompatibilities and risk of severe transfusion reactions mean cats should only ever receive a matched blood transfusion (where the donor has the same blood group as the recipient).

Read our separate article to learn more aboutblood groups and blood incompatibility in cats.

## Is it safe for my cat to have a blood transfusion?

Although transfusions can be lifesaving, because of the possibility of severe transfusion reactions, great care is required to ensure the transfusion does not do more harm than good.

Steps must be taken to ensure, as far as possible, that the donor and recipient cats’ blood are compatible

The donor cat should be screened for blood-borne infections includingfeline leukaemia virus (FeLV), feline immunodeficiency virus (FIV), and Mycoplasma haemofelis (the cause of feline infectious anaemia), plus other infections depending on where the cat lives in the world

Great care is required during the transfusion to avoid overloading the recipient cat’s circulation, as this can lead to heart failure – generally, a transfusion needs to be given slowly because of this

Recipient cats should be monitored very closely, especially during the start of the transfusion, to identify any reactions

#### Blood collection from a donor cat must be undertaken carefully to ensure risks are kept to an absolute minimum. Current recommendations are that the donor cats must be

Healthy, fully vaccinated and ideally living completely indoors

Less than 8 years old

Over 4.5 kg lean bodyweight

Normal number of red blood cells

Free of FeLV/FIV/Mycoplasma haemofelis

Not stressed by a visit to the vet

Further tests may depend on local rules and can include a check of the donor cat’s heart and blood pressure.

Even when all these criteria are met, donating blood is not without risks – donor cats can develop low blood pressure, and occasionally, previously undetected heart disease may become apparent after a cat donates blood, which can cause significant issues. Thankfully, the vast majority of donations are performed without complication, provided the donor is selected carefully.

Feline blood banks

With the precautions necessary for blood transfusions, it’s not always easy to find a suitable donor cat in an emergency. Blood banks exist in some countries, allowing vets to order blood of the correct type.
