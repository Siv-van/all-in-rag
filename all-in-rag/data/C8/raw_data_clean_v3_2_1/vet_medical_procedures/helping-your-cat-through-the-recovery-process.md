# Helping your cat through the recovery process

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/helping-your-cat-through-the-recovery-process

---

It can be a very stressful and worrying time when our cats need to visit the veterinary clinic, especially if the reason for the visit is illness or injury. Sometimes, your cat may need to return to see your vet for further investigation and/or surgery, or they may need to be admitted immediately for emergency procedures. You may feel helpless at this stage, but there are things you can do to help your cat through the recovery process.

## What can I do to help my cat recover from illness or injury?

Planned sedation or general anaesthetic

Your cat may be booked in for a procedure which requires heavy sedation or general anaesthesia. This can range from an elective procedure, such asneuteringor dental treatment, to investigations such as x-rays and surgical operations. For these planned procedures, always follow the instructions given by your veterinary team.

You will likely be asked to withhold food from a set time the night before, although your cat should still have access to fresh water. To allow this, and to ensure your cat doesn’t get access to food elsewhere, it’s a good idea to keep them indoors overnight the night before with access to a litter tray – ensure windows and doors are secure, and cat flaps are locked to help prevent escape. Make sure your cat has had an opportunity to urinate/defecate before taking them to the veterinary clinic.

Ask your veterinary team if you can leave a blanket or item of clothing with your cat during their stay in the veterinary clinic, so your cat has a familiar and reassuring smell with them. Bear in mind that this item may get soiled or lost, so make sure it’s something you are prepared to part with. You should also make sure your veterinary team has up-to-date phone numbers to contact you during your cat’s stay.

Veterinary teams do a fantastic job of helping our cats to feel well enough to go home, but recovery doesn’t stop there. As a cat owner, you have an important role to play in getting your cat back to their normal self.

Coming home

Once your cat is home, remember that they are recuperating and require some special treatment during this time.

Always follow your veterinary team’s instructions regarding exercise, medications, feeding, dressings, etc. If your cat has been given an Elizabethan collar to wear, make sure it’s worn at all times. As tempting as it is to remove it, it takes just a few seconds for a cat to pull a stitch out, meaning a trip back to the clinic! If the collar is very stiff and seems uncomfortable, ask your veterinary team if something more comfortable is available, as there are several different types of collars, some more cat-friendly than others ie soft collars. If your cat is wearing a collar, they may require their food and water bowls slightly lifted off floor level to help them reach the bowls comfortably – try placing them on a couple of books, a step or a small box.

Keep your cat indoors in a warm, quiet area to rest during the recovery period. Your clinic may offer an alternative to a collar, such as a medical pet shirt. Whether your cat tolerates these will depend on their individual preferences. Some cats may not like the restriction in movement or will not be used to wearing items of clothing on their bodies and will not have been familiarised to these during their key socialisation period when they were kittens (2-7 weeks of age). Being introduced to them at a time when they are feeling unwell/recovering from surgery for the first time may elicit protective emotions (fear/anxiety/frustration), which can potentially delay healing and recovery. Cats should be closely monitored when they are wearing a protective item to prevent wound interference. Be sure to follow your veterinary team's advice and report any concerns you have to them immediately.

Your cat may need to be confined to a crate or small room (for example, after a fracture repair), so make sure you have all the resources they need (bed and blanket, food bowl, water bowl, litter tray, scratching resource) ready and set up before you collect them from the veterinary clinic. Cats are very good at hiding pain and may only display very subtle behavioural changes. If you are concerned that your cat may be in pain or if they are behaving differently than normal (bearing in mind that your cat may be a bit subdued), contact your veterinary team for further advice. Although it’s important to give your cat lots of tender loving care, they also need space and time alone to rest and recover.

Food is important

Nutrition is particularly important when an animal is recovering from illness or injury. Cats that don’t eat enough are prone to malnutrition, which could lead to complications such as poor wound healing or a less robust immune system. Inadequate nutrition during recovery can delay the recovery time, as well as increase veterinary clinic visits and overall costs.

It’s important that overweight pets still eat during recovery – even though they carry a few extra pounds, it doesn’t mean that they are any less affected by a lack of nutrient intake. In fact, overweight cats who don’t eat enough are at higher risk of developing hepatic lipidosis – a life-threatening condition of the liver.

Your vet may recommend a suitable recovery diet – there are some tasty foods available to help your cat recover.

There are very few reasons to withhold food when a cat is ill. If you are concerned that your cat might be feeling nauseous, or you have any other concerns, contact your veterinary team who will be able to provide further advice. Cats who haven’t eaten for 2 days or have eaten less than 80% of their daily food requirements for 5 days, are at risk of malnutrition and must be seen by a vet as soon as possible.

## How can I encourage my cat to eat?

#### There are several things you can try toencourage your cat to eat, such as feeding by hand. You can also try

Warming your cat’s food – wet food can be placed in the microwave for just a few seconds (make sure it isn’t too hot in the middle)

Adding warm (not boiling) water, the water from a can of tuna in spring water, or the water left over from cooking chicken to dry food and allow it to soak for a while

Plenty of tender loving care – give your cat attention and encourage them to eat by hand feeding if required but never force them to eat as they could learn negative associations with the food. Try sitting with them, talking quietly and offering food

Resist the urge to offer ‘human’ foods such as chicken or tuna as this can encourage bad feeding behaviours once your cat is feeling better. If you reach the point where you feel the need to try human food because your cat is still not eating, even after trying the tips above, speak to your veterinary team for further advice

Once your cat has fully recovered, you can reintroduce their regular food. This should be done slowly over 7 days so as not to cause any digestive upsets

Remember, your veterinary team will always be happy to answer your questions or concerns, so if something is worrying you about your cat’s health, be sure to contact them – there is no such thing as a silly question.
