# I’ve found a stray cat, what do I do?

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/ive-found-a-stray-cat-what-do-i-do

---

Because in some parts of the world, including the UK, cats are mostly allowed outside to roam where they wish, it can be difficult to know whether a cat that comes onto your property needs help or not. Not all cats that are unowned are stray pet cats – some are free-roaming, unsocialised and living around people because they provide a plentiful source of food. These would be referred to as street cats and the concept of reuniting them with estranged owners is not applicable.

All cats look similar, whether they are owned pets or not, and you mustn’t make assumptions about their back stories. The cat may look unwell and in need of help but is still being cared for by someone, for example, an older cat may lose weight and condition, or may be having treatment for a problem. Equally, if the cat looks healthy it may still be unowned. You do not want to intervene with a loved owned cat, or to ignore a cat which needs help. Here is a useful check list to consider before rushing to do something.

Checklist

## How many times have you seen this cat?

Firstly, wait and see! If this is the first time you have seen this cat, it may be that the owners have only moved into the neighbourhood recently. It’s wise not to encourage the cat onto your property, particularly if you have cats of your own. If the cat returns repeatedly, or stays in your garden overnight, then this would be more unusual and you can start investigating further.

## Is the cat trying to get into your home?

Is the cat confident and trying to get inside your home? This might give you an indication that this is someone’s pet which is either visiting from its own home or has become lost or abandoned.

## Does the cat appear hungry?

This isn’t always a good way to establish whether a cat needs your help, as cats are opportunistic feeders and even the most well-fed cat will seek out food in places where they do not normally find it. If you start feeding this cat, then it will continue to return and if it is a pet belonging to a neighbour you may end up confusing the cat and upsetting its owner. Many acrimonious disputes with neighbours can start with something as simple as a bowl of cat food left outside. Bear in mind any food left out, whether intended for a cat or not, may attract one!

## Does the cat have a collar with owner details on it?

Can you get close enough to this cat to check out any collar or disc with contact details? Don’t persevere if the cat becomes frightened or distressed at being handled by a stranger as they may bite or scratch you. Even if the cat does not have any visible identification, it may still have a microchip implanted that registers the details of its owner.

## Does the cat have a piece missing from the tip of its ear?

If the cat has the tip of its left (usually) ear missing, in a neat line across, then this indicates that the cat has been neutered as part of a Trap, Neuter, Return (TNR) programme which is a humane way of managing free-roaming, unowned street cat populations. The cat is already neutered (which is very important) and previously was returned to a place where someone kept an eye on it and perhaps provided food, but the cat lived independently. This may be near you and the cat has roamed onto your property. A local homing organisation or TNR group may be able to help with where the cat came from. You may decide you are happy to have the cat on your property and agree to support it with food and shelter; if not, local cat organisations may be able to provide you with a humane trap that you can bait with food – they will give you instructions for its use or do the job for you, and the cat can be returned to their previous site, if it is available. However if that is nearby, the cat may return!

## Is the cat scared of you?

If the cat is obviously scared of you this could mean that they have not been socialised with people at an early age and therefore they may not be a pet cat. However, remember that even some loved pet cats are nervous of strangers. If you haven’t been able to get close to this cat or locate an owner, then they may not be a pet cat at all, but a street cat. The priority for these cats is to ensure they are neutered so that more kittens are not brought into the world, often to suffer and die. It is very difficult to establish whether females have been neutered (spayed), unless they are obviously pregnant, but intact males develop broad cheeks and narrow eyes and they have very obvious testicles too.

## Does the cat appear sick or injured?

You may decide this cat needs veterinary care if there is something about its behaviour, appearance, body condition or posture that suggests it is sick or injured, for example, it is limping, very thin, has a matted, dirty coat, obvious injury or has collapsed. People can often confuse a very old, emaciated cat with a young kitten, so the cat could even be an elderly and confused pet cat that has wandered away from a loving home, and forgotten how to get back, rather than an abandoned kitten.

## What do I do next?

Don’t forget to ask your neighbours, the cat may have a home and live close by even if you haven’t seen it before.

Bear in mind that cats have no respect for boundaries and travel from street to street via adjacent back gardens often, so home, from the cat’s perspective, may be several streets away. Sometimes a quick look at a map may give you a better idea of how many people you need, in theory, to contact. Local social media groups or a notice in a local shop can be great at spreading the word that the owner needs to be contacted.

Contact your local vet practices and homing centres to check whether any cats have been reported missing.

If it is safe for both you and the cat to do so, put a paper collar around the cat’s neck with your number on, asking the owner to get in touch. If no owner comes forward, you can then contact your local homing centre and they may be able to scan the cat for an implanted microchip that would provide the details of the owner, or agree to take the cat in and keep them for a period of time to give the owner a chance to become reunited with their lost pet. After that, the cat will be put up for adoption. More cats are actually reunited with their owners if left where they are, rather than being taken into homing centres, so you may be advised to only consider a homing centre if the cat is in danger or there is a specific reason why the cat cannot remain outdoors.

If the cat appears sick or injured, contact your local veterinary practice for instructions. If you cannot touch the cat to put it in a carrier, contact your local vet, or animal law enforcement agency, who will be able advise you on the best approach.

If the owner cannot be found, and no homing organisation can take the cat, feeding needs to be the first step in a plan to neuter, otherwise you are just making cats heathy to produce even more kittens. Some organisations will discuss with you what is best for the cat and, once the cat is neutered, they may suggest it remains in its current location, if it appears healthy and content, and someone takes the responsibility for its care. If you agree to do this, even if the cat never appears keen to come into your home, you still have a duty of care to provide veterinary treatment when needed and make provisions for feeding when you are away.

If you are planning to trap the cat as part of the TNR process, find out if anyone else is feeding the cat. If so, let this person continue, don’t confuse the cat by encouraging it to go to multiple locations for food - staying in one place will make trapping easier.

If the cat is unneutered and doesn’t come near you, this still doesn’t mean that the cat is not owned – as mentioned previously it may just be a timid pet that is wary of strangers. It is therefore important to establish the presence of an owner before you consider neutering.

## What do I do if I find a nest of kittens on my property?

If you ever find a nest of kittens, always observe from a distance, if the mother does not return within three hours then contact your local homing centre, vet or animal welfare organisation. If the kittens can be heard crying and you can see them, and they are dirty, then they may have been abandoned by the mother or she may have been killed. If this is the case, these kittens need urgent care to survive, and your local veterinary practice or homing centre will provide assistance. They may arrange for the kittens to be taken in and hand-reared.

If the mother returns you can leave food nearby, but the goal is to feed her regularly and eventually trap her and her kittens for neutering. Always make sure, even in these cases, that every effort is made to find an owner.
