# What is Trap, Neuter, Vaccinate and Return?

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/vwhat-is-tnvr

---

Trap Neuter Vaccinate and Return is a humane approach to managing unowned cat populations.

## How does TNVR work?

Trap

Traps are used to safely and kindly catch the cats, ensuring they are not harmed and causing minimal distress. These cats can’t be handled by people, so the traps act as a humane alternative to handling the cat and placing them into a carrier. Trying to do that could cause serious injury through bites and scratches, as well as emotional distress.

Neuter

Neutering (also called castration for males and spaying for females, sterilisation, altering, fixing or de-sexing) is carried out by qualified vets. It ensures that the cats do not continue to reproduce, increasing populations. It can also reduce associated behaviours (eg, fighting, male urine spraying, night-time noise, etc) that communities often complain about.

The TNVR process also includes identifying cats that have been neutered, by removing the tip of the left ear under anaesthesia (referred to as ear tipping). This allows cats to be identified from a distance, ensuring they are not trapped again for neutering. In many cases, the ear tip serves as a trademark and, particularly in the USA, is seen as a sign of a safe and cared-for colony.

Vaccination

Vaccinationagainst diseases prevalent in the area (eg, rabies and/or flu viruses) takes place at the time of neutering.

Return

Returning the cats to their original site keeps them in a familiar territory and their presence acts as a deterrent to other cats who may be attracted by the food source.

#### It is important to

Monitor the colony of cats to make sure that any new arrivals are trapped and neutered

Continue to monitor the cats for signs of illness or injury and take action

Clear food waste and deal with any problems quickly to ensure the local community is happy with the continued presence of the cats

In some countries, you may also need to clear faecal waste

## How many cats within the group need to be neutered for this to be an effective solution?

#### The aim should be to neuter 100% of the cats in the group; however

It is recommended that a minimum rate of 75%+ of the cats should be neutered as quickly as possible for the programme to stabilise the population effectively

This figure is not a fixed formula — outcomes will vary depending on factors such as colony size, movement of cats in and out of the area, and local resources — but it is sound guidance to aim towards

The goal is for neutering to outpace both the birth rate and the arrival of new cats into the group. Any new cats arriving in the group must be neutered

If there is movement with other groups close by, they may need to be included to ensure success

Success is most achievable by focusing on one colony at a time, working intensively to reach the target rate quickly, and then maintaining it by monitoring and neutering new arrivals. This “front-loading” approach is far more effective than piecemeal efforts spread too thinly

Where breeding cycles exist, TNVR should be timed to align with it, ideally before females give birth. This increases the number of pregnant spays,

avoids difficult decisions about returning lactating queens, and reduces the need to trap and neuter kittens laterCats which have evaded trapping will require greater effort as they become more suspicious of the traps as time goes on

In addition, neutering of owned pet cats to a high rate (85-90% of the total pet population) should be encouraged to prevent the unowned population from increasing by the addition of abandoned, unwanted kittens or adult cats that owners no longer want due to their reproductive behaviour

On rare occasions, cats may need to be released to a new location because their original site is no longer available (eg, an area is being built on). Finding new sites can be difficult, and the cats may have to be trapped and relocated in small groups as sites become available. This is a last resort and needs careful preparation and planning to acclimatise the cats to their new environment and carers.

There will be a time lag in TNVR population reduction compared with inhumane removal methods such as culling (often using poison) because the cats are returned as long as they are healthy but obviously cannot reproduce. If incoming cats are neutered, numbers will stabilise or reduce as cats eventually die. There should be an improvement in cat welfare and a gradual decrease in the population size if managed well. If reproduction is prevented, people often enjoy having healthier cats in the area, as difficult, distressing situations are reduced, such as the sight of a sick kitten.

Typically, the impact on the population becomes noticeable over the first 2 years, but it can take up to 10 years to stabilise. Ongoing management will be required, otherwise the population is likely to increase again.

Other ways to describe TNVR

There can be local variations to this process, but the principle is fundamentally the same. The variation is often linked to particular situations, such as resources or local views on the cats.

#### Below are the most commonly used alternative terms to TNVR

TNRTF: Trap, Neuter, Return To Feeder - the cat is returned to a specific person responsible for feeding

TNRel/Tnt: Trap, Neuter Release/Translocate - cats are relocated for a specific reason, such as predation on wildlife, or demolition of their current site, etc.

•TNR: Trap, Neuter, Return

TNVRM: Trap, Neuter, Vaccinate, Return and Manage – the addition of a specified caregiver to feed and monitor

TTVAR: Trap, Test, Vaccinate, Alter, Release – cats are tested for specific diseases (and results acted upon) before neutering and release

CCM: Community Cat Management - A coordinated, humane approach to managing unowned cats, combining neutering, welfare support, and community engagement.
