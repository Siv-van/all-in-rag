# Why is iCatCare helping unowned cats?

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/why-is-icatcare-helping-unowned-cats

---

There are millions of unowned and unwanted cats worldwide. Everyone has seen these cats around their own towns and cities or when they travel. Some cats seen on the street may be neutered pets with free access outside that will return home, but there are likely to be many cats that are street cats and pet cats that have been abandoned or have strayed from home.

#### The cat is adaptable, clever and self-reliant. Cats can hunt, they can scavenge and they can encourage people to give them food, even if they don’t like people that much! What this means is that cats can survive even in tough circumstances and, true to the myths which surround them, are indeed very fertile – breeding from 4 months old and having several litters of kittens a year. Where food is abundant and the climate is not severe, populations increase in numbers and cause a nuisance for the local community because of

- Public health worries about disease or hygiene problems

- The noise, smell or presence of excessive waste material

- Concerns over impact on wildlife/vulnerable species/ecosystems

It also means that many cats and kittens will die from malnutrition, accidents, disease and even acts of cruelty.

## Who deals with the problems?

In the UK these problems are dealt with by animal welfare charities but across the world, it is often the responsibility of local government authorities or municipalities and, when they receive complaints, they may attempt to control numbers, often using poison or inhumane methods, or removing them to cages where they remain indefinitely. Their motivation is likely to be removing the problem from the streets, rather than being driven by cat welfare.

Cat lovers and tourists in many places are distressed to see cats suffering and want something to be done to help the cats. There can be passionate conflict about what should be done. Tourists may feed cats on their holidays or even bring a cat back home with them, but this, of course, is not a solution for most cats.

The answers are not simple and require collaboration for success and to make the best of resources (knowledge, effort, time and money). Charities and non-government organisations (NGOs) and individuals also try to help cats but homing centres can become overcrowded (owners may also be relinquishing cats there) and cat welfare suffers consequently. Government and NGOs may try and take a long-term view to reduce numbers by implementing humane trap, neuter and return (TNR) programmes which need to be well organised and planned, but can be effective in the long-term.

Any organisation or government tackling cat population problems must consider the bigger picture of contributing factors and the vital importance of neutering all types of cat.

## Helping is not straightforward

Even in countries where street cats are not numerous, people wishing to relinquish pet cats or kittens to homing centres can mean there is an unlimited need for help. It is very easy to keep taking cats into homing centres (or very difficult to refuse to take them), which can result in crowded, stressful accommodation for them, where it is easy to spread and catch diseases. Additionally, being in confinement with no control over their environment may be very distressing for some cats, especially if they are fearful of people and all the things which go on around them. Being stressed affects the immune system, making the cat more vulnerable to disease. What may seem to be a simple plan of keeping cats and finding them new homes is not straightforward, and caring for them in the right way is crucial. Unfortunately, good intentions alone may not result in good cat welfare, and people can easily become overwhelmed.

To make space for new cats to come into homing centres, cats must go to new homes. This relies on people wanting to take on cats which have been owned before rather than just getting new kittens. To go to new homes and make good pets, cats will need to have had the right start in life so that they are comfortable around people, otherwise, they may be highly stressed, hide away and avoid interaction with people. Different cats need different things, so while it may seem that helping cats is simple, it requires knowledge to do it successfully and to ensure cats do not suffer.

Unfortunately, in some places cats may be culled (selectively killed) to make space for more cats coming in. This is highly distressing for the staff and volunteers who want to help the cats When the onslaught of cat numbers is huge it is hard to take the time to consider the wider causes and solutions, and can result in a vicious circle of intake and culling.

iCatCare Cat Friendly Solutions for Unowned cats

International Cat Care has developed its cat expertise over 65 years working with the veterinary profession, with researchers and experts and those working in the field, to understand cats in these situations and created pragmatic, effective Cat Friendly Solutions. It provides advice and training for those working in the industry to help them to navigate these problems.
