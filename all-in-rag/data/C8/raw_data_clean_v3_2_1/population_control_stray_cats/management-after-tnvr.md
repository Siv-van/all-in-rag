# Management after TNVR

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/management-after-tnvr

---

It’s essential to provide ongoing care and supervision to the neutered group of cats to ensure the population remains stable or reduces, and that the cats’ welfare is monitored. Keeping records for cats that haven’t been neutered or trapped is important so they are not forgotten and can be targeted in ongoing management of the group. Ideally, regular caregivers will be able to monitor the cats, with cover for planned and unplanned absences. They will need to maintain good relationships with the local community and neighbours.

Monitoring the group of cats

Keep records for each cat and monitor for signs of absence, illness or injury

Note any incoming cats, check if they are lost pets and, if not, arrange for them to be neutered

Keep a record of all caregivers and their contact details, and provide them with a way to contact you

Organised feeding

If the cats haven’t been fed previously and are surviving well from scavenging and hunting, there’s no need to begin providing additional food. Feeding unowned cats is the primary cause of increasing numbers and populations growing out of control. Feeding cats that are already feeding themselves may attract more cats, and potentially, the existing cats may reduce their pest control function. This may lead to people finding their presence less positive

If the group is used to being fed, a feeding area should be set up to keep food fresh and protected from the elements, as well as deter wildlife/pests. It must be well-maintained and kept clean, with waste removed to avoid problems with smell, etc., for the community

Provide food and water at consistent times, eg, mornings or evenings. Dry food is easier to use, keeping wet food for when you need to trap or monitor the cats (as it’s more desirable) or to add treatments (for example, awormingtreatment). Try to keep the food consistent in terms of type and brand, or make a planned changeover by gradually mixing the food. Ensure the correct amount of food is provided to avoid cats becomingobeseand developing associated health conditions

Providing shelter

Shelter may be needed in poor conditions or for vulnerable cats; you can find some useful ideashere, and more are widely available online.

In colder climates, cats can get frostbite on their ears, nose and paws. Even though free-living cats typically grow a thicker coat for winter, this is only effective if the fur doesn’t become wet or frozen, which often leads to hypothermia. Shelters must be dry and draught-proof with loose insulating material that allows the cat to burrow inside. Straw is a good insulator as it repels moisture and is less likely to develop mould or rot than other materials. Using folded blankets, towels, or any material that holds moisture will make the cat colder.

Shelters don’t have to be that big; if the shelter is too large, the cat’s body heat won’t warm the shelter’s interior.

Monitoring the cat population

#### The population of a neutered group maydecreaseas a result of the following

- Natural death due to age/illness/accident

- Euthanasia due to illness/accident

- Adoption of friendly cats

- Cats moving to new sites

#### The population mayincreaseas a result of the following

- New cats join to exploit the available food source

- Abandoned pet cats join the group

- New cats arrive and reproduce

- Homing

There may be some stray or abandoned pet cats within a colony. These are likely to be friendly and approach people (even those who don’t have food to offer). In this case, you can ask a homing centre (if available) to help find them homes. If there is no local culture of homing cats as pets, then it’s perfectly acceptable for these cats to be neutered and left with the group, as they have been surviving there to this point.

If you do have plenty of homes for pet cats available, you will need to carefully assess each cat/kitten for friendliness and suitability to live with people in a domestic home. This is particularly important if you are working in a society where pet ownership is rare

Testing cats for diseases, such asFIVandFeLV, is advisable to stop the spread of disease if you plan to home cats, especially if they will be living alongside other pet cats

Some organisations will house cats with people who are temporary residents or with people overseas. Temporary residents may only be a short-term solution if they leave the cats behind when they return home. Sending cats to other countries is costly and time-consuming, and the resources are best used to focus on better population management on the ground
