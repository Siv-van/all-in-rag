# When I go on holiday and I see so many cats suffering, how can I best help them?

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/when-i-go-on-holiday-and-i-see-so-many-cats-suffering-how-can-i-best-help-them

---

For cat lovers sometimes a trip or a holiday to somewhere new or even somewhere local can be spoiled by seeing many cats which seem to be living on the streets. Some may look well, some may look thin and ill and there may be kittens which look unwell. There may be one particular cat or kitten which steals your heart and you want to do something, even considering bringing the cat home for a better life. Compassion for these cats is welcome and the more people who want to help them the better. However, what to do that is right for the cat needs thought.

The number of cats seen and the size of the problem can be overwhelming, which is why people sometimes focus on one cat, but even this can be fraught with difficulties. For every kitten seen there will be many more that will follow the same path. Making a difference by helping to change the bigger picture will help many more cats.

Knowledge is key to finding out how to help and to making a real difference. There may be a good project going on in the area which has a plan and is making a difference, but it just needs more resources. These resources may be money, time, food or lobbying voices. Here are some things to think about.

Learn about street cats in general

Many street cats are not used to living closely with people and would be terrified if they were ‘rescued’ and taken to someone’s home. The mix of cats found on the street can range from friendly pet cats (just out for a wander) to cats which have had little or no human interaction. This will affect how cats feel about being close to people.

Look at the cats in the area

Do the cats look thin or unwell or do they seem well fed? Is the tip of one of the ears (often the left one) removed? This can be a sign that the cats have been neutered and may well be fed by volunteers who are working to help street cats. The tip of the ear was removed under anaesthesia when they were neutered and is a sign that they are part of a Trap, Neuter and Return (TNR) project. This could be where you could help by providing funds to neuter more cats and kittens or to feed cats. This can be important in tourist locations where they are fed during the tourist season but then not during the quiet season – additional funds may help to do this.

Find out who is helping already

Ask about the local people or organisations who are working in the area. There may be individuals, charities, local municipality activity or non-government organisations (NGOs) working in the area to help the cats. They may know what needs to be done but do not have the resources to do enough. A donation to them may enable them to help a lot of cats, neutering to prevent the birth of more kittens. When numbers becoming overwhelming it is more difficult to help individual cats; fewer cats can be cared for better. They may run TNR programmes and work with other organisations to help the cats.

Make your voice heard

Local organisations may be working without help from the local municipality and may want to try and convince them to become involved in the right way. If the cats are in a tourist destination, then the voices of the people bringing money to the area may be influential, so letting them know that the state of the cats would affect a return visit or give the place a bad rating may have an effect. Writing to higher levels of government may also help them to understand that this is important to a lot of people who visit and influence how the area/country is viewed. Write to the tourist board to get your feelings across. It might be worth asking what the cat population management plan or legislation is there.

Share information

Many people work to help unowned cats but may not have as much knowledge as they need to make it successful or less stressful. iCatCare has a free membership for people working with unowned cats giving them access to a great deal of information which could help them.

Bringing cats home can be difficult

Bringing a cat that seems to be suffering home from holiday may be very tempting. It may help that individual cat if it is able to adapt to a new life, but this is not guaranteed. There is also a strong possibility that the cat will not fit into such a domestic situation without the right early experiences and it could be highly stressful for the cat.

Other cats in your home may be affected both physically (because disease may be imported with the cat) or mentally because the new cat is uncomfortable or frightened and may be defensive. Additionally, this will not help all the other cats in the same situation, whereas a donation to a local organisation and some of the other actions above may be able to do.
