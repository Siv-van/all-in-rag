# Admit Wisely

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/admit-wisely

---

When admitting cats into a homing centre, it’s essential to think about capacity, selection, and information - how many cats you can take in, which cats you should take in, and what you can find out about each one.

## How many cats should you take in?

It can be difficult to turn a cat away, but taking in every cat quickly leads to overcrowding. When this happens, even the most dedicated teams can struggle to maintain good welfare. Providing good care goes far beyond feeding and cleaning — it means meeting each cat’s individual needs and only admitting the number of cats you can realistically care for well. This requires assessing every cat for signs of illness or distress, and having the time, finances, staff, and housing flexibility to respond effectively.

Maximum vs Optimum Occupancy

Maximum occupancyis when your homing centre is at full capacity, using every available space to house cats.

Optimum occupancy, on the other hand, is the number of cats you can care forwell - minimising distress and ensuring each cat receives individual attention.

In practice, your optimum occupancy will likely be lower than the number of cats your centre can physically accommodate.

Finding your optimum occupancy

There’s no specific recommendation when it comes to the staff-to-cat ratio. Optimum occupancy often fluctuates depending on the requirements of the cats you have at the homing centre at any given time. For example, if you have complex medical cases requiring more staff time, you shouldn’t automatically bring a cat in from the waiting list when space becomes available. You need to account for the extra time required for the cats needing medical care.

Additionally, staff time shouldn’t be spent solely on caring for the cats at the homing centre. The priority should be finding the right homes for them as quickly as possible, so actively encouraging the local community to adopt cats is time well spent. Planning is key - maintaining a steady flow of cats going out to new homes allows you to take in cats that you know are waiting. It can also be useful, where possible, to work with other local organisations that may be able to share capacity or support the homing of particular cats.

Ultimately, working at optimum occupancy means each cat spends the shortest possible time in the homing centre. Cats receive individual care, experience less distress, and are therefore more appealing to potential adopters. It also reduces the likelihood of illness or infectious disease outbreaks which delays homing. As a result, more cats are homed so more cats can be taken in (as space becomes available), and more cats are helped in the long term

Key Points

Working atoptimumrather thanmaximumoccupancy is key. This ensures you can care well for the cats in the homing centre and have the resources to find the best homes for them as soon as possible. It may mean taking fewer cats in, but as they are homed more quickly, you rapidly create space for more cats to come in. Over time, you will be able to help more cats.
