# Return to Field

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/return-to-field

---

Any organisation working with unowned cats may face a situation where there are a large number of cats unsuitable for homing in their centre and/or a very limited number of homes available. This can leave the centre at or beyond its maximum occupancy and unable to take in more cats in need.

Return to Field is a solution that can break the cycle, provide a positive outcome for cats, and relieve pressure on staff and the centre itself. In some places, without using Return to Field, sadly, the only option has been large-scale euthanasia. This has offered no long-term solution and leaves staff both distressed and demoralised. Return to Field has formed an important part of the Million Cat Challenge in the USA, specifically, to reduce levels of euthanasia in shelters there.

Return to Field, sometimes also referred to as Shelter, Neuter, Return, is a process where healthy, neutered, ear-tipped andvaccinatedcats are returned to their original location. This is done whether the individuals in the community want the cats there or not. If the cats were healthy and thriving before they were taken in, the assumption is that this will continue to be the case once they are returned. There is no need for them to have a caregiver.

Return to Field should form part of a wider programme of population management involving Trap Neuter Vaccination and Return (TNVR) and community neutering programmes, with a focus on community engagement and public education. Without this, cats will keep reproducing, and centres will continue to receive an endless stream of unwanted cats from the public.

Planning a Return to Field programme

Communicate your intention to the communities and authorities involved; you may need permission to proceed, depending on where you are based. If possible, involve members of your community who are interested in supporting Return to Field – they can potentially help care for the cats you release. Developing a programme for people who want to support community cats can form a key part in the growth of public backing.

Considerations

Ensure that all staff and volunteers in your organisation are aligned and are consistent in their messaging to the public about what you are doing and why

Develop a policy for Return to Field that everyone can contribute to, establishing how the programme works for your particular situation. If other organisations are working with cats in the area, ideally, collaborate and work together, establishing a shared policy for the entire community

Training staff and volunteers to understand and implement Return to Field is key to ensuring it’s effective and has everyone’s support

Provide advice on how to prevent cats from causing a nuisance to those people who do not want cats on their property

Collecting comprehensive and accurate intake data is important to the effectiveness of the Return to Field programme, as you will need to know the specific location each cat came from, and as many details as possible about how they were living

Putting in place processes, such as appointment-only admissions or visiting the site beforehand, can help improve the information you have and potentially prevent cats from being taken into the centre

Reviewing your intake paperwork may also be necessary to ensure it prompts the right questions

## Which cats are suitable for Return to Field?

#### To decide which cats are suitable for Return to Field, use agreed-upon criteria. Here are some considerations you may like to include

Physical health: A cat with a disability or long-term health condition is unlikely to be suitable for Return to Field, but a cat with a short-term condition that can easily be treated will be. By working closely with your vet, you can ensure the right cats are selected

- Level of friendliness and ability to live with people: A friendly cat that is happy to approach people may not be suitable for release into a hostile community setting

Age: Once a kitten is neutered, they can be released and ideally returned with their siblings and mother. Whether you Return to Field kittens depends on your capacity to cage, foster, and adopt them. Elderly cats may be unsuitable for Return to Field depending on the location and the level of community support

Environment the cat is returning to: Are there dangers from construction or demolition, a very actively hostile community, or has the previous food source been removed?

- Length of time the cat has been confined: Most adult cats can readily adapt to a familiar environment after being away for a long time

- Individual needs of the cat: This will be relative to the information collected during their intake, and from your own observations, which should be considered in your decision-making process

- Returning the cats

Before the release date, check the return site to ensure the details you have on record are accurate and the ideal release area has been established. This may involve a physical visit or examining the site via online satellite images and maps

- Younger kittens will need to be released closer to the original site than older cats and kittens, who can work their way back from a short distance

- Once the release date and time are finalised, for safety and security, staff or volunteers should leave a record of where they are going

If there are people involved in caring for the community cats, you may like to involve them in determining the best time for the release, so that they can provide food, for example

- Remember, it’s never appropriate to enter private property without permission to release cats – there may be security cameras in place even if there is no one around to see you. Find a suitable public area nearby for the release

Returning cats may be done openly or quietly, depending on the setting and the level of community support. This can also guide the time of day, for example, you may choose a quiet time when there are few people around and the cats can find a safe place to settle.

If multiple cats are going to the same site, ideally, they should all be released together, rather than individually

Using private vehicles rather than those with organisational branding may help staff or volunteers to maintain a low profile when returning cats, but it’s important that people can be identified if challenged with ID or a uniform

Carrying leaflets explaining what you are doing can be a useful way of informing local people about what’s happening, and also advising them on how to keep cats away from their property

If you are confronted with aggressive or hostile individuals, and at any point you feel threatened, for your and the cat’s safety, retreat and do not release the cat if you have not already done so

After the release, your records will need to be updated so that you can easily identify the cat (s) if they are returned to you, or there is a query about them

If someone is caring for the cat (s), make a note of this, should you like to follow up and check that all is well further down the line. Occasionally, a cat may need to be taken back because they have become unwell, injured, or because they are failing to adapt to living in the community, for example

In some cases, a cat previously assessed to live independently may find it difficult. Perhaps the environment has changed, so in these cases, it may be best to reassess what the best outcome for the individual cat may be

Monitoring the programme’s impact

To help monitor the progress of your Return to Field programme, collecting data is essential as it helps establish the impact it may have on admissions, TNVR activity and community engagement. Knowing which areas have been successful for Return to Field will help you understand what works best in your community and adjust your programme accordingly. More people may come forward for help with neutering, or to volunteer to care for community cats or even adopt them. Measuring or estimating cat populations before and after Return to Field can help see the impact on the bigger picture, especially if you have chosen to pilot it in a particular area before expanding it more widely. You could also contact other organisations working with cats to see if they have noticed any impact on their work.

Key Points

#### Return to Field refers to healthy cats in homing centres being neutered, vaccinated and ear-tipped before being returned to their original site. It is used in the following situations

- Feral and street cats mistakenly admitted for homing as pets

- Where there is little or no prospect of finding pet homes

- When the only alternative is to euthanise on a large scale

- There is no requirement for the community to consent to the return or for there to be a caregiver if the cats were surviving on their own before being brought in

- Return to Field requires planning to ensure there are no immediate dangers and an assessment of each cat’s suitability

- Liaising with caregivers is helpful (if there are any)

- Collecting data about the cats released and their locations helps you to monitor the impact of your Return to Field programme
