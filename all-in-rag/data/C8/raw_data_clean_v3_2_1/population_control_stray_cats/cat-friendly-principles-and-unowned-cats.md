# Cat Friendly Principles and unowned cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/cat-friendly-principles-and-unowned-cats

---

All iCatCare’s work over the years has been underpinned by an ethos articulated as the Cat Friendly Principles.

These form the foundation for Cat Friendly Solutions for Unowned Cats and Cat Friendly Homing.

There are seven principles in total – three dedicated to the cat, and four related to ways of working with cats and each other to champion cat welfare.

When iCatCare refers to being ‘Cat Friendly’, this means embracing all of the seven principles. They can be used in the unowned cat sector (homing, Trap-Neuter-Return [TNR] and cat population management organisations).

Taking them one at a time explains how each relates to working with unowned cats, and in combination they form a basis for putting Cat Friendly Solutions into action.

Respect cats

#### Respecting the diversity of the species and understand the individual cat because

#### Cats have needs which are different to those of people. We can only care for them well if we respect and understand those needs. Responding to the individual’s needs also provides more successful long-term outcomes for the cats that come into care. Cats are

Obligate carnivores (they have very specific nutritional needs for components found in meat and these must be met to ensure health)

Both predators and prey

Don’t need to be with other cats and may be friendly with some if they choose

Cats’ common needs such as food and water, a safe place to rest, sleep and feel secure, somewhere to toilet, opportunities to communicate through scratching and facial rubbing and opportunities for predatory behaviours (even if it is through play) all need to be met

Cats vary greatly in their temperament and behaviour and these are influenced by a mix of: Genetics (parents and breed) The mother’s physical health and mental wellbeing during pregnancyThe cat’s experiences, particularly during the first two months of life

#### Cats vary greatly in their temperament and behaviour and these are influenced by a mix of

Genetics (parents and breed)

The mother’s physical health and mental wellbeing during pregnancy

The cat’s experiences, particularly during the first two months of life

Other factors, including life stage, health, reproductive status and stress, for example, impact on the behaviour of each cat

At the homing centre

#### If you visit a homing centre which takes these things into consideration, you will notice a number of things

They house cats in a way that better suits their needs, for example housed separately from dogs, given places to hide, and routines for their care and interactions which suit each cat and allow cats to be able to predict what is happening, thus reducing stress.

They try and move cats through the homing centre as quickly as possible because they know that keeping cats in cages for prolonged periods is a negative experience. They and will look for alternative ways to keep cats out of the centre, such as helping people to keep their cats or foster care in carer’s homes

They avoid taking in street and feral cats because these will not be suitable to be pet cats and will be distressed being in confinement. These cats are neutered and returned to site.

They identify cats unsuitable to be pets quickly and look for alternative ways to home cats which are not suited to living closely with people, such as supported care on farms, stables gardens etc

They recognise cats which have been pet cats and need to be with people and match them to homes which will suit them

Keep cats well

#### Giving equal consideration to the cat’s physical health and mental wellbeing because

Physical health and mental wellbeing are interlinked and directly impact on the cat’s life

Keeping cats well involves promoting good health, as well as responding to ill health

Mental wellbeing includes both how the cat feels and how it thinks

Cats are emotional animals and they experience a range of emotions

A cat’s physical health and mental wellbeing are affected when it is in pain, physical discomfort, injured or suffering from illness or disease, and when it is feeling distressed

At the homing centre

#### If you visit a homing centre which takes these things into consideration, you will notice a number of things

Staff and volunteers recognise and appreciate the significance of both physical and mental suffering, and identify cats that are uncomfortable or in distress.

The negative effects of confining cats in cages for prolonged periods of time are understood and other solutions found, including foster care and keeping cats within their community.

Cats are housed singly if possible and group housing is avoided. If there are groups, they are small and stable.

They may have protocols for how visitors or potential adopters see cats because they are aware of stress management and infectious disease control.

Cats are vaccinated, treated for parasites and neutered.

Do cats no harm

#### Ensuring cats are never worse off as a result of people or their activities because

Cruel, ill-informed or inappropriate handling of, or behaviour towards, cats can cause distress or harm

Harm can affect the physical health and mental wellbeing of a cat in the short and/or the long-term

Harm can arise in some cases from too much of something considered positive (e.g. food) as well as from things considered negative (e.g. forceful handling)

Certain medications and chemicals (even those suitable for other species) may be harmful to cats

At the Homing Centre

#### If you visit a homing centre which takes these things into consideration, you will notice a number of things

Processes prioritise the needs of the cat, such as whether the cat is taken into care, how that cat is cared for and what the best outcome is for it, to prevent long-term confinement and distress.

Cleaning products and medications used are not toxic to cats

People ‘think before they act’ and cats are not handled excessively or (if they do not want to be) not handled at all (eg, during the process of TNR).

Cats’ suitability to be pets is assessed, understanding what lifestyle the cat has adapted to.

Cats are treated as cats with compassion, but not attributed human thoughts and feelings which may detract from prioritising the needs of cats.

Staff recognise and act when a cat is experiencing health problems (such as injury or pain), negative emotional states or poor quality of life.

The final four principles are about people and how they work to champion cat welfare.

Be solution driven for cats

#### Finding evidence-based, pragmatic and sustainable solutions for cats because

Addressing the root causes, rather than symptoms of issues helps to develop effective and sustainable solutions

Making informed, well-considered decisions, no matter how complex or difficult, promotes good cat welfare. When there is a problem, doing nothing is not a Cat Friendly option

At the Homing Centre

#### If you visit a homing centre which takes these things into consideration, you will notice a number of things

The organisation is careful about how many cats it takes in and may ask people to wait or help them to keep cats out of the centre to avoid problems of overcrowding, stress and infectious disease, which in turn result in cats staying in care for long periods.

They work to an optimum capacity, allowing staff to become more productive, less stressed and help more cats to receive individual care, remain healthy and move through the system more quickly to their adoptive homes.

Support for and encouragement of neutering in communities to avoid unwanted litters.

They may undertake TNR programmes or working with other organisations who do as they understand that this the most effective strategy for long-term stability and reduction of populations of street cats

Communicate for cats

#### Communicating respectfully and sharing knowledge generously for the sake of cats because

Communicating about successful cat welfare-related collaboration encourages sharing and less ‘competition’ between organisations and individuals

Communicating regularly on the same topics can boost the impact of messaging

Adapting language and explaining their work helps people to understand what they are doing.

At the Homing Centre

#### If you visit a homing centre which takes these things into consideration, you will notice a number of things

A non-judgemental, empathetic, and respectful response to cat problems, encouragning the community to seek help before cat-related problems become emergencies.

Staff and volunteers give ongoing advice to new adopters to ensure that the transition to a new home environment goes smoothly, thereby preventing the risk of a return to the homing centre.

Staff and volunteers help pet owners to overcome difficulties and keep their cats, rather than relinquish them for adoption.

Any TNR work is communicated positively and professionally and explained clearly.

Collaborate for cats

#### Working together for cats, locally, internationally and with people from different back- grounds, always supporting and valuing each other because

Collaborating with other organisations brings greater perspectives and insight on cat welfare issues

Working together for common goals strengthens and spreads key messages.

Respectful collaboration helps to find common ground and solutions.

Understanding different socioeconomic, geographical, or ethnic influences can inform understanding, decision-making and effective communication, and make the difference between the success and failure of cat welfare projects.

At the Homing Centre

#### If you visit a homing centre which takes these things into consideration, you will notice a number of things

Organisations combine their strengths to provide all round solutions, for example, a joint approach on a neutering campaign to tackle overpopulation in an area, by one organisation focusing on TNR and another focusing on providing low-cost neutering for owned cats.

There are good relationships between cat homing and welfare organisations, local authorities and veterinarians.

Communities work together to improve local cat welfare by helping to address issues early and share their approach with others.

Approaches are tailored to local needs and culture and knowledge is shared so that people working directly with the cats feel supported in the work they do and valued for the part they play, encouraging more volunteers to become actively involved in managing cat populations and improving cat welfare in general.

Evolve for cats

#### Being innovative, embracing new knowledge, remaining curious and learning for cats because

Developing knowledge and skills shows commitment and delivers better results.

Research, expertise and new learnings lead to more effective solutions.

Learning from different sources and multiple disciplines can bring new solutions.

Monitoring progress and long-term outcomes can influence future decision-making.

Organisations which incorporate cat friendly principles into their work will be more confident with their decision making, help more cats effectively and can plan for the long term.

At the Homing Centre

#### If you visit a homing centre which takes these things into consideration, you will notice a number of things

There is better understanding of cats and their care.

People are courageous and try new ways of working.

Outcomes for cats are selected wisely and creatively.

The work is professional and staff enjoy their work
