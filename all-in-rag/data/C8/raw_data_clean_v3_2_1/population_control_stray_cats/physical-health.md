# Physical Health

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/physical-health

---

Every cat deserves to feel safe and comfortable from the moment they arrive. Good care begins with the basics - helping each cat feel comfortable, feel safe, and know when things are going to happen.

Care built on solid foundations

Each cat should live in a comfortable environment where they can rest without disturbance, with access to fresh food and water, a clean place to toilet, and a comfortable place to sleep. Calm, consistent routines help cats feel secure, while good hygiene, prompt relief of pain or illness, and attention to each cat’s environmental needs protect both their physical and mental wellbeing. Just as importantly, cats need a sense of control over their space and when (or if) they interact with people.

These essentials are the foundation of good care - the basics on which everything else depends. Only when a cat feels safe and well might they choose to play or interact. Relieving pain, discomfort, or distress must always come first.

Keep cats as physically healthy as possible

Maintaining good physical health in a homing centre can be challenging. Cats often arrive with unknown vaccination or medical histories, move in and out quickly, or are housed in facilities not designed for easy cleaning or disease prevention. Overcrowding and limited access to veterinary care add further pressure.

Because cats instinctively hide signs of illness or discomfort, problems can go unnoticed without careful observation. Physical health and mental wellbeing are closely linked - distress caused by confinement can weaken immunity and increase the risk of disease spreading.

## Working with your veterinary team

Managing physical health isn’t possible without veterinary support. Your veterinary team, whether part of your organisation or an external clinic, plays a vital role in safeguarding welfare through prevention, treatment, neutering, and, when necessary, euthanasia.

When working with homing organisations, vets often take ashelter medicineapproach, balancing the needs of individual cats with the wellbeing of the wider population. This means considering disease prevention and control, ethical decision-making, and the resources available.

A strong, collaborative relationship benefits everyone. Communicate clearly about your work and goals, share accurate records, and listen to veterinary advice on disease management and care decisions. Respect their clinical expertise, clarify diagnostic or treatment plans, and ensure your organisation’s policies - such as on euthanasia or neutering - are understood by all.

Assessment of cats’ health

Few homing centres have in-house veterinary care, so staff need to recognise when a cat requires urgent attention and when a routine appointment is sufficient. A brief visual assessment can be made before any handling takes place - ideally after allowing at least 15 minutes for the cat to settle quietly following transport.

When observing a cat, look for signs of good or poor condition. Check the coat for cleanliness and grooming, note whether the cat appears underweight or overweight, and look for signs of diarrhoea, discharge, or injury. Watch the cat’s breathing and general posture, and pay close attention to their behaviour and whether they appear relaxed, alert, or distressed. These early observations help identify cats that may need isolation, treatment, or further veterinary assessment.

Signs such as collapse or laboured, open-mouth breathing require immediate veterinary attention. If illness is suspected, or there’s any doubt about a cat’s condition, isolate them and seek veterinary advice without delay. Record all observations so changes can be monitored and acted on promptly.

Recognising pain

Cats have evolved to be self-reliant, which means they instinctively hide signs of discomfort or distress. As a result, pain can be difficult to spot. Careful observation is essential to assess each cat’s physical health and identify when veterinary attention is needed without delay.

Key Points

Good care is based on each cat feeling comfortable and safe, in a calm and predictable environment, whether in a homing centre or foster care

Physical health and mental wellbeing are closely connected, and distress can make illness worse, and illness can increase distress.

Clear communication with your veterinary team is essential; share policies on health matters and respect their clinical expertise.

Report any signs of illness or discomfort promptly.

Staff and volunteers should use evidence-based tools, such as body condition scoring, to guide decisions.

Signs of pain are subtle, but recognising and responding to these signs is an important part of a cat’s physical health assessment
