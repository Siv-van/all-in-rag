# Trapping

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/trapping

---

There are different types of traps. Some are automatic, others are triggered manually. They’re available in standard sizes for cats, along with larger and smaller traps for other species. A small animal trap may be useful to trap kittens that don’t weigh enough to trigger an adult trap. Manual traps also include larger sizes for trapping groups of cats.

Automatic Traps

These traps can be left unattended and rely on the cat triggering a mechanism (usually a pedal) when they go in for food

They are effective for trapping individual cats and those that avoid humans

They are useful if you are unable to remain on site for long periods and if you have a volunteer who can alert you or transport the trap to the veterinary clinic

Traps should be checked regularly (you could use webcams in WiFi-enabled areas) on enclosed or access-controlled property

In a publicly accessible space, automatic traps should always be supervised. If you are leaving a trap unsupervised, it’s good practice to leave notices advising people why they are there, and to reassure them that this is positive for the cats

Traps should be covered to increase the sense of security for the cats and limit the distress of the short-term confinement

Considerations

You may catch cats or other animals that you have not targeted

Some kittens may be too small to trigger the mechanism

Some cats may also manage to eat the food by avoiding the mechanism

Seeing cats in the traps may frighten away other members of the group, making them more difficult to trap

Any automatic trap can be converted to manual by propping open the door with an object, such as a full bottle of water with a string attached. The bottle is pulled away to spring the trap, rather than the pedal triggering the trap

Manual Traps

These traps rely on someone being close to the trap to trigger the mechanism at the right time

Manual traps give greater control of which cats are trapped, and catching pets or previously neutered cats can be avoided

They can trap more than one cat at a time (especially litters of kittens)

These traps may also reduce anxiety levels in the group, as the traps are triggered less often

The traps can be closed by a range of techniques – sticks, string, electronic fobs, etc.

Drop Traps

A drop trap can be suspended and dropped from above over a cat

The cat doesn’t have to enter a tight space where it may feel confined. Many cats that avoid entering a normal trap will enter a drop trap

Trap transfer carriers

It’s best to have cat carriers and traps that are designed to work together for ease of use. Trap transfer carriers have sliding doors that allow them to be placed against a matching sliding door on the trap for safe and easy transfer. Transferring to top-opening cat carriers or carriers with hinged doors can be very difficult in the field and may result in cats escaping.

Cats can be anaesthetised in the transfer carrier (if it has a restraint facility, previously known as a crush cage or carrier), or in the trap/other carrier using appropriate handling equipment.

Personal safety

People undertaking TNVR work will find themselves in a range of environments – domestic homes, abandoned buildings, city centres, hotels, tourist sites, factories, parks and open countryside. All will have different hazards that need to be assessed and considered.

Frightened cats that are not used to being handled can inflict wounds. Direct handling must always be avoided by using the right equipment – traps, transfer cages and carriers (with a restraint facility), and protective gloves. Never assume that a cat that you can touch while they are eating can be picked up and put in a carrier.

Considerations

Be aware of the surroundings, especially in unfamiliar places and when trapping at night

Seek local knowledge for advice, or ask locals to be around when trapping

Check the mobile phone coverage in case of an emergency

Ideally, you should always trap with at least one other person. This means that someone can watch over the cats while the other person puts the cats in the vehicle. It also makes the transfer to a carrier much easier. Most importantly, they can help with any problems that arise

Make sure you are wearing appropriate clothing and suitable footwear, depending on the environment and weather conditions

Have an emergency protocol so that you always know where fellow trappers are going and when

Ensure everyone has fully charged mobile phones (keep spare chargers in vehicles) and set up a code that allows you to ask for help discreetly. For example, you may have a pre-programmed text that you can send asking for help, or you may agree to call other trappers at certain times to check that everything is okay, and have a phrase that means help is needed. Asking people to call in when they are safely home is a good idea

There may be times when you have to deal with difficult situations where emotions run high, and people can become upset, angry or aggressive. You may want to consider training in managing/defusing these situations so that they don’t escalate, and people know when to back away

Make sure everyone has had the appropriate vaccinations to protect them if they are bitten, eg, tetanus and rabies (if present in your country). A protocol for what to do if someone is bitten is essential

Make sure everyone uses protection for other problems, such as mosquitoes. It’s important to remember that in areas where rabies is prevalent, a bite from a cat to a person can result in that animal being euthanised

Be aware of other diseases that can be transferred from cats to people (zoonotic diseases[1])

If you are doing lots of trapping, employing staff or working in potentially dangerous areas, a service such as Peoplesafe (in the UK), which provides cellphone/GPS-linked panic alarms, may be worth considering

Develop a relationship with local police, community support officers, municipality or community police, etc.

Link to zoonotic diseases article when it's live on the website

Make a plan for the actual group of cats you are going to work with

If you are choosing groups to tackle, prioritising the one with the largest number of kittens will have the greatest welfare impact. Understanding the population is important for planning – for example, knowing the ratio of male to female cats helps estimate neutering costs based on the price differential.

#### Make a full inventory of the cats you plan to trap as a baseline

Include approximate ages, sex, colour and identification ofany pregnant or sick cats and any kittens. Ensure those counting can differentiate between a healthy cat or kitten, and one that is ill or malnourished

Repeat at regular intervals

If you are working at a larger scale, you may need to take survey samples and use them to work out the whole population

If you are counting cats, you will need to consider where and when you record sightings. You can’t simply count the number of cats at one site where cats are more commonly found and then use that figure to calculate density across the whole area. GPS, Apps (eg, the OSM Tracker app for Android) or Google Maps can be useful for assessing initial populations, ensuring return to the correct location, and analysing the impact of the TNVR programme. Other considerations when counting cats

Weather, time of day, season and having a consistent methodology

Don’t rely on estimates from other people about the number of cats, as they are often inaccurate. You may also need to consider that some cats may be duplicated if they move between closely located colonies

Have a fallback plan if the veterinary clinic clears slots ready to neuter cats, but trappers can’t catch the cats. For example, having some homing organisation cats fasted (starved temporarily for anaesthesia) on standby for neutering, so the slot is not wasted. This will build up a relationship with the veterinary clinic so that they feel confident to book in significant operating time, knowing that they won’t be left without work if something goes wrong with the trapping

Take details of who feeds the cats and whose property they are on

You will need the cooperation of all feeders involved so that food is withdrawn before trapping

If the cats are not being fed, it may help to set up a short period of regular feeding, possibly using the unset traps to create a routine and familiarity

Proof of Permission

#### Have proof that you have permission from cat owners/caregivers (if there are any) and landowners to carry out TNVR

You must ensure that caregivers understand any likely outcomes. For example, if a sick/injured cat is likely to be euthanised or the kittens of pregnant cats aborted

Have an official contact number for the TNVR programme that can be used by the public, veterinary clinics and members of your team

Inform local people

Let the local community know when, where and why you are planning to trap, advising them to keep their pet cats in at the times you are trapping

You may also want to note any pet cats that roam the area or sometimes appear at feeding times. It can be difficult to distinguish an unowned free-roaming cat from a pet cat that’s seen outdoors

Identification by collar for pets may be useful for the duration of the trapping period

If there is a population of owned cats that are not neutered, consider offering subsidised or free neutering to their owners as a way of controlling the wider cat population and reducing the future intake to the free-roaming unowned population

Consider using media to raise awareness of the TNVR programme – this may be via traditional methods such as newspapers/radio/TV, or using social media

You may have access to volunteers or organisations with expertise in this area. The extent to which this is useful depends on the size of the programme and/or the area you are covering

## Working with vet clinics

#### Make a plan with the veterinary clinic you will be working with

Establish the capacity they have for neutering, when, and on which days

Ensure you have discussed and agreed with the veterinary clinic what to do about vaccination, testing for diseases like FIV, other treatments that may be required (if any), when it’s appropriate to euthanise sick or injured cats, and what to do about pregnant cats

Beforehand, agree on details such as: Type of spay surgery (midline or flank) Early neutering of kittensPain reliefUse of antibiotics

Feral and street cats have a shorter average life span and overall poorer health in comparison to a domestic pet cat. Treatment options and outcomes must be realistic for any cats that are trapped. For example, regular medication isn’t a treatment option for a street cat because they can’t be handled or confined for a period of time without causing distress, which negatively impacts their welfare.

#### Bringing in vets from other countries is a common way of putting TNVR programmes into action. However, in reality, this can bring problems

There may be a lack of adequate skills and training (especially if student vets are visiting to gain practical experience, and their work is not strictly monitored by experienced vets)

There may be licensing restrictions on vets from outside the country being able to practice, and a need for local licensed veterinary premises to be used

Unless these trips are also aimed at training local vets, there is a tendency for TNVR programmes to just rely on outside help, and there is a lack of continuity of cat group management between visits

If visiting vets bring medication or anaesthetic protocols which are not available in that country, training and continuity are difficult once they have left

It can help to bring in additional, experienced outside vets to assist in a local-led TNVR programme, which has the facilities for tackling large numbers, as mass neutering can enable a significant initial impact on numbers

#### Know what other facilities are available for the cats

If you intend to home any friendly cats (stray or abandoned pet cats) or young kittens that you come across as part of the TNVR process, ensure there is a reputable homing centre that can take them initially and home them quickly. You will need to agree in advance on the type and number of cats they can manage

If you are planning to use temporary accommodation, such as foster homes, this must be well planned to avoid people becoming overwhelmed with too many cats or keeping them for too long

If you don’t plan to home any cats, or if the quality of any local homing centre is poor (and cats are likely to catch disease or become distressed there), return any friendly cats or young kittens to the site after neutering

Setting up your own centre is costly, and you must be certain that you can manage this well and have sufficient homes for pet cats to avoid their lengthy confinement

#### Potential transfer of disease

Be aware that if you are a pet owner, you may transfer infectious diseases to your own cats after contact with unowned cats. As well as thoroughly washing your hands, you may need to change your clothes and remove your shoes before going into your home

Use a domestic wheelie bin as a trap-dipping bath to clean the traps

Trapping

Mass trapping over a short period is more effective than trapping a few cats at a time over several weeks or months. To do this, you’ll need to plan for the bulk of your resources (people, vets, money, etc.) to be available up front. However, you will need fewer resources overall by doing this. Ensure everything is in place before starting.

Timing

#### The timing of your trapping may be influenced by

The weather – avoid extreme events such as storms/heatwaves. There is no reason to stop trapping in winter in colder climates. Often, it’s easier, as there are fewer nursing mothers and pregnant cats

Busy times, such as when there are crowds around, rush hour in a city, public holidays, or peak tourist season, should be avoided

Using and maintaining traps

Each trap should be labelled with a number and your contact details. Keep a log of all your traps and who has them

Pre-weigh each trap/carrier and attach a waterproof label with this information on it so you can quickly and easily establish the weight of each cat. This is important information that the vet needs to work out anaesthetic or medication dosages

Utilising carriers and transfer carriers will allow you to reuse the traps more quickly. There are also hospital cages that can be used for short periods of confinement. These have a central divider that can be used to keep the cats at one end while feeding and cleaning take place at the other end

All traps and carriers need to be thoroughly cleaned with cat-friendly products to minimise infection risk and remove the odour of cats previously trapped

Any trap or carrier with sliding doors will need to have them secured once the cat is inside before moving it

Starting

Once you have your plan in place and have completed all the preparatory work, you are ready to start!

If possible, arrange for as many cats as possible to be trapped in one go. Catching those that are left will be easier when most of the cats are off-site. You should normally allow 2-3 days minimum for trapping

Do as much of your preparation as possible away from the cats to minimise noise and disruption. Check the mechanism of any automatic traps before you start

If possible, acclimatise the cats to the traps for a few days so that they become a familiar part of their routine and they can enter, eat the food, and leave the unset trap without incident. Wary cats may be reassured by watching this

Don’t feed the cats for 24 hours to ensure they are hungry enough to be attracted to the food in the traps. Water should not be withheld

Maintain a calm, quiet atmosphere and avoid direct eye contact with the cats (sometimes dark glasses can be useful in the daytime). Watch the cats without staring at them

Don’t place too much food in the trap – once they are trapped, cats are unlikely to continue eating, and the remaining food is often disturbed and makes a mess. You may want to place small amounts just inside the trap to encourage the cats in

Don’t place the traps in an open space. Instead, put them on a flat surface in an area where the cats normally eat, next to a wall, fence, the front bumper of a car, or a bush, so that the trap will appear to be part of the environment and will seem less threatening to the cats. Keep the traps a few metres away from each other, so when you trigger one of the traps, you won’t scare cats entering the others

Anything with a cat inside (trap, carrier) should always be covered – leave a cover with each trap ready to go. It can also help to cover part of the rear of the trap where the food is

If you are using manual traps, consider allowing the bold and confident cats that approach first to enter and leave, to encourage the more wary cats. The bolder cats can be trapped later

Check each trapped cat to ensure it’s not a pet – arrange for a vet to scan for a microchip if in doubt. Pet cats may be identified by a collar, microchip, ear tattoo or tag

Once cats are trapped, move the trap away from the main trapping area to avoid other cats being deterred, unless this disturbs other cats about to be trapped

If transferring to a carrier, use a solid object to push against (eg, a wall). Cats should be encouraged gently to move from the trap to the transfer carrier (by blowing or lightly tapping the trap). Covering the carrier and uncovering the trap may encourage the cat, as cats usually prefer darker spaces when feeling anxious or threatened

Traps and carriers should ideally be moved with one hand on the handle and one underneath – this reduces the likelihood of doors opening slightly under the weight

If there are extreme weather conditions, cats may need to be moved quickly to the shelter of a vehicle (make sure in hot weather, it’s in the shade and ventilated)

Cats may thrash about following trapping – this is perfectly normal and can be minimised by covering the trap. You may need to reassure caregivers and onlookers that this is okay, and the cat is not coming to any harm

Sometimes, a particularly panicked cat can be transported with the trap at a slight angle, causing the cat to focus on balance rather than trying to escape

Once you have trapped the initial batch of cats, it may help to leave the site for a while and return later when things have calmed down

You may need to leave an automatic trap overnight for wary cats or those that are irregular visitors. Only do this if you are confident that the area is safe for the trapped cat, and people know what you have done and what to do once the cat is in the trap

Wary cats can sometimes be encouraged to enter by camouflaging the trap with foliage

If you trap/catch young kittens, using a towel with their scent on can sometimes work as bait for their mother. Alternatively, putting the kittens in a small carrier behind the back of the trap can be used as bait

Sometimes, you may be able to use the mother as bait for her kittens by placing her in a carrier/trap behind another trap set for the kittens
