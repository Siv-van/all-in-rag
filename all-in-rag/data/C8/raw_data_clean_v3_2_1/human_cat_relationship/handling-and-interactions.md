# Handling and interactions

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/handling-and-interactions

---

It matters to cats that interactions with us are enjoyable, consensual, and not distressing. It matters to us, as we ultimately want cats to enjoy interactions and spending time with us if they wish to. It’s important that both cats and people benefit from human-cat interactions and feel safe – physically and emotionally.

Just like us, cats experience emotions and our interactions with them can influence their emotional state. It’s our job to always approach and handle them kindly and respectfully to ensure they are never worse off because of our actions.

As a species, cats tend to prefer positive, consistent and predictable human-cat interactions. Their interaction preferences differ from humans - we might enjoy a hug or kiss, but this type of interaction can be stressful for cats. Although some cats may seem to tolerate these types of high-intensity interactions, it doesn’t necessarily mean that they are enjoying them.

Instead, cats prefer low-intensity (gentle, non-restrictive touch) and high-frequency (repeated rubs/strokes) interactions with people. However, every cat is different and preferred interaction styles vary between individuals. It’s important to remember that just because one cat likes being stroked a certain way or on a specific area of their body, this doesn’t mean that all cats will.

Cats’ individual experiences, lifestyle, temperament, current state of health, mental wellbeing and the emotions they are experiencing in relation to the situation they find themselves in can all influence how they perceive and respond to interactions. Socialisation with people during early kittenhood also plays a key role in how likely they are to enjoy human interactions.

A cat’s interaction preference may also change as they get older and senior cats may desire less (or sometimes more) interactions with people. Any changes in behaviour are significant, as they could be an indication that your cat has a health condition. Speak to your veterinary team if you think your cat’s behaviour has changed in any way.

When we think about emotions in cats, it’s important to think about why they occur and how they might be useful for the cat (rather than them being positive or negative). Nowadays, we tend to use the terms ‘protective’ and ‘engaging’ when describing emotions in cats as all emotions serve a function and are not necessarily ‘bad’ or ‘good’. Emotions drive behaviours in cats, and these behaviours will usually benefit the cat in some way.

Protective emotions

Include emotions such as anxiety, fear, frustration and even pain (which has a sensory and emotional aspect). These emotions trigger behaviours that keep cats safe and aid their survival. We may see cats display behaviours associated with protective emotions if we interact with them in ways that they find uncomfortable and don’t feel in control or safe.

Engaging emotions

These emotions also help cats to survive, for example, ‘seeking’ emotions help cats locate (and consume) resources such as food or water or find shelter. Cats may also experience engaging emotions when interacting with people and may display behaviours associated with these emotions during human-cat interactions.

## What signs will my cat give me to show that they’re happy to be approached and touched?

Our approach to cats and any physical interactions we have with them should always be mutually beneficial – we should not simply interact with them or touch them because we feel like it, but rather let them begin the interactions, and decide how and how long that handling continues. Let’s be led by our cats!

Signs that your cat may want to interact include

Purring, chirruping or trilling

Face and body rubbing

Tail wrapping

Jumping on your lap

Slow blinking: cats will aim this non-physical interaction at people they feel comfortable and relaxed around. Some cats will slow blink back at us if we slow blink at them, making this exchange a positive experience for cats and people. Cats may slow blink as a friendly greeting and to acknowledge our presence. However, slow blinking can occur in cats that feel vulnerable, possibly to communicate friendly intentions and prevent conflict. Slow blinking may have more than one function, and although generally viewed as positive, it isn’t necessarily an invitation for you to touch or stroke your cat.

Don’t approach or interact with your cat if they are

Disinterested – e.g. they walk away from you or move their head away

Otherwise occupied – e.g. playing with a toy or watching something

Hiding or easily startled

Running away from you

Lowering/rotating their ears as you approach

Staring/fixated on you (large pupils, eyes wide open)

Suddenly freezing or becoming tense (musculature and body position) e.g. crouched/low body posture, all four paws positioned tightly underneath them, head drawn into the neck, tail wrapped closely around the body to try and to ‘look small’

Thrashing/thumping their tail

Tail stiff and lowered from vertical, or tip pointing downwards (when moving away)

Twitching/rippling skin along their back

Shaking their head

Licking their nose/lips (unless after eating)

Rolling over (social roll) - this behaviour is usually a friendly greeting and a sign your cat is relaxed in your company, but it is not an invitation for a belly rub!

Rolling over is not an invitation to touch a cat's tummy

#### Sometimes cats tell us (through their body language and behaviours) that they do not want to interact with us. However, these signs can be subtle and may be missed. In these situations, cats may resort to behaviours that intend to quickly increase the distance (and stop interactions) between themselves and a perceived or actual threat. This includes

- Vocalisations such as growling, hissing and spitting

- Swiping with paws and biting

We should always take notice if a cat reacts this way, as it is a clear sign that they are in a protective emotional state and do not want us to approach or touch them. In these situations, interactions should stop immediately, or you risk being bitten or scratched and the bond you have with your cat may be damaged.

If your cat suddenly starts to show these behaviours when previously they did not, and/or if these behaviours are persistent, contact your veterinary team for advice.

## How should I interact with my cat?

#### To show respect to your cat and ensure they’re happy and comfortable, you should

Talk quietly

Blink slowly

Move predictably

Approach them from the side on

Crouch down to their level and let them come to you

Interact with them for a brief period (3-5 seconds), then pause and let them decide whether to continue

#### You shouldn’t

Approach them directly, head-on or from behind

Corner your cat so they have no way to escape

Stare at them or maintain direct eye contact

Move quickly or unpredictably

Talk loudly or harshly

Restrain your cat or force interactions if they’re not engaged or are trying to get away

It’s important to ensure your cat always has their own space and free access to an escape route if they don’t feel comfortable in a situation. Cats who feel in control of their situation will offer love and affection on their own terms. Cats that live in multi-cat households may compete for our attention, so it’s important to ensure each cat in your home can interact with you without other cats interrupting.

Not all cats want or need regular physical interactions with people – this can depend on the individual and their emotional state at any time. Your cat may prefer to interact with you in a non-physical way sometimes – they may decide to sit near you or sit on your warm lap, but not necessarily want to be stroked. Engaging in interactive play sessions e.g. chasing a wand toy, is another type of interaction that doesn’t involve you touching your cat. Most cats love to play and using wand toys is a great way to strengthen the bond you have with your cat, without the need to touch or handle them. ## How do I pick up my cat?

Cats don’t enjoy being picked up or held for long periods. However, sometimes it’s necessary, for example, to be placed in their carrier or to administer medication. Your cat should always be handled gently and respectfully, and be given the chance to move away should they want to. Never use forceful handling if you need to hold or pick up your cat, and if they seem uncomfortable and are showing any of these behaviours stop. Cats should never be scruffed (held by the skin at the back of their neck), held when they don’t want to be, or held insecurely (e.g. held under the belly or dangled). These types of handling can cause your cat distress and increase the likelihood of you being bitten or scratched.

#### To ensure your cat feels safe and comfortable when being held

Pick your cat up gently from a secure footing

Support your cat against your body - place one hand under their chest and the other supporting under their back end

Grooming your cat

Some cats may need grooming, especially long-haired cats. Not all cats are comfortable being groomed to begin with, so grooming sessions should always start slowly. For more information, see ourGrooming your catarticle.
