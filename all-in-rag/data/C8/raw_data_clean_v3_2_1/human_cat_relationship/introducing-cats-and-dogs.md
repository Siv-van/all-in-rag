# Introducing cats and dogs

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/introducing-cats-and-dogs

---

Traditionally, we think dogs and cats will not live well with each other. However, they can get on very well.

Close relationships between dogs and cats are usually a result of positive early experiences with each species, coupled with carefully managed introductions.

If your dog or cat has had a positive experience living with the other species before, this can make the process easier, but care must be taken to ensure each animal’s safety is prioritised– especially if you’re unsure how your dog will react to a cat. If you have a terrier or chasing breed, such as a greyhound, they may be more driven by their instinct to chase than some other breeds/types of dog.

## What steps should I take to get things right from the start?

The first step to introducing cats and dogs is to ensure that whichever animal is already resident in the home is as unaffected as possible.

Consider the space

Whether it’s introducing a new cat to a resident dog, or vice versa, your existing pet needs to have a safe place which contains all their necessary resources including, food, water, toys, sleeping areas, etc. (and for cats, litter trays, scratching posts, perches and hiding places).

Ideally, this space should be where the resident pet normally chooses to be, and where they feel safe and happy. For example, if your dog usually spends most of their time downstairs, leave them there and make that the centre of their world for a while; or if your resident cat likes to be upstairs, that’s where they should stay.

Baby gate on stairs separating upstairs and downstairs creating safe spaces for both the dogs and cat

Plan who will live where – initially, your home will need to be ‘time-shared’ so that each pet can have their own safe space where they feel comfortable. This space should allow for your existing pet to live life as normal. For example, can your cat get to the cat flap when they want to, or their favourite perch on the top of the bookshelf without encountering the dog? If your cat has outdoor access, ensure outdoor access is continued without having to meet or pass by the dog to do so – providing multiple safe and elevated perches and/or resting areas in the home gives them choice to get away from the new dog should they need to. Keeping to your resident dog/cat’s normal routine can help minimise any disruption and stress they might experience.

Set new items up gradually

Plan where your new cat/dog’s bedding, food and water bowls, toys, etc will live, and start gradually introducing them to the home a few weeks in advance. By doing this, things won’t all change at once, giving your resident pet time to adjust to new items in their home before your new cat/dog arrives.

Whatever your pet loves, do it – this will build positive associations with their new situation and your new pet. However, new situations can also be tiring, so make sure each animal has plenty time to rest and sleep.

## How, when, and where should I introduce my dog and cat?

Allow your new pet time to settle in. They should be confident exploring and accessing all their resources before you think about introducing your other pets. The introduction should be gradual - taking it slowly will hopefully lead to a peaceful home with happy pets, who may become friends, or at least accept each other’s presence. Take care to avoid any bad experiences, such as seeing each other when they are excited or fearful, which may lead to barking or hissing at each other.

Initially, the space should be divided – your cat in one area of the house and your dog elsewhere. A physical space should separate them (a corridor or staircase blocked by baby gates or doors, for example) so they can’t make physical contact with each other unless supervised.

Step 1: Scent swapping

The first step in introducing the new housemates should be swapping each other’s scent, rather than face-to-face introductions. Both dogs and cats rely heavily on scent and chemical communication, so they can gather information about each other by sniffing and getting used to each other’s scents. How they react to each other’s scent is a good indication of how much interest they have in each other. For this reason, it is often part of the selection process for homing organisations when matching animals to households with a resident cat or dog.

Place a small, single blanket or towel in your dog’s bed and allow your dog to sleep/rest on the blanket for a few days before placing it into your cat’s area, and vice versa. The scented item should be placed away from your cat’s valued resources (like food and water), in case it causes anxiety and prevents your cat from accessing what they need. Repeat scent swapping over a few days, as long as the animals continue to appear relaxed about the other’s smell. Scent-swapping will also occur naturally as you move between each animal's space, carrying their smells on your hands, clothing, etc.

Step 2: Investigating each other’s areas

When both pets appear calm and interested (or ignore) the scent of the other pet, allow them to investigate each other’s areas. This can be done, for example, by allowing your new cat to explore the ‘gap’ area outside their space (e.g. the corridor) and beyond. Once they return to their own space, your dog can investigate where your cat has been exploring. As things progress and your new pet appears comfortable investigating further, take your dog for a walk and allow your cat to explore an area your dog spends time in, and vice versa (placing your cat in another part of the house with a puzzle feeder or game). Because of the way dogs are generally trained and socialised, it may be easier for a new dog to comfortably explore, whereas it may take a new cat longer to feel relaxed in a new territory, especially one with a dog in it.

Step 3: Visual contact

If all goes well, small amounts of limited visual contact can begin. Both pets must be calm and able to escape if they’re not comfortable. There must be no opportunity for either pet to a) directly approach, or b) become trapped or have their retreat access blocked.

IMPORTANT – Be very careful about using items that restrain an animal and give it no opportunity to hide or move away. A cat should never be placed in a crate for cat-dog introductions. However, if a dog is crate-trained and relaxed in a crate and shows no fear of the cat, a crate may be useful. However, a harness and longline can be a useful alternative, which gives the dog more autonomy over its behaviour and space. Ensure ample hiding/safe places for both animals.

Visual introductions are best through a partially covered barrier e.g. a baby gate (mostly covered by a draped towel), whilst both pets are positively engaged in a calm, enjoyable activity like playing with a toy or a puzzle feeder. Initially, keep a distance between your pets and keep the sessions short.

If both pets are relaxed with increasing visual access (with a gradually uncovered baby gate), allow them to move a little closer to each other, keeping the sessions short with lots of calm, positive experiences and interactions from you and other family members. Ensuring the dog has basic commands such as come, sit, lie down and ‘look at me’ can greatly help manage their arousal levels and ensure staring at the cat does not happen.

Always supervise and be vigilant for signs of emotional arousal, such as fear or excitement, and changes in body language or posture. End sessions while both pets remain relaxed in each other’s presence. However, if either animal becomes aroused or anxious, end the session immediately.

As your pets become used to each other, your interventions (providing rewards for calmly noticing the other pet’s presence) can be reduced, allowing your pets more time to engage with each other and make good decisions to be calm/leave each other alone (this should also be rewarded). The goal is for both animals to live happily in the same space doing their own things – not to encourage lots of interaction.

Step 4: Actual contact

If possible, have two people involved in the process so you can supervise a pet each.

Depending on each pet’s individual needs, there will come a time when your cat and dog have smelt, heard, and seen each other for long enough to know about each other.

When you’re confident they can share a space comfortably, the barriers can come down for a while. Introductions should always be under direct supervision – this means actively monitoring and preventing problems through positive distractions, such as food or toy luring if necessary. You can use food or toy lures to create space between your pets by luring one away from the other if any tension occurs or if one gets too close and the other shows signs of discomfort.

Avoid “crisis management”, i.e. swooping in and grabbing one of your pets if things look tense, which will elevate arousal levels and could lead to a negative experience that may be detrimental to future relations

Never punish your cat or dog if they respond negatively

Your dog should be on a harness and lead, with their behaviour and body language monitored for increased arousal (e.g. vocalising, stiff body, hair raised, excitable movements), and your cat should be able to move and retreat freely, providing they are not coming directly into your dog’s space. Your dog’s lead is not there to provide restraint, but as a “just in case” – it should be loose, with your dog relaxed. If the lead is tight, the situation is overwhelming for your dog, so end the session and try again when there is more distance/space, and your dog is positively engaged. If your cat is boldly approaching your dog, and your dog is uncomfortable, gently interrupt and divert them away from each other with food, treats, play, etc. If your cat appears anxious, gently encourage them to retreat, hide, perch, etc to feel safe and watch from a distance

Keep initial sessions brief and positive, gradually allowing more time for them to ‘just be’ together

When the time is right (both pets are comfortable and relaxed), your dog’s lead can be dropped and allowed to trail, so it can gently be picked up if necessary

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you’re struggling with introducing your cat and dog, or there’s been a breakdown after an initially good introduction, we recommend seeking professional help. Get in touch with your veterinary team, who may be able to offer specific advice or recommend a local, qualified clinical animal behaviourist to help.
