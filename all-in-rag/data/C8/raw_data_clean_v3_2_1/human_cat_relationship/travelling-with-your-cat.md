# Travelling with your cat

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/travelling-with-your-cat

---

Most cats aren’t particularly happy travellers. They are usually strongly bonded to their own territory where things are familiar, and they feel safe and secure. Cats can feel vulnerable away from home, where they might be exposed to stressors such as new sights, sounds, smells and unfamiliar people and animals.

If you’re planning to take your cat on a car, train, or air journey, you must ensure they’re safely and comfortably secure in an appropriate carrier. They will also need to be kept in a confined space at the end of the journey, at least until they’ve become bonded to their new territory.

Of course, some cats frequently travel with their owner and don’t panic or run off in an unfamiliar environment – but for most cats, this is a high risk, and caution must be taken to keep them safe.

## What do I need to consider when travelling with my cat?

## Travelling by car

It can be dangerous to have your cat loose in the car. Not only could they cause an accident by becoming entangled with the driver or startling them, but also if a window or door was opened or an accident happened, your cat could escape and become lost or injured.

To safely transport your cat, you’ll need a cat carrier which is strong (to prevent your cat from escaping) and easy to clean, should they urinate, defecate, or become sick during the journey. There’s a wide range to choose from, including wicker, solid plastic, fibreglass, and plastic-coated wire mesh. However, some carriers are better than others. It’s best to avoid cardboard or cheap, light plastic boxes, which are suitable only for short journeys or very temporary confinement but would not be strong enough for longer periods, especially if they become soiled and wet. Fabric and wicker carriers can be difficult to clean if your cat happens to soil on their journey. Backpack carriers can be unpleasant for cats if they are unable to hide, ventilation is poor and there is a lot of movement during travel. Solid plastic carriers can be easily cleaned if soiled, are easily accessible with quick-release clips, have a secure door to help prevent escape, slats to aid ventilation and a removable top half to allow your vet to examine them more easily.

You should also consider the weather you will be travelling in – both your present situation and the expected temperature of your destination. If it’s likely to be very hot, use a basket which allows a good airflow through. If it’s going to be cold, then one which can provide draft-free warmth while still allowing a good airflow will be more suitable for your cat. If your car journey is going to lead to another type of travel, such as on a plane, then you need to find out the type of carrier the airline prefers or specifies.

If you have a large crate (such as those used for a dog when in the back of the car), you may wish to put your cat in this. However, bear in mind that larger is not necessarily better when it comes to your cat feeling safe and secure – cats like to sit in a small space and are less likely to move around. If you’re using a larger crate which fits in the back of the car, you will still need a small carrier which can be carried to and from the car to keep your cat safe at either end of the journey. If you are using a large crate, you may be able to provide your cat with a litter tray, although it’s unlikely that they will use it during the journey. It may be better to line the carrier with absorbent pads in case of accidental soiling and take some familiar-smelling bedding if you need to replace it.

Place the carrier where it will be secure if you brake suddenly but also has good air flow. Don’t put your cat in the boot and be mindful of the back of hatchbacks – ventilation may be poor, and your cat may overheat. You can secure the carrier in the footwell behind one of the front seats or use the seatbelt to hold it in place securely on the back seat. Your cat may meow initially or even throughout the whole journey – speak calmly and reassuringly, but don’t let them out of the carrier. Eventually, the constant motion and noise of the car will help your cat to sleep or settle down.

Check your cat regularly, especially in hot weather. Don’t underestimate how rapidly the temperature inside a car can rise and bear this in mind if you stop for a refreshment break and plan to leave your cat in the car. It’s not enough to park in the shade and leave windows open – ideally, someone should always stay with your cat, secure in their carrier, but with doors/windows open so that air can flow through the vehicle and keep them cool while you’re stationary. If it’s hot, take a picnic and eat nearby with your cat secure in their carrier outside the car or with all the doors open. Heatstroke can be fatal, so keeping your cat cool during warmer weather is essential.

## Travelling by train

If you’re travelling by train, you will need a very secure carrier which your cat can’t escape from, which is also light enough to carry. You may want a carrier with a solid base in case your cat urinates. Line it with absorbent pads and take spare bedding. You will often be able to keep your cat in the carrier on your lap, depending on the type of train and the space available.

## Travelling by air

If you’re planning to fly with your cat, you need to plan well ahead. You may have a choice of airlines, and how they transport your cat may influence your choice. Most airlines do not allow cats to travel with their owners in the cabin, and they must travel in a special part of the hold that is temperature-controlled and pressurised.

We don’t recommend allowing pregnant cats or kittens under three months old to travel. It’s also worth noting that not all flights are licensed to carry animals, so your cat may have to travel on a different flight to you. If possible, book your cat onto a direct flight so that there is no need for them to be disturbed during a transfer, and it prevents any problems associated with waiting around in a very hot or cold country – this may also affect the timing of the flight you choose. The International Air Transport Association Standards recommend a container large enough to stand up in and turn around with ease – check with individual airlines on what they need.

## When should I introduce my cat to the cat carrier?

For cats, a cat carrier usually means a trip to the veterinary clinic, so they’re often not too happy to get into it! Let your cat become comfortable with the cat carrier or travel crate well before the journey and make it a pleasant place to be. The video below shows how. Feed your cat treats inside the carrier and make a cosy bed of familiar-smelling bedding, which can be used on the journey. Leave the door open and encourage your cat to go in and out and to sleep in it. Then, when it comes to the actual journey, your cat is at least familiar with their immediate environment. You may also want to consider the use of synthetic feline pheromones to help reduce anxiety. Sprays are available that can be applied to the bedding inside the carrier. It’s best to wait at least 15-20 minutes before placing the cat inside, as the spray initially smells of alcohol, which can be offensive to the cat.

If you have more than one cat, it’s better to give them separate carriers which allow better air flow, more room, and less chance of overheating. Even the best of friends may become stressed during a journey and behave uncharacteristically, which could lead to a breakdown in their relationship. Placing some bedding or a familiar-smelling blanket inside each cat’s carrier may provide some comfort for socially bonded cats during travel.

Withhold food for about four to five hours before the journey in case your cat is sick while travelling. Offer water up until the time you leave and again during the journey if possible. You can buy bowls that attach to carriers, which are easy to fill without opening the carrier should there be a delay during your journey.

## What should I do when I arrive at my destination?

When you arrive, place your cat in one room and make sure the room is secure (to prevent escape) and comfortable. Offer water and a little food, although your cat may not be interested in eating until they’re more settled.

If you have adopted an adult cat or moved home, don’t let your cat go outside for at least two to three weeks, and make sure they are identifiable if they become lost (e.g., with a microchip). Read our separate article onletting your cat outside for the first timefor more advice.

Additional Considerations

Use of medication

If you know your cat struggles with travel and has previously been sick or become very anxious on a journey, it’s worth speaking to your vet about medication that may help.

Travelling outside your own country

Remember to check all regulations if you are taking your cat from one country to another. Preventative health requirements, such as rabies vaccinations (or even quarantine), will vary, as will those for worming/flea treatments, and testing for various diseases. Failure to comply may mean your cat isn’t permitted to continue with the journey, and they may be at greater risk of contracting diseases. Your cat will likely need a pet passport to travel abroad – speak to your veterinary team for further advice.
