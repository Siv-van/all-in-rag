# Cats and your pregnancy

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/cats-and-your-pregnancy

---

Worries about the health of an unborn child weigh heavily on the mind of an expectant mother, and there seem to be potential hazards everywhere you look. People may warn you about the dangers of picking uptoxoplasmosisfrom your cat (a zoonotic disease spread between animals and people), but don’t panic – the risk of getting toxoplasmosis is extremely low, and with some sensible precautions, you can protect yourself further.

## What is toxoplasmosis?

Toxoplasmosis is caused by infection with a parasite calledToxoplasma gondii (T. gondii). If a pregnant woman is infected during pregnancy (especially the first trimester), it can cause problems for the developing foetus. Cats can carry the parasite if they eat infected wildlife; however, it can only be passed on via the cat’s faeces for two weeks after the first infection, and you must swallow the parasite to become infected.

## How do I protect myself from toxoplasmosis?

#### Cats do not present the greatest risk of contracting toxoplasmosis – undercooked meat and unwashed vegetables are a bigger risk. There are simple precautions which you can take to avoid the risk

Clean litter trays daily and, if possible, get someone else to clean the litter tray while you are pregnant, or wear rubber gloves

Follow normal hygiene precautions – keep your cat off kitchen surfaces and wash your hands regularly

Wear gloves if you are gardening

Wash vegetables carefully

Wash your hands after handling raw meat and ensure it is cooked properly

Problems are rare and easily avoided if simple hygiene precautions are taken. If you are at all worried, consult your doctor for more information.

Additional considerations

Speak to your vet to make sure that your cat’s routine preventative healthcare is up to date, particularly against parasites. This combined with taking normal hygiene precautions will ensure there is no added risk to your pregnancy.

You may also want to plan, to ensure that your cat doesn’t feel anxious when your baby first comes home. Read our article oncats and babiesfor further advice.
