# Introducing cats to children

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/introducing-cats-to-children

---

Many children have fantastic relationships with their cats, which helps them learn about respecting other creatures and interacting gently. However, when welcoming a new cat or kitten into your home, it’s important, as parents, to teach children from the beginning how to approach, stroke and handle cats gently and treat them with respect.

## What should I consider before introducing my children to a new cat or kitten?

When weighing up the pros and cons, parents need to accept that the majority, if not all, of the cat’s care, will be carried out by them. A new kitten or cat needs a great deal of commitment, particularly in the early stages, so the whole family has a role to play.

Choosing the right cat or kitten for your family is vital, as some cats are more ‘family-friendly’ than others. Look for a confident cat or kitten that’s been well-socialised with adults and children and is happy to accept handling and affection. However, even with the most tolerant cats, it’s important to teach children how to behave appropriately around cats, how to read the signs when their cat has had enough and to respect their need for time alone. Households with boisterous children aren’t well-suited to cats, and very nervous or timid cats will find living with children incredibly stressful, therefore, cats with these temperaments should be avoided.

A homing centre will assess each situation individually, carefully matching the appropriate cat or kitten with the right family. If you’ve decided you’d like a pedigree cat, do your research first and consider breeds with temperament traits that can be better suited to children.

## Which family member is responsible for each aspect of care (e.g. feeding, providing fresh drinking water, litter tray cleaning)?

## Where will your cat sleep (although the bedroom is often an exciting prospect, this should be discouraged if your child suffers from allergies. Cats are also naturally active at dawn and dusk, which can disturb a child's sleep)?

## Which rooms will be out of bounds?

## What level of attention is appropriate during the settling-in period?

Which areas of the home are designated for your cat only? While your cat is in their own space, children should be discouraged from disturbing them. Such spaces should include when your cat is eating or using their litter trays, their resting places, high platforms etc.

## How do I settle a new cat or kitten into a home with children?

Before your new cat arrives, you’ll need to register with your local veterinary clinic, as cats and kittens need regular treatment forwormsandfleaswhich could be a potential health hazard for your family. Your veterinary team will guide you on all your cat’s preventative healthcare needs.

Your new cat should be settled into their own room with all necessary resources available – read our article onhelping your new cat or kitten settle infor detailed advice. This room should be in a quiet part of the house.

Every family member should understand the importance of security by keeping external doors and windows closed during the first few weeks when your kitten or cat is settling into their new home. Your new cat will need plenty of escape opportunities from excitable children, such as shelving, tall cat activity centres/scratching posts, tops of cupboards or wardrobes, cardboard boxes and under beds. Now is the time to help your children learn more about how to be gentle around cats and to hold a cat appropriately – cats need support under their front and hind legs. Children should only pick up cats that tolerate being picked up, and only if they are strong enough to support all their cat’s weight.

Young children should always be supervised. This will prevent your cat from having unpleasant and stressful encounters with children as they settle into the home. It will also help safeguard against any bites or scratches if your child pushes things too far before their cat feels comfortable around them. Regularly picking up cats should be limited to kittens or very sociable and tolerant adult cats that actively enjoy it.

Small children often squeal with excitement, chase and grab, so care must be taken when introducing cats and toddlers. Again, opportunities to escape are essential for your cat. Baby gates with integrated cat flaps can help allow your cat to feel in control over their environment by being able to leave and not be followed, and this alone can help your cat settle into a busy family environment.

It’s important, especially with young children, that there’s at least one room your cat can retreat to as a ‘safe place’ within the house. This is an area that the children cannot access, and somewhere your cat can go if they feel too overwhelmed.

#### A few extra considerations include

- Litter trays, food and water bowls are often irresistible, so these should ideally be in areas where your child does not have access.

- Create positive associations with the presence of children by offering food treats and play opportunities.

- For older children, allow them the opportunity to feed their cat treats to help build that positive association.

- Letting children play with their cat with interactive wand toys is a great way to get children involved, without physical handling if the cat doesn’t enjoy this.
