# Introducing Cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/introducing-cats

---

Welcoming a new cat into your home can be an extremely rewarding experience, but, as with all things when it comes to our cats, time, care and patience are required to ensure it’s a stress-free experience for all – particularly if you already have a resident cat.

Cats are naturally solitary animals, so it’s important to appreciate that introducing cats can be difficult. Even when the utmost care is taken, some cats simply don’t want to live with other cats. Recognising this and acting accordingly is vital to ensure all cats in your care have the best welfare possible in terms of both physical and mental wellbeing.

Slow, careful introductions give both cats the best chance of accepting each other and developing a positive relationship moving forward – so, don’t be tempted to rush this process. You’ll be glad you took the extra time and will find it was much less stressful for both you and your cats in the long term.

Ready to get started? Read on for our simple steps for introducing cats.

## How do I start the process of introducing the cats?

#### Before we take any steps towards introducing the cats, it’s important to give your new cat time to settle in. To keep this as stress-free as possible, we recommend choosing a room that your resident cat doesn’t use, and that you don’t need constant access to, and confining your new cat to this room initially. Set the room up with

Food

Water

Comfortable resting areas with beds

Hiding places

Toys

These items need to either already belong to the new cat or be brand new. Using some of your resident cat’s belongings can cause stress – your new cat will smell another cat on the items, and your resident cat may become anxious at the loss of a resource, such as a litter tray.

You may also find synthetic feline pheromone products helpful when used in your new cat’s room and in areas where your resident cat spends most of their time. This creates feelings of familiarity and security within the environment, speeding up the process of settling in for your new cat, and helping to prevent your resident cat from feeling as though its territory is being threatened.

Synthetic feline pheromone diffusers

Your new cat will need plenty of time to get used to their new home and allow their scent to become a part of the room. So, how does this happen?

Actively – by rubbing the scent glands on their face against furniture and on the corner of walls, and by scratching their scratching post

Passively – by sleeping on bedding and playing with toys

How long this settling in period takes will depend on your cat, varying from several days to a week or two.

#### Signs that your cat is comfortable with their new home include

- Friendly behaviour when you enter the room (rubbing against your legs, chirruping, purring, meowing)

- Resting on their side with their belly exposed and rolling over

- Playing with toys

- Facial rubbing on furniture, walls, and other items in the room

- Normal eating, drinking, toileting, scratching and grooming behaviours

If your new cat doesn’t seem comfortable with being kept in a single room, it’s worth considering whether you can increase their space by giving access to another room or corridor that your resident cat doesn’t use. If this isn’t possible, you may need to start the introduction process sooner to prevent your new cat from becoming frustrated.

#### Signs that your cat is frustrated with being kept in one room include

- Scratching/pawing at the door and windows

- Meowing consistently for several minutes at a time

- Pacing by the door

- Rearing up at the door

- Swiping at you when you try to leave the room

## When and where should the cats meet?

Once your new cat is comfortable in their own space, it’s time to begin the process of introducing the cats. Follow our five steps below, and remember – patience is key!

Step 1: Scent swapping

Cats use the scent of individual cats to recognise whether they’re in the same social group or not. The aim of scent swapping is for the cats to recognise one another as part of the same group by creating a communal scent. By doing this, both cats become comfortable with the smell of the other cat before physically meeting, giving a greater chance they’ll accept each other when they meet.

Now we know why it’s so important, how do we go about scent swapping? Let’s start with the bedding. Take one piece of each cat’s bedding (for example, a single blanket) and place it in one of the other cat’s beds – just make sure there’s enough bedding available so that neither cat is left without enough sleeping or resting places. The aim is that each cat lies on a piece of the other cat’s bedding, mixing their two scents to create a communal scent. If you notice either cat showing negative behaviour towards the bedding (actively avoiding or hissing at it), it could be a sign that the cat is less likely to accept a new cat into their home, so you’ll need to progress through the steps at a much slower pace. Once both cats are relaxed around the bedding, it can be returned to the original cat’s room to mix the scents further. This can be repeated with other pieces of bedding as required.

Step 2: Allow access to each cat’s area

If scent swapping is a success, it’s time to move onto the next step. Start by briefly confining your resident cat to one area of the home (only do so if this won’t cause your cat distress) and allow your new cat the opportunity to explore the resident cat’s area of the home. You can then take your new cat out of their room and allow your resident cat in to explore – only do this once your new cat is fully settled in and comfortable in their own space.

Step 3: Allow them to see each other

#### Once both cats are fully relaxed in the home in general, and comfortable with each other’s scent, the next step is to allow them to see each other. This needs to happen through a physical barrier, for example

A glass door or screen or one that’s ajar that the cats can’t pass through

Both cats should create positive associations when in view of each other. The cats should be happy going about their business, glancing occasionally at each other but not staring each other down. You can allow the cats to sniff each other through the barrier, but at any sign of negative behaviour (hissing, growling, rotating of ears with a tense body posture) attempt to distract the cat. Try using a distraction, such as by calling their name to lure the instigator of the negative behaviour away. And remember, don’t punish your cat for showing negative behaviour – this will only frighten your cat more.

When introducing your cat to another cat/kitten, it's important not to use food or play to distract or reward your cat during the process. Cats are naturally solitary when it comes to eating and playing and asking them to do these things in visual contact or nearby another cat, can make them feel stressed or uncomfortable. Instead, if your cat needs a break or you want to guide them away from the situation, gently call their name and encourage them to follow you to a separate, quiet room. Once they’re in a safe space, you can offer a toy or a puzzle feeder there—away from the other cat—so they can relax and enjoy it on their own terms.

Do you have more than one resident cat at home? To avoid overwhelming your new cat, start by introducing just one resident cat and your new cat at any one time. This can be built-up gradually to include more resident cats, if your new cat is comfortable. Keep the sessions short and always end on a positive – if you notice signs of distress or negative behaviour, separate the cats and try again another day. Certain synthetic feline pheromone products can be helpful if any concerns arise during introductions (they can also be useful if there’s a breakdown in their relationship further down the line). Pheromone diffusers should be plugged into areas of the home where the cats spend most of their time or where the conflict commonly occurs, and should help them to feel more comfortable around each other.

Step 4: Physical, supervised access

Once both cats are fully comfortable seeing one another through a barrier, you can quietly remove or open the barrier when the cats are otherwise preoccupied. Don’t force the cats together. Our aim here is that the cats are comfortable in each other’s presence - they don’t need to be physically interacting. If there are any signs of distress or negativity towards one another, replace the barrier and go back a step. If both cats appear comfortable in each other’s company, repeat this step as often as possible.

Step 5: Short periods of free access without supervision

Once you can get through Step 4 without any negative behaviours, you can allow free, unsupervised access for short periods of time - we recommend just a few minutes to begin with. Repeat this step as frequently as possible, with your new cat still being kept separate in between sessions. If both cats seem friendly and comfortable around each other, the length of time they spend together can be gradually increased – just make sure they can retreat to their own areas if needed. It’s important to make sure that each cat has their own resources (food and water bowls, beds, litter trays, scratching areas), plus one extra, spaced out around the house so that they don’t have to share or feel in competition with one another.

Over time, as long as both cats are comfortable, the separate room can be left open, and the cats can come and go as they please. If at any point in the future you notice conflict, a single area can be made accessible to one cat only. This allows all resident cats access to the whole home, but the affected cat can use their own space and avoid the cat they’ve had conflict with if necessary. Using vertical space, such as shelves and perches, also allows cats to enjoy their own space. And remember – relationships between cats can change over time. It’s always important to monitor how your cats behave around one another and adapt as needed.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you’re struggling with introducing your cats, or there’s been a breakdown after an initially good introduction, we recommend seeking professional help. Get in touch with your veterinary team, who may be able to offer specific advice or recommend a local, qualified clinical animal behaviourist to help.

## The Science

Synthetic feline pheromones - feline facial pheromones are important signals that are naturally produced by glands in a cat’s face. Synthetic versions of these pheromones are available as plug-in diffusers and sprays for use around the home. They can have a useful calming effect during stressful situations, such as introduction of a new cat or when moving house.

Additional resources and Practical tips

Remember to make sure all cats have plenty of resources. The less the cats feel they need to share, the more they are likely to get along.
