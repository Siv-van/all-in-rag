# Will my cat accept a new cat?

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/will-my-cat-accept-a-new-cat

---

It’s difficult to predict whether your cat will accept another cat into their household. As a species, cats have become more socially flexible, to an extent, during the process of domestication, but individuals still vary hugely in how accepting they are of other cats. Additionally, their ability to change their sociability is limited once they reach adulthood.

## What does sociability mean in cats?

Sociability refers to how comfortable a cat feels around both people and other animals, including cats. A cat can be confident but will not necessarily accept a new cat because it’s not sociable with cats. Likewise, a cat can be highly social towards people but not enjoy the company of other cats.

Scientists have identified some of the factors that influence how sociable a cat will be to other cats, but there are likely to be more factors that still need exploring.

Taking these factors into consideration, several areas should be given careful thought before making the final decision. These factors are all likely to interact rather than act in isolation, and therefore, the more that are answered in favour of another cat, the greater the chances of a new cat being accepted.

## How do I decide if my cat will accept a new cat?

Answer these questions separately for each cat in your household and the potential new cat.

## Is the cat male or female?

In unowned cats, cat groups tend to be made up of females and their offspring. However, we currently don’t have evidence as to whether the gender of a cat influences their ability to get along with other cats when the cats are neutered.

## Is the cat related to the other cats in the household or the new cat (s)?

Studies on both unowned cats and owned pet cats show that cats that are related tend to show more friendly behaviours to one another than those that are not. Where possible, think about getting siblings if you’re looking to welcome two new cats into your home that are related and have been reared together.

## Is your cat neutered?

Unless your cat is used for breeding, all cats should be neutered to prevent litters of unwanted kittens. Additionally, neutered cats are generally much more likely to get along with each other because there are no circulating hormones which can greatly influence behaviour.

## How is the health (physical and psychological) of the cat?

It’s generally not a good time to get another cat if your cat is unwell. Cats suffering from injury, disease or stress-related problems are likely to be in a period of self-protection and are more likely to find an additional cat distressing rather than enriching.

## How old is your cat?

Generally, the younger your cat, the more likely they are to accept another cat into their household. Introducing a kitten or adolescent to an elderly cat could be a problem if the young cat directs too much play towards the older cat, who won’t necessarily want to play as much and may have some degree of degenerative joint disease which can make the boisterous advances of young kittens uncomfortable. In these situations, two kittens who can play with one another may be a better option than a single kitten, provided you have the time and resources to cope with two additional kittens rather than just one.

## How would you describe the cat’s temperament?

Temperament (personality) in cats has been well studied, and just like people, cats differ. Some are confident while others are timid. While two timid cats may actively avoid one another or seek confidence from one another, two bold cats may clash by competing for resources. There is no set rule for which types of temperaments are most likely to get along, but it’s still important to take some time to think about the temperament of your current cat (s) and the new potential cat (s), particularly in terms of how they respond to new things and to change, and how sociable they are.

## What do I know about the parents and the early experiences (first 12 weeks of life) of the cat?

The temperament of a kitten is shaped by both their genetics and the environment. Kittens born to parents that are sociable and friendly to other cats are more likely to be sociable and friendly to other cats as well. However, the environment also plays a big role. The period where cats are most receptive to learning about the social environment (including other cats) is the socialisation period, which occurs between approximately 2 and 8 weeks of age. Kittens who have friendly encounters with other cats during this time are more likely to perceive other cats positively throughout their life. Therefore, a cat that was well-socialised with other cats during this sensitive period is more likely to be accepting of other cats later in life. The chances of acceptance are likely to be increased if the cat continues to have frequent, positive interactions with other cats throughout kittenhood, adolescence and into adulthood.

## What are the cat’s previous experiences with cats as an adult?

Cats can learn to fear other cats from just one negative experience. Negative experiences have very powerful effects on learning and memory. Because of this, it’s important to know if any of the cats have previously had any negative experiences with other cats and how this may have changed how the cat now behaves around other cats.

## What are my cat’s current experiences with cats?

If your cat currently lives with other cats, what are their relationships like? If your cat has outdoor access, how do they behave towards other cats outdoors? If your cat is in any way anxious or fearful, bringing another cat into the home may exacerbate these emotions and behaviours.
