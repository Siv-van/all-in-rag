# Cats and babies

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/cats-and-babies

---

Having a baby is life changing. So, if you have a cat at home, you may wonder how your cat will react to your new arrival, and what things you need to consider to ensure bringing your baby home is as stress-free as possible for all family members - including your cat.

Cats thrive when they have routine and predictability. In addition, their primary attachments tend to be to their physical home rather than the people within it. This is because they are territorial as a species, which means that disruptions to the home (e.g., new furniture, baby-related products, moving furniture around the home from its original locations), can cause some cats anxiety. However, many cats will take it all in their stride, and be incredibly good with children if they are introduced carefully and taught to respect the cat.

## Why might a baby seem daunting for your cat?

Cats use their excellent sense of smell to help them know what belongs to them. They rub their faces on items around the home, such as furniture and door frames, to deposit their unique scent profile. We cannot smell these deposits, but they provide the cat with a sense of familiarity and security. Smells from new items that are brought into the home for the baby and the smell of the baby themselves can disrupt these scent profiles and the cats feelings of familiarity and security within their home.

Along with the baby, comes a whole host of new items to the home, small and large, such as the cot, pram and toys. Often these toys have flashing lights and make sudden, strange noises which can alarm the cat.

Babies smell different from adults – the prominent smells being milk and dirty nappies!

Babies require a lot of their parents’ time and as a result, the predictability of your cat’s care routine can often be disrupted. Certainly, in the early stages of parenthood, there’s less time to devote to your cat, such as playing with them and social interactions. New parents are likely to be awake through the night to tend to their baby, which may also disrupt the cat’s normal routine.

Babies are more unpredictable in their behaviour (including the noises and movements they make) compared to adults and older children, which can be daunting to cats.

Most cats enjoy physical interaction in the form of stroking on their head and face regions. However, baby-cat interactions can be challenging for your cat to cope with because interactions may be more unpredictable with a baby. Cats should never be left unattended with babies, and physical interactions should never occur without adult supervision. This is so that if needed, an adult can intervene to ensure safety and that the cat always has choice and control over the type and duration of the interaction.

## What can help my cat feel safe and secure before the baby comes home?

Ensure that any changes made to the home occur gradually. For instance, if the cat currently sleeps with you in your bedroom at night but won’t be allowed access when the baby comes home, it’s important to make this change very gradually and provide alternative, safe and comfortable sleeping areas for them.

Give your cat the opportunity to investigate and accept new items to the home by face rubbing, so they smell familiar to them.

Make the new item smell like part of your home by rubbing a cloth gently on your cat’s cheeks and/or the area in front of their ears when you are stroking them (where their scent glands are located), then rub this cloth on the new items. The cloth will pick up chemicals (that people can’t smell) known as facial pheromones, that your cat will recognise as familiar, and encourage your cat to feel safe and secure, but only do this if your cat is comfortable with this process.

Ensure your cat has plenty of places throughout the home to feel safe and secure out of the baby’s reach – cardboard boxes, perches, shelves, cat activity towers/trees and igloo beds are all really useful, as are behind or on the back of sofas, beneath beds and on top of furniture (cupboards and wardrobes)

You may wish to change who feeds your cat before the baby is born if the new mother isn’t able to in the early days after giving birth. This minimises disruption to your cat at a time when everything else is changing.

Before bringing the new baby home, let your cat hear the sound of a baby crying by playing an audio clip (there are many freely available online). Play it at a volume only just audible and, if your cat shows no interest, you can gradually increase the volume. The idea is to help your cat to learn that such a sound is nothing to worry about (this is known as desensitisation)

Once the baby is born, he or she may spend a night or two in hospital. It’s a good idea to bring home a worn baby grow to let your cat sniff and investigate. Getting them accustomed to the smell of the baby before he/she arrives home will help your cat feel less daunted by the new presence

Bringing your baby home

Bringing your baby home can be another tense time, because you won’t know with any certainty how your cat will react to them. However, it is important to, stay calm, let your cat take their time to sniff your baby upon arriving home, and try not to create a tense atmosphere.

The wonderful thing about cats is that they are usually adaptable to most situations and will go back to their previous activity, such as retreating back to their bed next to the radiator, curling up and going to sleep! Occasionally, a very sensitive cat may become stressed, and this might lead them to displaying unwanted behaviours, such as house soiling, but this can be overcome by increasing their sense of security (see our urine spraying and house soiling articles for more information) and maintaining a good hygiene routine. The use of synthetic feline pheromone diffusers can help when a new baby is brought into the home, as they help cats feel more safe and secure in their home environment.

#### Some top safety tips include

- Wash your hands after handling your cat

- Wear gloves and/or wash your hand thoroughly after cleaning litter trays

- Where possible, keep cats off kitchen surfaces

- Clean kitchen surfaces regularly

- Keep the baby’s room as a no-go zone for your cat for your own peace of mind, especially when the baby is very small

- Put a net over a baby basket or pram to prevent your cat from getting in with them

- Keep up with your cat’s preventive health care (flea and worm treatments)

## What do I need to think about as my baby grows?

As your baby becomes stronger and more mobile, they are more of a hazard to your cat. This is because they will have easier access to your cat, limiting their options to retreat from them if they choose to. During this time, make sure your cat has plenty of places where they can get away from the baby. Using vertical space (shelves, cat trees, perches) and baby gates can be really helpful to provide your cat with safe places that your baby cannot access.

Babies and small children should always be supervised when close to your cat to prevent them from grasping your cat. Working together, you can teach your children that touching a cat needs to be done gently and with a flat palm, avoiding areas which as the cat’s paws or belly. Placing your hand under your child’s hand can help protect your cat’s fur from any pulling or grabbing.

By meeting your cat’s need for safety and security, predictability and control, you are on the right path to ensuring your baby develops a long and lasting friendship with your cat.
