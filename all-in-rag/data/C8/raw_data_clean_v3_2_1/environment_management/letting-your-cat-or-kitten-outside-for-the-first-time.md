# Letting your cat or kitten outside for the first time

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/letting-your-cat-or-kitten-outside-for-the-first-time

---

If you’ve chosen to let your cat or kitten have outdoor access, letting them out for the first time can be a big moment. Many owners worry that their cat may not come back in.

Some people take on adult cats that have never been outside before and wonder if they’ll be able to cope with going out into the big wide world. Cats are amazingly adaptable and the outdoors can provide increased opportunity to explore and express natural behaviours.

Your cat may be a little nervous at first, but time and patience go a long way in helping your cat get the most out of their new, exciting world.

## What do I need to consider before letting my cat outside for the first time?

Before allowing your new kitten or cat outside, make sure it can be identified either with asecurely fitted, ‘snap-open’ collarwith a tag (showing your telephone number) or, ideally, amicrochip (this is now a legal requirement for cats in England). Microchips not only help reunite lost cats with their caregivers, but they can also be programmed into some cat flaps to enable selective entry. This prevents unfamiliar cats from entering your house (which could be upsetting for your cat or kitten).

It’s worth also thinking about how you plan on giving your cat outdoor access in the long term. For example, are you happy (and available) to let your cat in and out through doors (or windows, where it’s safe to do so)? Or would a cat flap be more suitable, allowing your cat the freedom to come and go as they please?

Also, think about if you’d prefer to keep your cat in at certain times of day - for example, you may want to keep your cat in overnight or limit their access outside at times when local traffic may be busy. Some cat flaps can also be programmed to allow your cat outside or keep them in at specific times of the day or night.

To keep kittens protected from infectious diseases, we don’t recommend allowing them outside until at least a week after they’ve completed theirprimary vaccinations, at around 13-14 weeks old (depending on advice from your veterinary team and the vaccines given). After this point, they can explore outside under your supervision. However, before allowing your kitten any kind of outdoor access, make sure they areneutered (ideally at around 4 months of age), have fully healed from their surgery and have become used to life in your house, too. Most kittens will benefit from supervised time outside until they are 6 months old, so you can use this time to play with your kitten (using a wand toy or something similar) and teach them recall using a reward such as food.

Finally, before letting your cat outdoors for the first time, ensure your garden is safe - cover ponds, block holes in fencing, and remove any potentially hazardous garden items or poisonous plants or flowers.

## How do I prepare for letting my cat out?

It’s a good idea to get your cat used to coming to you when called, as this will make it easier to call them in from outside. You can do this at home first, by calling their name and a word such as ‘come’ and offering their favourite treats when they respond. They will soon associate the recall word with a treat and come running when they hear it.

## When should I let my cat outside for the first time?

Kittens

If possible, choose a dry day and a quiet time to let your kitten outside for the first time. Ensure you have enough time scheduled to supervise your kitten (a day when you are not working or have other commitments in the day is best). Go out with them and allow them to explore their new environment. Continue to accompany your kitten outside until they are familiar with your garden and can find their way back to the house with ease. We recommend not leaving your kitten alone outdoors until it’s 6 months old, and they must be neutered.

Adult cats

If you’ve adopted an adult cat, we recommend waiting 2-3 weeks before letting them outside, to give them chance to settle into their new home.

As with kittens, choose a quiet, dry day when you are home, and let them outside before a routine mealtime, as they are more likely to come back indoors when hungry. Go outside with them, and don’t panic if they dart into some nearby shrubbery - this is perfectly normal, as it allows them to observe their new environment without being seen.

Leave the back door open initially and, after 30-60 minutes, call your cat indoors for their meal and repeat the process the following day. During this time, you may wish to call your cat to you for a treat and then allow them to carry on exploring so you build up the familiarity of regular check-ins for your cat when outdoors. Most cats adapt extremely quickly to exploring their new outdoor space and probably won’t need such a gradual introduction, however, for timid cats, it’s a good idea to take it slowly, gradually increasing their periods outside in the first few weeks. ## Where should I let my cat out?

Your garden should be the first place you let your cat outside, but if you’d prefer to minimise the risks of outdoor access, while maintaining the high level of enrichment that the outdoors offers, you can create a restricted outdoor space for your kitten or cat.

#### Depending on the size of your garden and the features, options include

- Cat fence barriers – ideal if you have an existing fence, which you can attach an overhanging barrier to.

- Cat enclosures – cat fencing that can be used to section off parts of your garden or used where there is a hedge your cat could get through.

- Catios – suitable for smaller suburban patios or where a mesh roof option is required for high security.
