# Should I keep my cat indoors or allow outdoor access?

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/should-i-keep-my-cat-indoors-or-allow-outdoor-access

---

There’s no right or wrong answer when it comes to either keeping your cat indoors only or allowing them to explore outdoors. It all depends on you and your cat’s individual situation and often there isn’t a clear answer as to what’s best – but instead a careful consideration of the pros and cons of each option.

An indoor-only lifestyle for a cat is generally chosen by an owner because they are concerned for their cat’s physical safety e.g. being hit by a car, or because they are concerned about infectious diseases, being lost or stolen and/or concern for local wildlife populations. Owners of cats with very specific conditions or needs may also choose to the keep their cats indoors, for example, those with certain health conditions such as feline immunodeficiency virus (FIV), which pose a risk of infecting other cats with the virus (usually through fighting), as well as being more susceptible to picking up infections themselves.

Other examples include cats who are deaf, blind or those with limited mobility, as well as those affected by cognitive issues such as senility. These cats are less able to move to a safe place quickly and easily and are therefore at greater risk of being injured.

It’s not just health concerns that lead to some cats living an indoor-only lifestyle. For some, outdoor access may not be possible, for example, if they live in an apartment.

Owners who allow their cats outdoor access recognise the mental wellbeing benefits such as increased sensory experiences and greater control over the environment, as well as the physical exercise opportunities.

Some cats will have free access to the outdoors where they can choose when they go outside or come indoors e.g. via a cat flap, while others will have controlled access where their owner lets them in and out by opening and shutting windows and doors. It’s common for owners to have concerns about letting their cat out at night or between dusk and dawn, so they may decide to keep their cat indoors during this time for safety reasons or to protect local wildlife.

It’s important to consider your cat’s personality, health needs and the indoor and outdoor environment available to them when making a choice. We’ve listed the advantages and disadvantages of keeping cats indoors only and giving outdoor access below - hopefully, it’ll help you make the right decision for you and your cat.

## What are the advantages and disadvantages of keeping my cat indoors?

## Advantages

The main advantage to keeping your cat indoors is that generally, they’re exposed to fewer physical risks, so may live a longer life. Cats living indoors tend to be safe from territorial disputes between neighbouring cats, which can lead to cat fights, and in densely populated areas, this can be a common problem.

From a health perspective, indoor-only cats are more protected from picking up infectious diseases from other cats. There’s also less risk of serious injury, such as being attacked by a dog or being hit by a car. Indoor-only cats are less likely to ingest certain toxins such as ethylene glycol (antifreeze) too.

Some owners make a choice on their cat’s indoor lifestyle based on their concerns for local wildlife populations and the desire to prevent their cat from hunting. And of course, there are a few breeds or types of cats who may face problems outdoors - for example, the hairless Sphynx may suffer if exposed to cold weather or direct sunlight, whereas white-coloured cats are at greater risk of skin cancer.

So, we’ve looked at some of the advantages of keeping your cat indoors permanently, but what about the disadvantages?

## Disadvantages

Accidents can still happen in the home - after all, cats are curious, so they could still eat or chew on something they shouldn’t (e.g. poisonous plants, human medications or cleaning products). Cats can also burn themselves, become trapped or injured by furniture such as radiators and doors or fall from windows inside the home. In addition to this, if your indoor cat accidentally escapes, they’re likely to be at greater risk than a cat who is used to spending time outdoors as they won’t be used to navigating an outdoor environment.

Another consideration if you’re thinking about keeping your cat indoors is that they may see neighbouring cats in their garden from inside the home but won’t be able to chase them away and protect their territory. This could cause anxiety or frustration, making them more likely to redirect any defensive behaviours (such as scratching or biting) towards their owner or other animals in the home. If neighbouring cats spray urine in the garden or against the walls of your home, your cat may be able to smell this but can’t access it to mark over the scent. This could be quite stressful for your cat, as they may see the other cat as a threat but can’t mark their territory in response, which may lead to unwanted behaviours such as urine marking or scratching of furniture inside the home

However, the biggest issue facing indoor-only cats, is the potential negative impact on their mental wellbeing which can lead to long-term stress and subsequent physical illness. An indoor lifestyle can be predictable and monotonous, and there’s less opportunity to exhibit certain natural behaviours, such as hunting and exploring. You may also see unwanted behaviours such as scratching of furniture, urine spraying, excessive vocalising and anxiety-related problems if your cat is kept solely indoors and becomes bored, frustrated, or isn’t coping well with sharing a confined space with other cats living in the home. Read our article on making your home cat friendly for more advice.

Cats that live in multi-cat households but are kept solely indoors may suffer from chronic stress due to being unable to get away from other cats in the household, and this can lead to stress-related health conditions such as feline idiopathic cystitis or over-grooming. Indoor-only cats are more likely to develop obesity due to a more sedentary lifestyle and reduced energy expenditure.

## Keeping your indoor cat happy

If you’re planning on keeping your cat indoors, it’s important to make sure their needs are met and to make their home environment as stimulating and cat friendly as possible. Giving cats the opportunity to display natural behaviours, such as playing, climbing and exploring can help provide exercise and prevent boredom. Cats like to be up high, so vertical space such as shelves and cat climbing trees can add extra dimension to their environment. Vertical space can also give cats living in multi-cat households the opportunity to spend time off the ground and away from each other, which can help provide a safe space for cats to relax and prevent conflict. You can also dedicate time each day to play with your cat, using a variety of toys to keep things interesting.

## What are the advantages and disadvantages of letting my cat outdoors?

## Advantages

#### There are many advantages to giving your cat outdoor access. The outdoors can provide a larger, more varied and stimulating environment for your cat to enjoy. Other benefits include

- More control and choice with their environment

- Increased opportunity for physical exercise and exploration. Being able to hunt, climb and patrol their territory means cats get exercise and use up energy. Outdoor cats tend to be a more ideal weight than their indoor counterparts

- Opportunity for instinctive predatory behaviours (although often not considered an advantage by owners!)

- Ever-changing elements and environments that stimulate their senses

- Increased choice of locations for toileting

- Opportunity to move away from anything threatening or stressful within the home environment

- Cat urine spraying outside

The outdoors provides a greater outlet for a range of natural and normal cat behaviours that aren’t always appreciated by an owner if they occur indoors, for example, urine spraying, which is a normal way for cats to communicate with other cats in the wider world. However, spraying indoors can occur for multiple reasons and is usually a sign that something is causing distress, such as an underlying medical problem, conflict with other cats (inside or outside of the home) or something unfamiliar in the home, such as a new baby or dog.

Cats will also scratch for marking reasons, often on the boundaries of their territory, so if they’re kept purely indoors without enough suitable scratching posts spread out throughout the home they may resort to scratching furniture or door frames. An outdoor environment provides more choice of scratching surfaces for cats, such as a variety of trees, gates or posts.

In general, allowing your cat outdoors gives them more control over their actions and environment. Less confinement can lead to a reduced likelihood of cats experiencing frustration as they have control over how much time they spend indoors or outdoors. They can access resources such as toileting areas or shelter away from the home if they need to. Indoor cats may find changes to their home stressful, whereas cats that have outdoor access can choose to distance themselves from things they may find stressful, even temporarily, which can help them to cope.

In addition to this, providing outdoor access, and therefore, a larger home territory to cats living together in multi-cat households, gives them more space for themselves. Many unwanted behaviours displayed by cats are a result of stress caused by living with other cats. Being able to go outdoors means they can get away from each other if they want to and establish their own territory. This is particularly important for cats who don’t consider themselves to be a part of the same social group.

## Disadvantages

It’s easy enough to worry about the risks associated with allowing your cat outdoors - they could pick up a harmful disease, get into cat fights, eat something poisonous, be hit by a car, or get lost, to name just a few.

A less obvious downside to outdoor access is the impact on their social environment and, depending on the cat population outside the home, there can be serious emotional welfare implications for all cats concerned. It’s normal for cats to share their home range with other cats in the area, but, if possible, they tend to avoid one another and naturally develop a timeshare arrangement.

However, cats will actively defend the area they consider their territory from cats they don’t consider part of their social group. Read our webpage about thesocial structure of catsfor more information.

With the increase in popularity of pet cats, in urban areas, it’s common to find an increased number of cats attempting to monopolise a piece of land they all consider their own or a part of their own territory. This increase in social pressure can be difficult for some cats to cope with and can lead to fighting and injury throughcat bitesand scratches. Cats can also intimidate each other through staring and even invading another cat’s core territory by entering its home to access resources such as food. The caveat to this is there are always some cats that form friendships with neighbouring cats and enjoy their company, but this is generally in the minority – most will try to actively avoid each other.

The quality of outdoor access is also something to consider. Cats living in urban areas may only have access to a minimalist garden or courtyard, which may not provide the stimulation and facilities cats need to get the most out of their outdoor environment. ## How do I know what’s best for my cat?

Always make a decision for your cat based on who they are as an individual and what their preferences are, whilst also taking into account the type of environment they’ll be exposed to if they’re allowed the freedom to roam. There are advantages and disadvantages for both options, so do what you believe is best for your cat, and keep any risks associated with either indoor-only or outdoor living to a minimum.

Additional Considerations

Whether your cat lives indoors only or has access to the great outdoors, we still recommend ensuring all their preventative healthcare needs are met. This means having your cat microchipped, neutered, vaccinated and treating them routinely for parasites (particularlywormsandfleas). Indoor only cats may have a reduced risk of contracting a disease or picking up parasites, but the risk is still there, so it’s best to speak to your vet who will be able to advice you on what is best for your cat.

## FAQs

## How old should my cat be when I let them out?

If you’re planning to let your cat outdoors, consider their age. We don’t recommend allowing kittens free access outdoors until they’re microchipped, fullyvaccinatedand have recovered from beingneutered (you can neuter your cat from 4 months of age). It is advisable to start with supervised sessions outside until your kitten is 6 months old. If you’ve rehomed an adult cat, while there are no age restrictions, we recommend waiting two to three weeks so that they’ve settled into their new home before going outside.

## Will my cat come back if I let them outdoors?

Cats are generally quite cautious when they go outdoors for the first time, so aren’t likely to go too far from home. You can try to teach your cat recall inside the home first, by calling them and offering treats when they come to you - this can make calling them in from outside easier. ## The Science

There is debate as to which types of roads are most dangerous to cats, those that are constantly busy or those that have intermittent traffic. Rochlitz (2003b) found that proportionally more road traffic accidents (RTA) involving cats occurred in areas with higher levels of traffic.

Gruffydd-Jones & Murray (2017) found that cats living in rural locations were more likely to be involved in an RTA than those living in towns, cities or suburban locations. This may be because cats living in these areas are less ‘streetwise’ because they are not as frequently exposed to traffic. Traffic travelling on rural roads may also be faster-moving than urban traffic. Also, urban roads may be more likely to have pavements which cats can use.

Cats living in a house next to a long, straight section of road were also more likely to be involved in an RTA. Drivers may travel faster on these stretches of road and so be unable to stop in time. Finally, cats that were reported to hunt at the roadside were also more likely to be involved in an RTA.
