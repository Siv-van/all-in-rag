# Hazardous weather and your cat

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/hazardous-weather-and-your-cat

---

As we all know, the weather can be extremely unpredictable – one second, it’s hot and sunny, the next, there’s torrential flooding. As cat owners, it’s important to ensure that our cats are safe and cared for, regardless of what is happening outdoors.

## What are the risks associated with hazardous weather?

Cats are very good at taking care of themselves, but sometimes they need a little help. Extreme weather, such as heatwaves, floods or snowy/icy conditions, can all pose a threat in different ways to our cats' health and wellbeing. This article looks at those risks in more detail and how we can keep our cats happy and healthy, whatever the weather.

## How do I keep my cat safe during hazardous weather?

Heatwaves

Many of us love the warm and sunny weather, as do the majority of cats. However, if the temperature becomes extreme, ensure your cat has plenty of fresh water. Replacing their water several times throughout the day canencourage your cat to drink, as can using water fountains and placing multiple bowls around the house and garden to ensure that water is within easy reach wherever your cat may be resting.

Most cats will enjoy a snooze in the sunshine and then retire to a shady spot when they get too hot – after all, they have evolved from desert animals. Ensure your cat has a few cool and shady spots to retreat to around the house and garden – cats will normally find these themselves but providing places can help. Alternatively, if your cat has outdoor access and there is no option to provide shade, consider keeping them inside when the sun is at its warmest. Simply opening the windows to let in a breeze can also help provide a cool area to rest, or, when supervised, a fan can be provided indoors to help circulate the air. Don’t be surprised if you find your cat lying on the tiles on the kitchen floor or in the bath – tiles and materials such as porcelain keep cool, so cats often gravitate towards them when they are a little too hot. Pet-specific cool mats can also be provided in much-loved resting spots or frozen water bowls wrapped in a towel or blanket to help keep them cool.

Remember to provide shady spots outdoors too

#### Although most cats can look after themselves, they may be confined to a room or conservatory which gets very hot, and in this situation, heatstroke is a risk. The signs of heatstroke in cats include

- Excessive panting

- Lethargy/tiredness

- Drooling

- Fever/high temperature

- Vomiting

- Collapse or unconsciousness

If you are worried about your cat or suspect they are suffering from heatstroke, contact your veterinary team immediately.

Always check sheds, greenhouses and summerhouses before closing them up. Cats can find their way inside if they are seeking a shady spot to cool down and trapping them inside can lead to heatstroke and dehydration. The same goes for checking cars – if you leave your windows open to let the car cool down, always check that your cat hasn’t snuck inside before you drive away or lock it up for the night.

One final note on hot weather – don’t underestimate the risk of sunburn to your cat. Protect them with pet-safesunscreen, where necessary.

Floods

#### Floods can hit at any time of year, regardless of the season, and can affect hundreds of homes and families. If you are a cat owner in a flood-affected area or an area that is prone to flooding, consider the following

- Keep your cat indoors, preferably upstairs, until the risk of flooding has subsided

- Keep a supply of fresh water, food, bedding, litter trays and medication somewhere safe, dry and easily accessible

- Keep your cat carrier on hand in case you need to vacate your home temporarily

- Keep your cat’s essential documents (eg, microchip details, vaccination records) in a sealed bag or another waterproof container in an easily accessible location

If you have to leave your home, take your cat securely in their carrier, making sure to take food, bedding, water, bowls and documentation with you, in case it is some time before you can return home

- Snow and colder temperatures

#### Entering winter typically means a multitude of things – the nights get darker, the temperature gets colder and the weather gets worse. These changes not only affect us, but also our cats. Below are some practical tips that owners can use to keep their cats safe during the cold winter months

Litter trays

If you have an older or unwell cat, or the weather becomes very extreme and your cat is unable to venture outside, provide litter traysinside the house. These should be filled with your cat’s preferred litter and placed in quiet areas to encourage your cat to use them. If you have amulti-cat household, multiple trays should be provided at different locations around the house (to avoid competition and conflict over territory). Cats are clean animals, so spoiled litter should be removed and replaced as soon as possible.

Food and water

Over the winter, your cat may eat more which will help them with temperature regulation and insulation. Many cats prefer to stay indoors during cold and wet weather, so they may eat more and exercise less. Providing enrichment and monitoring their weight will ensure they stay healthy

Whether your cat can venture outside or is kept indoors, providing fresh, clean water at all times is essential. If you keep a bowl outside, regularly check it to ensure it hasn’t frozen over and that it’s clean. Avoid putting chemicals in ponds and water features where possible, as cats will often drink from these, which can be harmful and, in some cases, fatal (as with antifreeze). Ensure that any spills are cleaned thoroughly, as contamination of the fur and paws can also be harmful.

## Outdoors or indoors?

## Keeping your cat inside when they are used to going out may not always be possible, so if your cat is allowed out, ensure they are microchipped. Ensure they have shelter outside if they do want to venture out, that is waterproof and secure but make sure they can also easily get back inside the home (eg, the cat flap is accessible in icy conditions as it may freeze and prevent entry).

If there are roads and pavements near you that are gritted/salted and your cat is likely to wander over them, wipe their feet with a damp towel to remove excess rock salt or other chemicals that could be groomed off and ingested (this could be harmful). Additionally, check their paws regularly for signs of injury, frostbite or dermatitis (sore and irritated paws caused by contact with rock salt, etc). Most pet cats sleep in our homes, but if you have a street or feral cat which you feed and that sleeps in the garage/shed rather than the house, make sure they have a warm bed that is free from drafts, and blankets to keep them warm overnight. When temperatures get very low, bring cats indoors to sleep.

In snow/ice storms or very low temperatures, cats should be kept inside to keep them safe. Storms can be very disorientating, and your cat may lose their way or become injured (freezing temperatures can also result in injury). Keeping cats inside means that you must provide them with enrichment. This can be in the form of a cat tower, scratching post, puzzle feedersortoys, which will keep them stimulated (and help them get a little exercise).

Hotspots

Cats enjoy being warm, so will look for ‘hotspots’. Warm engines in parked cars attract cats, who may keep themselves toasty by curling up in wheel arches, between the wheels or under the hood. If you have a local cat that does this, before starting your car, always tap on the hood and check between the tyres to ensure that a cat isn’t hiding there. As they enjoy being warm, cats may also gravitate towards radiators and fireplaces. Prevent potential burns by protecting your cat from open fireplaces, wood stoves and space heaters. You can also place blankets over radiators to keep your cat happy and safe.

Elderly or sick cats

Cats which are inactive, have lost muscle tone/weight or have trouble maintaining their body temperature will need some extra care during the winter. Ensure they have lots of warm, draft-free places to sleep and relax – anarthriticcat will especially appreciate a bit of extra warmth. Cats needing a little extra help getting around may find normal tasks (like getting through the cat flap), harder when the floor is icy and the cat flap is a little harder to push open – so be mindful of this and help them where possible. Keeping older and sick cats inside during the cold will help keep them healthy and safe.
