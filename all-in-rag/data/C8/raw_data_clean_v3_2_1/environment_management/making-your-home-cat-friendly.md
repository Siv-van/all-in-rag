# Making your home cat friendly

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/making-your-home-cat-friendly

---

As a territorial species, your cat’s home plays an important role in their happiness. So, as an owner, think about everything you need to provide for your cat to meet their needs and optimise their home – particularly if you keep your cat exclusively indoors or they have restricted outdoor access. However, the following suggestions for a cat friendly home should be applied to all pet cats, even those with free access to the outdoors.

A cat friendly home considers their unique needs, and should be both safe and stimulating for the cat.

Most homes aren’t naturally set up to meet a cat’s needs, so you’ll need to provide ways to allow them to perform natural cat behaviours. Without an environment that provides the right natural behavioural opportunities, cats can develop physical and/or emotional problems associated with boredom, frustration, anxiety and lack of activity. Without the challenge of hunting and exploration, cats will fill their time with activities that are available to them, such as sleeping and eating, which can lead to physical problems associated with a sedentary lifestyle, for example, obesity orfeline idiopathic cystitis. The good news is, there are plenty of ways to create a cat friendly home, keeping your cat healthy and happy.

## How to create a cat friendly home

Open-plan living spaces with clean lines and lack of clutter may be the basis of the modern style, but this couldn’t be further removed from your cat’s natural habitat. Although domestic cats are very adaptable, they still need a degree of camouflage to enable them to roam inconspicuously. This camouflage can be found almost anywhere apart from the contemporary living space; it’s very hard to disguise a cat in an empty room! In contrast, a slightly more cluttered home is a haven of possibilities for a cat, offering high places to sit and plenty of places to disappear from view. Pulling furniture away from walls can give cats ample hiding places.

Keeping safe

Make sure you’re aware of any potential household hazards to keep any risk to your cat to a minimum.

Cat flaps

Although they provide cats with the opportunity to come and go from home as they please, the cat flap can be perceived by a cat as a ‘weak spot’ in their home defence. It’s a place where other cats can potentially gain access to their territory. Installing a microchip-controlled cat flap will prevent neighbouring cats from entering your home and upsetting your cat. Read our advice on how to choose and use a cat flap. For homes with a cat flap, position all your cat’s important items (food and water bowls, litter trays, beds, scratching posts etc.) well away from the cat flap, to help your cat feel that access to them is safe.

Avoiding competition

#### Your cat’s resources (eg, food and water bowls, scratching facilities and litter trays) should be

Available in sufficient numbers ie, a minimum of one per cat, plus at least one extra

Spaced out around the home away from each other

Suited to an individual cats’ needs (eg, a kitten or elderly cat may need a litter tray with lower sides to be able to access and use it)

This set-up optimises use of the resources and helps prevent cats in multi-cat households from competing over access to them, which could result in fighting or long-term stress. Cat ‘resources’ within the home represent everything that provides nourishment, entertainment, stimulation and security for your cat.

Food

Offer food in wide shallow ceramic or glass bowls that give room for sensitive whiskers. Plastic can give off a slight smell that cats can find off-putting, and the reflective nature of metal can be frightening for some cats.

Cats in the wild spend up to half of their day looking for, catching and eating prey - using a lot of physical and mental energy. With small mammals forming most of their diet, they eat little and often. Therefore, the predictable availability of food twice daily in a bowl doesn’t challenge your cat, and leaves most cats with a void of several hours that they’ll need to fill with other activities. To make feeding time more challenging, and entertaining for your cat, try usingpuzzle feeders. The followingFive-A-Day Felixreport on feeding cats provides some more tips.

While humans are social feeders, it’s important to remember that cats are not – they are solitary hunters and feeders. For this reason, it’s best to not feed cats together, but to feed each cat separately, out of sight of other cats and animals. Visit our article onfeeding your cat or kittento learn more.

Water

Providing water beside the food bowl can stop some cats from drinking as the water can become easily contaminated by food. Each water bowl should have its own location within the home, and while being in the same room as food may be good to encourage your cat to drink after eating (especially if on a dry food diet), the bowl should be far enough away to prevent food from being knocked into it.

Glass bowls help cats to judge where the surface of the water is, and a wide bowl prevents their sensitive whiskers touching the sides. Most cats also like their bowl to be full to the brim, so they can lap without putting their heads down (important for keeping an eye on their surroundings). Double food and water bowls are best avoided as they are often too small, and as previously mentioned, cats prefer their food and water not to be placed directly next to each other.

If your cat doesn’t like tap water, offer filtered or boiled water left to cool. Some cats prefer running water, so pet drinking fountains can be used.

Senior cats or those affected by medical conditions may benefit from increasing their water intake.

High resting places

Suitable high resting places include cat trees or towers, that your cat can rest on top of or inside

Cats are natural climbers, and need to be able to rest and watch over their surroundings from high places, as this is where they feel safe.

You’ll need to provide your cat with different options where they can rest up high and survey their home, including giving them access to areas such as shelving, cupboards or other platforms – just make sure your cat can get down easily, especially older cats, who may have limited mobility.

#### Suitable high resting places include

Cat trees or towers – often available as modular units, floor-to-ceiling or barrel-like structures that your cat can rest on top of or inside. Many cat trees provide perching platforms and enclosed beds for resting and offer a challenging climbing frame for your cat. It’s important to ensure the beds on cat trees are large enough for your cat to rest comfortably, otherwise they are less likely to use them.

Freestanding cupboards and wardrobes – these have large areas where your cat can rest or hide in a high place. You may need to place other furniture nearby to give your cat a halfway platform for easy access.

Radiator beds – these can be comfortable and cosy, especially for cats living in colder climates. Older cats with arthritis may also benefit from radiator beds, as the warmth can be soothing and these beds are not situated too far from the ground, which provides easy access for most cats. The tube-like radiator beds can offer additional cover for shy cats, but higher resting surfaces should also be offered as an option to provide an additional sense of security ie, away from animals or people.

Shelves – these can be made specifically for your cat’s use. It’s important to provide a non-slip surface, as many wooden shelves are extremely slippery. Bookshelves and other shelving can also provide sanctuary if a small area is cleared for your cat’s use

Private places

Taking time out from social demands is an essential part of everyday life for a cat, so secret hideaways are important. These can be created by making space available under the bed, inside cupboards or wardrobes or behind the sofa, for example. Your cat should never be disturbed while using a private area unless you have any reason to believe that they’re not well.

If you make your cat’s private place a warm one, too, you’re providing five-star accommodation! Microwaveable or plug in heat pads are particularly appreciated by cats in cooler climates.

Beds

Cats spend most of their time asleep, so it’s important to provide a suitable bed. Cats prefer warm places to sleep and many like to have a strong, familiar scent of themselves and their home to give a sense of safety and security.

Provide a selection of beds in different locations including warm, sunny, quiet and communal areas. They don’t have to be specific cat beds, as many cats are content with their owner’s bed, chairs or sofas, so it may just be a case of placing some warm bedding in your cat’s favourite sleeping spot. Make sure your cat’s bedding is washable, but don’t clean it too frequently, unless it’s soiled, as cats gravitate towards places with a familiar scent.

If you want to offer a cat bed, make sure it’s placed in a raised position (many cats feel vulnerable sleeping on the floor), near a source of heat or an area of sunlight in cooler climates. In a very warm climate, your cat may appreciate a cooler location. If your cat prefers their own space, provide beds in quiet areas of your home. If your cats live in a multi-cat household, offering several beds that have more than one entry/exit point can be useful to ensure your cats don’t prevent each other from accessing or leaving their favoured resting spot. A combination of covered and open resting areas can work well.

Litter trays

Litter trays are essential if your cat is kept indoors or has limited access outdoors and are highly recommended even if your cat is free to roam. This is because cats that have outdoor access may still need to use their litter tray on occasion if their outdoor litter facilities are not accessible eg, in poor weather. Read our article on how to choose and use a litter tray for more tips on picking the best tray for your cat.

Position the litter tray in a discreet corner, away from food and water, full-length windows and busy thoroughfares. Cats may see external doors and cat flaps as dangerous places, so locating them as far away as possible from these potentially stressful areas is important for your cat.

As with all resources, you’ll need to provide one tray for every cat in your household, plus one extra, placed in different discreet locations around the home. This doesn’t mean that each cat will automatically choose their own tray, but it will give a general sense that there’s plenty of choice. Spacing out litter trays throughout the home is especially important in multi-cat households, as cats need to use their toileting facilities without fear that other cats will attempt to block access or ambush them after use. Remove faeces and urine-soiled litter at least twice a day and clean the entire tray weekly, avoiding strong smelling cleaners. Avoid scented litters and litter liners as cats tend to find both off-putting. Ensure that litter trays are not placed alongside each other, as some cats will view these as one litter tray and will be less likely to use them if one is soiled.

Scratching posts/areas

Cats need to scratch to maintain their claws and mark their territory – without scratching posts, or other suitable scratching surfaces, cats will scratch items of furniture. Scratching posts should be as tall as possible to allow your cat to scratch vertically at full stretch – alternatively, panels can be attached to walls at the appropriate height if you don’t have much space at home. Some cats prefer to scratch horizontal surfaces, such as mats, or corrugated cardboard scratchers, and they will usually have a preference for what material they like to scratch too eg, sisal, cardboard, carpet etc. Providing a variety of scratching areas and surfaces gives your cat choice and helps identify what their preferred scratching surface is. Read our article on how to choose and use a scratching post for more information.

Social contact

Under the right circumstances, some cats enjoy the company of their own species. However, problems can arise when cats reach social maturity (usually between the ages of 18 months and four years) and find themselves living together and competing for limited resources within a relatively small territory ie, the home environment. These problems can potentially be minimised by providing plenty of resources in the home, keeping and not getting any more cats if you are already struggling to offer enough resources throughout the home, or if there is already tension between the cats living in your home. These issues can be minimised by choosing cats that are more likely to be compatible. Read our article on whether your current cat will accept a new cat for more information.

Social contact with people is important, but the amount will vary according to your cat’s personality. Allow your cat to dictate the amount of interaction and to initiate most of the contact on their own terms. Read our handling and interactions article to find out more about how to interact with your cat in a cat friendly way.

Play with toys

Every cat is an individual, but most prefer toys and games that are as close to the natural hunting experience as possible. Toys that move randomly are great; those that are motionless and left lying around soon become predictable and boring. For this reason, toys should be put away in a drawer or box and access rotated to ensure they remain interesting your cat. Toys made from material similar to prey (eg, feathers) and that are of a similar size to prey animals are popular, as are those impregnated with catnip (a herb that some cats find particularly attractive). Toys can easily be made at home from scrunched up paper to sewn or knitted catnip mice. Interactive wand toys are a great way to play with your cat and enhance the bond you have with them. More tips can be found in our article on playing with your cat.

Novel/challenging items

It’s good for indoor cats to be exposed to new things occasionally, otherwise, a constant, unchanging environment can become too predictable, and some cats may then find new experiences or situations stressful.

New items can be brought into the home regularly to challenge your cat’s sense of smell and desire to explore.. Wood, stone, plants, cardboard boxes or paper bags, for example, can be placed in various locations and left for your cat to decide whether or not they’re worth exploring.

Windows to the outside world

Cats usually prefer smaller windows in darker rooms if given the choice. Large windows can be confusing to cats; they see the garden or street outside and all the potential dangers that they contain but don’t understand that they are safe indoors. It all comes down to camouflage. Glass doesn’t give cats any opportunity to hide while they check out the territory, and if it’s full-length, they may come face-to-face with a neighbourhood cat on the other side.

If you think this could be a problem for your cat, try attaching sheets of decorative static film to the lower portion of full-length glass – to your cat, this will look like a more solid defence. Alongside this, you can make sure your cat has a high perch somewhere near the window where they can look outdoors from a safe position. Alternatively, put some potted house plants near the glass to provide enough camouflage for your cat to feel safe.

Fresh air

Don’t underestimate the value of fresh air. This alone will carry challenging and interesting smells from outside and be a focus of attention for an indoor cat – just make sure you cover open windows with grills if you don’t want your cat to go outdoors. Outdoor ‘catio’ enclosures are an alternative to providing full outdoor access, and these can be decorated with walkways and elevated places for your cat to rest. You could also consider fully enclosing your garden so that your cat still benefits from outdoor access, but cannot venture any further, thus reducing exposure to certain hazards. Read our article about fencing in your garden for more information.

Synthetic pheromones

Feline facial pheromones are important signals of familiarity and security secreted naturally from glands in a cat’s face. Providing synthetic feline pheromones can have a calming effect on your cat if you move house, decorate, add furniture, visit the vet or even if you consider introducing a new cat. Speak to your veterinary team if you’d like more advice on when and how to use these products.

Additional considerations

There are many ways you can ensure your home meets your cat’s needs and by doing so, your cat will enjoy a happy, healthy life. If you’d like more specific guidance on any aspects of providing a cat-friendly home, contact your veterinary team, who will be happy to help.
