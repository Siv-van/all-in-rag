# Multi-cat households

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/multi-cat-households

---

Felidae (the biological family of cats) are solitary predators that don’t live in socially structured groups (with the exception of lions). However, felis catus (the domestic cat) is a remarkably adaptable species. While retaining their roots as a solitary hunter, in many situations, they will adapt to group living through the development of social structures.

Following simple guidelines, owners can help reduce stress and make a multi-cat household a positive experience for owners and cats alike.

## What is the social life of the domestic cat?

People usually choose whether cats live together. Cats may be acquired at the same time, for example, kittens who are related (siblings from the same litter) or at different times e.g. a new kitten or cat introduced to an adult resident cat.

Most pet cats are neutered and are either confined solely indoors or have restricted access to the outside, with no opportunity to put distance between themselves and the other cats within the home. Some cats are given free access outside, so can move away from the other cats when they choose to. However, there may be neighbourhood cats in their outside territory and, in urban areas, these cats live near one another and will compete over territory.

Cats are adaptable, and some cats will choose social contact with their own species, but others will mostly avoid it (given the choice) and opt for a solitary existence.

## What happens when it goes wrong?

If tension arises between cats in a household, or if key resources (food and water bowls, litter trays, beds, scratching posts etc.) are limited, cats will either avoid each other or suppress their natural behaviour to avoid conflict. If a cat can’t adapt, they can develop chronicstress, which may lead to further problems such asurine spraying, idiopathic cystitis or over-grooming. Cats with outdoor access may choose to leave their home environment and establish a new territory with enough resources to fulfil their individual needs.

## How can I have a harmonious multi-cat household?

#### Multi-cat households can work well under the right circumstances. There are three important factors

Compatibility of the cats within the group

Availability and accessibility of resources

Population density of the cats in the territory

Compatibility

Siblings that have been brought up together are more likely to get along well – particularly if they were very sociable as kittens.

For multi-cat households with several cats, you won’t always find they form one single, cohesive social group. They often form sub-groups with two, three or more in each group and with some cats on their own. These groups live together within their territory while making every effort to avoid other groups and keep their distance.

If you can tell which groups have formed, you can ensure resources are distributed around the home accordingly, so cats don’t have to share if they don’t want to.

Identifying social groups

It’s possible to tell which social groups have formed by watching your cats. Which cats spend time together, groom each other, sleep close together, play together (more common in kittens and younger cats) and touch noses when they greet each other? On the other hand, you can learn which cats don’t get along by looking for cats that are reactive towards each other’s presence or behaviours. You can observe which cats leave the room when another enters and which cat stares at other cats in the home or blocks access to litter trays or food. It can be helpful to make notes of any behaviour you notice your cats displaying towards one another, as this can help pinpoint which cats have grouped together and which prefer to be alone. More obvious signs such as hissing, chasing, swiping and fighting are indicators of tension and conflict between cats. If this is the case, speak to your vet about your cat’s fighting, as referral to a veterinary or clinical animal behaviourist may be needed.

House Maps

Plans of your home layout can be helpful to note how each cat or group of cats uses each area (territory) within the home. By watching your cats, you can establish which rooms they regularly spend time in and which they avoid. You can then identify where resources are located and if that causes cats to cross over common areas, which may become potential problem areas.

Accessibility of resources

Cats don’t share important resources with other social groups. These resources include everything a cat needs to survive and thrive, such as food bowls, water bowls, litter trays, beds, high resting places, private areas, scratching posts, entry/exit points and toys.

If enough of these resources are provided and they are distributed so they are accessible for each cat or social group in an area where they spend most of their time, tension and conflict can often be avoided. In terms of appropriate numbers of resources, a commonly used formula is one resource per cat plus one extra, positioned in different locations.

For example, in a home where there are three cats (who are not in social groups), provide four of every resource (food/water/scratching/beds/high places/toys/litter trays) spread out throughout the home in separate locations. Avoid grouping litter trays in one location, as cats will view this as one litter resource, which could discourage use, and cats are also more likely to block each other from using the litter trays.

Feeding areas

#### Cats are solitary hunters and feeders, meaning they would naturally choose to eat alone. However, they will suppress this natural behaviour as getting enough food is a priority for survival. This doesn’t mean they should be forced to eat in the company of other cats, as this can lead to conflict in multi-cat homes. To help your cats, try

Avoiding feeding them all in the same location with bowls placed next to one another

Providing a visual barrier between each cat, such as a wall (different rooms) or feeding on different levels (one cat on the floor and one on a table)

Providing measured amounts of dry food left throughout the day and night so that each cat can choose when to eat

Providing more frequent mealtimes for wet food

Positioning bowls away from corners/walls to allow your cats to face in any direction while eating. This allows them to monitor their environment and look out for other cats while eating

Consider the use of microchip feeders for each cat. These can be placed in separate locations in the home and only permit access to a specific cat

Water bowls

#### Cats prefer to drink away from feeding areas and may avoid drinking altogether if challenged by another cat. To help your cats, try

Placing water bowls in different areas of the home, away from food

Providing large ceramic, glass or stainless steel bowls (although some cats don’t like stainless steel bowls as they are reflective and noisy if the cat’s collar tag chinks against the bowl) filled to the brim so your cats can remain vigilant while drinking

Position the bowl to allow your cats to face any direction when drinking

Litter trays

#### Cats pass urine and faeces when and where they feel safe, away from feeding and hunting areas. Some cats prefer to go outside, but many prefer using indoor facilities. Cats may block or guard access to litter trays to prevent others from using them. If cats don’t feel safe toileting, this can lead to stress-related health and behaviour problems. To help your cats, try

Positioning litter trays away from full-length windows, cat flaps, noisy appliances (e.g. the washing machine), external doors, thoroughfares and busy areas

Considering using open trays, which allow cats to have a full view of their surroundings or remove the door/flap from covered trays

If in doubt, provide a choice of litter tray and litter and keep a note of their use to learn what each cat’s individual preferences are

Most cats prefer fine, soft, sand-like clumping products that are easily cleaned. Wood pellets can be uncomfortable for kittens and adult cats to stand on and may be more difficult to dig/rake

Litter trays should be scooped out at least twice a day and completely cleaned once a week if non-clumping litter is used and every 2-4 weeks if clumping litter is used.

Private areas

#### Cats need ‘time out’ from each other and from humans too. This is usually a dark, warm place that they feel is safe. To help your cats, try

#### Providing soft bedding, for example

Under beds (if appropriate)

Inside wardrobes

Cardboard boxes

Cat carriers or covered beds

Behind sofas

Do not disturb or interfere when they are using these resources

Ensure minimal disruption through cleaning, always leave a familiar smelling blanket behind and wash bedding on a rotation basis

Beds

#### Cats need somewhere safe to rest, free from danger and interference, preferably off the ground, warm and free from draughts. Cats may block or defend beds or ‘steal’ them from other cats to demonstrate their ability to control resources. Cats also use sleep or feign sleep as a coping strategy during conflict, so safe beds are important. To help your cats, try

Providing soft, warm, raised beds in multiple locations throughout the home

Provide beds with a source of heat, e.g. heated pads and/or radiator beds to encourage use in separate locations

Provide something with your scent on (e.g. clothing, towels) if it seems to make your cat/s feel more secure

Scratching posts

#### Scratching posts provide a visual and olfactory (scent) mark, as a means of territorial communication. They’re also necessary for claw maintenance and exercise. To help your cats, try

Providing multiple vertical and horizontal surfaces for them to scratch as many cats will have an individual preference

Ensuring the posts or panels are tall enough for your cats to stretch fully

Making sure there are vertical ridges on the scratching surface

Using rigid structures that resist scratching and don’t move

Providing different material options to find out each cat’s individual preference (e.g. sisal rope/cardboard/wood/carpet)

Placing scratching surfaces near to where cats rest on exit/entry points in the home (doors, windows) as this is where they are most likely to scratch

Entry/exit points

#### Cats may guard, block or intimidate other cats at an entry and exit point as this may be the only possible access to the only available litter tray or toileting area. To help your cats, try

Providing two entry/exit points on different aspects of the house, e.g. WindowsAccess from the upper floor to the flat roofFront/back doors, side entrancesCat flaps in multiple doors

Toys/play

#### Play is a solitary leisure activity for cats and insecure cats will not play in front of a more confident cat. To help your cats

Follow the resource formula (if toys are provided for object play)

Play separately with your cat without another cat present

Owners are important too!

Cats will withdraw from owners if other cats are more assertive, and contact can be location and time specific. To avoid conflict, allow your cats to dictate the quality and quantity of interaction and avoid ‘sharing’ affection.

Population density in the area

#### Cat population densities vary enormously depending on the country and whether the specific area is urban, suburban or rural. Domestic pet cat population densities exceed those that happen naturally, particularly as each cat household has a separate social group/s. The external population of cats also has an impact, with a high population density creating a high-stress factor, irrespective in many cases whether the resident cats have an indoor or outdoor lifestyle, as cats can be seen through windows and smells can come through doors and windows. To help your cats, try

Avoiding multi-cat households (or limit to two cats) in areas of high population density

Securing garden to exclude other cats (cat-proof fencing)

Using safe deterrents in the garden if cats are coming into the garden and bothering indoor cats
