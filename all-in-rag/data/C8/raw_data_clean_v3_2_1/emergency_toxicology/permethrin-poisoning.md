# Permethrin Poisoning

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/permethrin-poisoning

---

Permethrin is an insecticide used in some flea products for dogs and occasionally (in lower doses) in flea powders and collars for cats. Exposure to high doses of permethrin, often found in spot-on dog flea treatments, can result in serious illness in cats and even death.

So, why is it safe for dogs but not cats? A cat’s liver can’t break down certain chemicals into a harmless form, and so the harmful chemical builds up in the cat’s body, causing serious illness. Permethrin is an example of this, and is just one of the reasons why we must never assume that a drug or product which is safe for dogs will also be safe for cats.

If you’re worried you may have applied a flea product containing permethrin to your cat, or if your cat has been in contact with a dog treated with permethrin, contact your veterinary team immediately.

## How do I know if my cat has been poisoned with permethrin?

#### Permethrin affects the cat’s nervous system, and signs of poisoning include

- Tremors

- Shaking

- Twitching

- Oversensitivity to touch and sound

- Wobbly when walking (loss of coordination)

#### And in more severe cases

Seizures or fits

Less commonly, cats may have difficulty breathing, and may become blind.

## What causes permethrin poisoning in cats?

The most common cause of permethrin poisoning is accidental by people. Some owners use a dog flea product on their cat by mistake, assuming if a smaller quantity is used, it will be safe. Sadly, this isn’t the case, and even a very small amount of permethrin can make a cat seriously ill.

Poisoning can also happen when a cat comes into contact with a dog that has recently been treated with a permethrin-based product. When a cat rubs against a dog, or even sits on the same furniture, permethrin can transfer to the cat’s coat and cause poisoning.

Permethrin is also sometimes infused in clothing to repel ticks, mosquitoes and flies. There’s no evidence to show that cats have been poisoned through exposure to these items of clothing, but if there’s any concern, we’d recommend contacting the manufacturer of the product.

## The Science

Permethrin is a synthetic pyrethrin – a naturally occurring insecticide taken from the flowers of Chrysanthemum cineariaefolium.

## FAQ

## How can permethrin poisoning be prevented?

#### Permethrin poisoning is completely avoidable. Here’s how

Neveruse a dog product on a cat

Always buy flea treatment that’s only designed for cats – it can be easy to pick up the wrong product when dog and cat treatments are displayed side-by-side in shops

Be very careful when shopping online. Many products don’t list their active ingredients

Always read your chosen product’s instructions carefully and take note of any warnings

If you have dogs and cats at home, choose a flea treatment for your dog that doesn’t contain permethrin

If you choose to use a permethrin-based product for your dog, keep your dog and cat apart for 72 hours

If you have any concerns about any medication, always contact your veterinary team for advice

Whenever permethrin poisoning is suspected, it’s important to notify the manufacturer of the product and the local drug regulatory authorities – your vet can help you with this.

## What is the outlook for a cat that’s suffered permethrin poisoning?

For cases caught early and treated quickly, the outlook is good, and the cat should make a full recovery. Sadly, more severely affected cats (especially those suffering from fits that are hard to control) have a poorer outlook and may not survive.

## How to safely treat your cat for fleas

There are many different products available for treating fleas in cats, and they vary in their effectiveness and safety. If in doubt, always speak to your veterinary team for guidance on the most appropriate treatment for your cat.

#### Commonly used products include

‘Spot-on’ products– some of the most popular as they’re generally easy to apply and are widely available. Some products kill adult fleas, others interrupt the development of fleas, and some do both. Your vet can help you choose a product best suited to your cat

- Tablets– can be useful if you find them easier to give your cat than a spot-on. Do you find it difficult to give medications to your cat? Read our advice on how to give your cat a tablet

- Pump action sprays– can be an alternative to spot-ons and tablets, and the pump action minimises any distress to the cat

Insect (flea) growth regulators– as well as being present in some spot-ons or environmental sprays, flea growth regulators may also be available as an injection or as a product that can be given by mouth. These may be an easier solution for some cats, but should always be combined with a product that kills fleas

- Treatments to avoid

We don’t recommend using flea powders or aerosol sprays, as there are more effective, safer, and easier to administer flea treatments available. Similarly, we don’t recommend flea collars purchased from pet shops and supermarkets, only the types specifically recommended by and purchased from a veterinary clinic.

With any product you use, it’s always important to follow the manufacturer’s instructions. If you’re treating a kitten, pregnant, or nursing cat, always check the product is clearly labelled as suitable for them. If you have any doubts, or if you’re not sure what’s best for your cat, always speak to your vet.

## Glossary of Terms

- Insect growth regulators (IGRs)– a treatment that interferes with the flea growth cycle. These can be an effective method for controlling fleas but do not actively kill adult fleas.
