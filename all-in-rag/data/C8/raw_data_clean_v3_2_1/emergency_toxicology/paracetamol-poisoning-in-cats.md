# Paracetamol poisoning in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/paracetamol-poisoning-in-cats

---

Paracetamol (acetaminophen) is a painkiller that can be found in almost every household. It’s available under different brand names (common brands include Panadol and Tylenol) and comes in many formulations, including tablets, capsules, liquid suspensions and powder preparations. It’s also available in compound products with other drugs, including codeine, caffeine, aspirin, ibuprofen and decongestants.

## What is the problem with giving paracetamol to cats?

For people, paracetamol is a mild painkiller, but for cats, paracetamol is extremely toxic.

Cats can’t break down paracetamol to a safe metabolite as people and dogs can, and so toxins are produced that cause serious damage to their liver and red blood cells. Even just part of a single tablet can cause severe toxicity and death in cats.

Cats might be exposed to paracetamol accidentally after eating or drinking some form of paracetamol or a product that contains it, or it may be given to them to treat pain by their owners, who are unaware of how dangerous the drug is.

## How do I know if my cat is showing signs of paracetamol poisoning?

Paracetamol is rapidly absorbed. If you have given your cat paracetamol, contact your veterinary team immediately. If you’re worried your cat may have accidentally eaten paracetamol, signs of paracetamol poisoning can usually be seen within 4 hours, but almost always within 6-24 hours.

#### Signs of paracetamol poisoning include

- Gums and tongue turning brown

- Weakness

- Lethargy/tiredness

- Increased heart rate

- Fast, laboured breathing

- Depression

- Vomiting

- Going off their food

- Vocalisation (crying out)

If you’re worried your cat may have eaten paracetamol in any form, seek immediate veterinary advice.

## How is paracetamol poisoning treated?

#### Treatment for paracetamol poisoning includes

- Decontamination (trying to remove the drug from the stomach, depending on when your cat was exposed)

- Medication to counteract the effects of the drug

- Monitoring and supportive care (your cat will need to be hospitalised and may need to be placed on a drip and receive intensive treatment)

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you have given your cat paracetamol, think they may have accidentally eaten paracetamol, or if they’re showing any of the signs listed above, contact your veterinary team immediately.

## FAQs

## What’s the outlook for a cat that’s suffered paracetamol poisoning?

The outlook for a cat poisoned with paracetamol varies and depends on how much paracetamol is eaten and how long it takes before veterinary treatment is given. Paracetamol poisoning is an emergency. The sooner your cat is seen by your vet, the better chance they have of successful treatment (though this also depends on the amount of paracetamol eaten).

## Can paracetamol poisoning be prevented?

Yes, paracetamol poisoning can easily be prevented. Never give your cat paracetamol or any medication that hasn’t been prescribed by your vet. If you’re worried your cat may be in pain, make an appointment with your veterinary clinic.

At home, always store human medication, such as paracetamol, safely out of your cat’s reach, ideally in a cupboard, and always be cautious when taking tablets yourself that nothing is dropped or easy for your cat to access.
