# Are weed killers dangerous to cats?

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/are-weed-killers-dangerous-to-cats

---

Certain weed killers are poisonous to our pets. For that reason, it’s vital to always read the packaging of herbicide products and use them according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

With free-roaming cats in particular, it can be difficult to prevent them from coming into contact with plants treated with weed killer or from spills. It may be best to avoid their use altogether and control weeds by manually removing them instead to keep your cat safe and help protect other cats in the area.

## What are the risks with weed killers and cats?

Weed killers or herbicides are used for the control of weeds. Many products are available, but these domestic products (as opposed to professional agricultural products) only contain a few different herbicidal compounds. Cats are generally exposed to weed killers during, or soon after, their use by walking on treated grass or brushing against wet plants and then grooming. They may also walk in, or lick up, spills or drips from sprayed weeds, chew treated plants or (rarely) be exposed to ‘spray drift’.

If your cat comes into contact with weed killer, it’s important to note the name and ingredients, so that if your cat needs treatment, your veterinary team can tailor their approach accordingly.

## How do I know which weed killers are toxic to my cat?

Below are some of the most common herbicides.

Glyphosate

Glyphosate is found in many products and is effective against different types of weeds. Mostly found in liquid formations, it’s low in toxicity.

#### Signs of glyphosate poisoning

Vomiting

Loss of appetite

Lethargy/tiredness

Diarrhoea

Tremors

Drowsiness

Dilated pupils

Severe breathing problems (which can be life-threatening)

Eye and skin irritation

Chlorophenoxy derivatives

These are often found in combinations in products and are used in lawn feed and weed products (available as grains or liquids).

#### Signs of poisoning with chlorophenoxy derivatives

Excessive drooling

Vomiting

Painful belly

Lethargy/tiredness

Blood in the faeces

Loss of appetite

Weakness

Ulcers in the mouth

Ferrous sulphate

Ferrous sulphate is used as a moss killer. It may be available as the chemical itself, but is more commonly found in lawn feed, weed and moss killer products which contain a fertiliser (the feed) and herbicides (the weed killer, often a chlorophenoxy derivative).

These products are generally used to revive lawns between March and October, (in the Northern Hemisphere) and are available as grains for sprinkling on the lawn or products to be diluted in water and poured over the lawn.

#### Signs of ferrous sulphate poisoning

Vomiting

Diarrhoea

Irritation of the paws

Excessive drooling

Drinking more than normal

Fatty acids

Octanoic acid (caprylic acid), decanoic acid (capric acid) and nonanoic acid (pelargonic acid) are examples of naturally occurring fatty acids found in some weed killers, particularly those labelled as organic.

#### Signs of fatty acid poisoning

Irritation or ulceration in the mouth

Loss of appetite

Excessive drooling

High temperature (fever)

Severe skin irritation if there’s prolonged contact with the product

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you know or are worried that your cat has come into contact with a weed killer, contact your veterinary team immediately for advice. Where possible, have the product's name and ingredients available, as this will help your veterinary team decide the best course of action.

## How do I keep my cat safe from weed killers?

Always read the label carefully before using anything in your garden that could be potentially harmful to your cat. It may be best to avoid their use altogether and manually remove weeds from your garden. Where weed killer is used, keep your cat in the house and away from the treated area after use. Remember, always store containers out of reach, and clean up spills immediately.
