# Disinfectant poisoning in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/disinfectant-poisoning-in-cats

---

Many household disinfectants, antibacterial products (including wipes) and patio cleaners, contain the cationic detergentsbenzalkonium chlorideordidecyldimethylammonium chloride (DDAC). Cationic detergents are irritants and can make cats sick if they have licked treated surfaces or walked on them and then ingested the chemical when grooming or licking their paws.

## What are the signs of disinfectant (cationic detergent) poisoning in cats?

Drooling can be one of the signs of poisoning in cats

#### Normally, signs of cationic detergent poisoning aren’t immediate, and it can take several hours for them to be noticeable. You may not even realise your cat has been exposed until signs start to develop. Signs include

- Drooling

- Red, inflamed tongue and mouth pain

- High temperature/fever

- Reduced appetite and/or drinking

- Red, irritated skin

- Breathing difficulties and coughing

If you think your cat has been exposed to disinfectant, wash the product off with water and call your veterinary team immediately.

## How do I keep my cat safe around household disinfectants?

#### Some simple steps can be followed to help prevent exposure to benzalkonium chloride or didecyldimethylammonium chloride (DDAC)

Dilute the product according to the manufacturer’s instructions

Don’t allow your cat to access the area where it has been used until it’s dry

Don’t allow your cat to walk over a recently treated surface

Store products out of sight and reach of your cat

If used outside, try not to allow your cat to access a recently treated surface that has become wet after rain

Do not use antibacterial wipes intended for cleaning or for use on people to clean your cat

Clean up spills quickly

Alternative cleaning methods

If your cat walks on kitchen surfaces, other cleaning methods might be safer.

Steam cleaning, depending on the surface, is a chemical-free alternative

Non-ionic or anionic detergents can be used (eg, diluted washing-up liquid) as these are less irritant

Correctly diluted bleach is a good antibacterial agent if disinfection is necessary

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team immediately if you know or suspect your cat may have been in contact with benzalkonium chloride, or if they’re showing any of the signs listed above.
