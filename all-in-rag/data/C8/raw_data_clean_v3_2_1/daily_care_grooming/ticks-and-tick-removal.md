# Ticks and Tick Removal

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/ticks-and-tick-removal

---

Cats that spend time outdoors can pick up ticks, which are external parasites (ectoparasites) that live on the skin and feed on blood. When a tick attaches to your cat, it can cause skin irritation and potentially transmit diseases. In severe cases, a cat with a lot of ticks can becomeanaemic (low red blood cell count).

## What can I do to protect my cat from ticks?

Cats can be protected from ticks by routine treatment (spray, spot-on, tablets) – speak to your veterinary team about the most suitable preventative treatment plan for your cat. However, even when using preventative products routinely, it’s important to check your cat for ticks on a regular basis.

Ixodes tick attached to back of the neck of a cat feeding

If you find a tick on your cat, it’s important to remove it carefully and correctly. Incorrect removal can result in the mouthparts of the tick remaining inside your cat, or squeezing fluids from the tick into your cat, both of which can lead to infection and/or skin reaction.

As ticks can carry infectious diseases, we recommend wearing gloves when removing a tick. You can use a pair of tweezers to remove the tick (fine-tipped tweezers work best), however, there are specialist tick-removal tools available which make it easier to remove a tick safely and successfully. You can get these from pet shops/online or your veterinary clinic.

## How do I remove a tick from my cat?

Tick being removed with tick hook

Follow the advice below to help you safely remove a tick. If you are at all concerned about removing it, speak to your veterinary team for help and advice.

Always be calm, gentle and reassuring around your cat

It may help to have someone hold your cat while you remove the tick

Have everything you need with you so you can work quickly to minimise any distress to your cat

Choose somewhere suitable to place your cat when removing the tick. If there are two of you, a stable surface, such as a table, is a convenient height for you to both stand while removing the tick. If you are on your own, you may find it easier to kneel on the floor behind your cat

Cats like to have all four paws in contact with the surface they’re on. If someone is helping you, they should gently hold your cat around the front limbs and shoulders, with the forearms gently restraining the body and your cat’s rear against the helper’s stomach. Alternatively, your cat can be held in a side-on position against the helper’s body with one hand around your cat’s shoulders and the other across their body – again, the helper’s body is acting as a ‘backstop’ to prevent your cat from backing away. If you are on your own and kneeling, hold your cat between your knees to prevent them from backing away. If your cat becomes distressed, or tries to bite or scratch, discuss with your veterinary team how to remove the tick

Clean the bite site using salt water

Get rid of the tick by crushing it using tissue paper, to avoid coming into contact with any of the tick’s fluids (which may be infectious), and dispose of it safely into the bin

Keep a close eye on the bite site for any signs of infection or irritation (redness or swelling), and on your cat’s behaviour for any signs of illness. If you are worried, contact your veterinary team

Specialist tick-removing tools

Most tick-removing tools use a ‘slit and rotation’ mechanism. The slit in the device is placed around the head of the tick, and a curve in the device cups the tick’s abdomen, which reduces the pressure exerted on the mouthparts, making it safe to use a twisting action to ‘unscrew’ the barbed mouthparts from inside your cat. The device is twisted in one direction (either clockwise or anticlockwise, it doesn’t matter which) to remove the tick.

Using tweezers to remove a tick

If you don’t have a specialist tick-removing tool, use tweezers. Hold them parallel to your cat’s skin and grasp the tick as close as possible to your cat’s skin (gripping them close to the tick’s head to avoid leaving the mouthparts embedded inside your cat, which can cause infection). Be very careful not to squeeze the tick’s body with the tweezers, as this can squeeze fluids from the tick back into your cat, which can cause infection. Pull the tick upwards with a steady even pressure. Never twist the tick using tweezers, as this risks breaking off the tick’s mouthparts.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you notice a tick on your cat and you’re not comfortable with removing it yourself, contact your veterinary team. If you’ve removed a tick from your cat, monitor the bite site and your cat closely for signs of infection or ill health. If you’re worried about your cat, make an appointment to see your vet.
