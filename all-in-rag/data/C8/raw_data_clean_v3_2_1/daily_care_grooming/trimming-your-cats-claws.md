# Trimming your cat’s claws

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/trimming-your-cats-claws

---

Though cats’ claws continually grow, most cats don’t need their claws trimmed because they naturally maintain them at a good length through scratching. Providing cats with the opportunity to perform scratching behaviours has many benefits including sharpening claws, removing the old claw sheath and exercising the front legs.

Routine trimming of a cat’s claws isn’t advisable as they need healthy, sharp claws for climbing, hunting, playing, and defence. However, as cats get older and less mobile, they may be physically unable to scratch as easily due to painful arthritic joints. Claws can then overgrow and become caught in soft furnishings or even grow into their pads, which can be very painful and lead to infection if not resolved quickly.

## How do I know if my cat’s claws need trimming?

Visibly see the nails on a cat that is resting

#### Signs your cat’s claws may need a trim include

- Getting their claws caught on items like bedding, soft furnishings and carpets

- Not using or struggling to use scratching spaces

- When your cat is resting and their paws are relaxed, you can visibly see their claw

- Their claws make a tapping sound when walking on hard surfaces, such as laminate or tile

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you’re worried your cat’s claws are too long, contact your veterinary team for advice. They will be able to trim your cat’s claws if necessary and show you how to safely do this at home, should you want to, without causing your cat undue stress.

## How do I trim my cat’s claws?

If you think your cat’s claws need trimming, it’s best to discuss this with your veterinary team as they will be able to assess your cat’s claws and demonstrate how to do this safely at home.

## Tips for trimming your cat’s claws

Choose a time when your cat is relaxed

Ensure you have good light before starting

Position your cat facing away from you – an ideal time is while your cat is relaxing on your lap. Alternatively, gently wrap your cat in a towel with one leg free

Take your cat’s paw and gently press the top of each toe to extend the claw outward. Remember to be extra gentle when handling senior cats, as they may have painful arthritic joints

Examine the claw carefully, and using claw clippers, snip off just the transparent tip, avoiding the blood vessel (quick) visible down the centre of the claw

If your cat becomes anxious, stop and try again another time

Ensure claw trimming always ends on a positive note – for example, playing with your cat or giving them a tasty treat as a reward for allowing you to trim their claws

Claw covers

We don’t recommend using covers (nail caps) over your cats' claws (e.g. to protect furniture from scratching) as this can be very distressing for them and lead to problem behaviours from being unable to display normal behaviours, such as scratching, retracting their claws and grooming. Learn more about other ways to prevent scratching on furniture or carpetshere.
