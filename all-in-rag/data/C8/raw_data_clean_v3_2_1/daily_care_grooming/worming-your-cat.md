# Worming your cat

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/worming-your-cat

---

If you’re lucky enough to share your home with a cat, you’ll know the importance of worming them regularly. The most common intestinal worms affecting cats are roundworms and tapeworms, and we recommend a preventative worming regime that covers both types. Depending on where you live in the world, you may also need to consider other parasites such as heartworm or lungworm.

Cats don’t always show signs of having worms unless they have a heavy worm burden. When this is the case, some of the signs you can expect to see are weight loss, vomiting, diarrhoea, irritation around the anus, and a failure to thrive. Heartworm and lungworm can cause coughing and breathing issues.

While worms can sometimes cause problems for cats, some can also be passed on to humans. On rare occasions, they can even cause serious disease - making regular, preventative treatment throughout your cat’s life even more important.

## What types of worms can cats get?

#### As previously mentioned, the most common worms infecting cats are roundworms and tapeworms - but let’s look at all the worms cats can get in more detail

## Roundworms

Roundworms are the most common intestinal parasites in cats of all ages around the world. The two most common roundworms are Toxocara cati and Toxascaris leonina. Eggs from these worms are passed in the faeces and can live in the environment for several years. These eggs can then infect cats in two ways: either when a cat eats eggs directly from a contaminated environment, or when a cat eats an ‘intermediate host’, such as a mouse, that is infected.

Toxocara cati is also passed from mother cats to kittens through her milk. How does this happen? Whenever a cat is infected with roundworms, some immature forms (larvae) remain dormant in tissues in the body. This usually causes no harm, but when a female cat becomes pregnant, the larvae move to the mammary glands and are passed in the milk she produces for her kittens. This is a common route of infection, so it’s safe to assume that every kitten will be infected with Toxocara cati as a result.

#### Other roundworms that may infect cats in various parts of the world include

- Ollulanus tricuspis (found in the stomach)

- Gnathostoma spp

- Physaloptera spp

- Strongyloides spp

## Hookworms

Hookworms are a small intestinal worm found in most countries, but they’re more common in some countries than others. These worms can damage the lining of the intestine (gut) where they attach to the surface, and this may result in weight loss, bleeding and anaemia (loss of red blood cells).

Cats can be infected by eating eggs from the environment, from eating an infected intermediate host, or by the larvae in the environment burrowing through the cat’s skin.

Common cat hookworms include Ancylostoma tubaeforme, and Uncinaria stenocephala, but other species occur in some countries.

## Tapeworms

You may be familiar with tapeworms if you’ve ever seen what looks like grains of rice on the hair around your cat’s anus, in their faeces, or on their bed - these are tapeworm segments. Mature segments containing eggs are released from the end of the tapeworm (a long, flat worm containing multiple segments) and can also be passed in the faeces.

To complete their life cycle, tapeworms need an intermediate host (such as a mouse) to eat the eggs from the environment, and then the cat will become infected. The most common tapeworms that infect cats worldwide are Dipylidium caninum and Taenia taeniaeformis.

Dipylidium caninum is spread to cats by fleas. The immature flea larvae eat the eggs of the worm, and infection is then passed on to a cat when it swallows an infected flea during grooming. If your cat has fleas, assume they’re also infected with Dipylidium caninum (and vice versa).

Taenia taeniaeformis is passed on when cats eat small rodents infected with eggs. This infection is very common in cats that hunt.

#### Tapeworms found in other countries include

- Diphylobothrium latum (fish are intermediate hosts)

- Spirometra spp (amphibians, reptiles and rodents are intermediate hosts)

- Diplopylidium spp (reptiles are intermediate hosts)

- Joyeuxiella spp (reptiles are intermediate hosts)

- Echinococcus multilocularis (rodents are intermediate hosts)

Other worms

#### In addition to intestinal worms, cats can be infected with a variety of other worms in other parts of the body, although these worms aren’t present in all regions of the world. These include

- Dirofilaria immitis– heartworm

- Aelurostrongylus abstrusus– lungworm

- Capillaria spp– lungworm

- Troglostrongylus brevior- lungworm

- Thelazia callipaeda– eyeworm

## How do I treat my cat for worms?

It can be hard to know which worming product to use for your cat with so many available. While it’s possible to buy these from pet shops and supermarkets, these products may not be the best for your cat. For this reason, we always recommend seeking the advice of your veterinary team, who will know the types of worms present in your local area and can advise you on the most effective and safe treatment for your cat.

Worming products come in different forms, such as tablets, liquids and ‘spot on’ products. Which you choose depends on how easy you find it to medicate your cat, along with which worms the various products treat.

## When should I treat my cat for worms?

#### Roundworms are extremely common in kittens, and as kittens can be infected from the mother’s milk, we should assume that all kittens are infected, so worming should start at a young age. Recommendations will vary according to the product used, the cat’s lifestyle and parasite risk. We commonly recommend

Treating kittens for roundworms fortnightly from 3 weeks of age until 8 weeks of age, then monthly to 6 months of age

Treating adult cats (over 6 months of age) every 1-3 months depending on risk (influenced by hunting behaviour and outdoor access)

Treating adult cats (over 6 months of age) every 1-3 months with a product that is effective against both tapeworms and roundworms (depending on risk - influenced by hunting behaviour and outdoor access)

A product active againstDipylidium caninumshould also be used in kittens that have fleas

Treatment to prevent heartworm or lungworm infection may be recommended in some areas of the world. Discuss with your veterinary team if this is needed where you live and which products to use.
