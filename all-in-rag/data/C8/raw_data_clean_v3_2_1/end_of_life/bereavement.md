# Bereavement

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/bereavement

---

Everyone who loses a cat experiences some degree of grief. How that grief is expressed will depend upon a person’s personality, upbringing, life experiences and numerous other factors. As grief is a very personal thing, it’s extremely hard to predict exactly how each person will react.

The stages of grief

There are several recognised stages of grief that you may experience at the loss of a loved one, including when that loved one is your cat or any other pet. You may feel an initial sense of shock and denial. You may experience a distinct inability to concentrate your mind on the concept of death and the fact that you will never see your cat again. The sense of denial can be so strong for some owners that they may keep their cat’s body at home for a prolonged period before being encouraged to make the appropriate arrangements that need to be made when a pet dies.

After this stage of numbness and denial may come a phase of anger. This is a period when you may feel tremendous guilt and wonder whether your cat’s death happened through some omission or negligence on your part. “Could I have done more?”, “Should I have spotted the signs sooner?” or “Did I allow my cat to suffer for too long?” are some of the common thoughts that affect bereaved cat owners. After this period, some people experience depression and may believe that they won’t feel truly happy again.

There is no set way to grieve. Although stages of grief have been recognised and described, not every individual experiences each phase or passes through them in any particular order. As grief is such a personal thing, it’s difficult to explain how an individual may feel when it happens.

Expressing grief

How someone shows their grief will differ from person to person, and any number of physical, emotional, intellectual and social signs can be experienced as part of a perfectly normal but painful journey to recovery.

Physical signs may include crying, shortness of breath, tight chest, nausea, loss of or increased appetite, tiredness, dizziness, inability to sleep or disturbed sleep, and general aches and pains.

The emotions experienced range from sadness, guilt and irritability to loneliness, helplessness and even relief, particularly if your cat suffered towards the end of their life. You may experience confusion, feel unable to concentrate, or even think you hear the cry of your cat at night. You may need to talk about or rationalise the loss and become preoccupied with death and the concept of an afterlife. All of these feelings may seem alarming but are well-documented and are part of the process for some people.

Complicated grief

Many complicating factors can exaggerate or prolong grief, for example, you may not have experienced a death previously, or you may have received insensitive comments from others who have trivialised the loss, where maybe a person doesn’t understand the bond between an owner and their pet. Whatever happens, it’s important to accept that grief is inevitable and allow yourself to grieve. Problems can arise when you don’t allow these feelings to express themselves fully.

A common consequence of all these mixed feelings is withdrawal from contact with others, even family and friends, and a rejection of help when it’s offered. However, support from friends and family is invaluable, and it’s often this that helps people to eventually accept their loss and feel ready to move on with their lives.

Help and support

Not all bereaved pet owners are fortunate enough to have supportive people around them, and some may find themselves having to cope with their grief in complete isolation. This is incredibly difficult, no matter how strong you are, so reaching out for a helping hand can be hugely beneficial. Your veterinary clinic or doctor’s surgery should be able to provide you with details of local bereavement support groups. These are run by volunteers who have been trained in helping the bereaved. They will inevitably have been through a similar loss and have an immediate empathy with you. Never be afraid to ask for help.

It’s important to look after yourself at this difficult time but accept the need to mourn. When the time is right, which could be weeks, months or years, you may consider sharing your life with another cat, seeing this not as a betrayal but as the ultimate compliment to the memory of the departed.
