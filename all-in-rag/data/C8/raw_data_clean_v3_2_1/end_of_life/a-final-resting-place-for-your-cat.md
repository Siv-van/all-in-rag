# A final resting place for your cat

https://icatcare.org/articles/a-final-resting-place-for-your-cat

The loss of a pet can be an extremely upsetting and difficult time. It may help you to decide in advance what your preferred option would be after saying goodbye to your cat e.g. burial or cremation. We hope that the following advice will help you to choose a final resting place for your cat, and remember, your veterinary team will be on hand to support you throughout this time. You may also find our article on dealing withbereavementhelpful.

For more information on pet cemeteries and cremation, visit the Association of Private Pet Cemeteries and Crematoria.

## What options do I have when choosing a final resting place for my cat?

Cats can be cremated or buried.

Cremation

#### There are two types of cremation service available

This is where several animals are cremated together. If you choose this option, your cat’s ashes will not be returned to you but will be scattered or buried at a licensed site, such as a memorial area on the facility’s grounds. Speak to your veterinary team for more information on this or contact the pet crematorium directly.

This is where animals are individually cremated, and you can have your cat’s ashes returned to you, to scatter, bury, or keep. A pet’s ashes can be scattered on land you own – if you want to scatter elsewhere, e.g. in a meadow or woodland area, you will need to contact the land owner, local authority or Environment Agency. In some countries*, such as the UK, it is illegal for a pet’s ashes to be scattered in a human burial ground or placed in an owner’s coffin before burial or cremation. However, there are some crematoriums and burial grounds that allow both human and pet cremations and burials, so that you and your cat can remain close together after death.

Individual cremation is more expensive than communal cremation. Speak to your veterinary team or the pet crematorium for more details.

Burial

In some countries*, pets can be buried either in the property where they last lived, or in a licensed pet cemetery. You cannot bury your cat on land that is owned by someone else, or public land if you do not have permission to do so. If in doubt, contact your local authority.

Make sure you check with your vet that it is safe to bury your cat at home without posing a risk to you or other animals. When choosing a burial site, make sure it’s not close to a waterway, well or spring so as not to contaminate water sources. Consider wearing gloves and wash your hands after the burial. You should aim for a depth of at least one metre (three feet). Make sure you cover the burial site with something heavy such as a paving stone or heavy pot, to stop wild animals digging there.

Some people may be worried about leaving their pet’s grave behind if they move house, or do not have a garden to bury them in, so burial at a pet cemetery may be preferable. All pet cemeteries are different, and visiting before deciding is important. A burial at a pet cemetery is more expensive than burying at home – you will need to purchase a plot, and a coffin, and there is usually a maintenance fee to ensure the grounds are maintained.

*Please check the law in your country
