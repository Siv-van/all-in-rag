# Should I feed my cat wet or dry food?

It can be hard to choose the best food for your cat. Walk into any pet shop or supermarket and you’ll find a huge selection of different cat foods – wet, dry, complete, complementary. There’s so much to choose from, but what does it all mean?

#### Cat food is generally classified according to its

- nutritional adequacy (complete vs complementary)

- life stage (kittens, adult, senior/geriatric)

- sensitivities and lifestyle (hairball prevention, neutered, indoor, sensitive skin, etc.)

- price (economic, premium, super premium)

Complete foods give your cat all the nutrients they need (except for water) and are designed to be the main part of your cat’s diet. Complementary foods are typically treats and are nutritionally incomplete – they should form no more than 10% of your cat’s diet.

Life stage diets are nutritionally balanced for your cat’s age and lifestyle. For example, growing kittens require more protein and calories, neutered/indoor cats require fewer calories, and in senior cat diets, phosphorus levels are reduced to help support ageing kidneys. On top of this, some diets are also adapted for specific requirements, for example, fibres such as psyllium can be added to help prevent hairball formation, and diets can be enhanced with fatty acids to help with skin and coat condition.

You know how old your cat is and if they have any specific requirements, but should you feed wet or dry food (or a combination of the two)?

Let’s look at the facts.

## What are the benefits of both?

## The benefits of wet food

Cats have evolved from dry geographical locations, and their response to low moisture foods is to concentrate their urine rather than drink more water. In the wild, a cat’s typical prey has a moisture content of >60%, so they have less need to drink. Feeding wet food provides cats with a source of water.

#### Wet foods are particularly useful for

Urinary health – wet foods can help to produce urine that is more diluted, which can help to manage urinary tract problems, such as cystitis and bladder stones

Weight management – water doesn’t provide calories; therefore, wet food is always lower in calories than dry food. Typical dry diets provide more than 3–4 kcal/g (some diets even higher), whereas wet food provides 0.8–1.5 kcal/g, with some weight loss diets providing even less. For this reason, wet food is bulkier and can help with weight loss or weight prevention plans

Constipation – dehydration is a risk factor for constipation and feeding wet food can be beneficial in some cats with constipation

Preventing dehydration – as mentioned, cats tend to concentrate their urine rather than drink more water. So, if they have a condition resulting in dilute urine (kidney disease, diabetes) they can become dehydrated

## The benefits of dry food

The main benefit of dry food is its ease, convenience and cost. Dry food allows for free-feeding and the food can be left out for prolonged periods of time without spoiling. Some cats prefer grazing their food over the day rather than at specific mealtimes, which is the main feeding method when wet food is used.

Dry food is easier to use with food dispenser toys and puzzle feeders as a means of environmental enrichment and to provide mental stimulation.

There are some dry diets that have beneficial dental effects, either by reducing tartar formation or slowing down plaque accumulation (by mechanical scraping of the tooth as your cat bites into the kibble). That said, not all dry diets will have adequate kibble texture to address plaque and, even if they do, they might not act on all tooth surfaces. In any case, the gold standard when it comes to protecting your cat’s dental health is tooth brushing. Should you choose to use a diet to slow down plaque formation, use products assessed as effective by the Veterinary Oral Health Council.

Finally, dry food has a higher energy density than wet food. While this can be a problem for cats unable to self-regulate their energy intake, in cats that are thin or have a picky appetite, dry food will provide energy and nutrients in a concentrated, small volume, making the most of the nutritional supply.

Additional Considerations

Both dry and wet food have pros and cons and the majority of cat owners feed a combination of both. Wet food is more expensive and less easy to use but can be beneficial in cats prone to lower urinary tract disease, constipation and that are overweight. Dry food can be a very efficient way to provide calories in thin cats with food volume limitations and allows for the use of puzzle feeders.

Some cats have preferences for wet or dry food which may be formed as kittens. For these cats trying to encourage them to eat the other texture can be challenging.

To decide whether wet or dry food is best for your cat, speak to your veterinary team. A complete nutritional evaluation, including diet history and body condition score, can be carried out and your vet can advise you based on their findings – this can be done during a routine check-up (for example, at the time of vaccination).

## My cat only eats dry food – how can I make sure they’re getting enough water?

#### In cats fed dry (or mostly dry) food, it’s important to encourage drinking, particularly to prevent urinary problems. A constant supply of fresh, clean water should always be available. Other factors that canencourage your cat to drinkinclude

- Location of water bowls: keep the water station separate from both food and litter tray. Choose a quiet place where your cat will not be disturbed while they drink

- Choice of water bowl: cats seem to prefer ceramic or steel, since plastic can give water an after taste. Use a wide, flat bowl, as some cats prefer that their whiskers don’t touch the rim of the bowl

- Multiple drinking stations: can help increase water intake. A must in multi-cat households to ensure this resource isn’t restricted due to conflict

- Water fountains: some cats prefer running water. Fountains should be cleaned often, and the filters changed as recommended by the manufacturer

Add water to the dry food: to achieve similar moisture to wet food, the ratio is 2–3 cups of water to 1 cup of dry food, but some cats will refuse this type of food and it does alter the nutritional content (eg, fewer calories and nutrients) so should only be done if accepted and on veterinary advice

- Flavoured water: chicken broth (without garlic or onion) or tuna ‘spring water’ can be added to the cat’s drinking water
