# Pica in Cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/pica-in-cats

---

Pica is a term used to describe a cat eating non-edible materials e.g. fabric such as wool, or harder materials, such as cardboard or plastic. There may be a genetic aspect to pica, as it’s more commonly seen in certain breeds such as Siamese, Burmese, Tonkinese and other Oriental types, but any cat can suffer from pica. Pica can be a problem behaviour that in some cases, results from underlying medical problems.

Pica explained

## What materials are eaten?

‘Wool eating’ is the name given to the behaviour shown in most Oriental breeds, as that’s often the material they choose. That said, cats can start to eat other natural and synthetic fibres, such as those found in clothes or soft furnishings, and objects made of rubber, wood, leather, plastic, cellophane, paper and cardboard are also popular. Shoelaces, strings and thread may also be ingested.

Pica can also be caused by medical conditions, such as anaemia, and affected cats tend to lick concrete or eat cat litter. The frequency of feeding and fibre content of a cat’s diet are thought to play a role, but this is not yet clear.

## At what age does this occur?

Kittens as young as three months old (sometimes, even younger) can start chewing their bedding, but they may grow out of the habit as they mature. Some cats, however, continue the habit into adulthood and it can be difficult to stop them.

Pica due to medical problems can occur at any age.

## How will I know if my cat has pica?

It’s not unusual for cats to chew and tear objects when showing natural predatory behaviour during playtime. However, usually pieces are torn off and not eaten. Cats with pica will hold the chosen object in their mouth, tilt their heads to one side and grind repeatedly with their back molar teeth before swallowing. It’s a highly compulsive and rewarding behaviour for some cats, and while we don’t fully understand why certain cats are so highly motivated to actively seek out and chew and eat fabric and other objects, one theory suggests that the act of chewing releases certain neurotransmitters (chemical messengers in the cat’s nervous system) causing a feeling of intense pleasure. This then becomes addictive.

## Is pica dangerous for my cat?

Radiograph showing a needle obstructing the intestinal tract needing surgical removal

Simply, yes. Unfortunately, many of the items' cats with pica eat can’t be digested in the same way as food. This leads to the risk of a blockage in the intestines, which can be life-threatening if left untreated. In these cases, surgery is required to remove the ‘foreign body’ (material) and in severe cases, sections of the gut may need to be removed. Despite this, most cats usually make a full recovery.

For pica due to conditions like anaemia, the underlying condition needs to be treated and can be serious. Additionally, eating cat litter or other non-food items could additionally make the cat unwell.

## What are the signs of pica in cats?

#### If you know your cat suffers from pica, it’s vital to monitor for signs of a blockage in the intestines. These signs include

- Vomiting

- Diarrhoea

- Constipation

- Lethargy/tiredness

- Reduced appetite

## What causes pica in cats?

There can be several causes of pica in cats. Genetics, as we’ve already mentioned, may play a part. With some cats, there may be a behavioural aspect to it if they arestressed, anxious, bored or frustrated. Certain diseases can cause cats to eat or lick unusual substances because of a specific craving - these can include anaemia or gastrointestinal issues. Cats with severeworm burdensor long-term or chronic long-term dietary deficiencies can also suffer from pica. Dental painor abdominal discomfort may also be a contributing factor. in cats with pica. dietary deficiencies can also suffer from pica. Early weaning and small litter size has been also linked with pica behaviours in Birman cats, but not Siamese cats.

Most cats eating inedible items because of an underlying medical cause are likely to show other signs that they’re unwell.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat is showing any of the signs listed above or seems generally unwell, contact your veterinary team for an appointment.

How your vet will diagnose pica

Your vet will diagnose pica by taking a detailed history from you on the behaviour your cat is showing and performing a physical exam. To rule out underlying medical conditions, they may also take blood, urine and faecal samples if required.

## How is pica treated?

#### Treatment depends on the cause and there’s no specific treatment for pica itself. If an underlying condition is found, that will require specific treatment. In pica with no associated disease, your veterinary team may ask you to try

Optimising a cat’s environment to meet their needs, helps provide them with mental and physical stimulation and prevent boredom and stress

Optimising your cat's environment, to meet their needs, provide mental and physical stimulation and prevent your cat from getting bored or stressed

Reducing your cat’sstressby managing interactions with people or other animals inside or outside of the home

Pica-proofing your home - in other words, keeping any items your cat seeks out to eat, well out of reach!

Offering referral to a veterinary or clinical animal behaviourist for further advice and support

Prescribing certain medications to your cat if the behaviour is anxiety-related

Cats suffering from pica often have restricted lifestyles, for example, living exclusively indoors, or they may be more susceptible to stress. Indoor cats need plenty of stimulation and, ideally, free outdoor access, but if this isn’t possible or safe, then asecured garden or outdoor enclosurecan be a good option.

Remove any objects your cat may eat and increase opportunities for play with moving objects, such as fishing rod toys. If there’s an underlying dietary issue, your vet may recommend feeding your cat more frequently and/or changing to food with a high-fibre content.

Not all pica habits are serious addictions, however it’s best to speak to your veterinary team for advice if you are not sure if you cat is showing pica behaviours.

Cats should never be punished for pica behaviours, as this could worsen any anxiety they are experiencing, increase the behaviour and have a negative impact on the relationship between you and your cat.

## FAQs

## What if I’ve tried and failed to stop my cat’s pica?

Pica can be very difficult to manage. Get in touch with your veterinary team, who may be able to offer specific advice or recommend a local, qualified vet or clinical animal behaviourist to help.

## As this behaviour may be inherited, should I inform the breeder?

As there’s some evidence to suggest that pica may be genetic, breeders need to be aware of this problem to take the necessary steps. If your pedigree cat has pica, it’s a good idea to let the breeder know.

## My cat eats litter, should I be worried?

If adult cats start to eat clay-based litter this can also indicate the presence of disease. Cats withanaemiamay lick or consume litter. If your adult cat starts eating litter, contact your veterinary team straight away for advice.
