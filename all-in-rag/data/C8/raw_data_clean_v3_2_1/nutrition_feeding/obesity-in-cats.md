# Obesity in Cats

---

If a cat is 10 to 19% heavier than their ideal weight, they are considered overweight. Research has shown that being either obese or too thin can shorten a cat’s lifespan.

## What are the health risks of obesity in cats?

#### Obesity increases the risk of development, or the progression, of many diseases

Diabetes mellitus

Increased anaesthetic risk

Lower urinary tract disease

Decreased immune function

Joint stress and aggravation ofosteoarthritis

Dystocia (difficulty giving birth)

Non-allergic skin diseases

Constipation (difficulty passing faeces)

Hepatic lipidosis (fat deposited in the liver)

Decreased stamina and exercise tolerance

Respiratory disease (eg, asthma, chronic bronchitis)

## How do I know whether my cat is an ideal weight?

Monitoring your cat’s weight can be useful to pick up changes in bodyweight i.e. weight loss or gain. However, an ‘ideal weight’ depends on the age and breed of your cat. Therefore, a scale assessing the body condition or amount of body fat (body condition score, BCS) is often used.

This scale grades your cat’s body condition from 1-5, where a BCS of 1 is very thin, 3 is ideal and 5 is obese. An obese cat has a thick layer of fat covering their ribs, making them hard to feel. They also have fat covering all of their bony areas and a pendulous ‘skirt’ (bulge under the belly), with no waist.

## Are some cats more prone to being overweight or obese?

#### Weight gain occurs when cats take in more calories than they use, and the excess energy is stored as fat. The body can usually regulate energy intake, so it matches energy use, maintaining the body condition around its ‘set point’. However, certain factors can affect this set point and predispose your cat to weight gain

Pedigree cats are less likely to develop obesity than domestic cats

Neutered cats tend to gain weight more easily, as when a cat is neutered, the metabolic rate decreases by about 20%, so neutered cats need less food to maintain their body condition

Cats who are less active or have restricted exercise opportunities are at a greater risk of gaining weight than active cats (e.g. indoor cats)

Cats under 2 years old are less likely to be overweight, whereas cats between 2 – 10 years require less energy and are, therefore, more likely to be overweight. Cats over 10 years can begin to lose weight, but can also remain obese

Feeding a palatable (tasty), energy-dense diet can cause cats to overeat and may cause obesity, especially if such foods are available freely to the cat at any time

Excessive treats and human food in addition to normal meals adds calories

Some medications predispose cats to weight gain, either by increasing their appetite or decreasing the metabolic rate

Stresscan result in undereating but also overeating

Helping your cat to maintain an ideal body condition

#### If your cat is overweight or obese, do not restrict food intake without discussion with your veterinary team. Sudden food restriction can cause illness in cats and there are also some health checks that may be needed before embarking on a feline weight-loss plan. We also know that, just like people, a weight-loss programme works best with support, which your veterinary team can provide. They may run specific weight-loss clinics you can attend. In general, there are some tips that might help your cat slim down

Get them moving! Most cats will love chasing a ‘wand’ toy (also known as a fishing rod toy) or chasing a ball - anything that increases activity can help. For older cats, speak to your vet first as they might need pain relief or other treatment to get moving if their joints are sore

Cut down the treats. We might love offering our cats something from our plate or a few treats here and there, but it increases your cat’s calorie intake

Cat eating from a puzzle feeder

Puzzle feeding. Not heard of it? It’s a way of encouraging your cat to work for their food, and helps keep them mentally active

Weighing dry food will help with portion control

Discuss a diet change with your vet. Specialised diets are available to help reduce hunger whilst making sure your cat is still getting much-needed vitamins

Weigh the food. How much are you really feeding? Even using a scoop or measuring cup, it’s easy to accidentally overfill. Get advice from your veterinary team on how much you should be feeding, and weigh the portion of food out with kitchen scales – it’s easy to add a few more biscuits by mistake

Once your cat has reached their target (ideal) weight, they may benefit from a ‘light’ or low-calorie food. These diets are designed for less active cats and contain less calories than normal maintenance foods. While it’s hard to see weight loss in a cat you see every day, it’s equally difficult to see the early stages of weight gain. Regular weight checks are important to ensure that your cat is not starting to gain weight again. These weight checks can be performed at your veterinary clinic, or at home using baby scales.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you’re worried your cat is obese, contact your veterinary team.

It’s dangerous for cats to lose weight too quickly as they can develop hepatic lipidosis, a potentially fatal liver disease where fat accumulates within the liver. A gradual, steady decrease in bodyweight is ideal, and it may take up to a year for a severely overweight cat to reach their ideal body condition. Your vet can develop a weight loss programme for your cat that combines a suitable feeding and exercise plan with careful monitoring.
