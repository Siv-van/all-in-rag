# How to encourage your cat to drink

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/how-to-encourage-your-cat-to-drink

---

Every cat has different preferences, so encouraging your cat to drink can be challenging. But, with time and patience, it’s achievable – once you understand what your cat prefers, you can make gradual changes to meet their needs.

## Which cats may benefit from an increased water intake?

#### There are some medical conditions and situations where it can be beneficial to encourage your cat to drink more water. Examples of this include

- Cats withkidney disease– these cats are more vulnerable to dehydration, which can worsen their kidney disease

- Cats withfeline idiopathic cystitis (FIC)– producing larger volumes of more dilute urine helps to prevent relapses

- Cats with urinary stones (uroliths) – increasing water intake can help prevent further stones from forming

- Cats withdiabetes mellitus– diabetic cats need to avoid becoming dehydrated

- Cats onnon-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) and other medications – maintaining your cat’s hydration when on certain medications can help to avoid complications

## How should I offer water to my cat?

Type of water bowl

Choose a bowl your cat will want to drink from.

Experiment with different types of bowls. In general, cats prefer glass and ceramic bowls to plastic ones

Most cats prefer a wide shallow bowl so they don’t need to put their head inside the bowl to drink, which can make them feel vulnerable. Additionally, cats don’t usually like their whiskers to touch the side of their water (or food) bowl

Some cats like drinking from glasses or tumblers (choose a non-breakable one if placing it on a raised surface)

Fill the water bowl to the brim – cats like to drink from a full container

Arthritic catsor those with reduced mobility will benefit from a bowl that’s raised from the ground, as crouching down may be uncomfortable

Number of water bowls

Have several water bowls available so your cat doesn’t need to go far to find one.

For example, one on each level of your home so your cat doesn’t need to go up or downstairs to find water

If you have more than one cat, you need to provide at least one water bowl per cat, plus one extra

Location of water bowls

#### Water bowls should be placed

Away from the food bowl: cats prefer not to drink next to their food

In a quiet location – away from doors that are heavily used, especially busy places in the home or machinery (eg, washing machines)

In easily accessible areas – away from walls, so that your cat can access their water bowl from all sides (having their back to a room can make them feel vulnerable)

Avoid double bowls (one side for water and the other for food). Your cat will drink more if food and water are offered in separate locations

Outdoor bowls should be placed near the entry and exit points of the home

Other water sources

#### Consider running water sources as some cats like to drink from moving water. This can include

- Pet water fountains

- A clean shower tray or bath with a small amount of water in it

Placing a ping pong ball inside a large diameter bowl full of water – some cats will enjoy playing with this and it can stimulate drinking as well as help to create some movement of the water, which many cats like

- Pet water fountains should be introduced gradually. You can find more information on how to do this in ourcat caregiver guide on increasing your cat’s water intake.

- Different types of water

- Consider experimenting with different types of water to see if your cat has a preference. Many cats do not like heavily fluorinated water.

#### Try

Tap (‘faucet’) water (as long as safe for human consumption)

Collected rainwater – many cats prefer to drink from puddles. This can be mimicked by collecting rainwater in a clean container

Still spring water (non-carbonated)

Filtered water

Other liquids to try

Water left over when a piece of chicken or fish has been cooked by poaching (with no stock or added salt)

Liquid from a can of tuna in spring water

Prawns, fish or meat liquidised in water to create a soup or broth

Tuna in spring water, frozen into ice cubes and added to water bowls

Flavoured products are available, specifically designed to increase a cat’s water intake

A small amount of water can be added to a cat’s normal wet food, but too much can deter a cat from eating and unbalance the diet so discuss with your veterinary team

Liquid to avoid

Salty liquids such as brine, stock or gravy

Milk (cow’s/sheep’s/goat’s milk cannot be fully digested by cats so it may cause diarrhoea)

Anything containing onions or onion powder eg, stock. Onions are poisonous to cats

Additional considerations

Offer water and other liquids at room temperature where possible (flavour is reduced in cold liquids)

Feed a wet diet (tins or pouches) rather than a dry diet – check with your vet before changing your cat’s diet

Adding extra water to wet food can also help increase water intake. Some cats will happily eat food that resembles soup (add water at room temperature rather than cold water)

If your cat will only eat dry food, try adding water to their food, but be aware some cats do not accept this change of texture and may be put off eating

Be persistent

Be gradual in making changes – cats don’t like sudden changes to their food or water

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if you think your cat is drinking too much or too little. In cats drinking too much (polydipsia), you may notice other signs such as weight loss, increased appetite or behaviour changes.
