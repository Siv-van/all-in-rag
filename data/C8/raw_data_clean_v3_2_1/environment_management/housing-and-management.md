# Housing and management

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/housing-and-management

---

Homing centres bring together many cats of unknown health or vaccination status, often in busy spaces with shared equipment and frequent movement. These conditions make the spread of infectious disease a serious risk. An outbreak can cause suffering for many cats and may even halt admissions until resolved

One of the biggest risks is staff or volunteers not fully understanding how disease spreads or why infection control matters — the same is often true in foster situations, where procedures may be less formal or absent altogether. Even simple actions, like washing hands between handling cats, can make a big difference but are sometimes overlooked. Some cats may appear healthy while carrying infections, so consistent hygiene and careful management are essential.

How diseases spread

Diseases can spread directly between cats through sneezing, grooming, sharing litter trays, or fighting, or indirectly, when people carry infection on their hands, clothing, or equipment. Clear, well-designed protocols are essential to prevent transmission. All staff and volunteers should understand bothwhythese measures matter andhowto apply them in their daily work.

Infectious diseases in homing centres

The types and prevalence of infectious diseases vary by country and region. Common examples include: cat flu (a group of respiratory diseases including feline herpesvirus and feline calicivirus), bordetella bronchiseptica, feline chlamydia, feline infectious enteritis (also known as feline parvovirus, feline panleukopenia virus or FPV), feline leukaemia virus (FeLV), feline immunodeficiency virus (FIV), feline infectious peritonitis (FIP), rabies — in countries where this is present, ringworm, infectious causes of diarrhoea, and parasites like worms or fleas

Reducing the risk

#### Effective disease control depends on three main areas

Design and structure of housing

Cat units should be easy to clean, well-drained, and separated by barriers to prevent contact or sneezing between cats. Temperatures must be well-regulated, and all accessible surfaces smooth and in good repair. Individual housing offers the best control; if group housing is used, keep groups small (ideally six or fewer) depending on available space and resources. An isolation area should always be available for sick cats.

Good management

Prevent unnecessary mixing of cats, especially from different sources. Identify illness early, begin treatment promptly, and limit spread through consistent cleaning and record-keeping. Use vaccination, parasite control, and quarantine or isolation as appropriate to your facility and the cats’ health. Limit staff movement between cat areas, wash hands and equipment between cats, and keep stays as short as possible to reduce exposure risk.

Cleaning and disinfection

The importance of cleanliness should never be underestimated - it protects cats’ health and reassures visitors. Tailor your cleaning regime to the infectious diseases common in your area, and ensure everyone follows it consistently.

A good routine removes all soiled bedding and litter, cleans visible dirt or waste, and washes surfaces with hot water and detergent before disinfecting. Always use products that are safe for cats and follow manufacturer instructions, including contact times. Food and water bowls, bedding, and uniforms should be washed separately in very hot water, and mops or high-pressure hoses should be avoided as they can spread infection. Regular handwashing between handling cats, and frequent litter tray cleaning, especially in group housing, are vital.

Between cats, cages should be fully cleaned and disinfected, but daily routines must balance hygiene with welfare. If no infection risk is present, usespot cleaning - tidying beds, scooping trays, and refreshing food and water while the cat remains in place - to preserve familiar scents that help cats feel secure.

Quarantine and isolation

Quarantine helps protect healthy cats from those that may be carrying disease. How it’s used depends on your set-up and resources. New arrivals with unknown vaccination or disease status should be quarantined where possible, depending on your facility’s design.

In purpose-built individual units, strict hygiene may make quarantine unnecessary. Where cats can touch or sneeze on each other, or share resources, an isolation period helps reduce risk. Multi-cat housing of cats from different origins, especially where litter trays are shared, increases risk significantly, so quarantine is particularly important.

Keep in mind that quarantine can extend confinement and require additional cat moves between pens, both of which are stressful. Wherever possible, balance infection control with each cat’s wellbeing.

Zoonotic disease

Some diseases can pass from cats to people. It’s important for everyone working with cats to understand potential zoonoses and how to minimise risk through good hygiene, protective clothing where appropriate, and clear protocols for cleaning and handling.

Disease control in foster care

Foster homes can provide valuable care for small numbers of cats, but infection risks must be managed. Avoid contact between foster cats and resident pets, screen for diseases where possible and treat forwormsandfleasbefore entry, and maintainvaccinationand hygiene standards equivalent to the homing centre.

Preventative health care

Preventing disease is always easier than treating it. Work with your veterinary team to maintain parasite control, vaccination, and other preventive care tailored to each cat’s health and circumstance

Record health and monitor

Keep clear records of each cat’s food and water intake, toileting habits, and behaviour. Good daily monitoring supports early detection and prompt treatment. Cats under veterinary care need clear written instructions for medication, check-ups, and progress tracking.

Neutering before homing

Population management is essential, so every cat or kitten that comes into your care should beneuteredbefore leaving for their new home. Veterinary clinics often neuter male and female kittens from pet homes at 4 months of age, and unowned cats from 8 weeks.

Key Points

Homing centres are high-risk environments for infectious disease, so prevention and early action are essential.

Effective design, good hygiene, and consistent management are the foundation of biosecurity.

Staff and volunteers must understand how disease spreads and follow clear, practical protocols.

Quarantine, vaccination, parasite control, and isolation protect both individual cats and the wider population.

Cleaning routines should balance hygiene with welfare - preserving familiar scents helps cats feel secure.

Record-keeping and communication with your veterinary team support early detection and effective treatment.

Zoonotic disease awareness and good hygiene protect people as well as cats.
