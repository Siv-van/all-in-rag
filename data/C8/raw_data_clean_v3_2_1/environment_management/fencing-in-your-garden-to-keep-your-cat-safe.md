# Fencing in your garden to keep your cat safe

Something that worries many cat owners is how to keep their pets safe and well without restricting them from leading a normal, happy outdoor life. The good news is that it’s possible to keep an active cat in a safe outdoor environment, so, in most cases, there’s no need to keep them solely indoors.

Cats that have always been kept indoors adapt well, provided they’re given plenty of companionship, attention, and things to stimulate them. However, with most cats, being confined indoors isn’t ideal, so a solution where they can have safe access to an outdoor space, as well as the house, is perfect.

There are many advantages to providing your cat with an enclosed outdoor space, with their safety high on the list.

## What can I do to keep my cat safe outdoors?

How you provide controlled outdoor access for your cat depends on how much, and what kind, of outdoor space you have. ## How do I choose between a fence and a purpose-built enclosure?

Fencing your garden

Fencing your garden, either completely or in part, can prevent cats from getting out and some fencing can be adapted to prevent outside cats from getting in. If trees overhang the fence, this can cause a problem as they may allow cats to climb and escape – pruning may help, or you may need to fence the tree itself so that your cat is unable to climb. If you live in a designated conservation area, if trees in your garden are subject to a preservation order or other regulatory control depending on where you live, you may need to seek permission before trimming or pruning any trees.

You’ll need a border fence of at least 1.8m (6ft) to make a garden cat-proof – which means uprights of at least 2.3m (7ft 6in) so that the fencing is securely fixed into the ground.

It’s a good idea to discuss plans for a new fence with neighbours to make sure there are no objections. In the UK, fences over 2m high (6ft 6in) will need to be discussed with the local authority planning department as planning permission may be needed. Permission is always needed for any fence or enclosure above 1m (3ft 3in) along a road frontage, and some housing developments may have legal covenants and/or local restrictions on fencing – always check your local government regulations before taking on the expense of building a new fence.

Once the fence or wall has been built to the required height, a horizontal section needs to be built at the top, jutting out at right angles into the garden for at least 0.5m (18in–2ft), creating an overhang. Alternatively, the overhang can be built at an upward 45° angle. Overhangs can be made from wood, wire, or you can buy purpose-built overhangs from specialist companies.

Purpose-built enclosure

The alternative to fencing is to create a purpose-built enclosure specifically for your cat. It’s important to make sure your cat is protected from the weather at all times of year, either by having access to the house through a cat flap or window, or by providing a purpose-built cat house within the enclosure itself. The enclosure can be open at the top, with an overhang like that used on fencing, or completely enclosed with a roof.

#### You’ll need to consider access to the enclosure, both for your cat and you. The two options are

A run attached to the house, so your cat can have equal access to the house and their outdoor space

If using a self-contained run (sometimes named a “catio”), make sure there’s some form of cat house within it, giving your cat somewhere to escape any bad weather. This house needs to be big enough for them to have key resources such as food and water bowls, beds, scratching posts, toys and litter trays if there’s nowhere in the outdoor run for them to toilet. It will also need to be large enough for you to access it easily to clean. A small, insulated garden shed works perfectly.

If the self-contained run is detached from the house, you’ll also need to consider how your cat will access it – will you carry them there daily? It may be easier to build a run either attached to the house or linked to it in some way, providing your cat with free access to your house, and preventing the need for a separate cat house within the run itself.

Whatever type of enclosure is created, it’s important to provide play areas for your cat. If the garden doesn’t already offer any, providing scratching posts, shelves and climbing frames made from tree branches will help keep your cat entertained.

Additional considerations

Maintenance

Wooden structures will need to be protected from weather damage – just check that any wood preservatives used don’t contain substances that are harmful to cats and ensure the product has dried fully before allowing your cat access to the area. Where wiring of any kind is used, check it regularly for signs of wear and tear or damage.

Security

In the same way that doors and windows of a house are locked when no one is home, any doors or gates providing people with access to the cat enclosure should have a security catch which can be closed and locked with a padlock. This is particularly relevant where the enclosure is attached to the house and where the cats are given access through an open window.
