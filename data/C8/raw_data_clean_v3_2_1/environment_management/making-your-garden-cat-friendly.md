# Making your garden cat friendly

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/making-your-garden-cat-friendly

---

If your cat has outdoor access, there are a few ways you can help them feel safer outside and enjoy spending time in the garden.

## What are the benefits of a cat friendly garden?

Providing a cat friendly garden can help your cat feel safe outdoors. In addition, providing different areas to explore encourages their natural instincts, keeps them active and entertained and benefits their mental wellbeing.

## How do I make my garden cat friendly?

Avoid vast open spaces outdoors

Cats can feel vulnerable when roaming across wide lawns or big open areas and will avoid them. Break up your garden into smaller areas by planting hedges or shrubs to give your cats some seclusion when exploring the garden.

Strategically positioned pots can even do the trick, particularly around the cat flap, as cats can feel exposed when first stepping out into the garden without any immediate seclusion nearby to allow them to survey the environment for danger.

Provide opportunities to explore

Cats love to climb and view their environment three-dimensionally. Providing vertical areas at various heights will instantly create more space for your cat. Tables, chairs, trellis, pergolas, wall tops, and shelves attached to fencing can all be used to create extra areas and walkways.

Provide opportunities for predatory behaviour

You might not be happy with your cat hunting prey, but there are other ways to provide an outlet for this natural behaviour outside. Try scatter feeding their dry food outside or encouraging your cat to usepuzzle feeders (just clear up any missed food after they have finished).

You can also try playing with your cat outside, as there is often much more space than inside! Drag a wand toy through the long grass and plants to allow your cat to stalk, chase and pounce! Senior cats can benefit from playtime in the garden too. Just be mindful to keep things gentle and adapt the game to suit their level of mobility. Even if your cat lies down to play, they will still benefit both physically and mentally from one-on-one playtime with you.

Planting lots of insect-attracting plants, such as colourful flowers, will also give the cat the opportunity to chase flies and butterflies.

Provide safety and shelter

As cats can feel vulnerable outdoors, make sure you provide secure places to give your cat spaces to retreat to or hide under if they feel threatened. Items such as strategically placed garden furniture, purpose-built cat houses or elevated platforms are great choices to give your cat a safe place to sit and survey their surroundings.

If you can, try to create an outdoor space that allows your cat to use it in all weather. Cats love to bask in the sun, so make sure to provide resting spots in sunny places. Cats will also appreciate shade when it gets too hot and sheltered places when the weather is wet. A cat house with a roof can provide an opportunity for an enclosed warm and dry area to escape from adverse weather – your cat may even rest on top of the roof in warm weather, after all, cats are the ultimate sun worshippers!

You may be on the fence aboutkeeping your cat indoors or allowing outdoor access, whether this is free access to the outdoors, containing them in an enclosed garden or allowing them a smaller section of the garden. There’s no right or wrong answer when it comes to either keeping your cat indoors only or allowing them to explore outdoors. It all depends on you and your cat’s individual situation and often there isn’t a clear answer as to what’s best – but instead a careful consideration of the pros and cons of each option. It's also important to be aware that in some countries, there are laws preventing cats from free-roaming outdoors.

If you decide to allow your cat outdoors but want to restrict where they can go, there are a number of options for fencing in your garden you can explore.

Opportunity for chemical communication

Cats naturally spend time depositing chemical messages outside, particularly via scratching, so it is a good idea to provide them with materials on which they can do this. Wooden posts and tree trunks can make ideal scratching sites, while shrubbery can provide ideal sites for natural urine marking (spraying) behaviour. You might also see them using gate posts, garden furniture and planters as sites to facial rub.

Opportunity for drinking

Some cats prefer to drink from outdoor water sources, like a clean container that collects rainwater. Therefore, it can be beneficial to provide water bowls and clean containers outside – even a water fountain can encourage drinking. Make sure these are placed in positions within the garden where your cat feels safe and secure.

Places for your cat to interact with you

Try to create at least one area in the garden where people can sit, as this will provide an opportunity for social interactions for friendly cats that enjoy interactions with people. This can be anything as simple as a seating area in a sunny spot to something as elaborate as a summerhouse.

Toileting facilities

Many people don’t provide any toileting facilities for their cats outdoors but would rather not find that their cat has toileted in the flower bed. A purpose-built latrine area will help prevent your cat from toileting in the flower beds and allows you to monitor your cat’s output, which can be a useful indicator of their health. Sand or soil (with good drainage) works well, and daily scooping is still required. Choose a location where the cat feels safe and secure – trellis, fencing and/or plants can be used to create a secluded area separate from the rest of the garden.

Cat friendly plants

Try planting some greenery especially for your cat to enjoy! Catnip and catmint are favourites with many cats and can be easily grown in the garden. Cats like to lie and roll in these plants as well as bite and chew the actual plants – these plants also create a catnip response because they contain a chemical called nepetalactone that can excite many (but not all) cats, which triggers a sense of euphoria and playful behaviour. Cat grass (common oats) is another popular option and is easily grown from seed. Many cats enjoy chewing the grass.

However, as much as our cats may enjoy exploring and chewing on plants in the garden, there are some plants that could actually cause your cat harm and should be avoided. For a list of garden plants that are potentially harmful to cats, read our article onpoisonous plants.

Additional Considerations

While it’s important to make your garden cat friendly, you may also want to considerhow to keep neighbouring cats off your garden.
