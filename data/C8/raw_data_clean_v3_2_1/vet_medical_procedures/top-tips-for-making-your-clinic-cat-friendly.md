The little things matter, and we believe that in every clinic there is at least one thing that can be changed for the benefit of cats. Browse the sections to see our suggestions on what you can do to make your clinic more cat friendly.

## The Waiting Room

Cats will inevitably be anxious and stressed in the waiting room as they are outside their normal territory and will feel particularly vulnerable. Here are some ideas of what you can do in your waiting room to make it more cat friendly.

Prevent visual contact between cats and dogs by providing areas separated by a sight barrier to prevent cats seeing dogs and vice versa.

Separate waiting areas for dogs, and cats and small animals

Some clinics separate the two areas with a temporary or semi-temporary barrier or partition – for example, a wooden or metal barrier, or even a barrier composed of densely potted plants.

In some situations, a clinic may be able to have an entirely separate cat waiting room – this is an ideal situation but still needs additional thought about the layout

If separation is not possible in the waiting room, consider blocks of consultations for cats that are at separate times from those offered to dogs.

Cats also need to be protected from each other – they are not naturally social animals so direct eye contact between cats should be avoided. Sneeze barriers help prevent spread of infectious diseases. Provide a supply of clean blankets or towels that an owner can be asked to place over the carrier, ideally sprayed with synthetic feline pheromones.

Provide ‘cat parking’ to keep carriers off the floor and make cats feel less vulnerable. As a minimum, cats in their carriers must be placed on a seat alongside the owner. Having a strong shelf or unit on which to place carriers, with solid vertical barriers in between, is ideal and this should be 1m or more off the ground Make sure there is clear signage and clients are verbally directed too

Ideally the cat waiting area should also be situated in a way that minimises passing by dogs when cats come in and out of the clinic or move into a consulting room.

Take measures to keep dogs quiet in the waiting room, and perhaps have a separate room for noisy dogs or ask owners of noisy dogs to wait outside the clinic until they can be seen.

If a cat is becoming particularly stressed in the waiting room, having an empty consultation room into which the cat and owner can go is an advantage, but this should not be the only way of separating cats and dogs in the waiting area.

Have a low-level reception desk – or an area that is at a lower level – to provide an opportunity for cat carriers to be placed on the reception desk rather than on the floor when clients book in/out. Alternatively, a shelf or table can be provided next to the reception desk to place the cat carrier on, make sure cat owners are encouraged to use this.

Synthetic feline pheromone diffusers are a useful addition to the waiting room – if used along with other measures to make a waiting room cat friendly they may help reduce cat stress.

Have a display board in the waiting room with pictures of the staff, who they are and what they do. It is a good idea to also have this information on a clinic website. It should be clear who the ‘Cat Advocate (s)’ in the clinic are. Clients should know they can approach them for further information or advice if desired.

Make relevant cat healthcare information available to clients such as our client leaflets

## The Consulting Room

#### In a Cat Friendly Clinic, the consulting room also requires some careful thought. Points to consider include

Ideally, there should be at least one consultation room exclusively or primarily dedicated to feline use – this helps avoid the smell of dogs in the room and so may reduce anxiety for the cat. If this is not possible, ensure that the room and equipment is thoroughly cleaned between patients.

Ensure that the table used for consultation has a non-slip surface – this may be achieved by placing a rubber mat or towel/blanket on the table.

Equipment suitable for cats should be available (e.g, a cat or paediatric stethoscope, ophthalmoscope, thermometer and accurate weighing scales).

An indirect blood pressure monitor should be available for measuring blood pressure in a conscious cat – a portable Doppler or oscillometric blood pressure monitor is suitable for this purpose.

It is important that consultation times are of sufficient length for cats – allowing the cat time to acclimatise to the room and to settle down is important. We recommend a minimum of 10 minutes (ideally 15 minutes), more for cats undergoing procedures or those known to be anxious in the clinic.

Allow the cat to get out of the carrier of its own accord. If the cat choses to remain in the relative safety of its carrier, carefully remove the top of the carrier and examine the cat in the base. Forcibly removing the cat from the carrier must be avoided.

Synthetic feline pheromone diffusers are also a useful addition to the consulting room

#### Important considerations include

Some people are naturally more empathetic towards cats than others – try to use this to your advantage. If you have vets and/or nurses and technicians who enjoy cats and are more empathetic with them, use these staff members primarily for your feline patients.

Although some people are better and more empathetic with cats than others, everyone can learn and improve their cat knowledge and cat interaction skills – make sure this happens in your clinic.

Ensuring that all staff are welcoming to cats and treat them with gentleness and respect. Cats respond better to gentle handling than to heavy restraint.

Staff should be able to recognise when cats are displaying fear and anxiety, and act appropriately (for example, by leaving the cat in a quiet environment for a while to calm down).

The clinic environment should be as quiet as possible, and consideration should be given to odours and bright lights that may be stressful for the cat.

Avoid direct eye contact with cats as far as possible as this may be perceived as threatening.

Allow the cat to initiate contact when possible (for example by placing your hand near the cat and allowing it to sniff and rub your hand).

Use towels or blankets, when needed, so the cat can hide and be gently restrained while being examined.

Make sure the cat is placed on a surface where it can grip properly, will feel secure, and will not slip.

If a cat becomes fearful or anxious during an examination, take a break and allow the cat to settle down again.

Hospitalisation facilities

Hospitalisation criteria are an important part of the Cat Friendly Clinic programme, as the welfare of cats during a stay in a veterinary clinic is vital. The hospital ward, hospital cages and the cage furnishings and layout are all critically important to creating a calm environment and reducing stress for a cat staying in a clinic.

Hospital ward

Ideally, a separate cat ward should be available where only cats are housed. If a separate room is not possible then dogs and cats should be separated from each other as far as possible within the hospital ward, and ideally barriers used to prevent cats seeing dogs and vice versa – the best solution is to have some form of barrier between a dog and cat section of the ward.

Regardless of whether there is a separate cat ward, cats should be hospitalised in a quiet, relaxed, comfortable environment. Noise should be kept to a minimum and very bright harsh lighting should be avoided unless there are times when this is absolutely needed.

Cats should not be able to see each other (or other animals), again to reduce stress. A configuration where there is a single bank of cages on a wall is ideal (with no cages opposite).

If cages do face each other, to prevent aerosol spread of infection there should be a minimum gap of 2m between the closest parts of the cage. Alternatively, a solid opaque barrier can be used between the cages to act as both a sight and sneeze barrier.

Hospital cages for cats

It is important that hospital cages for cats are secure and escape-proof. They should be constructed of solid material that is easily cleaned and disinfected. Plastic and fibreglass are warmer to the touch than metal, although insulated metal cages can overcome this problem.

Ideally, the walls, floor and ceiling of the cages should be solid and opaque to provide an effective sight and sneeze barrier. Cages constructed of metal mesh/bars can be used, but only if a clean, easily disinfectable cover is used. A wire mesh floor is never acceptable – this must always be solid.

The front of the cage may be metal mesh, glass or plastic but should allow easy vision of the interior of the cage. Glass or clear plastic fronts have the advantage of providing a sneeze barrier and the lower half of the door can be translucent providing some sight barrier for the cat which may reduce stress.

Door latches can often be very noisy as these are frequently constructed of metal. Providing a plastic cover or stopper to reduce noise and allow quiet opening and closing of the cage is highly desirable.

The cage size is an important criteria – a larger size allows more flexibility in the arrangement, a greater separation of resources (bed, water bowl, food bowl and litter tray), greater freedom and choice of the cat, and therefore greatly helps to reduce stress during hospitalisation.

Comfortable soft bedding should always be provided – newspaper or disposable paper can be used to line the floor of a cage if required, but soft bedding (blankets, towels, acrylic bedding etc.) should be used over most of the floor to provide a soft comfortable surface.

A resting area, food and water bowl and litter tray should be placed as far apart as possible in the cage.

Making provision for the cat to hide is a very important welfare consideration – a ‘sack bed’, an ‘igloo bed’ or even just a cardboard box with appropriate high sides are all examples of this. If a cat has the ability to hide in its cage this will significantly reduce stress and enhance welfare.

Ideally, in addition to having somewhere to hide, providing facilities for a cat to sit up high will also improve welfare. This can be in the form of a shelf in the cage, a box that the cat can hide inside of or sit on top of, or perhaps even a cat carrier so the cat can go inside to hide or sit on top of.
