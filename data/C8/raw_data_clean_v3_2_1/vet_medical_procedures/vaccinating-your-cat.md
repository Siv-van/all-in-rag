# Vaccinating your cat

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/vaccinating-your-cat

---

Vaccines are usually given by an injection under the skin, although sometimes may be given as drops into the eyes or nose. They are designed to protect against a specific infectious disease by stimulating an immune response that will protect your cat if they are later exposed to the infection.

## What do vaccinations contain?

#### Vaccinations may contain

- Live organisms (‘modified live vaccines’) – this is where the infectious agent (organism) has been modified so that it won’t cause disease but can replicate for a short time after the vaccine has been given to induce a good immune response

- Killed organisms (killed or inactivated vaccines) – this is where the organism has been killed and is then combined with other agents/chemicals to help induce a good immune response

- Recombinant vaccines– this is a newer type of vaccine where parts of one organism may be incorporated into another organism (that has been altered and will not cause disease), which may then be used to vaccinate a cat

- All vaccines must undergo rigorous safety and efficacy testing before they’re licensed for use in cats by regulatory authorities. When used appropriately, and as recommended, they provide crucial protection for cats against several diseases.

- ‘Homoeopathic vaccines’ should never be used – they have no scientific basis and cannot provoke the specific immune response that is needed to provide protection. Only use vaccines that your veterinary team recommends.

## What diseases can and should I vaccinate my cat against?

The availability of vaccines will vary between different countries, because some diseases are not present everywhere and because vaccines are not necessarily licensed (and therefore available) in every country.

#### The most commonly available vaccines are used to protect against the following infections

- Feline panleukopenia virus (FPV, feline infectious enteritis; feline parvovirus)

- Feline herpesvirus (FHV, cat flu)

- Feline calicivirus (FCV, cat flu)

- Feline leukaemia virus (FeLV)

- Rabies

- Chlamydia felis

- Bordetella bronchiseptica

Other vaccines are available against feline immunodeficiency virus (FIV) and feline infectious peritonitis (FIP) in some countries.

## Core and non-core vaccines

Vaccines can be divided into core and non-core vaccines. Core vaccines are considered essential for all cats (including indoor-only cats) because of the widespread and/or severe nature of the diseases being protected against. Non-core vaccines are only given to cats if there’s a genuine risk of exposure to the infection and if vaccination provides good protection. Decisions on whether non-core vaccines are needed can be based on a cat’s age, lifestyle and contact with other cats. Always discuss which vaccinations your cat needs with your veterinary team, as they can make recommendations based on your cat’s individual circumstances.

## Core vaccines

Feline panleukopenia virus (also known as feline parvovirus or feline infectious enteritis) is a severe and often fatal cause of haemorrhagic gastroenteritis. Vaccination against this highly contagious virus is very effective. The virus can survive for long periods in the environment, so vaccination is an effective way to protect cats.

Vaccines for feline herpesvirus (FHV) andfeline calicivirus (FCV) are always combined, as these two viruses are the main causes ofupper respiratory tract infections in cats (cat flu). As infection is so common and can be quite severe (especially in younger cats), vaccination is important for all cats. Although vaccination doesn’t always prevent infection, it will help greatly reduce the severity of the disease if a vaccinated cat becomes infected.

Although more common in dogs (and more commonly passed from dogs to people than cats to people), cats can be infected with rabies and can be a source of infection to people.

For these reasons, where rabies is present in a country or region, cats should be vaccinated. Vaccination is very effective in preventing disease and may be mandated by law in some countries. Rabies vaccinations may also be required for travel.

Other vaccines are considered as ‘non-core’ and are used when vaccination will provide valuable protection for an individual cat.

Feline leukaemia virus (FeLV) is a disease that can be spread through fighting, mutual grooming, and sharing of food/water bowls and litter trays. Most persistently infected cats will die because of their infection.

FeLV vaccines are available and are effective in protecting cats. Generally, cats that go outside and may come across other cats of unknown status may be at risk of being exposed to FeLV, and vaccinating such cats can be very valuable (although the risks will vary between different regions).

Chlamydia felisis a type of bacteria that mainly causes conjunctivitis (inflammation of the membranes inside the eyelids) in cats. Affected cats can be treated successfully, but vaccination may help in some circumstances as part of a control programme in an infected household.

Bordetella bronchisepticais another bacterial infection that can cause cat flu in cats. It’s not as common as FHV or FCV, but can be a problem, especially in stressed cats and cats from large colonies. It can be treated successfully, and vaccination isn’t necessary for most cats, but can help to protect cats in colonies with repeated outbreaks of disease.

Feline immunodeficiency virus (FIV) vaccination is available in some countries. This virus is quite common among cats, especially cats that go outdoors and are involved in fights (infection is mainly spread through cat bites).

There are many different strains of the FIV virus, and it’s not entirely clear how well the available vaccine protects against all these different strains, but studies suggest that it provides a valuable amount of protection for cats at risk of exposure. Vaccination may affect the results of tests for FIV, so always ensure records of vaccination are kept and the veterinary team are informed that your cat is vaccinated.

## How frequently should my cat be vaccinated?

All kittens should receive their core vaccinations and any others recommended by your veterinary team. The initial vaccine course is often started at 8-9 weeks of age, with a second injection 3-4 weeks later. It’s now common to recommend a third vaccination (especially for FPV) at 16-20 weeks of age to ensure the kitten is protected. Earlier vaccination may be recommended in some circumstances where the risk of infection is very high eg, rehoming centres.

A first booster vaccination should be given 12 months later (this is occasionally given sooner) to ensure a good level of continued protection. However, after that, booster vaccinations may only be necessary every 1-3 years depending on the vaccine, disease and risk to the individual cat. Your veterinary team will advise how frequently your cat needs vaccinating, and with which vaccines and can take advice from international guidelines such as those produced by the World Small Animal Veterinary Association (WSAVA).

Other vaccines

#### Several other vaccines are available in certain countries. The effectiveness of these vaccines should be considered carefully, and none of these are ‘core’ (or essential) vaccines. These include

- Feline infectious peritonitis

- Microsporum Canis

- Giardia lamblia

## FAQs

## Are there any potential problems with vaccinating my cat?

Adverse effects from vaccines are very rare. The most common side effects are mild, and include lethargy/tiredness, eating less or tenderness at the injection site - usually lasting no longer than a few days. More noticeable side effects may include vomiting, diarrhoea, lameness (limping), high temperature, signs of a respiratory tract infection, or lumps at the site of injection (usually, the scruff of the neck). Consult your vet if you are worried about complications of vaccination.

Some vaccines may be given in different locations, such as the tail or lower legs – all areas that have been injected should be monitored.

In very rare cases (around less than 1 in 20,000 vaccines given), a feline injection site sarcoma (FISS) could develop. This is a Feline injection-site sarcoma, a type of malignant (cancerous) tumour which can develop at the site of any injection. After your cat receives any vaccine, monitor the injection site and if swelling persists for more than 2-3 weeks or appears to be getting larger, get it checked immediately by your vet.

Glossary of terms

Gastroenteritis– inflammation of the stomach and intestines (haemorrhagic gastroenteritis is a condition that causes vomiting and diarrhoea containing blood)

Conjunctivitis– inflammation of the conjunctiva (pink membrane lining the inside of the eyelids and part of the eye)
