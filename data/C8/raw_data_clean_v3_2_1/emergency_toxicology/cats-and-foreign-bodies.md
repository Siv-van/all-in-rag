# Cats and foreign bodies

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/cats-and-foreign-bodies

---

The assumption that cats are fussy eaters is no guarantee that they won’t eat something they shouldn’t, and the ingestion of foreign bodies is actually quite common. It’s more likely that young cats, who are generally more curious, will ingest foreign material, and cats kept solely indoors may be more likely to eat something they shouldn’t if they don’t have adequate stimulation in their environment.

## What is a foreign body?

A foreign body is any material (a non-food item) your cat swallows that is not a normal part of their diet. Ingestion of a foreign body can lead to serious health problems in your cat, and you should always contact your veterinary clinic as soon as possible if you believe your cat has eaten something they shouldn’t.

## What should I do if I think my cat has swallowed a foreignbody?

#### If you believe that your cat has swallowed a foreign body, it’s important to speak to your veterinary team. You shouldn’t assume things will ‘pass through’ as they often don’t. Some signs that your cat may have swallowed a foreign body that is causing an issue include

- Refusing food

- Lethargy/tiredness

- Retching

- Nausea

- Vomiting

- Diarrhoea

- Abdominal pain

Cats often hide illness well, so your cat may just seem to sleep more and be less keen to interact. If you notice any of the above signs, contact your veterinary team as soon as possible, as early treatment is important.

## What types of foreign bodies are commonly seen in cats?

#### Some of the foreign bodies that are commonly ingested by cats include

- Needles and thread, string and rubber bands

String and string-like objects such as needles and thread, wool/yarn, string found around meat and tinsel (which you may hear referred to as ‘linear foreign bodies’) are some of the most common, and if ingested, they can cause the intestines to bunch up and make your cat very unwell, usually requiring surgery to remove the item.

Hair bands and rubber bands are also commonly ingested, along with fibres and stuffing from inside cat toys, carpet fibres, ribbon, dental floss and blind cords.

Small objects

Small round objects seem irresistible to some cats, but unfortunately, they are the perfect size to block their intestines, requiring surgical removal. Coins, buttons, earplugs, fruit stones, nut shells, bottle tops and almonds are not uncommon.

Bones

Bones can also cause a problem for bin-raiding cats, or cats with access to kitchen worktops, as cooked chicken bones, in particular, can be very sharp and lodge in the intestines, causing severe illness.

Additional considerations

Being aware of the types of objects that can cause issues is important, as you can ensure these items are secured safely around the home in areas your cat cannot access. Giving your cat safe items to play with can also help prevent curiosity from getting the better of them. Read our article on making your home cat friendly for further advice. ## FAQs

## How is a foreign body diagnosed?

If your vet suspects your cat has a foreign body (based on your cat’s history and a physical examination), they may perform x-rays and/or an ultrasound to confirm a diagnosis.

## How is a foreign body treated?

Surgery is commonly required to remove foreign bodies, but your veterinary team will guide you on the best form of treatment, based on what your cat has swallowed and where in the body it is.
