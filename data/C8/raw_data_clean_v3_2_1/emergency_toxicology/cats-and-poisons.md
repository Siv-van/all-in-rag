# Cats and poisons

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/cats-and-poisons

---

There’s a misconception that because cats can be fussy eaters, they’re less likely to be poisoned than dogs. However, because cats are curious by nature, will groom any substance off their coats and swallow it, and may have access to the outdoors, accidental poisonings are not as uncommon as you may think.

When cats eat something they shouldn’t, their small body size, ability to hide (so that signs of poisoning aren’t immediately obvious), and the fact that their livers are unable to break down certain chemicals, means when they do ingest something toxic, recovery is more difficult.

## What are the common poisons that affect cats?

#### Often, the cause of poisoning in a cat is unknown. However, there are several substances in and around your home that are toxic to cats

Household products

Cleaning and hygiene productssuch as bleach, cleaning fluids and creams, deodorants, deodorisers, disinfectants and patio cleaners (particularly those containing quaternary ammonium compounds, such as benzalkonium chloride and didecyldimethylammonium chloride [DDAC]), laundry capsules and concentrated liquids, furniture and metal polishes. Detergents can cause burns to the skin and paws, particularly if not removed promptly

#### Human medicinessuch as laxatives, aspirin, paracetamol, ibuprofen, minoxidil, psoriasis creams, 5-fluorouracil and antidepressants. Paracetamol (acetaminophen) is sometimes given by owners who are worried their cat is in pain, however, it’s highly toxic and even a small dose is enough to cause severe illness and, in many cases, it can be fatal. If you’re worried your cat is in pain, always make an appointment with your vet for a full check-up and appropriate medication if required. You can learn more about paracetamol poisoning by watching our video

Motoring productssuch as antifreeze, brake fluid, petrol, and windscreen washer fluid. Antifreeze often contains ethylene glycol (ethanediol) or methanol, which are toxic to cats (also found in car screen washes and de-icers). Ingestion of even the smallest amount of ethylene glycol can lead to kidney failure and death

Beauty productssuch as hair dyes, nail polish, nail polish remover, skin and hair treatments containing glycolic acid and suntan lotion

Decorating materialsand DIY products such as paint, varnish, paint remover, and white spirit (turpentine substitute). These can be poisonous if groomed from the coat or can cause burns, blisters, and irritation to the skin and footpads or severe irritation in the mouth

Miscellaneous household/garden itemssuch as weed killer, reed diffusers, rodent control products (such as alphachloralose, cholecalciferol, anticoagulant rodenticides), chocolate and essential oils (such as tea tree oil)

If you have any of these products in your home, always ensure they are stored safely out of reach, ideally in closed cupboards (as cats will often push things off shelves), and anyspillages are cleaned up immediatelyand carefully. Never give cats products intended for people, unless instructed otherwise by your vet. If you’re concerned your cat has come into contact with any of the substances listed, contact your veterinary team immediately. The sooner veterinary treatment is received, the better their chances of survival.

Pesticides

#### Commons pesticides that are toxic to cats include

- Insecticides (including some flea and tick treatments intended for dogs) such as organophosphates and pyrethroids

- Molluscicides (slug and snail killers) such as metaldehyde and methiocarb

- Rodenticides (rat and mouse killers) such as brodifacoum, difenacoum, chlorophacinone, coumatetralyl, alphachloralose and cholecalciferol

Rodenticides are the most common pesticides seen in cat poisonings, usually because the cat has eaten poisoned prey (rats and mice). The other pesticides are normally safe for cats when used at their correct working strength, if cats are excluded from the treated area until the spray has dried. Always follow instructions carefully.

If you have any of these products in your home, always ensure they are stored safely out of reach, ideally in closed cupboards (as cats will often push things off shelves), and anyspillages are cleaned up immediatelyand carefully. When using pesticide sprays or herbicides in the garden, keep your cat in until they have dried.

Never give cats products intended for people, unless instructed otherwise by your vet. If you’re concerned your cat has come into contact with any of the substances listed, contact your veterinary team immediately. The sooner treatment is received, the better their chances of survival.

Dog flea treatment products

Permethrinis found in many spot-on flea and tick products for dogs. Poisoning occurs when cats are mistakenly treated with dog flea products, rather than a product specific to cats, or after close contact with other animals treated with the product.

Dog flea products should never be used on cats. If you’ve accidentally used a dog flea product on your cat, or if your cat has been in contact with a dog recently treated with a permethrin-based product, contact your veterinary team immediately.

Plants

There are many commonly grown plants, both houseplants and garden plants, that are toxic to cats or that can cause skin irritation. Most cats that go outside don’t eat poisonous plants but will nibble grass and other herbs. However, cats kept permanently indoors without access to grass may try eating other things, either out of boredom or to try and access some plant material.

Curious kittens can also chew on plants, and because they’re little, a relatively small amount can cause toxicity. Some houseplants, such as the Dumb Cane (Dieffenbachia), are so poisonous that it’s best to avoid them altogether where there are small children or pets in the house.

It’s not just growing plants that can be a problem – cut flowers such aslilies (plants of the Liliumspecies) are highly toxic – not just the leaves, but the flowers and the pollen, too. Less than one leaf eaten, or a small amount of pollen licked off the coat by a cat is enough to cause kidney failure andurgent veterinary treatmentis required, or it can prove fatal. Always check flower labels for warnings of toxicity to animals. A comprehensive list of potentially harmful plants can be foundhere.

## How can a cat be poisoned?

#### Cats can accidentally be poisoned in several ways

Directly ingesting a toxic substance, either by eating it or by eating poisoned prey (for example, a mouse that’s eaten rat bait)

Swallowing poisons while grooming contaminated fur

Absorbing toxins through the skin (particularly the paws) or where a substance has been splashed on the skin

Breathing in the poison

## What should I do if I think my cat has been poisoned?

Remove your cat from the source of the poison and isolate them from other animals

Contact your veterinary team for adviceimmediately; make sure you know when, where, and how the poisoning occurred. If possible, take the packaging, plant, or substance with you to the veterinary clinic

If the poison is on their skin, coat, or paws, try to prevent your cat from grooming itself further and wash them thoroughly with mild shampoo and water under instruction from your veterinary team

Poisonous substances can be ingested from the fur when the cat grooms themselves

## My cat has got something ‘chemical’ on their coat, what should I do?

It may be possible to treat your cat at home - but only if the contamination is mild and confined to their coat. Treatment aims to prevent further contamination and should be discussed with your veterinary team. Often washing and clipping is best done at the veterinary clinic as it can be stressful for your cat.

Your cat’s collar should be removed as it may also have been contaminated, and some flea collars contain chemicals which may be harmful to sick cats. To remove chemicals from your cat’s coat, it’s best to clip off contaminated hair and then wash your cat in warm soapy water.

It’s important to remove as much of the contamination as possible before washing because washing can increase the absorption of some chemicals. Dry your cat thoroughly. If they’ve got an oily or sticky substance on their coat, rub it with clean, warm vegetable oil (eg, olive oil, sunflower oil, margarine) - essentially, you’re removing oil with oil. Then wash the oil off with detergent.

If you’re worried your cat may have groomed off some of the toxin, contact your veterinary clinic. Your cat should also be kept in a quiet room for 24 hours and monitored closely for any signs of illness.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team immediately if you think your cat may have come into contact with a poisonous substance, or if they’re showing any of the signs listed above. Your vet will let you know how urgently your cat needs to be seen and if there’s anything you can do to help your cat before any potential veterinary treatment is given.
