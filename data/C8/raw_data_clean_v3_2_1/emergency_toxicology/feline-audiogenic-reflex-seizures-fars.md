# Feline audiogenic reflex seizures (FARS)

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/feline-audiogenic-reflex-seizures-fars

---

The name given to this syndrome is feline audiogenic reflex seizures (FARS), with ‘reflex’ referring to the fact that the seizures are brought on by a stimulus, and ‘audiogenic’ referring to the fact that this stimulus is a sound.

However, it’s also been dubbed ‘Tom and Jerry syndrome’ after the cartoon character Tom, who has a strong startle reflex and often reacts with involuntary jerks to sounds.

## What are the signs of feline audiogenic reflex seizures?

#### There are three types of seizure associated with FARS

generalised tonic-clonic seizures

myoclonic seizures and

absence seizures

Generalised tonic-clonic seizures

A generalised tonic-clonic seizure is what most people think of as a ‘classic’ seizure.

#### Signs include

- Falling to the ground/collapse

- Shaking/paddling of the legs

- Chomping and chewing

- Foaming at the mouth

- Sometimes, passing urine and faeces

- The seizure usually lasts no more than a few minutes, but your cat may be very disorientated afterwards.

- Myoclonic seizures

Myoclonic seizures often only last for a fraction of a second, and many cats will appear to remain conscious throughout. Signs include brief, involuntary muscle jerks or spasms. In almost 95% of the FARS cats studied, this was the most common type of seizure seen.

Absence seizures

The third type of seizure is called an absence seizure (sometimes called apetit mal seizure). With absence seizures, your cat may simply lose awareness of their surroundings for up to 20 seconds or so. They may stare vacantly into space and not respond. Absence seizures were only occasionally seen in the FARS study, but they can be difficult to spot at times.

## What causes feline audiogenic reflex seizures?

It’s unclear how the condition develops, but it’s more common in older cats and certain breeds, so there may be an inherited component. The FARS study found that cats typically developed the syndrome from around 15 years of age, and around half of the cats were also found to be deaf or partially deaf. Loss of hearing can be part of getting older, and usually, the ability to hear higher to middle frequencies is lost first. In cats, there’s reason to believe that higher-pitched, ‘ultrasonic frequencies’ are less affected, which may explain why high-pitched sounds become more startling to them, and that can trigger a seizure.

Triggers

#### The sounds that trigger seizures vary between individual cats, and a huge variety of different sounds were identified as triggers. Some of the more common trigger noises included

- Crinkling tin foil

- Chinking or tapping of glass

- Crinkling of paper or plastic bags

- Tapping on a computer keyboard or clicking on a mouse

- Clinking of coins or keys

- Hammering of a nail

- Clicking of an owner’s tongue

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat is showing any of the signs listed above, or you’ve noticed any change in their overall behaviour or health, contact your veterinary team for further advice.

How your vet will diagnose FARS

Your vet will take a detailed history from you, including the signs your cat is showing, when the episode happened and how long it lasted. It can be helpful if you’re able to video your cat, so your vet can see exactly what happens to your cat during an episode. Your vet will perform a full examination of your cat and may take a blood sample for testing. In some cases, your vet may refer your cat for an MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) or CT (computed tomography) scan.

## How are feline audiogenic reflex seizures treated?

Phenobarbitone is the drug most used to treat different forms ofepilepsy in cats. While it may help some cats with FARS, alternative medications such as levetiracetam may be prescribed.

In some situations, the triggering noise can be avoided, and cats may not need medication. Your veterinary team will discuss treatment options with you and may also refer you for advice from a specialist veterinary neurologist.

## What’s the outlook for cats diagnosed with FARS?

There’s no cure for FARS, but the frequency of seizures can be reduced with the correct treatment. By working closely with your veterinary team, there’s a good prognosis when it comes to controlling the seizures and keeping your cat comfortable.
