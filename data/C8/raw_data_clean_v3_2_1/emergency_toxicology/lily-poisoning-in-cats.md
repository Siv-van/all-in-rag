# Lily poisoning in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/lily-poisoning-in-cats

---

Lilies are attractive, fragrant flowers which commonly feature in bouquets and flower arrangements. However, many people are unaware of the danger they pose to cats.

Eating any part of the lily – flowers, leaves, stem or pollen (including licking it off their fur when grooming) – is extremely dangerous to cats and can cause kidney damage and even death.

Pollen-free lilies are often marketed as a ‘safe’ option for cat owners. However, as all parts of the lily are toxic, not just the pollen, a pollen-free lily will not be safe for cats, and the advice for cat owners toavoid liliesremains the same.

Lily poisoning in cats explained

Lilies contain an unknown toxin that makes eating even the smallest amount of any part of the plant – flowers, leaves, stem or pollen – extremely dangerous to cats. This includes licking the pollen off their coat or drinking water from a vase containing lilies. Once ingested, the toxin causes severe damage to the kidneys, which can cause the kidneys to fail and can even result in death.

## Which type of lily causes a problem?

Lilies, specifically all species of Lilium (true lily) and Hemerocallis (day lily), are poisonous to cats.

It’s important to be aware that many plants have lily in their name such as lily of the valley (Convallaria majalis), peace lily (Spathiphyllum species) and calla or arum lily (Zantedeschia aethiopica).

## What are the signs of lily poisoning in cats?

#### Signs of poisoning occur within 1-6 hours and include

- Drooling

- Vomiting

- Loss of appetite/refusing food

- Lethargy/tiredness

- Weakness

If you think your cat has come into contact with lilies, seek veterinary advice immediately. The toxic effects are extremely severe and develop quickly, so ensuring your cat receives treatment as soon as possible is crucial to improving their chance of recovery.

## What causes lily poisoning in cats?

Cats are poisoned by lilies by eating any part of the plant. The reason why they are so susceptible to lily poisoning isn’t known. Ingesting less than one leaf, part of a flower, pollen, or even drinking water from a vase holding cut lilies can be fatal.

## Are all cats at risk?

Indoor cats, especially kittens, may be more likely to interact with flowers and floral arrangements as they make an interesting addition to an otherwise predictable home that might lack plant life. However, it’s also easier to control the presence of lilies inside the home. For free-roaming cats, removing lilies from the house and garden is no guarantee that they won’t come into contact with them elsewhere.

Prevention is better than cure, so the best way to keep cats safe from lily poisoning is to ensure they can’t come into contact with them – if you have a cat at home, don’t ever allow lilies into your home or garden. For free-roaming cats, knowing if they have had access to lilies is difficult, which is why knowing the signs of lily poisoning is useful – so that emergency veterinary treatment can be promptly sought.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you think your cat has eaten any part of a lily, contact your veterinary team immediately. This is an emergency and requires urgent veterinary care.

How your vet will diagnose lily poisoning

Your vet will make a diagnosis based on the signs your cat is showing and their history of exposure to lilies. Your vet will take blood tests, and on examination, they may find that your cat’s kidneys are enlarged and painful.

## How is lily poisoning treated?

Your cat will be hospitalised for supportive treatment. This may include emptying their stomach (by making them vomit), giving activated charcoal to prevent further absorption of toxins, cleaning any lily pollen off their coat, and placing them on a drip to flush out the toxins in the bloodstream and support your cat’s kidneys.

## What’s the outlook for cats that have suffered lily poisoning?

The outlook depends on how quickly your cat is treated. Prompt treatment gives cats a better chance of recovery, however, if treatment isn’t given quickly, lily poisoning can be fatal. Cats who survive lily poisoning may suffer permanent kidney damage which requires lifelong management.
