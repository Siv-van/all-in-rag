# Poisonous Plants

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/poisonous-plants

---

Most of us are surrounded by plants, both wild and cultivated, in our homes and gardens and come to no harm.

However, a small number of these plants have the potential to cause harm to ourselves and our cats. To keep our cats safe, it’s important to know which plants are poisonous or harmful to cats and what to do if contact is suspected.

## What are the risks with cats and plants?

Most cats are careful about what they eat, so poisoning in cats is generally rare. However, young inquisitive cats or kittens, and cats that may be bored, are more likely to eat harmful plants – particularly houseplants.

Cats do like to nibble on grass, and when they can’t, may turn their attention to household plants

When a cat lives entirely indoors, or only has access to an outdoor run or catio, it’s vital to make sure there are no hazardous plants in the home or their outdoor space. Cats with free outdoor access generally have other things to keep them occupied rather than chewing on unfamiliar plants. Still, they can be at risk if they accidentally eat needles or seeds that have become entangled in their coat during grooming.

All plants, even grass, can irritate your cat’s stomach and intestines, causing vomiting. Cats do like to nibble on grass, and when they can’t, may turn their attention to household plants.

Especially dangerous plants are Dieffenbachia (Dumb Cane) and lilies, which are popular in bouquets and flower arrangements.

## How do I know if my cat has my cat been poisoned?

#### Some of the signs your cat may show if they’ve eaten a harmful or poisonous plant include

- Collapse

- Vomiting

- Diarrhoea

- Drooling

- Redness, swelling and blistering of the skin, mouth or throat

- Reduced appetite

- Lethargy/tiredness

If you notice any of these signs, contact your veterinary team immediately.

If you see your cat eat something that you think is poisonous, don’t attempt to make your cat vomit. Contact your veterinary team and, where possible, make sure you have the plant label to hand. Make a note of the time your cat ate the plant and any signs your cat is unwell.

## Skin reactions

Certain plants may cause blistering or itching of your cat’s mouth and gums, sneezing and eye problems. Contact with the leaves of food plants, such as tomatoes, strawberries, rhubarb, parsnips, carrots, celery, marrow (also known as zucchini and courgette) and cucumbers can all potentially affect your cat in this way. Geranium and Primula leaves can also cause skin irritation. Many plants that are poisonous when eaten, may also have the potential to cause skin irritation upon contact with their leaves or sap.

## How can I prevent my cat from being poisoned by plants?

To keep your cat safe, familiarise yourself with commonly hazardous household plants and ensure these are never kept in your house. This is especially important for kittens and cats kept completely indoors.

Outdoors the story is not so simple. Free-roaming cats have access to many gardens, so it will be impossible to prevent all contact with potentially harmful plants. You can, however, remove the most toxic plants from your garden and make a note of any in your neighbours’ gardens that are potentially dangerous – recording both their common and Scientific names. This list may help your veterinary team if poisoning is suspected.

#### You can also make sure any new additions to the garden are safe. The Horticultural Trade Association has a code of practice for its members, and most garden centres and nurseries label plants that are toxic or cause skin reactions. Plants are grouped into three categories

B – Toxic if eaten

C – Harmful if eaten

You are unlikely to find a category A plant on sale – Poison Ivy being one example. Category B plants should be avoided.

After gardening, never leave hedge clippings or uprooted plants near pets. Their novelty value may encourage inquisitive chewing. Sap from damaged stems can cause skin irritation as well as being poisonous. Bulbs, rhizomes and roots can be the most hazardous parts of some plants.

## Which plants are poisonous or hazardous to cats?

A comprehensive list of plants that are potentially poisonous or harmful to your cat can be found here. Many of us are already familiar with plants that carry really toxic berries, such as Deadly Nightshade. Only a small quantity of these need to be eaten for it to be life-threatening to your cat. Other plants on the list may come as a surprise, such as daffodils.

Most potentially harmful plants have an unpleasant taste and are unlikely to be eaten in sufficient quantities to cause permanent damage. It’s often the fruit or seeds of plants that can be harmful. Woody garden plants are also unlikely to be eaten by your cat – tender household plants pose the most risk.

Lilies are extremely dangerous. All parts of the plant are toxic; the pollen, flowers, stem and leaves

Lilies are extremely dangerous. All parts of the plant are toxic; pollen, flowers, stem and leaves. Eating less than one leaf, part of a flower, pollen, or even drinking water from a vase holding cut lilies can be fatal. ‘Pollen-free’ lilies, which can be marked as safe for cats, are still unsafe and should be avoided in the same way.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if you’re worried your cat has been in contact with a potentially poisonous plant, or if they’re showing any signs of ill health. Where possible, have the plant label to hand (if there is one), and make a note of the time your cat ate the plant if you saw this.
