Always have a discussion and agreement in place with your chosen vet clinic before arriving with trapped cats for neutering. Without doing so, you could undermine your credibility for the future cats you aim to help, but more importantly, inflict unnecessary suffering on cats that may be left caged until the vet can neuter them.

#### To learn more about the restraint options for cats being neutered, watch our video

#### Each cat should have a trapping sheet detailing

Location where trapped

Identifying features - colour, markings, etc.

Whether male or female (if known)

Any known health issues/injuries

Whether there are any tests/treatment requests

Any specific information, eg, comes from a group with high FIV rates, or may be nursing kittens

Whether the cat is potentially owned and should be scanned for a microchip

Considerations during the neutering process

Whilst the cat is in your or the veterinary clinic’s care, dry food is easier to feed, and a small amount of water with ice (to gradually melt) can reduce mess and spillage. Bowls attached to the side of traps and carriers also help to prevent spills

Kittens should be fed sooner after surgery than adult cats and may require feeding before surgery - discuss this with your vet

Recovery accommodation is essential (this may be provided by the veterinary clinic). It should be quiet, secure, protected from the elements, and someone should monitor the cats, along with providing food/water. If there are cats you are concerned about, discuss them with your vet and, if necessary, arrange for an overnight stay. The aim is to ensure cats are fit to be released whilst minimising confinement, which compromises welfare by increasing disease, stressand infection risk

Hospitalisation cages may be useful for overnight holding. These can be divided into two compartments, making it easier to clean without disturbing the recovering cat

The recovery area floor should be covered with plastic sheeting that can be disposed of after the cats have gone, to minimise infection. Cover the plastic sheeting with a layer of newspaper, puppy pads or towels that can be disposed of or washed. If the floor is particularly cold, use tables or other surfaces to raise the traps/carriers. Again, all surfaces should be covered

Related cats should be kept in groups – eg, mother and kittens or siblings, and they should be released together where possible

One possible option, if you have access, is to use a mobile unit for neutering cats close to the site of trapping

Other Health Problems

#### It’s possible that other health problems can be treated by your vet at the time of neutering, taking the following into account

- Current health status of the cat

- Cost of treatment

- The amount of aftercare that will be needed

- The effect of the condition/treatment on the cat and their ability to survive without ongoing care

#### Some of the health problems that could reasonably be treated include

- Pyometra (infected uterus), as long as the cat has not become septicaemic (when infection enters the bloodstream, making the cat very sick)

- Umbilical hernias in females, as part of a midline spay, or large ones in males

- Abscesses

- Minor lacerations (cuts) and wounds

- Removal of loose teeth

- Minor skin, ear or eye infections

- Simple lumps or tumours that interfere with normal function but appear benign (non-cancerous)

Eye removal (enucleation) – it’s worth removing the eye in cases of recent injuries or where pain is likely, but not in cases where the cat is not affected by it or where the injury is old. The cat will need to be hospitalised for 48 hours post-surgery before release, but the majority of cats do very well

- Pinna (external part of the ear) amputation is performed routinely in white cats with sunburn or skin cancer (neoplasia)

- Identification of neutered cats

It’s essential to be able to easily identify neutered cats to prevent unnecessary trapping and surgery. It’s impossible to tell if a cat is neutered just by looking at them, so a universally accepted way of being sure they can be identified from a distance, and in less-than-ideal conditions (eg, poor lighting), is vital. Ear tipping is the standard and easiest way to identify neutered cats. Performed correctly under anaesthetic, when the cat is neutered, means that it’s not painful.

## How is ear tipping performed?

The vet removes 3-8mm (occasionally 10mm), depending on the size of the cat, from theleftear in a straight line (no small V-shapes, as they can look like injuries). If the site bleeds when it is done, then too much has been removed.
