# Where do all the unowned cats come from?

Unowned cats don’t appear from nowhere. They come from different sources, depending on local circumstances — and understanding these sources is the first step to finding solutions.

This video explains population dynamics in simple terms and shows how quickly cat numbers can grow without intervention.

## Where do they come from?

Feral or street cats

When there are no effective population management programmes in place, feral and street cats are rarely neutered — so they continue to breed successfully, and their numbers rise with every generation

Food sources also play a big role and are an important driver of population growth. Cats may be fed by local people or survive on waste that hasn’t been properly discarded. This steady, reliable supply of food enables far more cats to survive and reproduce than the natural environment alone would allow.

Strays, abandoned pets and inbetweener cats

People relinquish or abandon pet cats for many reasons such as a change in circumstances, cost, behaviour challenges, or simply not wanting the responsibility anymore. A smaller number of cats may be genuinely lost and never reunited with their owners. Many pet cats are also left unneutered, which means unwanted litters of kittens are born and add to the unowned cat population.

## What challenges does this create?

#### Large and unmanaged populations of cats can cause

Public health and hygiene concerns

Noise, odour, and waste problems

Impacts on wildlife and ecosystems

Pressure on rescue centres and shelters, which can quickly become overwhelmed

Poor welfare for the cats themselves, through disease, malnutrition, injury, or long stays in overcrowded centres

## How can these cats be helped?

Once you have established the issues, types, and needs of the cats you’re trying to help, the environment in which you’re working, and who else is working within it, you can start to find the appropriate solutions to help.

The best way to help unowned cats is to match solutions to the needs of each cat, based on the lifestyle to which they are adapted. Not every cat can, or should, live as a pet, and recognising this helps us choose interventions that improve welfare for the cat while also reducing challenges for the community.

Feral and street cats

Humane neutering through strategicTrap–Neuter–Vaccinate–Return-Manage (TNVRM) is the most effective way to prevent reproduction, stabilise populations and improve welfare. These cats are adapted to outdoor life and should usually remain in their environment, supported through community care and appropriate feeding.

Pet cats (including stray and abandoned)

Unwanted pet cats (those that have been appropriately socialised to people as young kittens) are suited to living with people, so the priority is to find them pet homes that match their individual needs. But when demand for pet cats is low, it can result in prolonged shelter confinement which harms welfare, so alternatives may need to be explored. Read about Cat Friendly Homing

Inbetweener cats

Inbetweeners’ discomfort towards people varies but what they share is a need for a lifestyle that respects that they are not pets, yet still ensures they are cared for. After neutering, many do well living a free-roaming life outdoors with a caretaker who provides food and shelter. This could be a smallholding, farm, restaurant or hotel with staff willing to provide for them.

Others may settle into a home environment with unrestricted outdoor access, but only if the people caring for them have no expectations for affection or typical “pet” behaviour. The best homes for these cats are with owners who are content simply to provide food, shelter, and full choice and control, without expecting anything in return.

## What tools are available to help me?

Cat Friendly Hominghas many tools that can support you in a wide variety of situations. For example, if you are running a homing centre that has become overwhelmed with large numbers of cats resulting in long periods of confinement, you are experienced outbreaks of infectious disease in the cats in your care, or there is a shortage of pet cat homes, or even if you have stressed and overwhelmed staff.

Understanding supply and demand

Have you considered the relationship between supply and demand? For example, taking in large numbers of cats with very few options to home them will likely lead to overcrowding. This can be made worse if you or neighbouring organisations have a ‘no kill’ or ‘open intake’ policy, which can have unintended consequences such as severe overcrowding, disease spread, and distressed cats. With limited resources, it’s essential to stay within your optimum occupancy, ensuring you can meet the needs of both individual cats and the wider group.

## Working with cats, as you probably already know, is really about working with people, so how we communicate with others and how we can engage them will directly improve our ability to care for cats.

Finally, do the best you can!

Cat Friendly Principles are all about doing the very best you can under difficult circumstances. Whilst of course there are ‘best practice’ options which are the ‘ideals’, sometimes, it’s just about identifying what you could do to have a positive impact. Your intervention and help, however big or small, can still have a positive impact on cat welfare. Often, something is better than nothing. When a problem seems insurmountable and the ideal outcome seems unachievable, be pragmatic in your solutions, as sometimes ‘good enough’ is appropriate.
