# What can you find out about each cat?

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/what-can-you-find-out-about-each-cat

---

Understanding a cat’s background helps you decide whether your organisation is best placed to help and, if so, how to provide the right care and outcome. Intake forms are useful, but the real insight comes from the conversations that accompany them.

The most valuable information often emerges through listening, clarifying, and interpreting what people say rather than what’s written down. Also, owners may be hesitant to mention issues such as problem behaviour if they fear it will affect their cat’s chances. Reassuring them that honesty helps their cat usually leads to more open and useful discussions.

To strengthen your intake process, use a consistent approach for all enquiries with clear record keeping, timelines, and responsibilities. Equip staff to offer advice and referral options - not just admission - and avoid reactive intake without triage, as many situations can wait for planned action. Provide guidance on when to escalate cases to senior or specialist staff, and make sure all personal data is stored securely and in line with legal requirements.

Intake paperwork

The person bringing in a cat usually holds valuable information that can shape care and outcomes. Keep questionnaires and interviews focused on what is relevant. Short, well-targeted forms are easier for people to complete and more useful for staff to interpret. Aim for simple, user-friendly digital formats so they can be filled in easily and returned quickly.

Useful details often include where and how the cat was reared, their past living environment, lifestyle, and any behavioural notes. Even small clues such as“affectionate on their own terms”can reveal important preferences.

Above all, information should guide care, not judge people. Many owners are making difficult choices with limited options, and empathy helps you get the best answers.

## What else should you consider?

Information from the person asking for help can reveal a lot about a cat’s temperament and needs. This applies to both owned and stray cats. For example, someone feeding a stray may notice whether the cat appears only for food or seeks contact at other times as well - clues that can be as valuable as details from a previous owner.

Remember that temperament helps predict how a cat might respond, but their behaviour is also shaped by the situation they’re in. Factors such as the number of nearby cats, access to resources like food or resting spots, and human or animal interactions all play a part. Even a confident, friendly cat can become distressed if overwhelmed - for instance, by too much attention, a family dog, orliving with other cats.
