Consider the millions of cats around the world which are surviving against the odds of disease, physical injury, inadequate diet, dangers of reproduction and the attitudes of those in the environment in which they survive – what care is available to them; from the public, from vets, from the government?

## How do we take this into consideration and how do we understand what cats need so we can help them?

International Cat Care’s Cat Friendly Solutions look at how we help unowned cats in a humane and practical way by providing information for those working on the front line. It is based on an understanding of the different lifestyles of cats and their needs. The problems are complex and, of course, the cat is a complex creature. Solutions for all these cats need to be appropriate to their individual needs.

Solutions for feral cats and street cats

Prevent reproduction using a humane method such as Trap, Neuter and Return, referred to as TNR

Manage the neutered colony with feeding and monitoring and continue to TNR any newcomers

## How does TNR work?

Cats are caught in traps (which do not harm the cats) because most of them are not able to be handled by people sufficiently to be placed in a carrier.

Trapping

Trapping avoids injury to cats and to people through bites and scratches.

Neutering

Neutering (also called castration for males and spaying for females, or sterilisation, altering, fixing or de-sexing for both) is carried out by qualified vets and ensures that the cats do not continue to reproduce and the associated behaviour (ie, fighting, male urine spraying, night-time noise etc) that communities complain about is reduced. The TNR process also includes identification of neutered cats by cutting off the tip of the left ear under anaesthetic (referred to as ear tipping). This allows cats to be identified from a distance and ensures they are not trapped again for neutering. In many cases the ear tip serves as a trademark and, particularly in the USA, is seen as a sign they are safe and cared-for.

Returning

#### Returning the cats to their original site keeps them in a familiar territory and their presence acts as a deterrent to other cats who may be attracted by the food source. It is important to

Monitor the colony of cats to make sure that any new arrivals are trapped and neutered

Continue to monitor the cats for signs of illness or injury and take action

Clear food waste (and faeces in some countries) and deal with problems quickly to ensure the local community is happy with the continued presence of the cats

Ensure population control methods chosen by municipalities/governments are designed with both cat welfare and number stabilisation/reduction in mind and cat owners (and caregivers for free-roaming street cats) understand that neutering ALL cats prevents further additions to the unowned cat population.

Solutions for pet cats - including stray and abandoned cats

Unwanted cats that are friendly towards people (pet cats) should have the opportunity to live with people in homes that particularly suit their individual needs. They may have been given up by owners for varying reasons, may have become lost or abandoned. Mostly they are brought together in a centre with the view to rehome them, but bringing together large numbers of cats can result in much greater risk of spread of disease. So, setting up a homing centre needs planning, and knowledge, not only of disease and health but of minimising stress and making sure that cats do not stay in the shelter for long periods of time – this is the basis of the Cat Friendly Homing programme, part of iCatCare’s Cat Friendly Solutions for Unowned cats. It is also important to remember that the demand for pet cats (particularly pre-owned ones) differs from country to country and if there is no demand, then confining cats with the view to homing should not be considered as an option because they may just be gathered, never to move from the homing centre. That can be more of a welfare problem than leaving them where they are.

Solutions for inbetweener cats

Depending on their tolerance of people, in-betweeners are neutered and may either be relocated to private land where someone can care for them from a distance, or go to a very specific kind of owner who will have them in their home but will adopt a ‘hands-off’ approach

Something to bear in mind!

The information available for people working with unowned cats around the world is patchy, inconsistent and often non-existent!

Committed and motivated people often get highly involved in ‘rescuing’ these cats but do so without the guidance or insights which could help them deal more successfully with the volume and type of cats they have to deal with.
