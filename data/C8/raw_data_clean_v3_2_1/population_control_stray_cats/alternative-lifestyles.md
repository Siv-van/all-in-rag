# Alternative Lifestyles

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/alternative-lifestyles

---

Inbetweeners are cats that have been kept as pet cats but are unable to live without chronic fear, anxiety or frustration in any conventional pet setting (e.g., living with some confinement and close to people), hence why so many of them are found in the care of homing organisations, often for prolonged periods.

## Why do cats become ‘in-betweeners’?

#### It could be because

They didn’t have sufficient interactions or the rightqualityof interaction with people as young kittens, so they are distressed in some way around people

They have a temperament trait that means they can be fearful, anxious or frustrated with people

For these cats, finding another conventional pet home will not be a successful solution. It is not the features of a specific pet home that cause the distress; it’s the nature of a pet home. Having the category of inbetweener allows homing organisations to identify cats that have lived unsuccessfully as pets, not because the specific home wasn’t right for them, but because no conventional pet home would suit.

Inbetweeners need your careful consideration to find an outcome that suits their particular needs.

Many in-betweeners do well living a free-roaming lifestyle where they have food, shelter and a person to care for them at a distance. This can be successful in a variety of environments, for example, hotels, stables, farms and even spacious gardens

Other in-betweeners need to be near people, even living in their homes, but don’t want the constant contact or focus that is typical of a more normal owner/cat relationship

Working Cats Programme

Battersea Dogs and Cats Home refers to its alternative lifestyle homing as Working Cats and includes not just in-betweeners, but also other types of unsocialised cats.

Battersea’s working cats are lower maintenance than domestic pet cats and generally only require food, water, warm shelter, a sufficient area to hunt and go to the toilet in, and someone to keep an eye on them. They usually prefer to come and go completely on their terms without the pressure to interact with people. Battersea working cats are microchipped, neutered, treated for fleas and/or worms, and given an initial set of vaccinations. Unless the cat becomes unwell, working cats should not require any further health or veterinary treatments.

## How does Battersea decide if a cat is suited to life as a working cat?

Decisions as to whether or not to place cats as working cats are taken on a case-by-case basis during consultation with a Battersea vet. Certain circumstances mean a cat is unlikely to be suitable for a working cat lifestyle.

#### The following are some examples of cats unlikely to be suited to a working cat environment

- Any cat that has a disease, condition, illness, or disability that affects their ability to maintain good physical health without careful, long-term management

Breeds of cats without much fur, eg, Sphynx or Rex cats, or cats with very dense, long hair that requires regular help with grooming from a person because they cannot maintain the coat themselves, eg, Persian, Maine Coon, Norwegian Forest cat, or cats with very flat faces, such as Persians or Exotics

- Elderly cats that are less mobile and more likely to experience age-related health issues in the near future

## What can carers expect from a Battersea working cat?

Unlike a typical family pet, a working cat is likely to keep their distance and stay away from people. They may also be more likely to hunt than a typical pet cat, and may need some space, but the working cat must be fed daily to encourage them to remain in the area.

Selling the benefits of the inbetweener

It can be difficult to sell the benefits of caring for an inbetweener cat. However, cats like this can give enormous satisfaction to their owners by thriving in a place where they feel totally in control of where they go and what they do. Research shows that owners who approach their cat more often for interaction actually get less contact with their cat. Allowing a cat to initiate contact results in more interaction, as the cat can decide on their terms and feel in control. Inbetweeners in a no-pressure atmosphere such as this often blossom.

Setting up an inbetweener project in your area

There will be in-betweeners that don’t want to live quite so close to people, and often, the general public is not aware of this type of cat and their specific needs. You may find that, where possible, setting up a specific project around inbetweener cats will encourage interest from the local community and, in turn, could encourage help and support. This could potentially be game-changing for the welfare of those cats that currently don’t fit into conventional thinking, and a new community of forward-thinking cat caregivers may well form as a result of this information.

Firstly, ensure all cats you feel would be eligible for free-roaming (able to go wherever they want to go but with a shelter and food provided by a specific caregiver) are in good health, neutered, vaccinatedandmicrochipped. For some, ear-tipping at the same time as their neutering surgery may also be helpful for ease of identification.

Make your inbetweener project open to communities as well as individuals, although one or two individuals must be made the cat’s (or cats’) official caregivers. Any person or community taking on a cat in this way would automatically become part of your inbetweener community.

#### Where possible, provide them with information, support, and, if resources allow, veterinary care for their in-betweeners. Potential caregivers could, for example, sign up to the following principles

- I will respect my inbetweener cat (s) and will not force my attention on them or try to change them

- I will happily interact if the cat chooses to come to me

- I will provide my inbetweener cat (s) with food and water every day and make provisions for doing so when I am not there

- I will maintain my inbetweener cat (s) shelter to ensure it remains water-tight and comfortable, not too hot in the summer or too cold in the winter

- I will monitor my inbetweener cat (s) for any signs of illness or injury and report this to the appropriate person

- I will allow your representative to call or visit to help when required

## What about the feral and street cats that slip through the net and end up in the homing centre?

From time to time, feral or street cats are incorrectly assessed as pets or find themselves in a homing centre for other reasons. It soon becomes apparent that these cats are likely to have come from some or many generations of non-pet cats; they had previously lived independently of people and had no experience of interacting with them.

The options for these cats are to return them to where they came from, after neutering, vaccination (if funds allow) and ear-tipping, or relocate them to a place where there is plentiful shelter and food. They may be relocated to a farm, smallholding or barn, for example, where someone takes responsibility to feed them, provide them with shelter, and monitor them from a distance for any signs of illness or injury.

Key Points

Inbetweeners are cats that have previously lived unsuccessfully as pets. They need an outcome that meets their specific needs, referred to as an ‘alternative lifestyle’, eg, free-roaming with food and shelter provided by a dedicated caregiver (eg, in a stable yard) orA special home with people who are happy with minimal interaction with their pet

Sell the benefits of inbetweener cats to potential caregivers to ensure the right environments can be found for them

It may take time for your local community to be comfortable with the idea of cats living ‘alternative lifestyles’
