# Questions to consider before TNVR

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/questions-to-consider-before-tnvr

---

TNVR (Trap, Neuter, Vaccinate and Return) requires careful consideration and planning, along with the right support, equipment and knowledge. It’s important to understand the environment you’ll be operating in and the bigger picture. Without this, it can cause more significant problems.

## What questions must be considered before implementing TNVR?

## Are there any legal concerns?

## Are there any legal considerations regarding intervening with cats in your particular country or region? Similarly, are there laws concerning trapping/neutering and the ownership/responsibility for unowned cats?

## Is there any infrastructure in the form of government or animal organisations already addressing cat population management, and are they aware of the problem in your area?

## Is there a local/national authority involved in developing solutions such as legislation and animal control systems?

## Who ‘owns’ the cats and the land? Is there someone you need to seek permission from to trap?

## What do you know about the cats in the area?

## Are you dealing with a small local population or the wider population in a city or country?

## What is the cat population there, and what impact is this having on people, other cats and wildlife?

## Do you have evidence to support that cats are a problem and intervention is needed?

## Are all the cats in the area unowned, or do people consider them to be pets (even if others do not)?

Have you identified the type of cats correctly? For example, are there just street cats, or are there also stray and abandoned pet cats? Are there free-roaming pet cats in the area? Do you have a plan for what you will do with each type of cat?

Will TNVR solve the problems caused by cats and meet expectations? (ie, is this the appropriate solution for the cat types?)

## Are cats impacting a particularly rare or fragile ecosystem, and so cannot remain in their current location?

## Is the cats’ welfare poor due to infectious or genetic diseases as a result of interbreeding?

## Is the site where the cats live being developed, so cats cannot be returned?

## Is the problem for the community the nuisance caused by noise, smell or concerns about hygiene and disease?

Is the problem about waste from the cats in areas where it may cause problems for human health (eg, where water is collected)? In this case, the return of cats (even if neutered) will not solve the problem.

## What is the community's attitude towards cats?

Is the community’s attitude towards cats generally positive or negative, and how does this impact population management plans? High levels of negativity from a significant proportion of the community will reduce the effectiveness of TNVR programmes and may lead to poor welfare outcomes for cats, even if they are neutered. Without the involvement and cooperation of local people, you will not be successful

## Are there cultural or religious beliefs that may influence people’s responses?

Are there other factors that the community values? It may be helpful to capture this information via questionnaires or discussions before and after trapping. This can help you measure success from their point of view, for example, there may have been concerns about cats fighting and making a noise at night, and this problem may have been solved

## Do you have the necessary skills, either personally or in your team?

## Can you communicate well with others and get them to work with you?

## Are you organised?

## Do you have enough knowledge about cats to ensure their wellbeing is never compromised?

## Can you talk to veterinary clinics about what you need from them?

## Are you prepared for some of the more distressing aspects of the work – dealing with very sick or badly injured cats, or angry and abusive people?

Are you confident in making pragmatic, humane decisions for the cats concerned? There will be some circumstances (such as illness) when the only solution is to trap and humanely euthanise cats. Well-planned euthanasia that is centred around cat welfare is preferable to the use of inhumane methods such as poisoning, but it may be upsetting for you and others to make those decisions

Are you able to dedicate a sufficient amount of time and be patient? It can take hours, days, and even weeks to trap a group of cats, and you may also have to explain to local people what you are doing, many times over

If the answer is ‘no’ to any of the above (and it’s worth noting, this complete skillset will likely only come from a group of people, rather than one individual), then you may need to seek other people and resources that can support you in these areas. You will then be able to work as a team with all the benefits this entails

## Are there other factors to consider?

Is there a free-roaming dog population that is being controlled? Dogs may prey on cats or compete more effectively for resources such as space and food. Cats may hide more if dogs are around. If there are plans in your area to manage the dog population, then it’s worthwhile working with the relevant organisations to introduce cat population management at the same time or immediately after

## Are you aware that other species (wild or pet, attracted by cat food) may be trapped too?

## Do you have knowledgeable vets to work with?

Will you be using vets in a large charity or government-run hospital? If not, those you use will be running their own business, and you will need to respect this when asking for discounted rates and arranging time for neutering. Vets and veterinary clinics play an essential role in your TNVR project, so good working relationships are vital

## Do they understand the issues around dealing with street or feral cats and have the equipment and knowledge to do so?

## Will they need additional equipment or to introduce different anaesthetic protocols?

## Are there issues with infection control and potential disease transfer to/from their clients’ pets?

You will need to agree on the process of TNVR with the veterinary clinic and gain their support in key areas, such as prepubertalneutering*, pregnant spays, infectious disease testing (eg,FIV/FeLV), vaccination, euthanasia and understanding the need to adopt a different approach to clinical decisions for unowned cats

## Can you collaborate with the veterinary clinic on practical issues such as drop-off and pick-up flexibility, or using a different entrance for pet cat clients?

## Can you discuss bringing in additional veterinary resources occasionally if there are only one or two vets in the practice?

## Do you know what you are going to do with the cats?

#### Any plan that involves trapping followed by long-term confinement is a negative intervention that will lead to poor physical and mental outcomes for the cats involved. With that in mind, the following needs to be considered

## Can the cats be returned to the site after neutering? If not, you should think carefully before intervening. Do you have alternative sites? Will you take responsibility for trapping and euthanising the cats if you think they may otherwise be killed by inhumane methods?

If you plan to relocate them, how will you do this? It’s better to relocate quickly in small groups than wait for a large site to become available that can accommodate the entire group. Any new location must also be far enough away to avoid cats returning to the original site, unless it’s accepted that some of the cats may return

## Do you have policies and procedures in place so that everyone knows the approach you are taking?

It’s easier to have policy discussions before individual cat cases arise, because emotions can get in the way of logical thought and decision-making.

Are you planning to establish policies and procedures? Everyone must be clear about the welfare outcomes and use of scarce resources. In most cases, funds are limited, and choices must be made about using them for the benefit of as many cats as possible

## Do you have a system for recording information?

Will your records be on paper or a computer? You will need to record details of the people involved, properties, and all the cats: their age, sex, colour, any treatments, and whether they were returned to the site, euthanised, released to an alternative site, or if they have previously been pet cats, homed as pets

## Can you show how funds have been used?

## Do you need to meet the requirements of any legislation for protecting personal data?

## Have you considered the need for insurance cover?

## Do you need public liability insurance?

## What if someone was injured by your traps, or one of your staff or volunteers comes to harm while trapping?

## Can you provide management of the colony or small groups of cats after TNVR?

Have you considered the ongoing care and supervision of the group? Read our article on management after TNVR

## Can you afford to do TNVR?

Do you have the funds to cover the cost of equipment, veterinary fees, insurance, a vehicle and any other related fees? Funds can potentially be raised by the affected community or via other charities, NGOs, government, grants, etc.

## Are there others you could work with?

Could you collaborate with other like-minded individuals, groups or charities? This can save time and resources, as well as bring invaluable expertise and experience. There may be a group that works with another species, for example, dogs, which can provide advice and support.

*Prepubertal neutering of kittens

Enables you to neuter and return kittens from the age of 8-9 weeks

Each kitten is assessed by both age and weight – normally, an 8-week-old kitten will weigh 1kg (2.2lbs), but this can vary depending on the mother’s condition, as well as the kitten’s condition and diet

Kittens recover quickly with less bleeding and without the complications of pregnancy or being in season

Kittens that are going to be returned but are not old enough to neuter, should be managed with minimal human contact for as short a time as possible until they are old enough for surgery

Suitably friendly kittens can be neutered before homing, so that they will not be able to add to the cat population
