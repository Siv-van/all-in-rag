# How to greet a cat

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/how-to-greet-a-cat

---

We all adore cats and many of us enjoy special interactions with the cats in our homes or with cats we care for in our work. We also recognise that cats vary hugely in how much interaction they like to receive from us. To get the very best out of those relationships and to ensure a cat is always comfortable, it’s essential our interactions always occur in a cat friendly manner – giving the cat choice and control. After all, that’s what cats thrive on!

## What do I need to consider when greeting a cat?

When thinking about how to greet a cat and invite them to interact with you, it’s important to remember it is just that – an invitation. A cat should always be given the opportunity to vote with their paws and politely decline the invitation by walking away. Giving a cat such a choice actually means they may be more likely to accept future invitations. As a cat learns that you always allow them to remain in control of interactions, they become more likely to want to engage in them.

## How should we greet cats in a cat friendly way?

Make sure our body language is as least threatening as possible. We should make ourselves small by getting down to a cat’s level. If a cat is on ground level, then our greetings are best received when crouching, sitting, or kneeling on the floor, avoiding leaning over or cornering them

Avoid directly facing a cat; look beyond rather than straight at them and blink slowly. Staring tends to create distance between individuals rather than reduce it

Make the greeting sound of cats known as the chirrup– it sounds like ‘ppppprrrrrrrppppp’ in an ascending tone and can be made by pushing air through closed lips. This lets a cat know you have friendly intentions

Outstretch your hand with a loosely clasped fist towards the cat to offer them a small-sized part of you to explore. Your loosely clasped fist is about the same size as a cat’s head, and cats tend to greet one another nose to nose. By stretching your arm away from your body, you prevent the urge to reach further forward when a cat approaches your hand, therefore preventing startling them

Now we have our greeting and invitation set up, let’s see if a cat accepts the invitation. If a cat approaches and sniffs our hand, it’s a great sign that they feel confident enough to learn a bit more about us. However, the biggest signal that a cat is likely to be comfortable with being stroked is whether they rub their face on your hand. If they do, simply stroke or gently scratch them a couple of times on the cheeks or under the chin for 3 to 5 seconds, and then stop, wait and observe. If the cat wants more interaction, they will likely rub against you again. If not, they are telling you that was enough of an interaction at that particular time. By following this pattern of stroking, stopping, waiting and allowing a cat to initiate more contact before continuing stroking, we are really demonstrating to them our understanding of their language.

Cats tend to prefer short, frequent, low-intensity interactions over long-lasting high-intensity bouts of stroking. However, every cat is different, but by using our greeting guide, you can start to discover how much interaction cats in your care prefer. When it comes to greeting cats, less is often more!
