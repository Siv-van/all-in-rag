# Moving house with your cat

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/moving-house-with-your-cat

---

Cats are a territorial species, and as such, they develop strong bonds with their home environment. Because of this, house moves are potentially very stressful for cats.

Planning ahead will ensure that the transition from one home to another goes as smoothly as possible, which can help reduce the risk of your cat feeling anxious about the situation.

## What do I need to consider on moving day to reduce any potential stress for my cat?

#### Below are our top tips for ensuring moving day is as smooth and stress-free as possible for both you and your cat

Before the removal van arrives, keep your cat confined to one room (often, a bedroom is ideal). Think about where the furniture in this room will go in the new house, as your cat may see this as their safe place in the future, and therefore, it needs to be a room your cat has unlimited access to. Ensure the door and windows remain shut to help prevent escape

Put your cat’s resources and familiar items; carrier, bed, blanket, food bowl, water bowl, scratching post and litter tray in this room, as these will help provide reassurance and allow them to perform normal behaviours

Place a notice on the door so the removal team and the rest of your family know that this door should be kept shut

When all other rooms have been emptied, the contents of the bedroom can be placed in the van. Before the last of the furniture is removed, your cat should beplaced in their cat carrier (covering the carrier with a light blanket can be helpful in reducing stress) and put safely in the car (rear footwell or on the back seat secured with a seatbelt) to make the journey to your new home. Don’t transport your cat in the removal van or the boot of your car

If it’s a long journey, you may want to stop and offer your cat some water or a chance to use their litter tray, although most cats won’t be interested. If you do choose to stop, make sure it’s in a safe space where your cat cannot escape

If you’re moving on a hot day, make sure the car is well ventilated. Neverleave your cat inside a hot car if you stop for a break

## How do I help my cat settle into their new home?

#### Arriving at your new home

Once you arrive at your new home, the bedroom furniture should be at the back of the lorry or van and therefore, can be the first to be unloaded into the house

Place a synthetic feline pheromone diffuser in a floor-level socket in the new room where your cat will be temporarily confined, ideally several hours before your cat is brought inside

Once the room is ready, your cat can be placed inside with their resources and familiar items; bed, blanket, food and water bowls, scratching post and litter tray, with the door shut. If possible, have a family member sit in the room with your cat for a while as they explore

Offer your cat some food

Once the removal has been completed, your cat can investigate the rest of the house. Keep exploration to one room at a time if there are any signs ofstress

Remain as calm as possible to signal to your cat that it’s a safe environment

#### Helping your cat to settle in

Keep your cat indoors for at least two to three weeks to get used to the new environment

Provide small, frequent meals

Maintain routines kept in your previous house to provide continuity and familiarity

Continue to use the synthetic feline pheromone diffuser in the original room. A spray can also be used on items such as their bedding and blankets

Extra care should be taken for permanently indoor cats, as a new environment will be potentially unsettling; it may take a little longer to adapt, but having familiar furniture will always help

#### Letting your cat outside

Keep your cat indoors for a couple of weeks to get used to their new home

Make sure your cat has some form of identification, such as a microchip, to ensure they can be returned to you should they become lost. Remember, it’s important to update your cat’s microchip details with your new address and home phone number as soon as possible

Ensure your cat’svaccinationsare up to date

Consider fitting a cat flap for easy outdoor access, once your cat is settled. Make sure it’s an electronically or magnetically controlled exclusive-entry system (such as a microchip cat flap) to avoid the risk of strange cats entering your home and causing your cat stress

Choose a quiet, dry day when you are home, and let your cat outside before a routine mealtime, as they are more likely to come back indoors when hungry

Introduce your cat to the outdoors gradually by opening the door and going together into the garden

Don’t carry your cat outside; allow them to decide if they want to explore in their own time

Keep the door open initially so your cat can escape indoors if something frightens them

Outdoor cats with a wider experience of change generally cope well; timid cats may take time to adapt to the new environment and should be accompanied outside until they build up their confidence

Additional considerations

Preventing your cat from returning to their old home

If your new home is nearby, your cat may explore when they first go out and find familiar routes that take them back to your old home. It’s a good idea to warn the new owners that your cat may return and ask them to contact you if they see your cat in the vicinity. They mustn't feed or encourage your cat in any way, as this will be confusing. If you have moved locally, we recommend keeping your cat indoors for as long as possible after the move. With patience and by following our advice above about settling your cat into your new home, along with the use of synthetic and natural scents to make the new home seem as familiar as possible, your cat should settle. If you’re struggling and they keep returning to their old home, speak to your veterinary team, who may be able to recommend a qualified animal behaviourist to help with the situation.

Lifestyle changes

It’s not ideal to change your cat’s lifestyle from outdoor to indoor, but occasionally, it may be necessary if you move house, and they need to be confined.

If your cat spends most of their time outside, it may be kinder to rehome them. If, however, your cat spends little time outside, they may not find it unduly stressful to be kept indoors in the future. Indoor cats require extra effort from their owner to stimulate them and encourage exercise to avoid boredom. Suggestions to enhance an indoor cat’s environment include: Hiding dry food around the house to provide opportunities to ‘hunt’

Providing plenty of high vantage points and scratching posts that your cat can climb

Regular predatory play sessions at least once a day

Introducing novel items to explore, such as boxes and paper bags (ensure handles are removed from bags to help prevent them from getting tangled)

Occasionally, owners are fortunate enough to move to a property where they can let their cat outside for the first time. The transition from indoor to outdoor cat, if taken gently, will enhance your cat’s emotional wellbeing and enable them to live a more natural life. Follow our guidance forletting your cat outside, but accept that the process should be gradual. Many cats, under these circumstances, may prefer to go outside only when you are there to provide reassurance.

Moving to a smaller property

#### If you have amulti-cat household, then your cats have become used to living with the available space of your previous home. Moving to a smaller property could potentially cause some tension between your cats. Limit the risk by providing sufficient resources, such as

Beds

Litter trays

Scratching posts

Food bowls

Water bowls

High resting platforms (eg, wardrobes, cupboards, shelves)

Private hiding places (eg, under the bed, bottom of wardrobe)

Ideally, there should be one of each key resource per cat, plus one extra, spaced out around the home.
