# Choosing a boarding cattery

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/choosing-a-boarding-cattery

---

It can be natural to feel reluctant to go away on holiday because you don’t want to leave your cat in a boarding cattery. However, there are many excellent catteries where cats are kept safe and happy and may even enjoy the experience.

Understanding what constitutes a good cattery will help you to look for the right things. Then you just need to book well in advance (good catteries are often booked up very early) and go away with a clear conscience knowing that you have done everything in the best interest of your cat’s wellbeing whilst you are away. So, what should you be looking for?

## What are the basics of boarding cattery care?

You want to be sure your cat won’t escape

#### This sounds very basic, but cats do escape if the cattery is not built, maintained or managed properly. The cattery must have

Individual cat units that are properly maintained and secure. There should be no holes or gaps that your cat can squeeze through

A ‘safety corridor’ or ‘safety area’ outside each individual unit. Cats can get past someone opening the door with food or to clean – some are pretty fast and slippery! The outside door must always be kept shut when the inside door is open, and vice versa. This ensures your cat is safely contained and cannot reach the outside. Trained staff who understand the importance of door-shutting are equally important, as the only way a cat can escape from this set-up is by human error, and this should be an exceptionally rare occurrence in any well-run cattery

You want to be sure that your cat will be warm and comfortable

Some catteries may have the option of an outdoor area (photo courtesy Sophie Angliss)

There are different types of catteries out there. Some are completely indoors, some have units like little chalets that also have an outdoor run, and some are in between. You may instinctively think you want your cat to stay indoors, but it can be much more rewarding and healthier for them to have an outdoor run (safely enclosed to prevent escape), which is in the fresh air, as this provides better ventilation, which will remove smells and infective organisms in the air. Inside the unit, there should be a cosy bed and a heater in case it gets cold. The heater must be safe – there are various types, from radiators or pipes to infrared bulbs. A cat flap will let your cat in and out of the chalet or unit into the run so that they can choose where they want to be.

Cosy and cat safe heat sources should be provided in cold weather to keep them warm

You want to ensure your cat doesn’t catch an infectious disease

Where you have a lot of cats together, there’s a chance of spreading viral or bacterial disease. They do this via grooming, through droplets from sneezes, bites, contact with faeces, from shared food bowls, or even from the clothes of people who’ve touched cats that are excreting viruses. If you have several cats that live together, they will probably already have shared each other’s bacteria and viruses. However, if cats are brought together in a cattery, the cattery must do its utmost to ensure they don’t catch anything from other cats that they’re looking after. This is where the design and management of the cattery come into effect.

Basic principles of disease control mean that cats from different households should never be able to touch each other, and they should be protected from the danger of another cat sneezing on them or from transmitting disease on the hands or clothes of cattery carers, food bowls or litter trays. How is this done?

Perspex screens between pens can create a sneeze barrier to help prevent the potential spread of disease transmission (Photo © Kayla Hillier)

The design of the cattery is important– the individual cat units can be alongside each other, but they need to be separated either by an impermeable barrier, such as Perspex or by a wide gap, often referred to as a ‘sneeze barrier’, as that’s its function. Cats should never be able to touch each other through the mesh

Avoid communal runs and exercise areas. Some catteries offer communal areas where cats share a large space with other cats (not from the same household). Many owners understandably think this will be lovely – their cat will have ‘friends’ and they will be able to run around and play with them. However, most cats don’t want to share space with a cat they don’t know and will be very stressed by this. There is also a potential risk of physical injury if the cats display protective behaviours towards one another (biting or scratching). In addition, such communal spaces enable cats to share litter trays, lick each other, and share food bowls – all potential ways to spread disease

Preventing the spread of disease through hand contact. You may see notices for visitors not to touch the cats because stroking them can spread disease. Cattery proprietors themselves should wash their hands between handling cats, and many now use hand-washes like those hospital staff use to prevent the spread of infection and MRSA

Vaccinationsare vital for any cat coming into a cattery because of the presence of lots of cats (and therefore viruses), and a cattery must ask to see each cat’s vaccination certificate as proof that their vaccines are up to date, to protect both your cat and the other cats in the cattery

Cleaning and disinfection. The cattery proprietor should understand the importance of keeping the cattery meticulously clean and disinfected and should be able to explain how that is done. The cattery should not smell, and litter trays should be cleaned regularly. Uneaten wet food should be removed, and food and water bowls kept clean. Food bowls should be washed in a separate area from litter trays

You want your cat to have room to move around

Cats should be in individual runs, except cats from the same household, which come in together, aresocially bondedand get along with one another. If you have more than one cat and they are not socially bonded or show antagonistic behaviours towards one another, it would be better if they were housed in individual runs, instead of being kept in an unfamiliar enclosed space which could heighten protective emotions (fear/anxiety/frustration) towards one another.

Cats should be kept in individual pens unless they live together and are socially bonded (Photo © Kayla Hillier)

Runs should be kept meticulously clean and cats should never be able to encounter cats from another household, as visual or physical contact with unfamiliar cats can make them feel anxious, fearful and/or frustrated.

If the cattery is well built and the runs are about 6ft by 4ft (1.8m by 1.2m), with shelves to sunbathe on, and something for cats to play with and sharpen their claws on, they’ll be very content.

You want your cat to be relaxed and secure (but not bored!)

Taking familiar items from home with your cat such as scratching posts and toys can help provide reassurance (Photo © Elly Chitty)

Barking and excited dogs in kennels near a cattery can be very stressful for cats, so choose a cattery with no kennels on the premises or one well separated from the kennel area

Many catteries will let owners bring in bedding or toys to help cats feel secure because they smell familiar. Likewise, a cat scratching post from home can help them settle in

Cats like high places – they will seldom sit on the ground if there is somewhere higher up. The same applies in a cattery. A shelf in the cat sleeping accommodation and the run will give your cat a chance to climb up and view the world, making them feel much more secure

Cats like to retreat to a safe place to rest and hide if they feel threatened. Safe places should be quiet, easily accessible and provide your cat with a sense of security. Also ensure your cat has plenty of places to hide in their run, such as concealed beds, beds that can be covered or have a high side, cardboard boxes placed on their side, and even their cat carrier with the door removed

Routine makes cats feel secure – they need to know when they’re going to be fed, and see when people come and go so they don’t feel threatened

Many excellent catteries plant butterfly-attracting plants around the cattery to provide interest and movement for the cats to view

You want your cat to eat well

Good cattery owners will find out about your cat’s regular food and feed the same food as at home. This may be difficult if you feed lots of fresh food or if you feed a special prescription diet (in which case, you will need to bring this in for the cattery to feed). They will monitor the amount eaten, and if your cat needs a little encouragement to eat, they will try different things to help your cat feel more relaxed and happier to eat.

You want your cat to be carefully monitored

Good proprietors will monitor your cat. Ideally, they should be tracking your cat’s toileting and eating habits daily. This gives them a good idea of your cat’s wellbeing and helps detect any changes early on.

Any unwell cat will be taken to the vet – and again, a good cattery will ask you for details of your own vet and get you to sign a consent form allowing them to contact a vet and give treatment if necessary.

Make sure you give details of how to contact you or ask someone you trust to make veterinary decisions to be the contact while you’re away in case your cat falls ill.

You want your cat to have tender loving care

For most of us, our cats are important family members, and we want them to be looked after with the same care and consideration we give them. That’s down to the people caring for the cats and the enthusiasm and dedication they give to their job.

If you look at the very best catteries, you’ll see that for the people involved, each cat is important to them. There’s attention to detail and knowledge behind that care, which comes from their love of cats.

Most cats adapt very quickly, and as long as they feel secure in their new environment and they have food, warmth, places to hide and toileting facilities, they make the best of the cosiness in their run to sleep and watch everything going on in the cattery’s daily routine.

Good catteries will take care of your cat with as much love and attention as you would (Photo © Ellie Chitty)

You need help with your cat’s special requirements

Most catteries will give medication if required, and a few of the best will even give treatments such as insulin injections for diabetic cats. If you have a higher-maintenance cat that needs such medication, you need to search for a cattery that’s capable of providing the required treatment.

If your cat is infirm or disabled, they may not be able to climb a ladder/ramp if the cattery is of a ‘penthouse’ design where the sleeping accommodation is a raised box accessed by a ramp. The cattery owner may have some units where the accommodation is at ground level or may be able to build a much more gently sloping series of steps up to the box.

## How do I find a good boarding cattery?

We always recommend visiting a cattery before booking your cat in for a stay. When you visit, ask questions about the topics raised in this article.

See if it’s clean and if the cats in the cattery look relaxed and happy. If the proprietor won’t let you see where the cats are kept, then go elsewhere – a good proprietor will have nothing to hide and will be proud to show you their cattery.

If you can’t find anything of good quality in your area (there are some very poor catteries out there, even though they should have been inspected by the local authority to get a licence to operate), you may want to look a little further afield. Even if your cat doesn’t like travelling in the car, an extra half-hour may be worth it for peace of mind while you’re away. Your veterinary clinic may be able to prescribe medication to help with travel-related anxiety and/or sickness if needed.
