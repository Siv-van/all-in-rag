# Progressive retinal atrophy (PRA) in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/progressive-retinal-atrophy-pra-in-cats

---

Progressive retinal atrophy (PRA) is the term used to describe a group of genetic disorders that result in deterioration and atrophy (loss or wasting) of the retina – the light-sensitive layer of cells at the back of the eye.

Progressive retinal atrophy (PRA) in cats explained

While PRA is quite a rare condition in cats, the degeneration of the retina can lead to a progressive decline in your cat’s quality of vision and, in some cats, can lead toblindness.

Owners may become aware of this condition when their cat’s vision becomes significantly impaired and, for example, their cat starts to become disorientated or bumps into objects.

## What are the signs of PRA in cats?

#### PRA affects both eyes and, initially, will make your cat’s vision worse at night. Other signs include

- Appearing disorientated

- Bumping into things

- Struggling to find food and water bowls, litter trays etc.

- Becoming withdrawn at night

- Reluctance to go up/down stairs

- Dilated pupils (when the black centre of the eye is larger than usual)

## What causes PRA in cats?

#### PRA is inherited, so one or both of your cat’s parents had a gene that causes the condition. Breeds predisposed to PRA include

- Abyssinian

- Somali

- Ocicat

- American Curl

- American wirehair

- Bengal

- Balinese

- Cornish Rex

- Munchkin

- Oriental shorthair

- Peterbald

- Siamese

- Singapura

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you notice anything unusual with your cat, or they’re showing any of the signs listed above or other signs of ill health, contact your veterinary team.

How your vet will diagnose PRA

Your vet will diagnose PRA by examining your cat’s eyes – they may, if necessary, recommend a referral to a veterinary ophthalmologist (eye specialist). Your vet may also perform blood tests and blood pressure measurements to rule out other conditions which can lead to blindness before making a referral.

## How is PRA in cats treated?

There is no treatment for PRA. While the condition will eventually lead to blindness, this condition won’t cause your cat any pain or discomfort, and your veterinary team will be able to advise you on the modifications you can make around your home to make life easier for your cat to cope.

Ongoing care

While many cats adjust well to vision loss, there are things you can do to make their life easier. Keep furniture in the same place, and make sure resources such as food, water, litter trays and a comfortable bed are easy to reach. Initially, it may be better to confine your cat to a smaller area or one room, so they can access everything they need easily. Once they are comfortable in that environment, larger areas of the house can be made available.

Avoid making noise around your cat and, in homes with children, ask them to move slowly and quietly around your cat so that your cat is not frightened.

Your cat may be able to go outside, but only into a safe, fully enclosed garden.

## Can PRA in cats be prevented?

Cats with breed-related vision problems, such as progressive retinal atrophy, should not be used for breeding. Genetic testing of predisposed breeds is recommended.
