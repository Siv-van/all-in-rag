# Hypokalaemia in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/hypokalaemia-in-cats

---

Hypokalaemia is the term used to describe low levels of potassium in the blood. Most of the potassium in your cat’s body is contained within cells, but a smaller amount is in the blood and the fluid around the cells. The correct balance between the amount of potassium inside cells and in the blood and fluid around cells is critical for their normal function, especially for cells such as muscle fibres.

Blood potassium levels may drop for several reasons, but if the levels drop too low, this can have multiple negative effects on your cat’s health. One of the most obvious signs that your cat may be suffering from hypokalaemia can be generalised muscle weakness which, depending on the severity of the hypokalaemia, can be anything from mild to very severe, with some cats even unable to stand up.

## What are the signs of hypokalaemia?

In mild cases, your cat will show no obvious signs and unless a blood test is performed, there aren’t any outward indications that potassium levels are low. However, low blood potassium can still cause unwanted effects – for example, damaging the kidneys and causing a poor appetite.

In more severe cases, the most noticeable sign of hypokalaemia is muscle weakness. If your cat has severe hypokalaemia, often, one of the first and most obvious signs will be your cat not being able to hold their head up in a normal position. Their neck will remain drooped (known as ‘ventroflexion’) – and while other causes of muscle weakness can also lead to ventroflexion of the neck, hypokalaemia is the most common cause.

Muscle weakness will affect other areas of the body, too. You may notice weak limbs, a reluctance to walk and move, and your cat may appear stiff and/or wobbly. In extreme cases, their muscles can become painful, your cat may be unable to stand, and if the respiratory muscles are affected, your cat may find it difficult to breathe (dyspnoea).

## What causes low blood potassium (hypokalaemia)?

In healthy cats, blood potassium concentrations are very closely controlled to ensure the body functions normally. Some potassium is lost in the urine, but usually, the potassium in a cat’s diet is enough to replace the amount lost in the urine each day.

#### Hypokalaemia can be caused by a variety of conditions

- Inappetenceor anorexia– long periods of eating less than normal may cause a mild drop in blood potassium

- Chronic kidney disease (CKD)– this is probably the most common cause of significant hypokalaemia in cats. For unknown reasons, some cats with CKD can lose excessive amounts of potassium in their urine and suffer hypokalaemia as a result

- Severe vomiting or diarrhoea– prolonged or severe vomiting and diarrhoea can result in excessive loss of potassium

Primary aldosteronism (Conn’s syndrome)– this is a disorder where there’s abnormal production of the hormone aldosterone, usually because of an adrenal tumour. With excessive aldosterone production, the kidneys will fail to conserve potassium and large amounts will be lost in the urine

- Periodic hypokalaemiainBurmeseand related cats – hypokalaemia may develop as an inherited disease in certain breeds. In these cats, there is intermittent hypokalaemia, although the exact cause of the low potassium is uncertain

Other disease– rarely, hypokalaemia can result from other diseases (for example, hyperthyroidism), or sometimes as a result of treatment of other diseases (for example, during treatment fordiabetesor when diuretics are prescribed)

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you notice any of the signs listed above, or you’re worried about your cat’s health in general, contact your veterinary team as soon as possible.

How your vet will diagnose hypokalaemia

Hypokalaemia is simple to diagnose as the potassium levels can easily be measured with a blood test.

If hypokalaemia is confirmed, further investigations may be required to determine the underlying cause – these may include further blood and urine tests, x-rays and/or ultrasound scans. Sometimes more specialist tests may be required.

## How can your vet diagnose periodic hypokalaemia in Burmese and related cats?

Periodic hypokalaemia of Burmeseand related cats is usually first seen in young kittens from 2-6 months of age, although signs may not be noticeable until around a year of age. You may see intermittent signs of muscle weakness (including ventroflexion of the neck) and affected cats often have moderate to severe signs for several days and then may have periods of normality in between that can last a few weeks. Signs may be brought on by exercise, stress or cold weather. Severely affected cats may collapse and even suffer respiratory paralysis, which is life-threatening.

This condition affects Burmese and related cats such asAsian, Australian Mist, Bombay, Burmilla, Cornish Rex, Devon Rex, Singapura, Sphynx, TiffanieandTonkinesecats. The gene controlling the disease is termed WNK-4, and a simple gene test is now available to determine the genetic status of a cat. All potential breeding cats from the Burmese and related breeds should ideally be tested before being used for breeding to eradicate this defect from the breed.

## How is hypokalaemia treated?

Treatment of hypokalaemia in cats will depend on the underlying cause, but usually potassium supplementation is needed to correct the immediate problem. Depending on the severity of the hypokalaemia, this will often initially be through a potassium-supplemented drip (intravenous fluids). Longer term, if supplementation is needed, it may be given by mouth in a liquid, powder or tablet form. Cats with periodic hypokalaemic polymyopathy usually need continued potassium supplementation to avoid recurrent episodes, but some cats spontaneously improve by the time they reach 1–2 years of age so may not need life-long supplementation. Cats with hyperaldosteronism may need other types of medical treatment.

Your vet will need to perform repeat blood tests in most cases to ensure the correct dose of potassium is being used, as this will vary from one cat to another.

FAQ’s

## How can hypokalaemia be prevented?

One way in which we hope to prevent hypokalaemia in Burmese and related breeds is to stop breeding from cats with the WNK-4 gene defect. Identifying affected cats is simple to do through testing.

In addition, for cats that have been tested, breeders can contact the relevant breed registers to find out about recording their cat’s results.
