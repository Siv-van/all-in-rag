# Ear Problems in Cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/ear-problems-in-cats

---

Ear problems are common in cats. They may affect the pinnae (the outer ears or ear flaps sitting on top of the head), the external ear canal, the middle ear or the inner ear. The most common diseases affect the external ear (the ear canal and pinnae).

Ear problems in cats explained

Conditions affecting the outer ear

Wounds and trauma: Wounds affecting the outer ear are often a result of cat fights. A bite or scratch wound may cause a full-thickness tear, and bite wounds may cause swelling and development of an abscess (a swelling filled with pus)

Haematoma: A haematoma is a large blood-filled swelling caused by the rupture of a small blood vessel under the skin (blood then accumulates between the skin and cartilage). Haematomas may be seen in cats secondary to disease of the deeper parts of the ear and result from shaking and scratching the area

Solar dermatitis: Repeated sun exposure and sunburn can lead to sore, damaged skin and even skin cancer . It’s more common in cats with white or pale-coloured ears and in countries with high levels of UV radiation.

SarcopticorNotoedricmange: This is caused by infection of the skin with mites (Notoedres catiandSarcoptes scabiei)

Harvest mites (Neotrombicula autumnalis): Also called chiggers or berry bugs, this can be a seasonal problem in outdoor cats with typical red-orange ‘pinhead’ larvae of the mite seen on the ears, face and feet, causing irritation

Conditions affecting the ear canal

The term ‘otitis externa’ is sometimes used and refers to any inflammatory condition affecting the ear canal (or even the pinnae). This is not a disease in itself, as most diseases affecting the ear canal will cause some degree of inflammation.

Ear mites (Otodectes cynotis): Ear mites can easily spread between cats. The mite is just visible to the naked eye as a dirty white speck, often actively moving. The mites generally spend their whole life in or around the ear canal, but may be able to survive for short periods (up to 2-3 weeks)

Bacterial infection: Often occurs secondary to another ear problem (eg, ear mites, foreign material within the ear [foreign body], trauma) although sometimes an infection will occur without obvious cause. Fungal infections (with yeasts) may also be present

Foreign bodies: Although more common in dogs, occasionally, a foreign object (eg, grass seed [grass awn] or blade of grass) can get stuck in the ear canal

Ear canal tumours: In older cats especially, tumours may develop inside the ear canal. These may be benign polyps or tumours (non-cancerous), but in many cases are malignant (cancerous)

Conditions affecting the middle and inner ear

Because of their proximity, diseases that affect the middle ear often also affect the inner ear, causing balance issues in cats. In some cats, disease of the middle ear will also spread to the external ear or vice versa, if the ear drum is damaged.

#### Some of the more common conditions affecting the middle and inner ear include

- Infection of the middle ear: this is seen more commonly in young cats than older cats and is usually caused by infection spreading up the eustachian tube (the small tube that connects the nose to the middle ear)

- Polyps: benign (non-cancerous) growths may develop within the middle ear or eustachian tube. Cats of any age can be affected, but it’s more commonly seen in young adults

- Tumours: rarely, the middle ear may be affected by the presence of benign or malignant tumours

## What are the signs of ear problems in cats?

#### The signs will vary depending on which part of the ear is affected, and the cause. Signs may include

- Swelling

- Redness/inflammation

- Pain

- Visible wounds

- Crusty, ulcerated skin

- Irritation, itchy skin (pruritis)

- Discharge from the ears

- Head shaking

- Holding the head to one side

- Smell from the ears

- Difficulty walking or walking in circles

- Changes in the eyes (eg, Horner’s syndrome – changes in pupil size, third eyelid protrusion and drooping of the eyelids)

## What causes ear problems in cats?

As outlined above, there are various causes of ear problems in cats. These include foreign bodies (eg, grass seeds/grass awns), infection, trauma (eg, a wound from a cat fight), parasites, UV exposure, polyps and tumours.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if you notice any of the signs listed above, or if you’re worried about your cat’s general health.

How your vet will diagnose an ear problem in your cat

Diagnosis will depend on which part of your cat’s ear is affected. Your vet will examine your cat thoroughly and look down their ears with an otoscope (an instrument with a light and magnifying lens that allows your vet to examine inside your cat’s ears as far as the ear drum). They may also take a swab to check for infection (eg, with bacteria or yeast). For inner ear problems, your cat may need x-rays or CT/MRI scans and occasionally a sample of fluid is taken from the middle ear to test for bacteria.

## How are ear problems in cats treated?

The treatment your cat needs will depend on the cause and type of ear problem, but may include parasite control, medication (eg, antibiotics, pain relief) as tablets or drops into the ear, removal of a foreign body or surgery.

## FAQs

## Can ear problems in cats be prevented?

Some ear problems can be prevented – for example, maintaining routine parasite control and applying pet-safe sunscreen to your cat in warmer months.
