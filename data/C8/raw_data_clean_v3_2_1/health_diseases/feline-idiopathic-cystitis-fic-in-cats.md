# Feline idiopathic cystitis (FIC) in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/feline-idiopathic-cystitis-fic-in-cats

---

Feline idiopathic cystitis (FIC) is a common condition affecting cats that causes signs offeline lower urinary tract disease (FLUTD), such as blood-tinged urine, straining or painful urination, and urinating in abnormal places. FIC is a complex condition caused, in part, by stress and can be very painful for affected cats.

Feline idiopathic cystitis explained

Feline idiopathic cystitis (FIC) is thought to be similar to ‘interstitial cystitis/bladder pain syndrome’ in humans and can be difficult to manage. It causes inflammation of the bladder and significant pain. Importantly, cats with FIC do not have a bacterial infection and should not be given antibiotics. Urinary tract infections can cause similar signs, but are far less common in cats.

If the inflammation is severe, it can lead toblockages of the urinary tract, which is an emergency and can be life-threatening if left untreated.

## What are the signs of FIC?

#### The signs of FIC are similar to other causes ofFLUTD

Difficulty passing urine. Your cat may strain, scratch in the litter tray for long periods, and/or cry out in pain

Passing urine more frequently, often in small amounts

Blood in the urine (this isn’t always visible and may only be picked up on tests performed by the vet)

Urinating outside of the litter tray and in unusual or inappropriate places

Overgrooming, often of the belly or genital area, sometimes to the point of causing hair loss

Straining to pass urine. Cats with inflammation due to FIC may have a blockage due to muscle spasm or from a urethral plug. This is mainly seen in male cats, as their urethras (the tube that connects the bladder to the outside) are longer and narrower than in female cats, and are more likely to become blocked. If this occurs, it should be treated as anemergency, and your cat should be seen by your veterinary team immediately

Behaviour changes – your cat may show behaviour changes such as loss of litter tray training, lashing out or being reactive, irritation, or be quieter than normal and reluctant to play or interact

Equally, if your cat is trying and failing to urinate or straining and producing just spots of urine, they need to be seen urgently by your veterinary team.

When it comes to FIC, it’s not unusual for cats to develop recurrent episodes. Signs may come on quite rapidly and then naturally reduce over 5-10 days, only to recur.

## What causes FIC?

#### FIC is a disease of unknown cause (idiopathic). That said, several abnormalities are common in cats with FIC and may contribute to the disease

Stress– there’s reason to believe that stress plays an important role in FIC, just as it does in people with bladder pain syndrome. In many cats with FIC, an episode of the disease may be triggered by a stressful event, and even where a direct association is difficult to make, stress likely plays a role. Cats kept solely indoors and cats living with one or more other cats, are typical examples of where stress can occur, even if there aren’t obvious signs

Abnormal stress responses – there’s also evidence that cats affected by FIC don’t handle stress in a ‘normal’ way due to their genetic makeup and early life experiences

Defective bladder lining – in cats with FIC, the mucus layer that protects the bladder wall is defective. This can lead to damage to the underlying cells, and the development of ulceration and irritation/inflammation

Nerve (neurogenic) inflammation – nerves in the bladder wall may be stimulated, either by direct irritation of the bladder lining, or by stimulation from the brain (for example, in response to stress)

We are learning more all the time about this complex disease, which likely occurs in susceptible cats living in stressful environments.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat is showing any of the signs listed above, contact your veterinary team immediately.

How your vet will diagnose FIC

There’s no diagnostic test that will confirm if a cat is suffering from FIC, so a diagnosis can only be made by ruling out other recognised causes of FLUTD. FIC tends to occur for the first time in young adult cats.

#### Cats should be investigated through

Urine testing

X-rays of the bladder (sometimes including contrast studies where a special dye is put into the bladder)

Ultrasound of the urinary tract (kidneys and bladder).

Taking a full behavioural history and looking at potential causes of stress, as well as the location and management of litter trays and otherresources

Treatment options

There are no medications available that specifically treat FIC, but in some cases, medication may have some benefits as it is a very uncomfortable condition for cats. In most cases, medication should only be used if dietary and environmental changes haven’t improved your cat’s condition. Giving multiple medications can actually cause a cat more stress.

FIC is a painful condition, so in acute (sudden short-term) episodes, and especially if your cat is showing signs of discomfort, the use of pain relief (prescribed by your vet) may help your cat.

## How is FIC managed?

The best approach to managing FIC in cats is with what we call ‘multimodal’ treatment - this means making several changes to reduce the chance that FIC will recur. Although medication may be used, long-term studies have shown that few drugs have any real impact on the disease. What is important, is to focus on your cat’s diet and home, as these aspects have a crucial role to play.

Dietary changes for cats with FIC

Encouraging more frequent urination and producing urine that’s more dilute (and therefore potentially less irritant to the cells lining the bladder) can help cats suffering from FIC. This can partly be achieved by changing the diet. Changing to a wet diet (tins or sachets) rather than a dry diet is important, along with other measures toincrease water intake. Always consult your veterinary team before making changes to your cat’s diet.

In addition to the water content of the food, some diets are designed to help in cases of FLUTD, but again take your veterinary team’s advice and never change your cat’s diet during an episode of FIC or other illness. Wait until they are feeling better and eating well.

#### Cats often develop a strong preference for certain diets, which means changing your cat to a new diet may be difficult. Follow our top tips to help your cat change diet

Always make a change in diet gradual – this may take at least several days, and sometimes will need to be done over a few weeks if your cat is quite fussy

Offer both foods (old and new) in small amounts next to each other, and slowly reduce the amount of the old diet and increase that of the new diet

You can also mix a very small amount of the new food with your cat’s old food, but this can deter some cats from eating, so try a small amount first and mix well

With either method, just make sure to increase the amount of the new food slowly

Warming the food to body temperature (around 30°C) can help encourage some cats to eat

If necessary, talk to your vet about using medication to increase your cat’s appetite to make the transition easier

Other tips to increase water intake

Make sure fresh water is always available – cats should beencouraged to drinkby offering water from different bowls

Use flavoured waters (for example, flavoured with chicken or tuna – without stock or salt), hydration supplements or water fountains to encourage drinking

Add more water to the food (only if your cat will tolerate this and it doesn’t affect their appetite)

Home changes – drinking and urinating

Stress plays a very important role in triggering FIC, so making changes to the home to help reduce stress and encourage your cat to drink and urinate more frequently is important.

Placing the water bowl away from the wall allows the cat to see what is happening in the room around them

To encourage cats to drink, offer water in different places where your cat is comfortable to spend time. Avoid noisy places or anywhere close to a litter tray. Ideally, provide water (and food) in several locations and use ceramic bowls rather than metal or plastic, which can leave an unpleasant smell or taste. Try using shallow bowls rather than deep-sided bowls, so that your cat can see what’s going on around them as they drink and their whiskers don’t brush against the sides of the bowl, which some cats find irritating. Move bowls and water fountains away from walls so the cat can see the room when drinking.

Make sure your cat has every chance to urinate frequently. In multi-cat households, there needs to be at least one litter tray for every cat in the home and one extra (ideally more than this for cats with urinary problems). Try putting litter trays in different locations and use different types of litter to find the location and type of litter your cat prefers. Most cats prefer sandy-type litters. Avoid putting the litter tray in noisy or busy areas, as cats will generally try to avoid these places.

Home changes – reducing stress

It’s important to identify and modify or avoid any specific stress triggers in your cat’s home – this could be another pet in the house, sudden changes in diet, overcrowding, and even our own stress or changes to the people in the house. Where possible, if specific stress triggers are identified, they should be minimised or avoided. Discuss this with your veterinary team.

The single most common cause of stress in cats with FIC is conflict with another cat in the household. This may be difficult to detect, as cats generally avoid fighting, but should always be suspected in a cat with FIC where there are other cats in the same house. Although we know it’s difficult, in some cases, rehoming the cat with FIC to a single-cat household should be seriously considered as this may dramatically improve the situation. Cats may also be stressed by cats outside the home. Microchip cat flaps can prevent intruders from entering the house, and gardens can be cat-proofed in some situations.

#### Cats that spend a large amount of time indoors are also prone to stress. Domestic cats often live in confined spaces with little to occupy their time and their minds – this lack of ‘environmental enrichment’ can cause significant stress. Simple measures can help, such as

Spending time playing with your cat regularly – set aside time each day and use new and different toys

Allowing your cat some outdoor access, even in an enclosed run (catio) or fenced garden, to provide interest and stimulation

Making changes to your cat’s home so that there’s plenty to keep them occupied and places for them to rest. Cats need space and need to be able to control their environment, at least to some extent. It’s important that they can explore their home and have hiding places – ideally in elevated locations (for example, on top of furniture or cupboards)

Using synthetic feline pheromones may help – either as a spray on bedding and furniture, and/or as a plug-in diffuser to help reduce stress and anxiety

Cats with FIC may benefit from referral to a veterinary behaviourist.

## FAQs

## What’s the outlook for cats with FIC?

With increased water intake and environmental enrichment to relieve boredom and stress, most cats with FIC will improve significantly. In general, cats will also improve as they get older. Some very severe cases may be more difficult to manage and should be discussed with the veterinary team and, if necessary, a feline specialist veterinarian and behaviourist.

Additional resources and practical tips

#### Your veterinary team may ask you to collect a urine sample from your cat, if possible. If so, follow our steps below

## How to collect a urine sample from your cat

#### You will need

Non-absorbable litter (can be purchased online or from your veterinary clinic)

A urine pot (if you’ve bought non-absorbable litter, it may come with one - if not, pick one up from your veterinary clinic)

#### How to collect the sample

Fill your cat’s litter tray with non-absorbable litter

Once your cat has used the tray, tip the litter tray so that the urine collects in one corner and use the pipette to collect a sample

Put the sample into the urine pot and seal the lid tightly

Write your cat’s name, the date, and the time the sample was collected on the label

## Glossary of Terms

- Cystitis– inflammation of the bladder (not always caused by bacteria)

- Dysuria– difficulty passing urine (signs may include straining, crying out in pain and scratching in the litter tray for long periods of time)

- Pollakiuria– passing urine more frequently, often in small amounts

- Haematuria– passing blood in the urine

- Periuria– urinating outside of the litter tray and in unusual or inappropriate places

- Stranguria– straining to pass urine

- Urethra- the tube between a cat’s bladder and outside to pass urine

- Urolith– stones in the urinary tract

- Glycosaminoglycans (GAGs)– cells that make up the lining of the bladder wall and act as a protective barrier

- Biopsy- tissue sample
