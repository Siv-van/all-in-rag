Cats commonly suffer from problems with their urinary system. Feline lower urinary tract disease (FLUTD) is a term used to describe a combination of signs, such as passing urine more frequently, blood-tinged urine or urinating outside the litter tray or with pain. It is not a diagnosis and has several causes that will need investigating by your veterinary clinic.

Feline Lower Urinary Tract Disease explained

Feline lower urinary tract disease (FLUTD) isn’t a specific disease, but a term used to describe the signs of conditions that can affect the urinary bladder and/or urethra of cats.

Although many different diseases can affect the lower urinary tract, many cats develop a disease without any obvious underlying cause – known as ‘feline idiopathic cystitis‘ or FIC, which can be difficult to manage. FIC is the most common cause of FLUTD.

## What are the signs of FLUTD?

Cat overgrooming, showing a bald belly and genital area

#### Cats with FLUTD usually show one or more of the following signs

- Difficulty passing urine. Your cat may strain, scratch in the litter tray for long periods, and/or cry out in pain

- Passing urine more frequently, often in small amounts

- Blood in the urine (this isn’t always visible and may only be picked up on tests carried out by your vet)

- Urinating outside of the litter tray and in unusual or inappropriate places

- Overgrooming, often of the belly or genital area, sometimes to the point of causing hair loss

- Straining to pass urine. This may indicate aurethral obstruction (blockage), which is an emergency - if your cat is showing these signs, take them to your vet immediately

- Behaviour changes – your cat may show behavioural changes such as loss of litter tray training, aggression, irritation, or be quieter than normal and reluctant to play or interact

## What causes FLUTD?

Urinary stones surgically removed from the cat’s bladder

#### Some of the more common causes of FLUTD include

Feline idiopathic cystitis (FIC)- in many cats (around 60-70%), no specific underlying disease can be identified as the cause of FLUTD, and these cats are classified as having inflammation of the bladder without a known cause. Stress plays a major role in this condition.

Urolithiasis (urinary tract stones)– just as with humans, cats can develop stones in their kidneys, bladder or urethra (tube that connects the bladder to the outside). The most common stones affecting cats are calcium oxalate and struvite (around 80-90% of cases of bladder stones). In general, bladder stones account for around 10-15% of FLUTD cases.

Bacterial infections– bacterial cystitis is the most common cause of lower urinary tract disease in many animals but is relatively uncommon in cats. Most cases of FLUTD do not require antibiotics. Bacterial infections of the bladder tend to be diagnosed in older cats and cats with other conditions, such as chronic kidney diseaseordiabetes mellitus.

Urethral plugs– ablocked urethrain male cats may occur as the result of a ‘urethral plug’. This is where an accumulation of proteins, cells, crystals and debris in the urine combines to form a plug that can’t be passed. Other causes of urethral obstruction (blockage) include small bladder stones becoming lodged in the urethra or severe muscle spasms.

Anatomical defects– sometimes a defect in the lower urinary tract that a cat is born with may cause signs of disease.

Cancer– although uncommon, a tumour affecting the bladder or urethra can be a cause of FLUTD, particularly in older cats.

## Which cats are most at risk of FLUTD?

#### FLUTD is thought to affect around 1-3% of cats each year, so is among the more common reasons for a cat to visit the veterinary clinic. Cats of any age, breed and gender can be affected by FLUTD, but in general, the disease is more common in

Middle-aged cats

Neutered cats

Overweight cats

Cats which take little exercise

Cats with little or no access outside

Cats that eat a dry diet

How your vet will diagnose FLUTD

#### Remember, FLUTD is not a diagnosis, but a collection of signs that need further investigation to try and find out the underlying cause. Some of the tests your vet may perform include

Urine testing– collecting a urine sample to analyse is an important step. Your vet may ask you to collect a sample from the litter tray, or may use a technique called ‘cystocentesis’, where they place a very fine needle into the bladder to take a sample of urine. This is a simple procedure, and most cats don’t even notice when it’s done

X-rays– some urinary stones are easily seen on x-rays, but in some cases, a procedure called ‘contrast radiography’ under anaesthetic is also needed. This is where a contrast dye is used to outline the urethra and the bladder and can help to diagnose some types of bladder stones, urethral strictures, and tumours

- Radiograph image of urinary stones

- Ultrasound– an ultrasound scan can help identify urinary stones and thickening of the bladder

- Blood tests– sometimes needed to check a cat’s underlying health, for example, looking for signs of kidney disease

- Biopsies– sometimes, taking a biopsy of the bladder wall may be necessary, especially if an underlying tumour is suspected

## When to contact your veterinary team

Make an appointment with your veterinary team if your cat is showing any of the signs above. And remember - if your cat is trying and failing to pass urine, or only passing very small amounts of urine, this is an emergency, and your cat needs to be seen immediately.

## How is FLUTD treated?

Treatment will depend on the underlying cause. In general, increasing water intake and encouraging more frequent urination are necessary in all cases of FLUTD. This may be helped by feeding wet (tinned or sachet) foods rather than dry foods, encouraging drinking, correcting obesity, encouraging exercise (for example, throughplay), and encouraging urination by considering how best to use litter trays. Always discuss diet changes with your veterinary team.

Once the underlying cause is identified, specific treatment can also be given to improve your cat’s condition - for example, removal of bladder stones through surgery and/or feeding a special diet.

Ongoing Care

As mentioned above, encouraging more frequent urination and increasing your cat’s water intake are essential to your cat’s urinary health moving forward. In addition to this, ifstressis a factor, taking the necessary steps to reduce your cat’s anxiety is vital.

## FAQ

## What’s the outlook for cats suffering with FLUTD?

The outlook depends on the underlying cause. In less severe cases, most cats will live a fairly normal life with some lifestyle changes and veterinary intervention where required.

## Glossary of Terms

- Cystitis– inflammation of the bladder (not always caused by bacteria)

- Dysuria– difficulty passing urine (signs may include straining, crying out in pain and scratching in the litter tray for long periods of time)

- Pollakiuria– passing urine more frequently, often in small amounts

- Haematuria– passing blood in the urine

- Periuria– urinating outside of the litter tray and in unusual or inappropriate places

- Stranguria– straining to pass urine

- Urethra- the tube between a cat’s bladder and outside to pass urine

- Cystitis– inflammation of the bladder

- Urolith– stones in the urinary tract

- Biopsy- tissue sample
