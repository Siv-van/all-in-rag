# Chronic upper respiratory tract disease

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/chronic-upper-respiratory-tract-disease

---

Chronic upper respiratory tract (URT) disease, also known as chronic nasal discharge, is a relatively common problem in cats and can have many causes.

URT refers to a chronic (long-term) disease affecting the nose or the nasopharynx (the air passage immediately behind the nose). One of the most common forms is calledchronic rhinitis. In this condition, it is suspected that previous viral infection (for example, with feline herpesvirus – FHV) causes damage to the delicate mucosal lining inside the nose, and even to the fine turbinate bones within the nose. The chronic signs relate to secondary bacterial infection that develops as a result of the damage to the nasal passages. This can lead to chronic persistent or intermittent sneezing, discharge from the nose, and nasal congestion.

## What are the signs of chronic URT disease?

#### The main signs to look out for are

Discharge from the nose (clear fluid, thick mucous, occasionally tinged with blood)

Sneezing

Difficulty in breathing (dyspnoea)

Noisy breathing through the mouth due to obstructed airflow through the nose or nasopharynx (this is known asstertorous breathing)

Gagging or choking noises, particularly when eating

Change to the cat’s face shape, such as swelling of the nose

Appearing tired or not eating as much as normal

Discharge from the eyes or ears

## What are the common causes of chronic URT disease?

#### Many different diseases can cause long-term damage to the nose or nasopharynx. Some of the most important causes include

Post-viral or idiopathic rhinitis– Acute upper respiratory infections with feline calicivirus (FCV) orFHVare common in cats, especially young cats. The severity varies greatly between cats, but in some cases (especially with FHV infection) the viral infection may cause permanent damage to the delicate mucosal lining of the nose and can also damage the underlying bone. This may result in permanent changes that leave the nasal cavity susceptible to long-term recurrent secondary bacterial infection. This is termed post-viral rhinitis (nasal inflammation) and is thought to be one of the most common causes of chronic rhinitis in cats. In severe cases, the bacterial infection can spread to the underlying bone and cause osteomyelitis (infection within the bone)

Fungal infections– the most common fungal nasal infection in cats is with Crytptococcus (Cryptococcus neoformansorCryptococcus gatii). Infections with Cryptococcus gatiiare more common in tropical and sub-tropical climates, whereasCryptococcus neoformanshas a wider distribution. Infection is caused by breathing in the organism from the environment. Aspergillusand other fungal organisms can also occasionally cause nasal infections in cats

Inflammatory polyps– occasionally, multiple polyps (small swellings/growths) occur in the nasal cavity. More commonly, a large polyp develops from the middle ear or eustachian tube (the small tube that connects the nose to the middle ear) and this may grow within the nasopharynx, obstructing breathing

- Cancer (neoplasia)– the two most common forms of tumour affecting the nasal cavity arelymphomaand adenocarcinoma

Foreign bodies– in cats, the most common nasal foreign bodies are plant material such as blades of grass or grass seeds (grass awns). These may be breathed into the nose or become lodged in the nose after being vomited by a cat which has eaten them

- Allergic rhinitis– although uncommon, some cats develop allergic rhinitis that is similar to ‘hay fever’ in humans

- Dental disease– sometimes, severedental diseasecan affect the tooth root (for example, forming an abscess) and may affect the nasal cavity

Other conditions– a variety of other diseases may occur, including disease due to facial conformation (for example, extreme ‘flat-faced’ Persian cats), nasopharyngeal stenosis (where the nasopharynx becomes abnormally narrowed), and trauma

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat is showing any of the signs listed above, or if you’ve noticed your cat losing weight or being reluctant to eat, contact your vet who will be able to advise on the best next steps.

How your vet will diagnose chronic URT disease

#### Based on your cat’s history, the signs your cat is showing, and an examination, your vet may have an idea as to what the underlying problem is, and further tests may not be necessary. In some cases, however, further tests may be required. These can include

- Blood and urine tests– including investigation for Feline Leukaemia Virus (FeLV) and when appropriate. Blood tests may also help to detect other infectious causes (such asCryptococcusinfection)

- X-rays- of the nose, nasopharynx, chest and possibly the belly. These can be very valuable to look for changes within the nose and elsewhere in the body

- CT scans– provide very detailed information about the nose and surrounding structures

Endoscopy (rhinoscopy)– while anaesthetised to take x-rays or CT scans, your vet will also examine your cat more thoroughly – often by using a small endoscope (flexible camera) placed through the opening of the nose and/or through the mouth to look at the back of the nose

- Biopsy– small biopsies can be collected from tissues within the nose to be examined at an external laboratory

- It’s important to remember that many of these tests may return normal or non-specific results in cases of chronic rhinitis, as this is a disease that’s often diagnosed by excluding other possible causes.

## How is chronic URT disease treated?

#### Treatment depends on the underlying cause. Some diseases will be curable, some manageable (signs are controlled but not completely cured). Examples of treatments include

- Tumours– cancer in the nose may be treated with chemotherapy or radiotherapy

- Fungal infections– may respond well to appropriate therapy with anti-fungal drugs, but in some cases, this needs to be combined with surgery to remove infected tissues

- Inflammatory polyps– often respond well to surgical removal, depending on the number and location of the polyps

- Foreign bodies– can usually be removed under anaesthetic - in some cases, surgery may be required

- Post-viral or idiopathic chronic rhinitis– can be more difficult to manage and realistically, controlling rather than curing the disease is the primary goal

#### Treatment options that may be considered include

Intermittent antibiotic use – the disease is often associated with secondary bacterial infection and antibiotics usually improve signs. Signs often won’t resolve completely with antibiotics and may recur after a while. However, long-term use of antibiotics should be avoided as it can result in resistant (untreatable) infections

Nebulisation therapy can help – nebulisers can be purchased from your veterinary clinic or online and produce a cloud of very fine water droplets which are breathed in and help to moisten the airways in the nose. This is helpful in chronic rhinitis as nasal secretions can be very thick, so nebulisation will make sneezing more productive. Cats need to be trained to tolerate the nebuliser near their face in a similar way totraining to an inhaler device

- Human nasal decongestant drops should be avoided – these can be toxic to catsand/or cause more severe disease

- Some cats may benefit from having their nasal passages gently flushed and cleaned under anaesthetic from time to time

- In some cases, the use of corticosteroids or other anti-inflammatories in the form of nose drops, tablets or injections can help to relieve inflammation

- Good nursing care is also extremely important – for example, keeping your cat’s face clean and clear of discharges, andencouraging your cat to eatby providing warmed-up food that is strong-smelling

## FAQs

## What’s the outlook for cats with chronic URT disease?

It depends on the underlying cause. Some causes can be treated, and the problem is cured (for example, removing a blade of grass from your cat’s nose) whereas some cats will still have chronic or recurrent disease. In these cases, medication may be required to make your cat more comfortable and improve their quality of life.

## Glossary of Terms

- Chronic- long term

- Nasopharynx- the air passage immediately behind the nose

- Dyspnoea- difficulty in breathing

- Stertorous breathing- noisy breathing through the mouth due to obstruction to airflow through the nose or nasopharynx

- Mucoid- like mucus

- Purulent- containing pus

- Rhinitis- inflammation of the mucous membranes of the nose

- Osteomyelitis- infection within the bone

- Polyps- small inflammatory swellings/growths

- Rhinoscopy- examination of the inside of the nose using a thin, tube-like instrument with a camera and light on the end
