# Persian cats and brachycephaly

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/persian-cats-and-brachycephaly

---

The Persian has a stocky body and is bred to have a relatively flat-faced appearance (brachycephaly), which, when severe, can lead to multiple health problems.

Persians are also characterised by having a very long coat with a very thick undercoat. Persian cats require commitment from their caregiver to help them with grooming. Many cannot keep their coat matt-free on their own, and the corner of the eye and side of the nose often need regularcleaning. The bottom area and underside of the tail are also prone to becoming stained with faeces, and care needs to be taken to keep this area clean to avoid this and uncomfortable clumps of fur and faeces from developing. Regular grooming is necessary to help remove fur so it is not ingested by the cat, as hairballs can be a problem.

Brachycephaly and other breed related concerns

Many different problems can arise directly from inadequate breeding.

An extremely flat face (brachycephaly) causes the cat to have large protruding eyes with reduced ability to close the eyelids resulting in increased exposure of the surface of the eye (cornea). This contributes to eye problems, such as keratitis and corneal sequestrum. Persistent discharge from the eyes (seerunny eyes) often occurs as a result of short tear ducts, which would normally drain tears into the nose (tears are constantly produced to help protect the eye). In brachycephalic breeds, these ducts are deformed, which makes it difficult for tears to drain properly.

The abnormalities of the skull mean that not only is the nose extremely short, but the jaws and teeth are also affected. In short-nosed Persian cats, the teeth do not align properly (dental malocclusion), so they cannot bite and chew properly. Jaw deformities can lead to dental disease and potential problems with eating, drinking and grooming. Persians usually have small nostrils and a soft palate which is too long, which can lead to severe breathing problems, and their eyes run constantly causing tear-staining on the face and sometimes sores due to the skin being always wet.

Brachycephalic obstructive airway syndrome (BOAS) is common in these cats. This is where affected cats have difficulty breathing due to a very narrow external opening of the nose (stenotic nares), narrow nasal passages or nasopharynx, and/or due to a relatively long soft palate – in some cases the breathing difficulties can be severe.

Severe brachycephaly can also cause excessive skin folds on the face, which may predispose to bacterial or fungal skin infections and may also contribute to the development of idiopathic facial dermatitis in Persians (seeFeline acne and stud tail).

Inherited diseases seen in Persian cats

Polycystic kidney disease (development of cysts in the kidney and sometimes in the liver). This condition was found in more than a third of all Persian and Exotic shorthaired cats in the 1990s when screening tests became available. Using screening, breeders are now working to try to eradicate the problem. If you are thinking about getting a Persian cat, always ask the breeder to show proof that they have tested negative for PKD (e.g. in the UK, ask to see the queen’s PKD certificate).

Heart disease (thickening of the walls of the heart; seeHypertrophic cardiomyopathy and Testing).

Progressive retinal atrophyis also present in the breed and hip dysplasia is seen more commonly in Persians than in many other cat breeds.

Alpha-mannosidosis, a rare inherited lysosomal storage disease has also been seen in Persians and can be associated with neurological and ocular signs.

Chédiak-Higashi Syndrome is a rare disease that can be seen in blue-smoke Persians with yellow eyes and can cause impairment of the immune function and blood clotting time, as well as ocular abnormalities

Cryptorchidism (testicular retention)- inheritance in Persian cats is suspected so male cats with a single testicle should not be used for breeding.

Some studies have also shownincreased predispositionin Persians to several diseases when compared with other cat breeds, including feline idiopathic cystitis, calcium oxalate urinary stones, dystocia (difficulty giving birth), pyometra (uterus infection), stillborn kittens, pulmonary carcinoma (lung cancer), aspergillosis (a respiratory fungal infection), intervertebral disease and entropion (abnormal eyelids).

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you want to breed from your Persian cat, contact your vet. It is always helpful to gather as much information as possible about the cat´s family as you can and to share this information with your vet. A physical examination with a vet is the first step of the genetic health screening, and in some cases, specific genetic screening will be recommended.

Ongoing Care

Besides the daily care described in the introduction, it is important to remember that purebred cats are more likely to suffer physical health problems than other cats. Therefore, regular veterinary check-ups, at least once a year, are particularly recommended.
