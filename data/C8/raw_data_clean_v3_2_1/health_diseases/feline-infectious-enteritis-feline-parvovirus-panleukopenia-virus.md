# Feline infectious enteritis (feline parvovirus, panleukopenia virus)

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/feline-infectious-enteritis-feline-parvovirus-panleukopenia-virus

---

Feline infectious enteritis (FIE) is a disease caused by feline parvovirus (FPV) infection, which also goes by the name feline panleukopenia virus.

The virus is a huge threat in environments with large numbers of unvaccinated cats and kittens, for example, in areas with unowned cat populations or rehoming centres. The virus often causes death, particularly in unvaccinated kittens. Parvoviruses are particularly dangerous as they’re able to survive for up to several years in the environment and are resistant to many disinfectants.

## What are the signs of FPV?

#### Signs can vary, but FPV often causes severe illness and can be fatal. Kittens over three or four weeks of age and adult cats are likely to show the following signs

- Vomiting

- Diarrhoea (this may contain blood)

- High temperature

- Lethargy/tiredness

- Loss of appetite

- Wobbly kittens, caused by brain damage where the virus is spread to unborn kittens from an infected mother

- Sudden death

## What causes FPV?

Cats can catch feline parvovirus from contact with an infected cat, or indirectly by coming into contact with something an infected cat has touched, for example, food bowls, grooming equipment, or bedding. Cats infected with FPV can shed the virus for at least six weeks after infection, and while FPV doesn’t affect dogs, it can be passed on by them.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat shows any of the signs listed above, contact your veterinary team immediately.

How your vet will diagnose FPV

Diagnosis will be made based on your cat’s signs and vaccination history. Your vet may also take blood and faecal samples to check for signs of the virus and antibodies.

## What is the treatment for FPV?

Cat with oesophageal feeding tube

The main treatment for FPV is supportive while the cat’s body fights the virus. Your cat will need to be isolated in the veterinary clinic (as FPV is highly contagious) and intensively nursed. This involves ‘barrier nursing’, where protective clothing is worn, hands are washed thoroughly after handling, and one or two vet nurses who don’t handle any other cats are assigned specifically to caring for your cat (where possible).

Affected cats are usually put on a drip (IV) for intensive fluid therapy to combat dehydration and may also be given broad-spectrum antibiotics. Medication to stop your cat from feeling sick and vomiting may be given. Small meals are fed but often a feeding tube is needed as affected cats may not want to eat.

Occasionally other medications are given, such as interferons, to try and manage the viral infection and blood transfusions.

## How can FPV be prevented?

FPV can be prevented withvaccination, so ensure your cat is vaccinated regularly throughout their life according to your vet’s recommendation. As FPV can live in the environment for such a long time, don’t let your kitten outside or allow them to mix with other cats until at least one week after they’ve completed their primary vaccinations (depending on the vaccine used – your veterinary team will let you know when it’s safe to let your kitten out).

In places where there are large numbers of cats and kittens, for example, in rehoming centres, vaccination and maintaining good hygiene protocols (including effective disinfection) and the use of isolation procedures is vital to prevent an outbreak.

## What is the outlook for cats with FPV?

Cats that recover from FPV should go on to live a normal, happy and healthy life.

## Glossary of Terms

- Panleukopenia- low white blood cell count

- Interferons- naturally-produced compounds that have anti-viral effects and modify immune responses

- Gastroenteritis- inflammation of the stomach and intestines
