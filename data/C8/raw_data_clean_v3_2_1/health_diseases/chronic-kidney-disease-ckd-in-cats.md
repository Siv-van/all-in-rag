# Chronic kidney disease (CKD) in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/chronic-kidney-disease-ckd-in-cats

---

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) can occur in cats of any age but is more commonly seen in mid to older-age cats (over 7 years). Estimates suggest that around 20-50% of cats over the age of 15 have some degree of CKD present.

CKD means that the kidneys are not normal. It is one of the most common conditions affecting older cats, and with most cats, there is a gradual decline and worsening of the disease over time - how quickly cats decline varies between individual cats.

#### Like all mammals, cats have two kidneys located in the belly, which perform a wide variety of important roles, including

- Removing toxins from the blood

- Maintaining water balance

- Maintaining salt balance (and other electrolytes)

- Maintaining the acid balance of the body

- Maintaining normal blood pressure

- Producing hormones

- Maintaining a normal red blood cell count

- Blood is constantly filtered through the kidneys to remove toxic waste products, producing urine in the process. To prevent dehydration, the kidneys also return water to the body, concentrating the urine.

So, we can see what a vital role the kidneys play in keeping cats healthy. Fortunately, in healthy animals - just as with humans - one kidney can be completely removed without causing any problems. In addition to this, around ⅔ to ¾ of the total functioning kidney tissue of both kidneys needs to be lost before signs of CKD will be seen.

## What are the signs of CKD?

#### The signs of CKD are often subtle and mild but can worsen over time. While rare, in some cats, signs may develop suddenly. The most common signs to look out for are

Weight loss

Poor appetite (inappetence)

Lethargy/tiredness

Increased thirst (polydipsia)

Increased urination (polyuria)

As cats with CKD struggle to concentrate their urine, they drink more to make up for this, which then leads to them needing to urinate more.

#### Other signs may include

- Poor coat condition

- High blood pressure (hypertension)

- Vomiting

- Bad-smelling breath (halitosis)

## What causes CKD in cats?

#### In most cats, the exact cause of CKD is unknown. However, some causes are well-recognised. These include

- Polycystic kidney disease (PKD) - an inherited disease seen in Persian and related cats, where normal kidney tissue is gradually replaced by multiple fluid-filled cysts

- Kidney tumours (cancer) – for example, lymphoma (a solid tumour of white blood cells) can affect the kidneys

- Infections – bacterial infection of the kidneys (known as ‘pyelonephritis’) may lead to enough damage to cause CKD

- Toxins – certain toxins and drugs can damage the kidneys

- Glomerulonephritis – the glomeruli (individual units within the kidneys that filter the blood) become damaged for several reasons and if prolonged, can lead to CKD

Other conditions such as birth defects affecting the kidneys, trauma, and hypercalcaemia (high blood calcium) may also cause CKD.

If it is possible to find an underlying cause, in some cats, this can be treatable which will stop the progression of CKD. With most cats, treatment aims to manage the disease (along with any complications that arise as a result) and slow the progression.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat shows any of the signs listed above, contact your veterinary team. For cats that have been diagnosed with CKD, if you see a worsening of their signs or any new signs, speak to your veterinary team as further check-ups/tests may be needed.

How your vet will diagnose CKD

Your vet will diagnose chronic kidney disease through blood and urine sample testing. They will be looking for raised levels of certain substances (urea and creatinine) in the blood along with less-concentrated (dilute) urine. At this stage, your vet may also be able to understand which stage of CKD your cat is at and look for other complications that may have developed.

As high blood pressure is common in cats with CKD, your veterinary team should check your cat’s blood pressure. They may also consider further tests such as x-rays, ultrasounds, or biopsies, depending on your cat’s circumstances.

As CKD is so common in cats, routine screening of all mature and older cats can help early diagnosis, which in turn may prolong a good quality of life. Yearly or twice-yearly routine veterinary check-ups are important, and as your cat begins to get older, urine samples, blood pressure and body weight must be monitored at each visit.

## How is CKD treated?

If a specific cause for CKD is found (for example, a bacterial kidney infection), treatment may be possible which then stops the progression of the disease. In most cats, however, treatment is aimed at improving your cat’s signs, minimising the complications of CKD, and supporting kidney function. If your cat is dehydrated, they may need to be admitted to your veterinary practice initially and placed on a drip. Once stable, they can return home to start an ongoing management plan.

Ongoing care

If your cat has been diagnosed with CKD, regular appointments with your veterinary team are vital to pick up complications as they arise and address them. These routine check-ups are likely to include a blood pressure check, and blood and urine tests.

Feeding your cat a specific diet is important to help improve their quality of life and slow the progression of disease. In addition to this, where required, your vet may also recommend certain drug therapies, depending on your cat’s individual needs.

The role your cat’s diet plays in the management of CKD

#### Dietary management is critical in cats with CKD, and there are three main aspects to this

Water intake

Cats with CKD are more likely to become dehydrated as their kidneys are less able to conserve water. As cats get much of their water intake from their food, whenever possible, your cat should be fed tinned (or sachet) foods rather than dry foods. See ourcat caregiver guidefor more information on increasing you cat’s water intake.

Reduced phosphate content

Restricting the phosphate content in your cat’s diet can help to protect their kidneys from further damage, helping to prolong their life in the process.

Protein content

Some of the toxic products found in the blood of cats with CKD are a result of protein breakdown. So, feeding a moderately reduced protein diet may help. That said, too little protein can also be harmful, so always seek advice from your veterinary team on the best diet for your cat.

Diets designed for cats with kidney disease also often have other benefits. For example, they are high in potassium, anti-oxidants and fatty acids.

With so much to consider, we recommend feeding a specific veterinary therapeutic diet that manages all these aspects.

Water fountains can help encourage drinking

Managing dehydration

#### As we’ve mentioned, feeding a wet rather than dry diet is important to increase water intake in cats with CKD, but if they are still not consuming enough water, additional measures should be taken. These can include

- Ensuring a good supply of fresh water is always available andencouraging your cat to drinkby offering water from different bowls

- Use flavoured waters (for example, chicken or tuna without stock or salt) or water fountains to encourage drinking

- Adding more water to the food (if your cat will tolerate this without it affecting their appetite)

- Your cat may occasionally need to be admitted to your vet practice and placed on a drip

- Your cat may benefit from subcutaneous fluid therapy (fluids being given under the skin), which can be given by your vet or sometimes, at home by you

- Additional Possibilities

- Phosphate binders

- If, despite using a reduced phosphate diet, the phosphate levels in your cat’s blood remain high, your vet may suggest adding a phosphate binder to your cat’s diet.

- Potassium supplementation

Some cats with CKD develop low blood potassium levels. This can cause muscle weakness, may contribute to your cat’s lack of appetite, and can worsen CKD. Supplementation can be simply achieved by adding tablets, gel, or powder to your cat’s diet.

Controlling blood pressure

Cats with CKD are at risk of developing high blood pressure, which can lead to additional health problems including blindness and worsening of their CKD. Your cat’s blood pressure should be routinely monitored and if it is found to be high, your vet will be able to prescribe medication to treat this.

Treatment of anaemia

In cats with advanced CKD, anaemia (low red blood cell count) is quite common. More severe cases of anaemia can cause tiredness, weakness, and a poor quality of life. Depending on the cause and severity, some treatment options are available to manage your cat’s anaemia.

Treatment of nausea and vomiting

Feeling nauseous and being sick are more common in advanced CKD and can cause your cat to lose their appetite, which may significantly impact their quality of life. If your cat is suffering, your vet will be able to prescribe drugs to help control these signs.

Use of ‘ACE inhibitors’ and ARBs

Your vet may prescribe drugs that reduce the loss of protein into the urine which may have benefits in cats with CKD.

Kidney transplants

In some countries, kidney transplants are offered by specialist veterinary centres as a means of treating cats with CKD. Although this can be successful, it raises many ethical questions including where and how a donor kidney is found. In addition to this, the procedure isn’t always successful and cats with a new kidney may not necessarily survive any longer than cats that are managed with good medical supportive care.

## FAQs

## What is the outlook for cats with CKD?

Some cats with CKD can enjoy a good quality of life for months or even years with the right treatment and routine monitoring. Eventually, though, the disease will progress over time. Once it is not possible to control your cat’s signs, and their quality of life is affected, it may be kinder to considereuthanasia. If you are at all concerned over your cat’s condition, speak to your vet, who will always be on hand to support you.

Additional Resources and Practical Tips

Managing the change to a new diet

#### Cats often develop a strong preference for particular diets, and low-protein diets tend to be less palatable (tasty). This means that changing cats with CKD to an appropriate therapeutic diet can sometimes be difficult. Follow our tips below to try and make the transition easier

Always make a change in diet gradually, over 7-14 days or longer ideally

Offer both foods (old and new) in small amounts next to each other, and slowly reduce the amount of the old diet and increase that of the new diet

You can also mix a small amount of the new food with your cat’s old food, and make sure it is well mixed, but this can deter some cats from eating so trial a small amount

Only increase the amount of new food slowly. Make each step of replacing old food with an amount of new food gradual

Warming the food to body temperature (around 30°C) can help increase its palatability for some cats

If necessary, talk to your vet about using medication to increase your cat’s appetite to make the transition easier

With enough time and care, most cats can successfully transition to a new diet, and as this is such an important part of managing CKD it is worth taking time to do this properly. If your cat refuses to eat any of their new diet, they must eat something, so keep offering their old diet and contact your veterinary team for further advice.

Collecting a urine sample from your cat

If your cat has been diagnosed with CKD, your vet may ask you to collect a urine sample for testing when they go for check-ups. Follow the steps below to collect a urine sample.

## How to collect a urine sample from your cat

#### You will need

Non-absorbable litter (can be purchased online or from your veterinary clinic)

A urine pot (if you’ve bought non-absorbable litter, it may come with one - if not, pick one up from your veterinary clinic)

#### How to collect the sample

Fill your cat’s litter tray with non-absorbable litter

Once your cat has used the tray, tip the litter tray so that the urine collects in one corner and use the pipette to collect a sample

Put the sample into the urine pot and seal the lid tightly

Write your cat’s name, the date, and the time the sample was collected on the label

## Glossary of Terms

- Anaemia - reduced number of red blood cells

- Polydipsia- increased thirst

- Polyuria- increased urination

- Hypokalaemia- low blood potassium

- Hypercalcaemia- high blood calcium

- Hypertension- high blood pressure
