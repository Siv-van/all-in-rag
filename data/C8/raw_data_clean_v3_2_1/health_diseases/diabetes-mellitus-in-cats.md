# Diabetes mellitus in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/diabetes-mellitus-in-cats

---

Diabetes mellitus (or ‘sugar diabetes’) is a condition caused when either the body doesn’t produce enough insulin (a hormone needed to absorb glucose [sugar] into the cells), or when the body doesn’t respond to insulin as it should. This results in a high blood glucose (sugar) and associated signs.

Diabetes mellitus in cats explained

Insulin is produced by the pancreas (an organ close to the liver), and it’s critical in controlling and using blood glucose (sugar). Insulin is produced and released into the blood in response to increasing blood glucose levels, allowing the glucose to be taken up by cells in the body and used for energy. This helps to maintain normal levels of glucose in the blood. Without insulin, blood glucose levels rise (hyperglycaemia), and the body can’t use glucose efficiently as an energy source. Cats with high blood glucose levels can become very ill without treatment.

Diabetes is one of the most common endocrine (hormonal) disorders of cats and can usually be diagnosed and managed successfully. However, management options can be quite complex, and treatment has to be adjusted to the individual cat and caregiver.

## What are the signs of diabetes in cats?

Diabetes is more common in middle-aged and older cats and is often seen more in male cats than females. The disease is also much more common in neutered cats and overweight cats.

In some countries, Burmese cats appear to be more prone to developing diabetes.

#### The most common signs seen in diabetic cats are

Passing urine more often (polyuria)

Drinking more (polydipsia)

Weight loss

Eating more (polyphagia)

#### The severity of these signs varies between cats, and additional signs may also include

- An enlarged liver (hepatomegaly) – this may be something your vet can feel when examining your cat

- Poor coat

- Weakness, especially in the back legs (walking with hocks low to the ground)

- Diabetes may predispose cats to secondary bacterial cystitis, so they may show signs of bladder infections (straining to pass urine, passing blood in the urine, leaking urine, urinating outside the litter tray etc.)

Most diabetic cats stay well in themselves, but severe diabetes can result indiabetic ketoacidosis. Signs of diabetic ketoacidosis include vomiting, diarrhoea, anorexia (decrease or complete loss of appetite), and collapse – if you notice these signs with your cat, contact your veterinary team immediately.

## What causes diabetes in cats?

Diabetes in cats appears to be very similar to type II or non-insulin-dependent diabetes in people. Abnormalities within the pancreas affect insulin production, and in addition, ‘insulin resistance’ can reduce the ability of the body’s tissues to respond to the insulin that is produced. Together, these combine to cause diabetes.

Diabetes can also occur as a secondary disease in cats as a result of certain types of drug therapy (eg, prolonged use of corticosteroids).

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if your cat is showing any of the signs listed above or if you’re worried about their health in general. If your cat is showing signs of diabetic ketoacidosis, urgent veterinary treatment is needed.

How your vet will diagnose diabetes mellitus

In addition to the signs your cat is showing, following a full check-up and after taking a detailed history from you, your vet will need to test blood and urine samples to confirm a diagnosis of diabetes.

Many diabetic cats have an excellent quality of life and live very happily with their diabetes if they are well managed.

## How is diabetes in cats treated?

How diabetes is treated will depend on the individual cat and their owner or caregiver. Options include insulin by injection or, if suitable, liquid or tablets. When diabetes is diagnosed, all cats should be checked for any other illnesses or infections that could complicate treatment.

Insulin

Insulin is given by injection twice daily between the shoulder blades. Although a daunting prospect, caregivers are taught the technique, and most are able to give injections effectively.

Your vet (and perhaps a vet nurse or technician) will help by talking you through the procedure and letting you practice before giving insulin to your cat. Sometimes, practising by injecting water into something like an orange helps to get a feel of handling the syringe and needle and gain confidence. When it comes to injecting your cat, it’s usually easiest if they are distracted with other things (eg, eating a tasty treat), and to begin with, it may be better to have a second person who can hold your cat - although with practice this won’t be needed. Insulin pen devices are available for some types of insulin.

#### Watch our video on how to give your cat an insulin injection for more information

Storing and handling insulin

Insulin should always be kept in a fridge and never frozen. Before drawing insulin into a syringe, gently mix the bottle to get an even suspension by rolling it in your hand. Don’t shake the bottle as this may damage the insulin.

Always follow your vet’s instructions when using insulin

Don’t ever give a second injection if you’re unsure whether an injection has been given successfully. It’s better to miss a dose rather than give too much insulin. Speak to your veterinary team if you’re worried

Never alter the dose of insulin unless specifically asked to do so by your vet

The dose of insulin needed by each cat will vary and adjustments will be needed depending on how the cat responds. It can take some time to find the right dose, and this may change over time. See below for information on monitoring diabetic cats.

SGLT-2 inhibitors

A liquid or tablet given once a day can control diabetes in some cats. Not all cats are suitable to receive this treatment, as they must be otherwise healthy apart from the diabetes and not have diabetic ketosis. Your vet will assess each diabetic cat’s suitability for this treatment.

Monitoring during treatment

#### Each cat may respond differently to treatment and will need monitoring and adjustments. Various monitoring techniques are used

Blood tests at the veterinary clinic

Blood tests of blood glucose levels at home

Continuous glucose monitoring (a small device is placed on the cat’s skin to monitor glucose levels)

Urine testing

Ongoing treatment

Diabetes mellitus is not a simple disease to manage, and it requires dedication and commitment from owners.

Dietary management

If your cat is overweight or obese, it’s vital to get them down to a normal weight. This alone may resolve the diabetes (because obesity interferes with the action of insulin). Discuss a safe weight loss plan with your veterinary team and never starve or restrict your cat’s food without their advice.

Cats with diabetes also seem to benefit from a diet that’s low in carbohydrates. This can make their diabetes easier to manage, can help to reduce the insulin dose needed, and in some cats, can resolve their diabetes entirely.

Your veterinary team will be able to advise you on the best diet for your cat to either help manage their diabetes or to help them lose weight.

Hypoglycaemia

#### Giving too much insulin can cause a condition calledhypoglycaemia, where the blood glucose concentration is too low. The signs of hypoglycaemia include

- Weakness

- Disorientation

- Appearing ‘drunk’

- Collapse

- Seizures/fits

- Coma

Hypoglycaemia is life-threatening. If your cat shows any of the above signs, contact your veterinary team immediately. In the meantime, it can help to give your cat some glucose syrup (available from your veterinary clinic or a pharmacist) – it’s always best to have a small bottle of glucose syrup at home if you have a diabetic cat, but veterinary treatment must still be sought.

Long-term management of a diabetic cat

Daily routines, feeding (type of food and frequency), activity, and body weight should be kept as constant as possible.

#### From time to time, your vet will want to

Check blood samples to look at blood glucose and fructosamine levels

Check your cat’s weight

Check urine samples (for glucose and ketones)

Check your cat’s general health

#### It can help your veterinary team if you’re able to keep a daily diary of

The time of injection and the amount of insulin injected or when the SGLT-2 inhibitor is given

Your cat’s appetite and the amount of food eaten

The overall demeanour of your cat, particularly if they become lethargic or sleepier than usual

Any vomiting or diarrhoea

If possible, measure the amount of water your cat drinks each day

#### It can also be useful to record

Weight – if possible, keep a weekly note of your cat's weight (scales suitable for babies can work well)

Urine glucose – your vet may ask you to collect a urine sample from your cat occasionally so that you (or your vet) can check for glucose in the urine with a simple paper-strip test

Presence of ketones – this is important for cats treated with insulin or SGLT-2 inhibitors

## What’s the outlook for diabetic cats?

The long-term outlook for cats with diabetes mellitus varies according to how old they are, how easy it is to stabilise their diabetes, whether they have any other diseases and how severe these are.

Many diabetic cats have an excellent quality of life and live very happily with their diabetes if they are well managed.

Practical Tips

## How to collect a urine sample from your cat

#### You will need

Non-absorbable litter (can be purchased online or from your veterinary clinic)

A urine pot (if you’ve bought non-absorbable litter, it may come with one - if not, pick one up from your veterinary clinic)

#### How to collect the sample

Fill your cat’s litter tray with non-absorbable litter

Once your cat has used the tray, tip the litter tray so that the urine collects in one corner and use the pipette to collect a sample

Put the sample into the urine pot and seal the lid tightly

Write your cat’s name, the date, and the time the sample was collected on the label

## How to test your cat’s urine

Home blood glucose testing for your cat

Weight loss tips for overweight cats

Weigh your cat regularly, either at home on baby scales or at your veterinary clinic

Weigh out the daily food allowance in the morning and place it in a container to divide between the allocated meals. This way you are less likely to overfeed your cat. You can take out a few kibbles to give as treats throughout the day

Increase your cat’s exercise levels gently eg, by encouraging them toplay with toys. Little and often is generally better for weight loss

Place your cat’s food in different areas or hide kibbles around the house/room to encourage your cat to exercise more. A food ball orpuzzle feedercan also help your cat work a little to get their food and entertains them at the same time

If you have more than one cat, feed them separately and watch as they eat

Tell your neighbours your cat is on a weight loss programme (otherwise, your cat may just look for food elsewhere!)
