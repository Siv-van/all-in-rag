# Cowpox virus infection in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/cowpox-virus-infection-in-cats

---

Cowpox virus infection is an uncommon skin condition, often affecting cats that hunt. The infection causes skin changes such as scabs, small lumps and abscesses that usually disappear over time. However, if your cat’s immune system is lowered through illness or medication, severe infection can develop.

Cowpox virus infection in cats explained

There are a variety of pox viruses that infect different species. Although cowpox virus infects cattle, they are rarely a source of infection for cats. The natural host for this virus is believed to be wild rodents.

Cowpox infection in cats is uncommon, but it varies geographically, and it’s more often seen in outdoor cats that are active hunters. When rodents are more widespread (eg, in the autumn), there’s also an increased risk to cats.

## What are the signs of cowpox virus infection in cats?

Cowpox virus enters the skin through a bite wound from a rodent, usually on the cat’s head, neck or leg. The viral infection is noticeable after a few days as a small, ulcerated nodule (an abnormal skin growth/lump with broken, infected skin). This may be followed by a secondary bacterial infection, producing cellulitis (inflamed, red and sore skin) and sometimes an abscess (a swelling filled with pus).

#### As the virus develops, other signs of cowpox infection include

- Fever/high temperature

- Depression

- Reduced appetite

- Discharge from the nose

- Ulceration in the mouth, stomach and intestines

- Diarrhoea

- Pneumonia (inflammation and infection of the lungs)

- Further skin changes – small oval to circular ulcerated papules (small bumps) and plaques (flat swollen areas) up to 1 cm in size

Infection usually resolves itself over six to eight weeks. However, cats on glucocorticoids (steroids) or those that are immunosuppressed (eg, from infection withFeLVorFIV) may develop severe infections affecting multiple body systems, often with life-threatening pneumonia.

## What causes cowpox virus infection in cats?

Cowpox infection is uncommon, but is seen more in outdoor cats that are avid hunters (© Laura Watson)

Cats infected with cowpox virus are usually avid hunters that come into contact with prey known to carry the infection, especially the bank vole (Clethrionomys glareolus), field vole (Microtus agrestis) and the wood mouse (Apodemus sylvaticus), among others.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if your cat is showing any of the signs listed above or if you’re worried about their health in general.

How your vet will diagnose cowpox virus infection

Skin biopsies (surgical removal of a small piece of tissue) are generally needed to confirm a diagnosis of cowpox virus infection. These are used to check for the presence of the cowpox virus itself. Occasionally, serial blood tests can be used to show the cat has increasing antibodies (proteins produced by the body after coming into contact with a specific infectious agent eg, a virus) to the infection.

## How is cowpox virus infection treated?

In most cats, the skin changes will heal within a few weeks of the initial infection. In mild cases, no treatment is needed. In more severely affected cats, and those with secondary bacterial infections, antibiotics may be prescribed and affected skin may need to be bathed.

Cats that aren’t eating may need to be hospitalised and placed on a drip, and may also need nutritional support via a feeding tube.

## Can cowpox virus infection be passed on to people?

In general, cowpox virus infection in people is uncommon, however, up to 50% of human infections are reported to come from cats. Although the risk is low, cats with suspected cowpox infection should be isolated from other cats where possible and handled by people with a low risk of infection (eg, avoiding handling by children, elderly people or immunocompromised people). Wear disposable gloves when handling your cat and, if possible, keep them in an area that’s easy to clean.
