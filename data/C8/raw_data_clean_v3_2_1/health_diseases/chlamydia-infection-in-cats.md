Many different strains of chlamydia-type bacteria exist, and most are species-specific, meaning each strain only infects one or a small number of different animals/species.

Chlamydiainfection explained

The bacterium that infects cats is specifically adapted to them, and mainly causes eye infections and conjunctivitis. Where the disease is causing problems in colonies of cats, vaccination is available and can help to control the disease. Chlamydiais very fragile and can’t survive for any significant time in the environment, so infection is caused mainly through direct contact between animals.

## What are the signs of Chlamydiainfection?

Watery discharge from the eyes which turns to a thicker, yellow discharge as the infection worsens

Partially closed eyelids due to pain and discomfort

Severe swelling and reddening of the conjunctiva (the membranes that cover the inner surface of the eyelids)

Occasionally, mild sneezing and discharge from the nose

Your cat may also have a high temperature, causing lethargy/tiredness and a reduced appetite (although most cats remain bright and well)

The condition may occur with other infections, such ascat flu, and affected cats may show signs of both types of infection

## What disease doesChlamydiacause in cats?

Chlamydiamainly causes conjunctivitis. Left untreated, this can persist for two months or more, causing pain and damage to the eyes.

Chlamydiacan also be found in the lungs, stomach and intestines, and reproductive organs. It may occasionally contribute to infertility in breeding queens.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if your cat is showing any of the signs listed above or if you notice any ongoing problems with their eyes.

How your vet will diagnoseChlamydiainfection

To diagnoseChlamydiainfection, your vet will take a swab from your cat’s eyes for laboratory testing.

## How is Chlamydia infection treated?

Chlamydiainfection is treated with antibiotics. A course of 3-4 weeks is needed and all cats in the same household should be treated. Occasionally the antibiotics are in the form of eye drops but most affected cats need tablets/liquid forms to clear the infection.

A vaccine is available in many countries to protect cats. This doesn’t always prevent infection, but it can help to prevent severe disease. Although it’s not generally suitable for the majority of pet cats, it can be useful in high-risk situations such as catteries with persistent problems.

## Which cats are at risk of Chlamydiainfection?

Chlamydiainfection is fairly common in cats and is thought to be the cause of up to 30% of cases of chronic (long-term) conjunctivitis. However, direct contact between cats is needed to spread the disease, so it’s much more common where large groups of cats are kept together (eg, multi-cat households, breeding colonies, catteries and rehoming centres).

Cats of all ages can be infected, although the disease is seen most commonly in kittens of around 5–12 weeks of age.

## Is there a risk to people with Chlamydiainfection?

People can be infected with Chlamydia psitacci, but the bacterium that infects cats (Chlamydia felis) is highly adapted to this species. There have been some cases of human conjunctivitis following contact with a cat with Chlamydiainfection, but the risk is extremely low. Maintaining good hygiene when handling and treating infected cats is important until the infection has resolved.
