# Feline herpesvirus (FHV)

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/feline-herpesvirus-fhv

---

Feline herpesvirus (FHV, FHV-1) is a highly contagious virus that is one of the major causes ofupper respiratory infections (URIs) or cat flu in cats, together withfeline calicivirus (FCV).

This virus is widespread and causes disease in cats all over the world.

Feline herpesvirus explained

Feline herpesvirus (FHV) is a virus that mainly causes upper respiratory infections (URIs) in cats, although it can also affect the eyes and skin.

## What are the signs of FHV infection?

Upper respiratory

#### Signs of FHV vary in severity. Upper respiratorysigns include

- Discharge from eyes and nose caused by cat flu

- Sneezing

- Nasal discharge

- Excessive drooling

- Pharyngitis (sore throat)

- Lethargy/tiredness

- Loss of appetite

- High temperature (fever)

- Coughing

These signs may last for a few days or a few weeks, and your cat will continue to shed the virus for around three weeks.

Eyes

#### Signs affecting theeyesinclude

- Discharge from the eyes

- Conjunctivitis

- Corneal ulcers (outer surface of the eye) - FHV infection causes the development of multiple small branching corneal ulcers, called ‘dendritic keratitis’

- Blinking frequently and redness around the eyes

- Chlamydia infection mainly causes eye infections and conjunctivitis

- Skin

#### Signs affecting the skin are rare but include

- Skin inflammation

- Ulceration, scabs and hair loss

This is most commonly seen around the nose and mouth, but can affect other areas, such as the front legs.

## How is FHV spread?

#### The virus is readily transmitted between cats through

Direct contact – with saliva or discharge from the eyes or nose

Breathing in sneeze droplets

Sharing food bowls and litter trays

A contaminated environment (including bedding and grooming equipment) – as the virus is fragile and is likely to only survive in the environment for 1-2 days

As the virus remains in the nerve cells, most cats will be latently infected with FHV, meaning they become life-long carriers of the virus. This doesn’t tend to cause a problem with most cats, as they don’t continue to shed the virus, so they’re not a risk to other cats. That said, some cats will intermittently shed the virus again, usually following episodes of stress or when their immune system is suppressed. When the virus is shed again, cats can show mild signs.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you notice any of the signs mentioned above, or you’re worried in general about your cat’s health, contact your vet for an appointment.

How your vet will diagnose FHV

In most cases, the presence of typical signs of URI is enough for a presumptive diagnosis of FHV (and/or feline calicivirus – FCV) infection. If a specific diagnosis is needed, swabs can be taken from the eye or throat and sent to a laboratory for testing. Evidence of the virus may also be present in skin biopsies (samples) and can be useful for the diagnosis of FHV-associated dermatitis (skin disease).

## How is FHV treated?

FHV infections are often complicated by secondary bacterial infections, so supportive treatment with antibiotics may be required. Good nursing care is critical, and your cat may need to be hospitalised for a drip and nutritional support in severe cases.

Steam inhalation or nebulisation can help in cats with severe nasal congestion, and, as your cat will not be able to smell food well, using tinned or sachet foods that are gently warmed will help. Eyes should be kept lubricated and antiviral eye drops may be used. Pain relief is needed for sore throats and eyes.

Antiviral drugs can help with managing the signs of the disease. Drugs such as ‘famciclovir’ can be effective for the treatment of FHV. Antivirals may also be given as eye drops.

In colonies of cats, any cat showing signs should be isolated and barrier nursed if possible (this is where personal protective clothing such as gloves and aprons are worn, strict hygiene rules are followed, and separate bowls, litter trays and beds are used only for the affected cat).

## How can FHV be prevented?

Vaccinationfor FHV is important for all cats. Two or three injections are recommended in kittens, starting at around 8 weeks of age. Cats should then receive a booster from 6 months to one-year-old, and after that, should receive further booster vaccines every 1–3 years, depending on their risk of disease.

Vaccination doesn’t necessarily completely prevent infection with FHV, but it greatly reduces the severity of disease.

In certain situations, such as rehoming centres or breeding colonies with FHV or upper respiratory infection (URI) problems, testing and earlier vaccination may be recommended. Please seek advice from your veterinary team.

## Glossary of Terms

- Pharyngitis- inflammation of the pharynx (also known as a ‘sore throat’)

- Conjunctivitis- inflammation of the conjunctiva (also known as ‘pink eye’)
