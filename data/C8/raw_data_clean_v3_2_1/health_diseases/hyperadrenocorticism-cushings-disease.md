# Hyperadrenocorticism (Cushing's Disease)

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/hyperadrenocorticism-cushings-disease

---

Hyperadrenocorticism is a disease affecting cats and dogs that’s also known as Cushing’s disease or Cushing’s syndrome. It’s much more common in dogs and is generally easier to control, although successful treatment is possible in cats.

Hyperadrenocorticism is uncommon in cats. It develops when there’s constant, excessive production of the hormone cortisol from the adrenal glands (two small glands that can be found close to the kidneys). This is usually caused by a problem with the pituitary gland, which controls the production of adrenal gland hormones (along with many others), though in some cats it may be because of a problem with the adrenal glands themselves.

Hyperadrenocorticism can also be brought on if high doses of cortisol-like hormones are used over a long period, which is sometimes needed to control certain diseases (for example, in cats with allergies) and then it is termed ‘iatrogenic hyperadrenocorticism’.

## What are the signs of hyperadrenocorticism?

#### Naturally occurring disease is more likely to be seen in middle-aged and older cats, with female cats more commonly affected than males. Cortisol can have many different effects, so signs can vary and may initially be mild and progress over time. The most common signs are

Drinking more than normal (polydipsia) and passing urine more often than normal (polyuria)

Increased appetite

Lethargy/tiredness

Muscle loss (wasting) and weakness

Poor coat condition

Hair loss

Curling of the ear tips

Thin and fragile skin

In some cats, the skin can become so thin and fragile that it easily bruises and can also easily tear. Wounds may be slow to heal.

Many cats with hyperadrenocorticism also havediabetes mellitusbecause the excessive cortisol interferes with the effects of insulin.

## What causes hyperadrenocorticism?

Latrogenic hyperadrenocorticism

Latrogenic hyperadrenocorticism is the name given to the disease when it’s brought on by the long-term use of high doses of cortisol-like drugs (eg, prednisolone).

Naturally occurring hyperadrenocorticism

Excess production of cortisol by the adrenal glands can be caused by an adrenal tumour or, more commonly, by a tumour affecting the pituitary gland (a small gland at the base of the brain). The pituitary gland produces several different hormones includingadrenocorticotrophic hormone (ACTH), which stimulates the adrenal glands to produce cortisol. If a pituitary gland tumour causes excessive production of ACTH, this will lead to the adrenals becoming enlarged and producing excessive cortisol.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if your cat is showing any of the signs listed above, or if you’re worried about their health or behaviour in general.

How your vet will diagnose hyperadrenocorticism

#### Diagnosis isn’t straightforward. Routine blood tests can help to initially rule out some of the more common causes of the signs your cat is showing, after which, more specific tests may be run for hyperadrenocorticism. These can include

- Urine cortisol measurements

- ACTH-stimulation test

- Dexamethasone screening test

- Measuring blood concentrations of ACTH

- X-rays and ultrasound

- Unfortunately, no single test is completely accurate and reliable in diagnosing hyperadrenocorticism, so more than one test is usually needed and sometimes tests must be repeated.

## How is hyperadrenocorticism treated?

Latrogenic hyperadrenocorticismis usually managed by a controlled, gradual withdrawal of the cortisol-like drug that’s being given to treat other conditions. If necessary, for example, if your cat has allergies, your vet may need to find an alternative drug to the drug that was being used.

Withnaturally occurring hyperadrenocorticismseveral treatment options are available, which include surgery to remove the adrenal gland or pituitary gland (only available at specialist centres and universities), radiotherapy for pituitary tumours, or drug therapy. Drug therapy is mostly used to control the disease.

## What is the outlook for cats with hyperadrenocorticism?

Hyperadrenocorticism is a serious disease with a guarded outlook. The disease can rarely be cured, but with appropriate treatment, many cats respond well and may have many months or even years with an excellent quality of life. If the underlying cause is a cancerous adrenal tumour, inevitably the outlook is more guarded.

Additional Resources and Practical Tips

Your vet may ask you to collect a urine sample to take in for testing when your cat goes for check-ups. Follow the steps below to collect a urine sample.

## How to collect a urine sample from your cat

#### You will need

Non-absorbable litter (can be purchased online or from your veterinary clinic)

A urine pot (if you’ve bought non-absorbable litter, it may come with one - if not, pick one up from your veterinary clinic)

#### How to collect the sample

Fill your cat’s litter tray with non-absorbable litter

Once your cat has used the tray, tip the litter tray so that the urine collects in one corner and use the pipette to collect a sample

Put the sample into the urine pot and seal the lid tightly

Write your cat’s name, the date, and the time the sample was collected on the label
