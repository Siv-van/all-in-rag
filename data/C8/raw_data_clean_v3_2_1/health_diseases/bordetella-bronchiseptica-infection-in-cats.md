# Bordetella Bronchiseptica Infection in Cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/bordetella-bronchiseptica-infection-in-cats

---

Bordetella bronchiseptica (B. bronchiseptica) can cause respiratory disease in cats but is mainly a problem where cats are kept together in large groups, such as rehoming centres and breeding colonies. Infections can be treated with antibiotics, and for situations where it causes significant ongoing problems, a vaccine is available in many countries.

B. bronchisepticacauses disease in dogs (one of the important causes of ‘kennel cough’), cats, pigs and rabbits, and can occasionally cause disease in people. B. bronchisepticais classified as a rarezoonotic disease (a disease which can pass from animals to people).

## What are the signs ofB. bronchisepticainfection in cats?

#### Infection withB. bronchisepticausually results in

Mild sneezing

Coughing

Discharge from the nose

Discharge from the eyes

High temperature (fever)

These signs typically last 7-10 days. However, in some cats (especially in young kittens, and cats experiencing high levels of stress) infection may be more severe and can sometimes cause life-threatening pneumonia.

B. bronchiseptica can also occur withcat fluand complicate that condition.

## What causesB. bronchisepticainfection/how is it spread?

Cats infected withB. bronchisepticashed bacteria in their saliva and discharge from their nose (and in droplets when they sneeze), so it can be spread through direct contact between cats or from a cat breathing in infectious sneeze droplets.

Although the bacteria can be killed by certain disinfectants, they can survive for 1-2 weeks in the environment. So, unless kept thoroughly clean, the environment, bedding, feeding bowls, grooming utensils etc. may also be sources of infection.

B. bronchisepticacan also be spread between dogs and cats. Cats can remain infected withB. bronchisepticafor several months after signs of infection have cleared, so recovered cats can also be a potential source of infection to others.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if your cat is showing any of the signs listed above, or if you’re worried about their health in general.

How your vet will diagnoseB. bronchisepticainfection

Your vet will diagnoseB. bronchisepticainfection by taking a swab from your cat’s throat (occasionally a sample from the lungs – a lung wash) for laboratory testing.

## How areB. bronchisepticainfections treated?

B. bronchisepticainfections are treated with antibiotics. Your vet will decide which antibiotic to use based on the results of the tests. If the infection is severe, your cat may need to be hospitalised at the veterinary clinic for further supportive treatment.

## How canB. bronchiseptica infectionsbe prevented or controlled?

#### B. bronchisepticais considered a minor infection and no specific precautions are necessary for most pet cats, as the risk of infection and serious disease is low. In rehoming centres and breeding colonies it’s important to minimise the risk by

Keeping cats individually or in small, isolated groups

Maintaining excellent hygiene, with the use of appropriate disinfectants

Isolating any cats showing signs of disease

IfB. bronchisepticacauses disease and is a persistent or widespread problem, an effective vaccine is available in many countries (vaccination is via drops placed in the nose), and this can help with disease control. However, in most situations, this isn’t necessary for pet cats.
