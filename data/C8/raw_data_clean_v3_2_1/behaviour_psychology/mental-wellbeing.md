# Mental Wellbeing

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/mental-wellbeing

---

A cat’s mental wellbeing depends on feeling safe, and that sense of safety comes from being able to predict what happens around them and having a perceived sense of control over what happens to them.

Being in a new place - especially one where the cat is confined and surrounded by stressors, such as a homing centre - is stressful for all cats, and particularly for those that haven’t had positive experiences with people or unfamiliar environments. Every cat’s ability to cope is shaped by their genetics and past experiences. Some adjust quickly to confinement, some cope only for short periods, and others, despite our best efforts, may not cope at all. Recognising and responding to these differences is key to protecting mental wellbeing.

By understanding what cats need, both as a species and as individuals, we can give each one the best possible chance to feel safe and cope in our care.

Recognising distress

Distress arises when a cat experiences fear, anxiety, frustration, pain, or discomfort that they cannot escape or control. It can be physically harmful, altering the cat’s immune system and impairing their ability to fight off infection, and mentally debilitating, potentially leading to long-term psychological damage.

Cats respond instinctively to threat by fighting to repel danger, fleeing to avoid it, freezing to assess it, or fidgeting to appease or distract it. Their preferred strategy is usually to flee and avoid conflict altogether — but in a pen or foster setting, that escape option isn’t available.

Cats are well-equipped to cope with short bursts of stress, such as escaping a predator, as part of survival. But when stress continues and they can’t escape or control what’s happening, it turns into distress, a chronic state of suffering that erodes both physical health and mental wellbeing.

Common signs of long-term distress

Chronic distress can be subtle and easy to miss. The signs vary between cats, but may include increased resting, pretending to be asleep, over-eating, lack of play, hiding, over-groomingoften causing hair loss) or reduced/absence of grooming altogether, toileting outside the litter tray, conflicting behaviours occurring at the same time, or sickness behaviours (including vomiting, diarrhoea, anorexia, or decreased food and water intake)

Key Points

A cat’s mental wellbeing depends on feeling safe, which comes from being able to predict and have some control over what happens to them.

Distress occurs when a cat experiences fear, anxiety, frustration, or pain they cannot escape or control.

Confinement is inherently stressful, especially for cats with limited positive experiences of people or new places.

Each cat’s ability to cope varies; some adjust quickly, others only for a short time, and some may not be able cope at all.

Recognising and responding to individual differences helps protect each cat’s mental wellbeing and quality of life
