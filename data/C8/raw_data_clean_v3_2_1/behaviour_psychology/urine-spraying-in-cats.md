# Urine spraying in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/urine-spraying-in-cats

---

Urine spraying is a form of territorial marking behaviour, performed by adult cats. It likely helps cats communicate without being in physical proximity, so they’re able to coordinate their movements within a territory – effectively ‘timesharing’ to help avoid any kind of conflict between one another, which may result in injury.

However, urine spraying can also reflect a cat’s emotional state. While it’s normal to see cats spraying urine outdoors against bushes, fences and other objects, if your cat starts to spray indoors, it’s an indication that your cat doesn’t feel secure, and is feeling stressed by something. Cats spray during socially stressful situations, possibly to increase their self-assurance and as a coping strategy for reducing stressful emotions, such as anxiety and frustration.

## What does it look like when a cat sprays urine?

Typically, you’ll see your cat backing up to a vertical surface (often after sniffing the area intensely), standing with raised hindquarters and with their tail up and quivering. They may ‘tread’ with their back legs while squirting a stream of urine (usually, less than 2mLs). Occasionally, cats will urine mark on horizontal surfaces (often on objects or items of clothing on the floor), either from a squatting position or by standing and spraying.

It’s not always easy to find spray marks, you may just be aware of the smell. You may see a small trickle of a yellow-brown, sticky substance on a skirting board, radiator or door with a small wet patch on the floor. However, in some cases, there may be a large puddle of urine on the floor instead (although this can also happen from urinating to relieve the bladder). Common targets for urine spraying include electrical items, full-length curtains, sofas or chairs, plastic bags, clothing and any new items in the home.

## Is it only male cats that spray urine?

Both male and female cats can urine spray, although it’s more common in males. It’s also more commonly seen in cats that have not been neutered, who are often more territorial and to provide chemical information about their readiness to mate with other cats. There’s usually a significant decrease in urine spraying after castrating a male cat or spaying a female cat.

## Is it a common problem?

In neutered cats, around 10% of males and 5% of female cats urine spray, but the likelihood of urine spraying increases in direct proportion to the number of cats within a household. This is because the greater the number of cats in the home, the more likely there will be an increase in social stress, as more cats are competing for space and resources.

## What should I do if my cat sprays urine indoors?

The first thing you should do is contact your veterinary team. If your cat is young and hasn’t been neutered, the most likely cause is that your cat is becoming sexually mature. Neutering should prevent your cat from urine spraying – speak to your veterinary team for further advice.

It’s important not to rule out an underlying medical cause for your cat’s urine spraying – for example, feline lower urinary tract disease, or chronic kidney disease. For this reason, your vet may want to test a sample of your cat’s urine to rule out any underlying disease and will be able to provide you with the advice and equipment to collect a sample. If medical causes have been ruled out, your veterinary team may be able to advise you on how to prevent spraying, or refer you to a qualified clinical animal behaviourist for further help.

## My cat doesn’t look stressed?

Cats are self-reliant as a species and don’t readily show when something’s wrong because this can make them vulnerable – this makes them very good at hiding signs of illness, pain or stress. Urine spraying is a sign that all is not right in your cat’s world. Read our guide on stress in cats to understand more about the signs and causes of stress.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if your cat starts spraying indoors. They will need to rule out a medical problem, and if one isn’t found, advise you accordingly on your cat’s behaviour or arrange a referral to a qualified clinical animal behaviourist.

## FAQs

## If my cat has been referred to a qualified clinical animal behaviourist, is there anything I can do while I wait for my cat to be seen?

#### Once you’ve seen your vet about this problem, it’s best to wait for the behaviourist to assess your specific situation and recommend changes that are tailor-made to suit the needs of your cat and their circumstances. However, there are a few general things that may help in the meantime

Blot away any urine, and then either use an enzymatic spray as per the instructions from the manufacturer or clean sprayed areas with a 10% biological washing powder solution, rinse thoroughly before drying and spray with surgical spirit. This will help to neutralise the smell of the urine and help prevent further episodes of marking as your cat will be less attracted to the area.

If possible, discard any heavily soiled fabric (rugs, carpets, chairs etc) as they will still be attractive for your cat to spray on, even when cleaned.

Once clean, try using synthetic feline pheromone spray to treat sprayed sites, according to the manufacturer’s instructions

Use a synthetic feline pheromone diffuser to help reduce stress

Provide safe indoor litter trays – one for each cat in the household plus one extra. Place these trays in different locations in your home, away from food, water and cat beds. In multi-cat homes where covered trays are used, remove the door (if there is one) from the tray to help prevent the cat from feeling trapped. You could also offer some open litter trays, as some cats prefer to use these – choice is good!

If you have a multi-cat household, ensure ALL resources (beds, food and water bowls, scratching posts, litter trays, high places to sit, hiding places, toys) are provided to the same formula (one per cat + one extra in different locations)

If full-length curtains have been sprayed on, once cleaned, consider pinning them up temporarily to avoid repeated spraying

If your cat has seen strange cats outside through full-length glass doors or windows, place static, opaque plastic film, or cardboard, over the lower section so they can’t see out and cats outside can’t see in.

You may also want to add some cover (such as plant pots) around the cat - flap area to give your cat some security when they exit/enter your home

My cat has stopped spraying now after a few weeks – do I still need to contact my veterinary team?

Urine spraying can come and go, depending on the presence or absence of whatever is stress ing your cat, so it’s worthwhile contacting your veterinary team regardless. Often the problem returns, and the longer you wait before tackling the problem, the more complicated it becomes. Urine spraying is difficult to resolve as it is normal behaviour for cats, and often, the stress triggers may be outside your control (such as cats outside). However, before a known and anticipated stressful event, it may help to use a synthetic feline pheromone diffuser for cats with a history of urine spraying when they’re stressed or anxious.

Additional resources and Practical tips

Your vet may ask you to collect a urine sample to take in for testing when your cat goes for a check-up. Follow the steps below to collect a urine sample.

## How to collect a urine sample from your cat

#### You will need

Non-absorbable litter (can be purchased online or from your veterinary clinic) - you may need several bags (they are usually quite small) to ensure the litter is deep enough to attract your cat to use it

A urine pot (if you’ve bought non-absorbable litter, it may come with one - if not, pick one up from your veterinary clinic). Do not use storage containers from home, as previous contents of the container can affect the urine test results

#### How to collect the sample

Thoroughly empty your cat’s litter tray of all old litter substrate, and then wash and rinse out the tray, ensuring there is no chemical residue left that can affect the urine test results

Fill your cat’s litter tray with non-absorbable litter

Once your cat has used the tray, tip the litter tray so that the urine collects in one corner and use the pipette to collect a sample

Put the sample into the urine pot and seal the lid tightly

Write your cat’s name, the date, and the time the sample was collected on the label and take the sample to your vet straight away
