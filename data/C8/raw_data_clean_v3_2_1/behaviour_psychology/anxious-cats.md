# Anxious cats

A cat’s behaviour and character are shaped by a mix of influences, including their genetics and experiences as a kitten. These elements create the cat’s unique personality. For example, bold, confident, reactive or sociable. Most adult cats, with the right upbringing, are confident enough to face what life throws at them. However, if a cat isn’t exposed to a full range of normal domestic situations from an early age, they’ll potentially flee from even the most harmless of things.

Cats assess danger quickly and act accordingly because of their highly developed survival instinct. Their response to perceived danger is usually to escape, rather than stay and fight. As a species, they are solitary survivalists and the potential of physical injury could pose a serious risk to their survival if they cannot obtain food or find shelter. Some cats are more prone to anxious behaviour, and, even when socialised from a young age, they don’t learn that domestic life is fairly safe for them.

Cats usually favour the option of escaping if they feel threatened

## What is anxiety in cats?

A state of anxiety means your cat anticipates something bad happening, particularly in unfamiliar situations. This feeling of anxiety can increase to fear if your cat decides that a specific stimulus is dangerous.

Fear is the natural trigger for the release of adrenaline. This prepares the body for tackling life-threatening situations by fighting, escaping, freezing (to remain undetected) or attempting to make peace with the enemy. Cats usually favour the option of escaping.

## When I choose a kitten how do I know that it won’t be a nervous adult?

It’s impossible to guarantee how your kitten will develop, but you can limit the risk if you choose wisely. Before visiting a kitten, ask what steps are being taken to socialise the kitten and expose it to normal domestic life.

Knowing a kitten’s background can make a difference in deciding whether you can help them or not. For example, some centres look for homes for feral kittens (born to a cat living wild). These kittens haven’t had contact with people in the early weeks of life, so handling and confinement can cause fear. A great deal of time and patience is required to get feral kittens to respond, and it may never be possible to make them confident and comfortable enough to live alongside people and other animals in a confined space. ## How should I behave around my anxious cat?

It’s better to give a nervous cat a feeling of invisibility, to allow them to move around their home without being the focus of attention. This means no direct eye contact, or verbal or physical communication unless your cat directly initiates it.

Food treats can create positive associations and develop a bond between you and your cat. Offering small amounts of meat, fish or other tasty treats in a bowl can encourage your cat to explore various rooms in the home or spend time close to you. Once they are comfortable eating treats scattered near you, you can then try offering them the same treats on the palm of your outstretched hand to encourage your cat to investigate and take food directly from you (if they seem comfortable doing this).

Playing with your catcan also reap rewards, as many cats find games irresistible. Using a toy attached to a string and a long rod allows your cat to enjoy the game fully without feeling that you are too close to them.

Never lose your temper or try to force interactions too quickly – this will reinforce your cat’s previous fears. Build on your success gradually. Remember – cats feel safe in high places, so provide them with a high perch where they can sit safely and watch the world go by beneath.

## FAQs

## Why does my cat hide under the bed?

Hiding under a bed may seem like a very sad and lonely existence to us, but if your cat feels like this is an escape from danger and a safe place, it will make them feel relieved

My cat hides under the bed most of the time, especially if there’s lots of activity in the house. Is this normal?

When you first bring a cat or kitten home, they may be quiet and wary for the first few days or weeks while they get used to their new home. However, some cats remain very fearful despite a gentle welcome and time to settle in. They may run and hide as soon as someone enters the house or if there’s a sudden noise. Such cats often spend a lot of time under the bed, behind or under the sofa or on top of the wardrobe, hiding from the world.

It’s important to understand a nervous cat’s expectations of life. Hiding under a bed may seem like a sad and lonely existence to us, but if your cat feels like this is an escape from danger and a safe place, it will help them feel less vulnerable.

As cats are a solitary species, they don’t have a pack to back them up if things go wrong. When they feel threatened, to them, their best chance of survival is to run, hide and stay quiet until the danger has passed. To stop your cat from running, you’ll need to offer something more rewarding than the feeling of safety and relief a cat feels when they follow their instincts, which can be very difficult.

## Why is my cat scared of feet?

## Was my nervous cat ill-treated when it was young as it seems to be terrified of feet?

It’s impossible to say if a particular individual was ill-treated as a kitten, but it’s more likely that your cat is nervous because of their genetics, lack of early socialisation with humans, or both. They may appear nervous of feet simply because that’s the part of your body closest to them.

## Is it a good idea to be quiet around my anxious cat?

## Is it a good idea to be quiet and walk slowly when I’m around my anxious cat?

Owners often behave a certain way around anxious cats, moving quietly around the home and speaking in hushed voices to avoid scaring them. Unfortunately, the air of tension this creates may increase your cat’s anxiety.

Acting normally and being relaxed will have a more positive impact. Remember to avoid moving them from their hiding place or focusing on them when they enter a room, as this will make you appear more threatening. Any interactions and contact should be initiated by your cat so that they feel in control.

## My cat has become more anxious than it used to be?

My cat was never anxious in the past but has recently been behaving as if they’re scared of everything?

Any behaviour change, particularly one as dramatic as this, shouldn’t be ignored. Contact your veterinary team and arrange for your cat to have a thorough examination. This behaviour may be a sign of illness or pain, however if no physical cause is found, your veterinarian will refer you to a local, qualified clinical animal behaviourist who will be able to help.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you’re concerned about your cat’s behaviour (and consequently, their quality of life), speak to your veterinary team for advice. They may recommend using synthetic feline pheromones to reinforce a message of safety and familiarity in your home, and if required, can refer your cat to a local, qualified clinical animal behaviourist to provide you with strategies to manage your cat’s anxiety.
