# Cat Communication

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/cat-communication

---

Cats communicate in a variety of ways, and to properly understand cats, it’s vital to appreciate how and why they communicate.

## What are the different ways in which cats communicate?

#### Cat communication can be broken down in four ways

Visual signalling

Tactile (touch)

Vocal

Olfactory (smell or scent)

Visual signalling

Visual signals are important for cats to communicate their mood and intentions. Body postures and language, facial expressions, pupil size, and the ability to stand the hair up on some areas of the body are all visual signals for cats.

The tail up position connotes positive body language

The tail position is a well-known way that cats communicate visually. Generally, the ‘tail up’ position (where the tail is held vertically in the air, at a right angle to the ground) signals friendly intent when a cat approaches another cat, animal or person. The ears are normally pricked up, and the whiskers are relaxed. Other tail positions and movements communicate different moods or intentions. For example, a tail tucked between the back legs is often a sign that a cat is anxious or fearful, whereas a tail held out, moving slowly side-to-side across the body may signal frustration. In more extreme cases, the tail may be held up and ‘puffed out’ (often referred to as a cat having a bushy tail) by raising the hairs (piloerection) to make the cat’s silhouette appear larger – this is often continued as a strip along the cat’s back as well. When a bushy tail is used with an arched back and sideways stance, this is a hostile, distance- increasing ‘stay away’ signal to other cats. Some cats, when suddenly frightened, will instinctively puff up their tail before investigating the potential threat further. A cat crouching down and thumping their tail on the ground is a classic defensive posture.

Piloerection, when a cats hairs stand up along the back and tail, is indicative of a defensive body position

It’s not all about tails, though – facial expressions are very important too. Changes in position or movement of the eyes, ears and mouth, along with the cat’s whole-body posture, are significant. Ears that are erect and rotated out along with narrow/small pupils are usually a signal of frustration, whereas dilated pupils with ears held flat against the head are often a sign of fear. Relaxed cats tend to have their ears facing forward in the normal position. A stretched-out, tense and forward-facing body may show confidence, or in some cases, that a cat is ready to attack, whereas an anxious or fearful cat will pull their head and neck in to look small, hold their body close to the ground and place four paws firmly on the floor in case they need to flee quickly.

Relaxed cats tend to lie, curl up or sit with some or all of their paws off the ground. They may sit perched with their paws tucked under their chest or stretch their bodies right out if they are fully comfortable in their environment. Cats also perform something called a social roll. This is when they roll around on their backs, moving from one side of their body to the other (or sometimes they just lay on one side), exposing their belly. This can be aimed at other cats (or animals such as dogs) or people, and is usually carried out as a friendly greeting, showing that the cat is relaxed and comfortable or is in a playful mood. Intact female cats will also perform a social roll during courtship towards the male and will roll after mating.

Eye contact is also important. Staring at other cats with wide eyes and little blinking occurs during antagonistic meetings but may also occur when they are fixated on a threat and attempting to gather more information. Cats that feel threatened may also look away and/or rapidly blink. However, if cats have relaxed eyes, with blinking and winking, sometimes with their eyes half-closed, these are non-threatening signals that show the cat is content.

When tense or highly alert, the whiskers will be fanned out and pointing forward in front of the face. When relaxed, a cat’s whiskers point directly out and are less spread out. A frightened or nervous cat may flatten their whiskers against the side of their face and bunch them together.

Tactile communication

Common forms of tactile (touch) communication include allorubbing (two cats rubbing their bodies against each other – often around the mouth/cheek area, but also along the body and tail), allogrooming (two cats grooming each other), nose touching, and resting/curling up together. Cats using each other as ‘pillows’ and backing up against each other and intertwining their tails is believed to be a form of social bonding.

Tactile communication partly overlaps with olfactory communication (see below) as there’s an exchange of scent marking during many tactile communications.

Vocal communication

#### Cats make a wide range of sounds directed at each other across a variety of contexts including

- greeting

- hostility

- courtship and reproduction

- mother-kitten interactions

Cats are said to have one of the widest ‘vocabularies’ (different patterns of vocalisation) of all carnivore species. This may be because, in their natural environment, they spend much of their active time in poor light (at dawn and dusk), so vocal communication is important.

#### In general, three basic forms of vocal communication are heard in cats

‘Murmuring’ sounds

These tend to be formed with the mouth closed and are generally sounds used for greeting, attention, acknowledgement and approval. This includes purring, which is used in a wide variety of contexts and a variety of forms. Kittens may purr when they are being nursed as a sign of contentment, and the mother may also purr to reassure the kittens. Later in life, purring is generally used as a greeting and is often used, for example, during allogrooming.

The ‘trill’ or ‘chirrup’ sound, which is used as a friendly greeting call, also falls into this category of murmuring sounds and are sometimes directed at people too. Chirrups (which sound a little like “ppprrrrrrp”) are also used by the mother as a contact call to their kittens.

‘Meowing’ or ‘vowel’ sounds

Cats have an extensive range of meowing sounds, varying in intensity, frequency and duration. Meows are produced in a wide range of contexts and are used to communicate different messages, but in most circumstances, meows are directed at people, rather than other cats. So, while meows are often heard in mother-kitten interactions e.g., when kittens make a high-pitched meow to indicate they are hungry, they are rarely heard during adult cat–cat interactions. The common occurrence of meows directed towards people suggests that people often reinforce the meow, which help shape it to take many forms!

‘Hostile sounds’

These are all made with the mouth held open and are characterised as growls, yowls, snarls, hisses, spits, shrieks, and wails. These are all sounds made with various forms of hostility, whether offensive or defensive and can be aimed at other cats or other animals.

Chemical communication

Cats have developed two systems for detecting different chemicals used for communication with other cats in their environment. The olfactory system is located in the nose and is used to detect smells or scents. The vomeronasal or Jacobson’s organ is located in the cat’s hard palate, and is used to detect pheromones. Pheromones are chemicals secreted in different areas of the body by all cats and are used for cat-cat communication.

Cats have an extremely well-developed sense of smell, and this is used extensively for communication. Chemical signalling involving odours and pheromones can be very specific, last for a long time and can spread over long distances. Signalling through scent and pheromones is vital to allow cats to identify their territory and to identify other ‘friendly’ individuals when cats are living in a colony, for example.

Urine and faeces

These are two obvious sources of chemical signals used by cats, as cats leave both odour and pheromone deposits in their urine and faeces. While cats typically bury their faeces when they’re within their territory, when they pass faeces on the edge or boundary of their territory, they’re more likely to leave it uncovered to mark their territory to other cats. Passing urine and urine ‘spraying’ may also be a way of marking territory, giving messages to other cats in a colony or nearby, and may carry other signals, too, such as the cat’s health or the sexual receptiveness of unneutered females.

Skin

Cat’s use their skin for chemical communication too –, particularly through sebaceous and sweat glands and the odours or pheromones they produce. Certain areas of the skin are rich in these glands, such as beneath the chin, the sides of the mouth and cheeks, the sides of the forehead, the tail base and along the tail.

Cats rubbing against each other helps to exchange scent. When your cat rubs around your legs to greet you, they are doing the same as they would in greeting another cat by mutual rubbing of the face and body. As they can’t easily reach your face, for convenience, they’ll use your legs. Afterwards, your cat will take themself off to groom and check out your scent. Similarly, cats will often rub against objects in their ‘home’ area depositing their scent. Cats rubbing their heads against objects is known as ‘bunting’. Bunting often happens in the ‘core’ area of their territory and seems to be associated with comfort, reassurance and friendly social interactions. Paws

Sebaceous glands are also present on the cat’s paws and between their toes, so your cat may scratch (on trees, posts etc.) to leave a scent mark, as well as a visual mark of their presence. Cats tend to scratch in the same places repeatedly, and these tend to be along the well-used routes in their territory rather than around the edge.

Since cats haven’t changed much from their wild ancestors, it’s likely that domestic cats communicate in similar ways to wild cats. However, because domestic cats live more socially and communicate more, they may have found new ways to use these signals.
