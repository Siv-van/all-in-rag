# Soiling indoors

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/soiling-indoors

---

Cats are usually fastidious about their toilet habits and will consistently use a litter tray indoors if it’s provided, or loose earth or sand outside in the garden. So, if your cat starts passing urine and faeces in your home regularly outside of the litter tray, it could be a sign that something’s wrong.

Whatever the cause, punishment is not the answer – this will only make your cat fearful and the problem worse. Despite the unpleasant nature of this problem, it’s important to remember it is a sign your cat is not happy - but instead feeling unwell or stressed. Your cat isn’t seeking revenge or making a point; something has gone wrong in their world, but with your veterinary team’s help, you can help.

Common sites for urine spraying inside the home include soft furnishing (sofas, curtains etc) on doors or windows, in hallways, on electrical items and sometimes on items brought into the home, such as shoes or bags.

## What is the difference between spraying and urinating?

## What is the difference between urine spraying and indoor urination that occurs outside the litter tray (passing urine and faeces in places your cat normally wouldn’t?

Cats use urine as a chemical signal (made up of odours and pheromones) or ‘mark’ for themselves and to communicate to other cats. Depositing urine to communicate chemical messages is very different from passing urine to relieve a full bladder. To pass urine, your cat squats and passes urine on a horizontal surface. They may then scratch and dig the area around the urine, although this isn’t always the case. Common indoor sites for urination outside of the litter tray include carpets, sofas, duvets, baths or sinks, although it can happen anywhere.

When urine spraying a cat will stand up, making a treading motion with their back feet, and quivers their tail

To spray urine, your cat stands up, usually making a treading motion with their back feet, quivers their tail and urine is sprayed backwards onto a vertical surface such as a wall, leaving an obvious scent mark. The amount of urine is usually less than that produced when the bladder is emptied through urinating. Common sites for spraying include doors, windows, around cat flaps, curtains, electrical equipment and on shopping or rubbish bags.

Once you’ve established which of the two your cat is doing, you can work to resolve the behaviour.

## Why is my cat soiling indoors?

There are several reasons why your cat may have started soiling indoors. If it’s become a persistent problem, contact your veterinary team for advice.

#### Some of the more common reasons for house soiling are

No litter trays

Much as litter trays can be undesirable in the home, as cats age, or in bad weather, they may not want to toilet outside. Providing a litter tray can solve house soiling problems easily if not already provided. Read more about providing optimum litter tray facilities here.

Dirty litter trays

Cats generally don’t like using a tray if it’s soiled. Litter trays should be cleaned out completely at least weekly and topped up with fresh litter daily once the solids and clumps are removed, which should be done at least twice daily. Cats like to urinate in one area and defecate in another and some may not use a tray if it has any deposits in it. Regular litter scooping and providing more than one tray is thus beneficial. If the litter is a non-clumping variety, it should be changed every couple of days (solids and dirty areas removed daily) since the build-up of odour from the urine passed can become extremely unpleasant for your cat. Remember that cats have sensitive noses, so even litter that is designed to last longer than a week or absorb the urine completely may be unpleasant for cats - even if we you can’t smell the soiled litter, it’s likely your cat can.

Put off by the smell of the litter

Using scented litter, deodorants or disinfectants with strong smells may put your scent-sensitive cat off using the tray. To avoid this problem, avoid scentd litters and use a mild detergent and hot water or disinfectant specifically recommended for tray cleaning and rinse the tray thoroughly before use. Avoid disinfectants that turn cloudy in water as these usually contain phenols which are toxic to cats.

Cat sniffing litter substrate in a tray

Cats learning to use the tray for the first time may need to establish it as an appropriate toilet site and cleaning the tray too often may weaken the association.

Using polythene litter liners

Avoid using litter liners as these can catch in a cat’s claws as they scratch and cause litter to spray upwards, which your cat may not like.

Wrong type of litter

Changing the consistency or type of litter may put your cat off using it. Cats can find hardwood-based pellets uncomfortable to stand on, particularly if they have arthritis. Instead, cats tend to prefer fine grain litter (unscented) with the consistency of sand. If you want to change the type of litter you use, mix the new one in gradually over a week to see your cat’s reaction, or place a new tray with the new litter and alongside allow your cat to choose. A minimum of 3cm of litter is recommended, but this can be increased or decreased depending on the cat’s size, preference, or additional needs. Some cats prefer deeper litter to defecate in.

Position of the litter tray

If the tray is positioned in the open or a busy area where your cat can be disturbed, they may feel too vulnerable to use it. Place your cat’s tray in a quiet corner where they only need to watch in one or two directions. Placing food near the tray will also put your cat off using it, so place feeding bowls elsewhere. Ideally provide trays in more than one location to give the cat a choice. It’s also best to spread out the location of litter trays throughout the home if you have more than one cat and several litter trays. This is so that each cat can access their litter facilities easily without having to encounter another cat. Cats living together will sometimes prevent other cats from accessing their litter trays if they are all located in one area, especially if they are placed next to each other, so spreading them out throughout the home helps prevent this problem.

Number of litter trays

Because cats don’t like using soiled litter and can compete over access to litter trays, it’s important to ensure there is a sufficient number of litter trays to support the number of cats in the household. As a general rule, one litter tray per cat and a spare is recommended, so a single cat needs access to two litter trays, two cats need access to three litter trays and so on. This is recommended even for cats that have outdoor access because they may still need to use their litter facilities if their outside environment Is not suitable e.g., due to poor weather for instance. Ensuring indoor-only cats have the right number of litter trays is very important as they are they only toileting choice they have.

Type of litter tray

Some cats prefer the security of a covered tray while others prefer an open tray as it offers more options for escape and allows them to position themselves more easily when in use, as well as dig and bury their urine or faeces if they wanted to. If you normally use an open tray, it may be worth buying one with a lid or vice versa to find out your cat’s preference. Older cats may struggle to climb into high sided trays and benefit from lower sided trays or trays with a side cut out for easy access. The tray should be big enough for the cat to turn around and posture, so general 1.5 times longer than the cat.

Bad associations

Occasionally, cats decide not to use a tray because they’ve had a bad experience there. Giving medication or touching your cat while it’s using the tray may be enough to create a bad association. Moving the tray to a quiet spot may help. If they have had pain when using the tray (e.g. urinary problems, constipation or diarrhoea) they may associate the tray with discomfort. Again, moving the tray, offering a different type of tray with sandy deep litter could help.

Early training

Kittens will often soil in the house when they’re young if they are given immediate access to large areas. Kittens bladder and bowel control are not as developed as an adult cat, so your kitten must have easy access to a clean litter tray at all times. It can help to keep your kitten in one room initially, with increasing periods to explore other areas after a few weeks. Placing more than one litter tray in the home e.g. one in the kitten’s original room and one where they spend time the most time with you can help prevent accidents.

Illness

Urinary tract disease ordiarrhoeacan cause soiling indoors as your cat may be uncomfortable or just unable to make it to the litter tray or outside in time. Feline idiopathic cystitisis stress-related and a common sign of this condition is passing urine outside the litter tray. Other diseases such ashyperthyroidismordiabetescan also cause cats to house soil. If your cat is showing other signs of illness, or you suspect the soiling may be caused by ill health, contact your veterinary team.

Old age

Senior cats may not want to venture out in bad weather, or they may be having problems using the cat flap because of painful joints caused byarthritis, which can lead to soiling indoors. It’s likely that, at some stage, your senior cat will need safe and easy-to-reach toilet facilities indoors – providing indoor litter trays often resolves this problem. Soft litter and low sided wide trays can also help as older cats may have sore joints. Visit our webpage onsenior cats - special considerationsto find out more.

Senior cats may not want to venture out in bad weather, or navigate the cat flap because of painful joints, provide a low sided litter tray, with soft granular litter and a step can also be provided to help with easier access

Fear or anxiety

Cats are at their most vulnerable when they’re passing urine or faeces outside and, if they feel threatened, it may stop them from doing so. Other cats are usually the biggest problem, but it could be a neighbour’s dog, a sudden loud noise, or even severe weather conditions. Providing your cat with discreet litter trays indoors will help alleviate anxiety while toileting.

Removal of a previously used site

Some cats are creatures of habit in their choice of toilet sites and redesigning a garden can have a serious impact on their behaviour. Always ensure that changes to your garden incorporate an area that can be comfortably used by your cat as a toilet. If you’re aware of your cat’s favoured site, keeping it if possible is ideal.

Providing an outdoor toileting area in your garden can also help. You could try finding a dry, secluded area around the outside of your garden, ideally surrounded by shrubs, hedges or flowerpot, as these will provide extra cover and security for you cat when they are toileting.

Then you can dig a hole at least 60-90cm deep and 60-90cm square and adding gravel to fill up 2/3rds of the hole, followed by sand or woodchip to the top. Alternatively, you could add some sand or woodchip to a cleared location in the garden that offers your cat some privacy. Remember, outside toileting areas still need any soiled areas to be regularly removed and fresh sand or woodchip replaced.

## How do I clean the soiled area?

Whether an accident or not, once your cat has passed urine or faeces in a particular area, its sensitive nose will encourage it to use that place as a regular toilet.

The best way to break the habit of soiling indoors outside of the litter tray is to keep your cat away from the area as long as possible and remove any smell that your cat can detect.

Wash the area with a commercially produced enzymatic cleaners designed specifically to remove the stain and odour of cat urine and faeces. If you do not have access to one, clean with a 10% solution of biological or enzymatic washing powder and then rinse with cold water before allowing the area to dry thoroughly. Once the area is dry, the final step is to spray lightly with surgical spirit (isopropyl alcohol) (using a plant mister), scrub gently and leave to evaporate. You may want to try a small area first on delicate fabrics. Avoid using ammonia-based products as these smell similar to urine. Any heavily soiled fabrics or furniture should be discarded where possible.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat has started soiling indoors regularly, contact your veterinary team for advice. Your cat will likely need to be examined by a veterinarian to rule out any medical causes for the sudden behaviour change. Referral to a veterinary or clinical animal behaviourist may be required.

If you notice your cat trying to pass urine and not being able to, or only passing small amounts, your cat must be seen urgently.
