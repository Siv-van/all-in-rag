# Problem behaviour in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/problem-behaviour-in-cats

---

Signs of stress in cats are often subtle as they do not want to show their vulnerability. It can be difficult for owners to realise there is a problem. The clues, though sometimes difficult to spot, are always there, however. For example, your cat may start sleeping under the bed instead of on top of it or go outside less or eat more or less than normal. Sometimes, the only sign may be that your cat is simply sleeping a lot more than usual.

## What are the signs that my cat’s behaviour may be due to stress?

Changes in your cat’s behaviour and normal daily routines are the first signs that something is wrong, and, if the issue isn’t resolved, your cat will experiencechronic stressthat needs additional support. Behaviours related to such stress may be distressing or difficult for you to live with, for example, your cat may toilet outside of the litter tray and be difficult to interact with.

Any concerning change in behaviour should be a sign to seek professional veterinary advice and establish the root of the problem as soon as possible as often the longer the problem goes on, the harder it can be to treat.

## What constitutes ‘problem’ behaviour in cats?

Your cat’s ‘problem’ behaviour will always be based on your own assessment and perception of what constitutes a problem and what feels wrong. This may be something as vague as a sense that “they’re not themself” or you’ve noticed your cat’s routine and patterns of behaviour have changed suddenly, such as sleeping more, not using the stairs to access the upstairs of the home or toileting outside of their litter resource.

However, many behaviours owners seek advice for have been misinterpreted as ‘bad behaviour,’ such ashouse soilingor scratching/biting. But many behaviours will be perfectly normal for a cat in stressful situations – for example, under stress or in situations where a cat feels in conflict, spraying urineis a form ofcommunication. To an owner, however, it isn’t acceptable for their cat to spray urine inside the house.

Kittens that have been taught to play with hands have the potential to cause harm to their caregivers as adult cats from biting and scratching, and these behaviours can them be misinterpreted as “aggressive” behaviours

Sometimes, the unwanted behaviour is something that has been unintentionally taught by the owner. For example, a kitten being encouraged to play roughly with the owner’s hand may be seen as fun while the kitten is small and it’s not too painful. However, if no attempt is made to interrupt the game when the biting becomes too intense, the kitten grows into an adult cat that doesn’t understand how to manage behaviour during play. This cat may then be misinterpreted as ‘aggressive’ because the biting and scratching now has the potential of causing physical harm to the owner.

## Is ‘problem’ behaviour always due to stress?

No – some medical conditions can cause abnormal behaviour, and medical causes should always be ruled out as a first resort. Chronic pain, such as witharthritis, for example, may cause your cat to behave defensively, leading to displays of fear-related behaviours such as hissing, growling, biting, and scratching if approached. This may also be directed at other cats if your cat feels vulnerable due to illness. They may also soil in the house if they are struggling to get in or out of their litter tray. House soiling can also be seen in cats withcystitis.

Problem behaviour with a physical cause usually resolves when the condition or disease is treated successfully. Some conditions require both medical and behavioural intervention, e.g., feline idiopathic cystitis. Therefore, this is why it is always recommended to seek veterinary attention to rule out an underlying physical condition where a problem behaviour is recognised.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat is showing any type of behaviour that isn’t normal for them, contact your veterinary team. If they rule out a medical cause, they can then work with you to establish the cause of your cat’s stress and put a plan in place accordingly.

It can be helpful to your vet if you can give them as much information as possible that relates to the problem: when and where it started, any triggers, how frequently your cat is displaying the behaviour and how it’s progressed to its current level. If you’re able to video your cat displaying the behaviour, this can show your vet exactly what you’re describing.

If required, your vet may refer your cat to a qualified clinical animal behaviourist for further support.
