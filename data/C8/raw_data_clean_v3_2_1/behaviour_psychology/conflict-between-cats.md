# Conflict between cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/conflict-between-cats

---

As a self-reliant species and solitary hunter, cats usually avoid physical conflict wherever possible, because fighting can result in injury and their ability to hunt, threatening their survival.

Thecat’s communication system– vocalisation, body posture, body language, facial expression and scent/pheromone marking – is used primarily to avoid conflict and diffuse tension with other cats.

However, fighting will occur if a cat feels that’s the only choice, they must protect themselves, or possibly when they are confident that their opponent won’t retaliate. However, conflict does not necessarily always mean fighting, cats can also display more passive responses during conflict, such as staring or blocking access to resources.

## What causes conflict between cats?

There are several reasons why conflict happens between cats, either within the home or in the cat’s territory outside of the home. Most cats will try to defend the area they consider most significant to them (i.e. their territory) against invasion by other cats. Confident neighbourhood cats may actively seek out opportunities to extend their territory, and less confident cats are seen as easy targets. If a cat can’t successfully defend their territory, they could potentially become housebound, as their boundaries have been pushed back so significantly that trips outdoors are seen as too dangerous.

Doors, windows and cat flaps can allow entry of unfamiliar cats into the home, which can lead to aggression between them

Open doors, windows and cat flaps, all allow neighbouring cats to enter homes, which can lead to fighting between the unfamiliar cat and resident cat. There may be times when the resident cat does not seem to react when an unfamiliar cat enters their home, but that’s usually down to fear, rather than acceptance of the visitor. Such situations can also cause significant friction between cats living in the same household, raising their anxiety and worsening any pre-existing tension. The number of cats in the neighbourhood can have a similar effect, as their presence becomes a pressure that cats in the home cannot overcome.

Conflict may be seen between cats that don’t get along in multi-cat households and where the cats are kept exclusively indoors. Individuals may be reluctant to venture out of one room, except at very specific times, as their territory becomes subdivided by the more confident cats within the home. Fighting can occur if escape or avoidance opportunities aren’t possible i.e. the cat cannot hide effectively or run away. Some cats resort to active repelling behaviours quicker than others, depending on their genetics, sex and early experiences.

Note: Repelling behaviours are displayed by cats that are experiencing protective emotions such as anxiety, fear and frustration. They happen when they want to increase the distance between themselves and an actual or perceived threat. This includes behaviours such as growling, hissing, spitting, swiping or biting. These behaviours do not mean that a cat is ‘aggressive’ but that they are merely reacting this way to try and keep themselves safe or protect their resources e.g. food, water, resting areas, toileting areas, toys and scratching surfaces.

Episodes of repelling behaviours can also be seen in homes with breeding females, particularly when mother cats are protecting their kittens. Females coming in and out of season within the group can also cause further strain on relationships.

## Are some cats just more likely to show repelling behaviours than others?

Some cats may be more likely to show repelling behaviours under challenging circumstances than others, and it is important to recognise these behavioural responses are not a reflection of the cat's temperament. They are responding to what they feel is a stressor within their environment, and that could be a person trying to initiate or force an interaction with them that they don’t want, another cat in the home that is trying to gain access to the same resource as them, such as a litter tray or food, or a visual stressor e.g. new lively puppy in the home attempting to interact with them.

## How can I recognise conflict between my cats?

## Is it always about fighting?

Cats are very adaptable and can share space within a home without having a mutual bond. In homes with several cats, you may find some form close social relationships as pairs or small groups, and others live together through tolerance or avoidance. But there can still be times when conflict is present, particularly if there’s a need to work out who gets access to a particular resource e.g. the caregiver’s bed.

Examples of such behaviour include staring at other cats (which is perceived as threatening), blocking access to key resources (such as litter trays, cat flaps or resting areas) by physically remaining in a specific place - this can be very subtle as the cat only needs to position themselves somewhere between the other cat and the resource to block access; so to the casual observer, this just looks like a cat resting in a perfectly harmless location.

Cats may also follow, chase, ambush or pounce on other cats to displace them from an area (such as a thoroughfare) or resource e.g. food bowl, or litter tray. Cats may also claim resting places or access to your lap by physically pushing another cat away. These behaviours may escalate to more obvious signs of conflict, such as hissing, growling, biting, scratching, chasing and wrestling.

Body language in cats that occurs during conflict with each other comprises of one cat standing tall, often over the other, and often side on to appear larger. The other cat may posture in the same way or become more passive and roll on their side or crouch down small (defensive body postures). Eyes are often open large with a fixed stare.

## What’s the difference between play fighting and the real thing?

Two socially bonded cat’s play fighting with one another

Many bonded cats (especially young cats or kittens) enjoy play fighting and will take it in turn to chase each other, roll around and bat each other with their paws. Play fighting is often silent, with plenty of gaps in between as each cat repositions themselves. The biting is gentle and causes no injury or pain to the other cat, and their claws are retracted. There’s no reason to break up a play fight if it continues at this level and both cats appear to be playing (as opposed to one playing and the other tolerating). There will also be a quick return to normal behaviour after play fighting and no obvious tension between the cats at other times.

## How can I stop conflict between my cats?

Conflict between cats living together can sometimes be managed by finding the primary trigger and removing it where possible. If the trigger is tension between cats due to their incompatibility, or because of constant competition for access to resources, the environment can be adapted to include additions that may prevent conflict. In some situations, the cats’ relationship can completely break down and they may be incompatible. In these circumstances re-homing cats shouldn’t be ruled out as it could be the kindest option for all, however, that doesn’t mean you should stop trying to achieve peace.

There’s no ‘quick fix’ for conflict between cats, and it’s important to remember this could be a natural response due to their territorial nature. However, cats in ongoing conflict often have impaired welfare so we recommend contacting your vet to rule out any underlying health issue that could be affecting cats’ behaviour. Referral to a qualified, clinical animal behaviourist may also be required.

#### In addition to this, you can follow some simple rules at home

All cat resources should ideally be provided as ‘one per cat, plus one extra, positioned in different locations’e.g. one litter tray per cat, plus a spare, in different areas/levels of the home. This helps to ensure all cats have access to resources without competing with other cats. If you have cats that have clearly paired up, and space is limited within the home, then ‘one per social group plus one extra’ may be sufficient.

Part of your cats’ normal food ration can be distributed to several new permanent feeding ‘stations’. This helps give a sense of abundance and enables cats to decide a time to feed. Cats see food as a valuable resource, so their bowls should be positioned away from each other, and so each cat can eat without the need to turn its back on a potential adversary (i.e. another cat). Feeding cats in different rooms e.g. where one cat is fed in the kitchen whilst the other cat is fed in the living room, or out of sight of each other e.g. at different levels, with one cat fed on the kitchen floor, whilst the other cat is fed on the kitchen table, helps cats feel safe at feeding time and can prevent competition and conflict over food.

Water is also an important resource for cats, so it’s best to offer several bowls/water stations in different locations throughout the home and away from their food, as cats like to eat and drink in different areas. This can help cats feel safe whilst drinking and encourage water intake (which may be useful for cats with chronic health conditions).

Even if your cats have access outdoors, it’s a good idea to provide indoor litter trays. If there’s any conflict going on outside, this gives your cats the option to toilet in a safe space indoors. If possible, providing two separate entry and exit points to your home (e.g. cat flaps, doors or windows) avoids the risk of other cats guarding or blocking access, enabling even timid cats to go outdoors unhindered.

Cats will often scratch when other cats are around to communicate boundaries of territories. A selection of scratching posts, boards or mats should be located near entrances/exits and resting areas to encourage use and give all cats in the home the opportunity to scratch.

Most cats prefer to watch what’s going on from a high vantage point, as this gives them a sense of safety, so it’s good to offer plenty of places for them to perch around the home. Private resting or hiding places e.g. comfortable elevated beds (such as those used for cat trees, towers or shelves) as well as raised beds or blankets on human furniture must also be provided, as every cat needs some solitude and a safe place to rest without being disturbed.

## FAQs

## My cats have suddenly started fighting, what should I do?

If intense fighting develops between two of your cats, the safest option is to separate them into different rooms for 24–48 hours until they’ve both calmed down and contact your vet immediately for advice – a veterinary examination may be required to rule out any underlying health issues that could be affecting your cat’s behaviour. Never try to pick up or interact with either cat during or immediately after a fight, as you are at high risk of being scratched or bitten. If this does occur and you are injured by your cat, it’s important to seek medical advice straight away.

If a re-introduction still results in active fighting, it may be necessary to separate them for a longer period and if possible, request a referral from your vet to a clinical animal behaviourist, as they will be able to provide you with the right advice and support to increase the likelihood of a successful reintroduction.

If a reintroduction is to be tried, it may be necessary to treat your cats like complete strangers meeting for the first time - read our article onintroducing catsfor more information on how to do this. Sadly, in some cases, it is not possible for cats to live harmoniously alongside each other, and rehoming may be the only option.

## Why did my cat attack their companion when they saw something through the window?

Repelling behaviours directed at one cat from another can be triggered by a sense of threat from an external source that cannot be accessed e.g. another cat, or a dog that can be seen through a window. Behaviours intended to be directed at the threat are instead directed to another household cat. Unfortunately, relationships between two cats in a household can sometimes be irretrievably damaged under these circumstances.

If this occurs, separate the cats into different rooms (without picking them up or touching them) for 24–48 hours until they have both calmed down. If a re-introduction still results in fighting, it’s best to get both cats health checked by a vet, and ask for referral to a clinical animal behaviourist, as fighting between cats in this situation can be serious and both you and your cats are at risk of being injured.

## If I introduce another cat to my existing one, will they fight?

Any addition to existing cat groups can trigger conflict or fighting as it’s perfectly normal for cats to resist the introduction of others to their territory, particularly if the newcomer is a mature adult. The sudden introduction of a new cat can create feelings of fear, anxiety and/or frustration. A gradual introduction using the process already outlined above is vital in these situations. Alternatively, you could seek help from a clinical animal behaviourist on how best to introduce the cats.

## My cat has been injured by another cat in the area, what should I do?

This can be extremely distressing, particularly as attacks often occur within the cat’s own home or garden. While it’s reasonable to ask the caregiver of the other cat to take certain measures to prevent this behaviour, you must also take steps to protect your own home, and therefore, your cat. If your cat is unable to deter intruders or defend their own territory, it’s time to intervene.

First, try to establish if the cat that’s causing the problem is owned, and if they are neutered. If they’re not owned, it may be possible to request help from your local cat charity for humane trapping, neutering and re-homing. If the cat is owned, you may be able to mutually agree with their caregivers on times when each cat is allowed access outdoors.

If neighbouring cats can easily enter through your cat’s cat flap, you could temporarily prevent this by locking it or placing a solid board over it. You may also need to close any doors or windows through which the unfamiliar cat entered the house. Microchip activated cat flaps can also prevent unknown cats from entering your home at any time of day. If you can find out where the cat is getting into your garden, you may be able to block their route and then escort your cat into the garden if they’re keen to go out. Providing an indoor litter tray is still a good idea as it allows your cat to still be indoors if that’s what they’re most comfortable with.

## My cat’s been in a fight, what should I do?

If you’re aware that your cat has just had a fight, it’s best to keep them indoors but not directly interact with them to start with – they need time to calm down. Superficial cuts and scratches are common and easily seen, but bite wounds are often difficult to detect as a cat’s teeth puncture the skin causing deep, but narrow holes that quickly heal over, trapping bacteria inside. If your cat hides, appears listless, withdrawn, lame, or doesn’t want to eat after a fight, contact your veterinary team as they may have acat bite abscessthat requires medical attention.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If you’re struggling to manage conflict between cats, or you’re worried about your cat in any way, contact your veterinary team for advice. They may be able to offer help or organise a referral to a qualified clinical animal behaviourist for further support.
