# Everyday care for your cat

The level of general care your cat needs from you will depend on their breed, length of fur (or lack of it), health and age. It’s important to know what’s ‘normal’ for your cat, in terms of their overall condition and behaviour, in order to pick up the subtle signs when something is wrong. Regularly checking your cat and monitoring how they interact with their environment can help you spot any changes affecting their health and wellbeing.

Below, we’ve listed the key things to consider to ensure your cat lives the happiest, healthiest life possible.

## What routine everyday care should I give my cat?

Caring for your cat’s coat and skin

There’s not a great deal to do with short-haired breeds, as they generally keep their coats in good condition with little or no help. However, as cats mature into their senior years, they may need grooming as they are less mobile. However, long-haired breeds will need a little help, particularly Persians, who have a very thick undercoat and a long top layer of fur. Read our article ongrooming your catfor specific advice on how to make grooming a positive experience for your cat, and which brushes and combs are best for different coat types.

It is important to keep the area under your cat’s tail clean. This is usually only a concern in breeds with a long coat, such as Persians, or cats that are unwell and havediarrhoea. You may need to clean or trim their coat to avoid faeces from sticking to it. Help may also be needed for older cats, who are not so mobile and cannot comfortably move into the necessary positions to clean themselves, especially those suffering from joint pain due to conditions likearthritis.

There are some breeds with little or no hair, such as the Sphynx, that lacks a fur coat but is instead covered in fine, downy hairs. While this may seem like a way to remove the need for any type of grooming, these cats may actually require more care for their skin. Cats as a species, developed to have hair because it has a range of roles for survival – warmth, skin protection, as a sensory mechanism, and as a way to distribute oil to keep the coat waterproof and hold scents. To keep this coat in top condition, cats have developed a highly effective grooming mechanism – a rough tongue with small barbs on the surface that works like a comb. Grooming also seems to make cats feel good – they will often groom after being startled or use it as a distraction. Therefore, to breed a cat with no coat, but with a desire to groom and a very rough tongue, seems unfair as they cannot fulfil these natural behaviours.

Breeds with no or little coat may also be prone to skin problems, such as rashes and fungal infections. It’s important to keep their skin clean and supple, so they will need weekly bathing or sponging to remove oily secretions.

Caring for your cat’s eyes

Most cats don’t need any help with their eyes – everything is kept clean when they regularly groom themselves. However, very flat-faced (brachycephalic) breeds, such asPersiansand Exotics, have been bred to have skulls with abnormal conformation. Often, the tear ducts (which let the tears that lubricate the eye drain away) have their path blocked by changes to the shape of the skull. The result is that the tears overflow down the face, causing staining and likely discomfort. This dampness can also cause skin problems.

If your cat suffers from tear overflow, gently wipe their eyes with a cotton wool ball dampened with clean water. Use a separate ball for each eye and dry with a soft tissue. Be mindful not to touch the cat’s eyeball, as this will be uncomfortable for them, and they may try to avoid the experience in the future.

Caring for your cat’s claws

Routine trimming of a cat’s claws isn’t advisable as they need healthy, sharp claws for climbing, hunting, playing and defence. Most active cats that go outside care for their claws without any help.. Indoor-only cats may need their claws trimmed, as they may not wear them down if they do not have the right opportunities to scratch. Providing scratching posts around your home can help them to perform natural scratching behaviours, limit damage to your furniture and maintain their claw health.

Older cats may not retract their claws as well, and they may begin to catch on carpets or furnishings. Their claws are also at greater risk of becoming overgrown and curving back into your cat’s pad, which can lead to infection. For these cats, keeping a close eye on their claws and trimming them when necessary is important. If you’re worried your cat’s claws are too long, contact your veterinary team for advice. They will be able to trim your cat’s claws if necessary and show you how to safely do this at home without causing your cat excessive stress.

Caring for your cat’s ears

If you think your cat’s ears are dirty, it might be tempting to clean them out with a cotton wool bud. However, most vets will advise you to avoid tampering with their ears at all, as the tissues lining the ear canals are very delicate and easily damaged.

Ears may be dirty due to wax build-up, but it may also be caused by ear mites, infection or skin irritation. Check with your vet who may give you a product to wipe out the outer ear, but avoid going too deep where you might cause more damage. If you have a hairless breed or one with curled ears, they may require more care – check with your veterinary team how to do this safely.

Caring for your cat’s teeth

Not many of us look in our cats’ mouths. Ideally, it would be a good idea to get your cat used to having their teeth cleaned from kittenhood, so they can learn positive associations with toothbrushing during their sensitive period. Read our article onhow to brush your cat’s teethfor more expert advice.

Cats are masters of disguise when it comes to pain, and they may be suffering quite badly from a painful mouth to the point of not eating before we notice. Bad breath (halitosis) may be a clue that all is not right, but a proper vet check is necessary to determine the extent of the problem. Research suggests that almost three-quarters of cats over three years old have some form ofdental disease, so it’s something to take seriously and to ask your vet about when you have your cat checked.

## How do I know if something is wrong with my cat?

Cats are very good at hiding when they are not feeling well. Owners need to be a little bit like detectives, picking up the subtle signs that their cat may be off-colour or not as they should be. Changes in coat, body posture or behaviour may all be signs that something’s not right – contact your veterinary team if you’re worried about your cat in any way.
