# Keeping your cat healthy

https://icatcare.org/articles/keeping-your-cat-healthy

---

Anyone familiar with the behaviour of cats will know that the saying ‘a cat is not a small dog’ is indeed very true. The same is true for health. You can’t just take the diagnoses and treatments applicable to dogs and scale them down for a cat. So, how do you best keep your cat healthy?

## What do I need to consider when it comes to keeping my cat healthy?

Cats have their own unique physiology, diseases, and reactions to medication and treatments. Cats also have their own set of behaviours that can make noticing illness very difficult. There are lots of things to consider when it comes to keeping your cat healthy, such as what preventative care they need, how you can spot the signs that something isn’t right, plus how you can transport your cat to the veterinary clinic and keep their stress levels low. We’ll explore all of these aspects, and more, throughout this article.

What to bear in mind

There are some key things to keep in mind to keep your cat as healthy as possible

Keep up with your cat’s preventative care

Just as with us, good nutrition, maintaining a healthy weight, and exercise are all important aspects in keeping your cat healthy. It’s also important to consider their preventative healthcare needs; that is, what we need to do to protect them from preventable diseases and conditions. Preventative healthcare includes keeping them free of parasites such asfleas, worms, andticks, ensuring your cat receives appropriatevaccinationand isneutered. Your veterinary team will be happy to discuss all the relevant preventative healthcare your cat needs to stay happy and healthy.

Cats are masters of disguise when it comes to showing pain and vulnerability

Cats are predators, and as such, we think of them always at the killing end of a predator/prey encounter and always on top. However, cats are small predators, and there are other animals (and even people) who treat them as prey. Cats are well aware of this. Unlike dogs, they don’t have a pack to hide within, no others of their kind that will stand up for them or provide a united front against a threat. They have only themselves to rely on as a solitary survivalist when it comes to avoiding or escaping from a dangerous situation. Therefore, they need to ensure they don’t show vulnerability or weakness that might draw an opportunistic predator’s attention.

Likely because of this, cats tend to mask the signs of illness very well. They often just become quiet and stoic, and unless you notice that this is, in fact, a change in their normal behaviour, you may not take a second glance. Where possible, cats take themselves off the radar and often hide away until they feel better. Spotting periods where your cat seems quieter than usual can be key in recognising signs of illness.

As cats age, more complex things can go wrong

Though young animals can become ill, they usually suffer from one particular illness at a time, which can be tackled individually. As a cat gets older, however, the chances of multi-organ issues increase, and there’s a likelihood that several things may happen at once as their body ages, such asarthritis, hyperthyroidism, dental diseaseandchronic kidney disease. Therefore, it pays to keep an even closer eye on your cat as they age and ensure they’reroutinely checked by your veterinary team.

Owners must be detectives when it comes to noticing behaviour changes

Cats often just become quiet when they’re ill, and in a busy household where your cat uses the garden rather than a litter tray as their toilet, there may be no obvious signs that they are unwell. Your cat may prefer to drink outside because they prefer rainwater to water in a dish, so you may not even notice anincrease in thirst and drinking. Without a litter tray, owners may not noticediarrhoeaor a change inurination, and may not pick up these indicators of a problem. Be mindful of what constitutes normal behaviour for your cat, and you can be alerted to problems more quickly.

Cats don’t like going in the car, let alone to the veterinary clinic

Cats usually dislike being put in a cat carrier. Then you have to put them in the car, where it’s often clear that they don’t enjoy the experience, and after that, you take them into the veterinary clinic – all of which can cause your cat a great deal ofstress. This is why it’s not uncommon for owners to put off a visit to the veterinary clinic. But while that may avoid the stress, it won’t always be in your cat’s best interests. There are ways to minimise any stress your cat may experience, and the cat carrier is a good place to start. It’s difficult to medicate cats

Giving your cat medication can be a major struggle, and it takes a dedicated owner to persist. Often, treatments are started and then abandoned, especially if there seems to be some improvement. However, it’s important that the complete course of medication, as prescribed by your vet, is given to your cat. If you’re struggling, don’t hesitate to speak to your veterinary team, who may be able to offer alternative medications in different formulations or help guide you on the best way to administer the medication that’s required.
