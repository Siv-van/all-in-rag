# How to brush your cat’s teeth

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/how-to-brush-your-cats-teeth

---

Brushing your cat’s teeth is the best way to keep their teeth and gums healthy. Just as in humans, brushing not only prevents plaque and tartar formation but also keeps gums healthy and reduces bad breath. If your cat is happy for you to brush their teeth, once daily is ideal. Don’t worry if you have an adult cat that’s never had their teeth brushed - this can be introduced to your cat at any age, as long as it’s done gradually, and carefully.

Despite our best efforts, some cats seem to be predisposed to developing dental disease. However, regular dental care at home can improve their dental health and reduce the need for dental procedures - so it’s worth persevering.

## What can I do to care for my cat’s teeth at home?

While the thought of brushing your cat’s teeth daily may seem a little daunting at first, it’s the best way to keep their teeth and mouth healthy. As with most things, the younger you start this, the more likely your cat is to accept it - kittens, in particular, don’t usually take long to get used to having their teeth brushed, whereas older cats will just need a little more time and patience.

Try to establish a daily routine - this will help to ensure that brushing is done every day, and as we know, cats like their routine. Choose a suitable time and stick to it as much as possible. Some people choose to clean their cat’s teeth after a meal, whereas others prefer to give some food as a reward for their cat having their teeth brushed. There’s no right or wrong, so find something that works for you and your cat.

In older cats, a home dental care routine is often started after your cat has had their teeth scaled and polished under anaesthetic - they may have even had some teeth removed. If this is the case, allow time for the mouth to heal before starting any kind of home care.

## What should I use to brush my cat’s teeth?

It’s not safe to use human dental products for your cat. Specific cat toothpaste and brushes are available - ask your vet if you’re not sure what’s best to use.

#### You will need

Toothbrush – a suitable toothbrush for cats (and a separate toothbrush for each cat in your household to prevent any infection spreading between cats through their salvia).

Veterinary/cat toothpaste – cat toothpaste is very different from human toothpaste - it usually comes in multiple flavours such as chicken, beef, or fish, so you may want to try the different flavours to see which your cat prefers. Human toothpaste should never be used as it’s unpalatable for cats and may cause irritation to the stomach

‘Finger’ brushes are available to use - we recommend using these with caution, and probably to avoid in most cases, to reduce the risk of being accidentally bitten!

Information on products accepted by the Veterinary Oral Health Council can be foundhere.

## How do I brush my cat’s teeth?

To get your cat used to having their teeth brushed, follow our recommended steps below.

Allow your cat to get used to being touched around its head and mouth. This should be a positive experience for them and starts with gentle stroking. Always end on a positive note with a reward, such as their favourite treat. Slowly build up until your cat is happy for you to lift up their lips to expose their teeth.

Initially, place some toothpaste on your finger for your cat to lick off. Some cats instantly love the taste, others may be a little reluctant. You may need to try different flavours to find the one your cat prefers.

Cat toothpaste on the end of the finger

Familiarise yourself with how to hold your cat’s head while brushing. So as not to startle your cat, try this when they’re relaxed, several times per day if possible, and at a different time to when you plan to brush their teeth.

Position your cat with their back towards you - this is less confrontational for your cat, and if they wriggle, it tends to be backwards, so you’ll have better control.

Spread your hand and place your palm on top of your cat’s head, towards the back. Use your thumb and second finger to gently grip around your cat’s cheekbone, below the eyes. Keep your index finger lifted, so as not to cover their eyes.

Gently tilt your cat’s head upwards a little and use your thumb to gently lift your cat’s upper lip.

Use the thumb or finger on your other hand to gently pull down your cat’s lower lip – this should give you a good view of all of one side of your cat’s teeth.

Allow the cat to familiarise itself with the taste of the toothpaste by allowing it to lick it off your finger

Stop if your cat shows signs of being uncomfortable and try again the next day.

For the next few days, hold your cat’s head in the same way, but instead of using your second hand to hold the lower lip down, apply some toothpaste to a cotton bud and gently rub the toothpaste onto their teeth in a circular motion.

Start with their back teeth - these are usually the ones that are difficult to reach, but the most important to brush! Gradually work your way forward along their teeth.

Repeat these steps little and often until your cat is comfortable, before moving on to the next stage.

Finally, you can start using the toothbrush. The process is the same - starting with the back teeth and moving in small, circular motions. Start with just ten seconds on each side, gradually building up to 30 seconds on each side. At first, you may notice a small amount of bleeding from the gums; this is common and will stop as you brush more regularly, and the gums become healthier.

And remember, it doesn’t matter how long it takes to build up through the stages as long as your cat is comfortable. If it takes more than a couple of days for each stage, that’s fine. The main thing is for home dental care to be a pleasant experience for your cat, and not a battle between you or something they find stressful.

Additional Considerations

#### Some cats simply won’t tolerate having their teeth brushed, no matter how much you persevere. If this is the case with your cat, there are other options you may like to try to help prevent plaque and tartar formation

Cat dental chews can help to reduce (but not prevent) plaque and tartar formation. Speak to your vet for guidance on the dental chews most suitable for your cat.

If your cat likes the toothpaste, try applying some to dry food or dental chews to allow the toothpaste to rub against the teeth.

Your vet may also suggest a ‘dental diet’ for your cat. Dental diets are specially formulated to help control plaque and tartar and are a good option if your cat won’t tolerate having their teeth brushed.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Your veterinary clinic may offer appointments with a vet nurse who can demonstrate how to brush your cat’s teeth. If you’re having problems at home, give your veterinary team a call.

In addition to this, if your cat is showing any signs ofdental disease, contact your vet for an appointment to discuss the most appropriate treatment.
