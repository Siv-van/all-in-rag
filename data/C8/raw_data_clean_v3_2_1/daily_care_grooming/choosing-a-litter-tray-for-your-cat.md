# Choosing a litter tray for your cat

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/choosing-a-litter-tray-for-your-cat

---

Litter trays are essential for all indoor-only cats, as well as cats that have limited outdoor access. However, litter trays are highly recommended even if your cat is free to roam and usually toilets outside, as they allow your cat to have a safe toileting area available at all times, day or night.

Issues such as house soiling and stress-related illness, such as feline idiopathic cystitis, are more likely to occur if your cat doesn’t have access to optimum toileting facilities (litter trays) inside the home.

## What is the best litter tray for my cat?

Litter trays come in various shapes and sizes, so how do you choose the right one for your cat?

#### Let’s start with the two basic styles

Open trays with a rim of varying depth, but no cover

Covered (or closed) trays with a removable lid and, in some models, a cat-flap entrance

Covered litter tray

Within these two basic designs are size variations (eg, small and suitable for a kitten). Many owners choose covered trays as they believe they offer cats privacy when they need to toilet, with the added benefit that the odour is kept to a minimum for people in the household. However, keeping the odour in for us means that regular removal of soiled litter (as soon as it has become soiled if possible) can be more difficult, making the litter tray less desirable for the cat to use as it smells unpleasant. Cats are less likely to use a litter tray that is already soiled.

While many cats adapt well to a covered litter tray, some feel vulnerable when confined in a small space with only one available exit point, particularly if they share their home with other cats. Large breed cats in particular may find smaller hooded trays challenging to use as they cannot position themselves to eliminate, or scrape and dig their litter easily. Some covered litter trays also have a flap, which can deter use (and contain odours further), so if you do go for this option its preferable to remove the flap. If this is the case with your cat, they may prefer an open litter tray, but you can try both types with your cat to see if they have a preference.

Open litter tray

Self-cleaning litter trays are becoming increasingly popular but while the appeal of not having to regularly scoop the litter tray is understandable, they can cause problems for cats. These trays often make random movements or noises that can startle cats and disturb their toileting behaviour. Many are quite small and may even deter your cat from using them.

Polythene litter tray liners and litter deodorants/scented litters are also available to help easily remove soiled litter and keep the litter tray and area around it smelling fresh. However, cats can find the scents overpowering and the constant catching of their claws in polythene can be unpleasant for them. This may cause to cats choosing to toilet elsewhere leading to the problem ofhouse soiling (urinating/defecating outside of the litter tray).

## How do I choose the right litter for my cat?

Litter is available in various materials including paper, wood, silica, corn, clay and other novel materials that are considered sustainable. Many of these are lightweight, biodegradable and have excellent odour-neutralising properties.

Wooden pellet substrate

Silica litter substrate

Providing their litter is clean and changed regularly, your cat will be happy, particularly if you stick to the same material that they became used to as a kitten, as litter preferences develop early in kittenhood.

The domestic cat’s ancestor is a desert-dwelling wildcat, so the ideal substrate to meet your cat’s preferences is likely a fine sand-like litter because they’re closest to the material your cat instinctively favours. Many cats also prefer using litter trays containing clumping litter. This is because litter that clumps when soiled is easier to remove, keeping the litter clean and making the tray more inviting to use.

#### Litter tray tips

Research shows that cats prefer a depth of approximately 3cm of litter in their tray

Cats prefer a litter tray that gives them enough space to move around. The ideal length of the tray is one and a half times the length of your cat from their nose to the base of their tail. Bear this in mind when purchasing a litter tray, as many of the standard-sized trays are too small

Rectangular litter trays are recommended as they provide more room than corner/triangle or oval-shaped litter trays

The position of the litter tray is important. It should be placed somewhere quiet, away from food and water and busy areas of the home

Cats are often reluctant to use trays near cat flaps or full-length glass windows due to the perceived threat from outside

Your cat’s litter tray must be kept clean ie, scooped out at least twice a day and completely cleaned out and washed (with new litter replacing any soiled litter) either every 2-4 weeks if you are using clumping litter, and weekly if you are using non-clumping litter. More frequent cleaning may be necessary if your cat has a medical problem or stress-related behaviour problem causing them to use the litter tray more often, eg, cats affected bychronic kidney disease, hyperthyroidismorfeline idiopathic cystitis.

Some disinfectants (like Chloroxylenol or Dettol) which go cloudy in water aretoxic to cats, so use hot water and biological detergent when cleaning out the tray. Heavily scented detergent should also be avoided. Always rinse and dry the tray thoroughly before use.

Multi-cat households

As with all other resources (food and water bowls, scratching posts, beds, toys etc.) in multi-cat households, you should provide one litter tray per cat, plus at least one extra. These should be spaced out around the house, away from food and water bowls, and not placed all together in one area, otherwise there is a risk that access could be blocked by other cats in the household.
