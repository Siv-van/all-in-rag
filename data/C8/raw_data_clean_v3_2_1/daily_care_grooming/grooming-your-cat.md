# Grooming your cat

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/grooming-your-cat

---

Most cats spend a significant part of their day grooming. With bodies that bend and flex, they can reach all areas with ease, while their tongue, covered with backwards-facing spines, is designed to groom effectively, removing loose hair and dirt.

A domestic cat’s fur is generally made up of three types of hair: down, awn and guard. Cats usually moult all year round, to varying degrees, so grooming helps to remove the loose hair. Much of this is swallowed, either passing through the cat’s digestive system or vomited up as a hairball. Grooming your cat can help prevent or reduce hairballs.

## What are the benefits of grooming my cat?

#### Grooming performs several important functions for your cat

Removes loose hair and smooths the coat to help insulate the body more efficiently

Regulates temperature in hot weather by spreading saliva across the coat that then evaporates, cooling your cat down

Keeps the coat waterproof by stimulating glands at the base of the hairs

Spreads sebum along the coat, producing Vitamin D when exposed to sunlight which is then ingested by your cat

Spreads your cat’s own scent across their body

Removes parasites

Maintains strong social bonds when cats groom each other

#### Grooming your cat, whatever their coat type, has several benefits

Improves muscle tone

Stimulates the skin to produce oils, giving the coat a healthy shine

Your cat will remove a large amount of hair themself when grooming, but if you groom them daily, particularly when they are moulting, you can remove most of the hair for them and prevent hairballs from forming

Provides the opportunity to give your cat a quick physical check, looking at ears, eyes and mouth and checking for fleas, ticks, ear mites or any lumps or bumps

The whole process can be very therapeutic for both of you and should be a positive way of reinforcing the bond between you

## How do I groom my cat?

Initially, some kittens and adult cats will be much more comfortable being handled and groomed than others. It’s important to build up your cat’s tolerance of handling and grooming gradually, so that it’s not a negative experience for either you or your cat. Your cat should never be forcibly groomed or stroked without the option to escape if they’re finding the experience stressful.

If you’ve recently homed a kitten, you can get them used to being groomed from an early age. Gradually build up the time you spend grooming your kitten until they’re happy to allow you to brush them.

Key behaviours to look out for when grooming or stroking cats

#### Some of these signs can be indicators that your cat isn’t comfortable with being groomed

Skin rippling or twitching

Tail swishing, thumping or twitching at the end

Sharply turning their head to face your hand or the brush

Suddenly freezing or becoming tense

Ears flicking or rotating backwards

Swallowing or licking lips

Shaking their head

Performing a short-lasting but rapid burst of grooming to their body

Growling or hissing

If your cat shows any of these signs, stop grooming them and see if they’ll tolerate it another day.

Grooming shorthaired cats

#### Gather the following equipment

Always begin your grooming session when your cat is relaxed and happy. Aim to groom your cat at least once weekly, using the flea comb first to check for flea dirt (tiny black comma-shaped dirt that runs red if a drop of water is added to it). If you find any, speak to your veterinary team about effective flea control. You can use your fingers to loosen the dead hairs in your cat’s coat by massaging against the hair growth in the direction of their head. Gently brush or comb your cat’s hair, using strokes towards their tail starting from the head region, in the direction that the hair grows. Use the bristle brush to sweep up the coat in the direction of their head if your cat will tolerate it and then smooth it down again (many cats may not like this bit if they’re not used to being brushed in this way, and some may just find the sensation unpleasant). This will bring all the loose hairs to the surface so that you can then use the rubber grooming glove or pad from head to tail, removing dead hair and stimulating the blood supply to their skin. Any loose hairs lying on the surface of the coat can be removed with a damp cotton or rubber glove to produce the final glossy finish.

These sessions should be as short or long as your cat is comfortable with and should end before your cat shows any signs of discomfort. They should also finish on a positive note, with treats and praise.

Grooming longhaired cats

#### Gather the following equipment

Longhaired cats need to be groomed at least daily but only do this for as long as your cat is comfortable. As for shorthaired cats, always choose a time when your cat is relaxed and happy to begin grooming. Massage the skin thoroughly before grooming by gently rubbing with your fingers against the hair growth from tail to head. As the hair is lifted, check for flea dirt. Using the wide-toothed comb, groom from head to tail to remove dead hair, taking extra care with the areas under your cat’s ‘armpits’ and between their hind legs, as the skin is very thin here and extremely sensitive. It’s also an area of friction where knots (matts) are common. If you come across a matt, tease it apart gently using your fingers – be patient, working slowly from the root towards the end of the hair. Don’t be tempted to use scissors – it’s extremely difficult to see where your cat’s skin ends and the hair starts when it gets really matted, and you could cut your cat. Another common area where matts form is between the toes and pads, so check each time you groom and tease out any accumulated debris gently.

Next, use the rubber mitten or pad to remove more dead hair and do a final sweep over the coat with a damp cotton or rubber glove to remove any loose hairs that remain. Finish off with the comb if your cat is enjoying the attention. If grooming is a struggle, it should be stopped. Start again at a time when your cat is relaxed and aim to have smaller goals for each grooming session, goals that your cat can easily tolerate. Always reward relaxed behaviour with praise and food treats throughout the grooming sessions.

There are several grooming products on the market, particularly for longhaired cats, e.g. rakes, slickers and detangle sprays. Find out what works best for you and your cat and causes the least amount of stress for all involved.

Additional Considerations

Removing matts

Through daily grooming, cats shouldn’t become matted. However, if your cat has become matted, it can be very uncomfortable. You may find a professional cat groomer who can manually groom these matts out, but in many cases, severe matts will need to be shaved off by your veterinary team. This often requires sedation or a general anaesthetic and cats that are severely matted will end up with fur only on their tail, head, and lower legs until it grows back.

Bathing your cat

If your cat is healthy there is no reason to bath your cat. Most don’t tolerate it well.
