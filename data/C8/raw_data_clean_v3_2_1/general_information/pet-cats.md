Don’t wait for adopters to find you - go out and find homes. Make sure your local community knows you’re there and that you always have wonderful cats waiting for homes.

You might try hold regular adoption events and invite the public in, using creative advertising and community networks to spread the word, and keeping a list of potential homes ready so that when the right cat comes along, the process is quick and easy.

Be visible, welcoming and proactive. People can’t adopt from you if they don’t know you exist.

Make adoption easy

Every step of the adoption process should feel welcoming and straightforward. Avoid unnecessary barriers that might discourage good homes. Some things you might like to consider are ensuring opening times are convenient for the public, offer appointment times to ensure staff/volunteers are available to spend time with potential adopters, and ensuring your facilities are an attractive place to visit (welcoming, clean and well organised).

Be flexible, not restrictive

It can be tempting to have rules about the suitability of certain people and their

circumstances (such as proximity to a busy road or the presence of young children), which means that you may reject potential adopters. Any restrictions should be based on a genuine risk assessment. In most cases, cats can live a good life with most people who come forward to adopt one.

Review your policies to ensure they are in the cats’ best interests and not overly restrictive. Check that requirements such as landlord permission for renters are reasonable, that adopters understand the ongoing costs of cat ownership (a handout can help), and that all adoption paperwork is legally sound and protects both the adopter and the cat.

Match cats with suitable owners and provide ongoing support

The heart of successful homing is good matching. Use everything you’ve learned about each cat - fromintake notes, vet records, daily observations, Traffic Light Assessments, and foster reports - to build a clear picture of their needs and preferences. Be flexible; there are no perfect matches.

The adoption interview

Each visitor to your homing centre is a potential adopter and should be treated with kindness and respect. Part of the adoption process is the interview, which helps you guide them toward the right cat and ensure they understand what cat ownership involves.

The prospective adopters may have either completed a questionnaire, or you may complete it together during a conversation. Your role is to recommend suitable cats and help adopters understand what’s involved in caring for them — time, cost, holiday provisions, vet care, and providing the environment for the cat to thrive.

Adoption interviews should feel relaxed but professional — a friendly, private chat that helps you get to know potential adopters. Keep them informative by providing handouts - will be a lot of information to discuss, and not all of the information will be absorbed at the time.

#### Use the time to understand what getting a cat means to the adopter, which cats they’re drawn to. If expectations seem unrealistic, guide them gently with helpful information. Some common topics are

Not all cats will want to sit on their owners’ laps, and many will have varying tolerance or enjoyment of playing with children. It can help to produce a small leaflet on cat ownership, or direct them to a reliable source of information online.

Cats are not small dogs, and every cat is different. They have their own unique set of needs as felines and as an individual cat, so they must be allowed to initiate contact with people on their terms.

Cats should have plenty of opportunity to express their natural behaviours (e.g., climbing, stalking, scratching, hiding, etc.)

If you have concerns about someone’s suitability to have a cat, consider your reservations rationally and discuss them with your team. Sometimes, reservations represent a prejudice you may have unintentionally developed, perhaps as a result of a previous bad experience. Explore the potential home fully and openly before deciding against it being a viable option.

## What if the potential owner wants a particular cat that you don’t feel is suitable?

If a potential adopter wants a specific cat you feel isn’t a good match, explain your concerns honestly and focus on finding a better fit. Use what you’ve learned about their home and expectations to recommend cats that suit them well - ideally including those who’ll make wonderful pets but are struggling with confinement, as they should be prioritised for homing. This is why it may help to complete the paperwork and interview before adopters see the cats available.

Bear in mind that red cats can seem like ‘hard sells’ because they may hide or appear withdrawn. If you’re confident the cat is well socialised to people and simply finding confinement difficult, reassure adopters that this behaviour reflects distress, not personality. With a little patience, kindness, and time to adjust, these cats often flourish in a calm, loving home.

If an adopter insists on a green cat and it’s still a good match, don’t risk losing the home - it’s still a great outcome! Green cats are usually easier to place, as they tend to have fewer specific needs.

## Pre-adoption ‘home-checks’?

Some organisations still carry out pre-adoption home checks, but these can delay cats going to their new homes and use valuable time and resources.

If your checks often uncover serious risks that could harm a cat, it makes sense to continue them. However, if you rarely reject homes, or only do so based on personal preference rather than genuine welfare concerns, it may be time to rethink how that time is spent.

There are quicker, effective alternatives to in-person visits, such as viewing the property on Google Maps or asking for photos of where the cat will live.

Offering potential adopters a simple self-assessment tool can also help them think through what caring for a cat really involves.

Support and guidance for new owners

Some new owners need extra support in the first few weeks after adoption. Often, assumptions can be made that a cat will be extremely grateful to be in a loving home and will transform into ‘the ideal pet’ almost instantly. To avoid disappointment, manage the owner’s expectations by explaining what to expect, such as that it’s not uncommon for new cats to remain hidden for the first week while they settle in. Cats are best left to adjust to their new circumstances in their own time. Advice onhow to help a new cat or kitten settle inis available on our website for new owners.

Key Points

Be proactive - promote your cats and stay visible in the community.

Make adoption easy - be kind, welcoming, and supportive.

Be flexible - focus on welfare, not unnecessary rules.

Keep interviews relaxed, professional, and informative.

Match cats and people carefully using what you know about each cat.

Guide adopters gently but persistently if a cat they want isn’t a good fit.

Prioritise to home red cats struggling in confinement.

Use practical alternatives to home checks.

Manage expectations early, and follow up with advice and support.
