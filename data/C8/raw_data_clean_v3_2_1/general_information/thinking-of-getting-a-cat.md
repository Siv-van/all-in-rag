# Thinking of getting a cat?

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/thinking-of-getting-a-cat

---

Before welcoming a new cat into your home, there are many things to consider. Can you provide everything a cat needs to live a happy and healthy life? Are there other animals and people at home to think about? Where should you get a cat from? What are you looking for – a companion to sit on your lap, or an independent cat who doesn’t need too much attention?

While there’s no guaranteed way to choose the perfect cat, understanding your expectations as well as what a cat needs from you will help make the right choices for both you and your new cat.

## What do I need to think about before getting a cat?

#### One of the first things to think about is if you’re able to meet a cat’sfivewelfare needs. You’ll need to be able to

1. Allow your cat to express natural behaviours

Make sure they have safe spaces to explore, and toys to mimic instinctive behaviours such as stalking, chasing and catching. Read our article on playing with your cat. Litter traysshould be easily accessible, contain suitable depth and type of litter for cats to scrape and bury their waste, be cleaned daily and should be provided in multiple areas of the home to encourage use.

2. Provide appropriate companionship

although domestic cats can form positive relationships with each other and are now considered a ‘socially flexible’ species, many still prefer living without other cats in the home. Siblings are generally more likely to form positive bonds, but there is no guarantee with cats, as relationships between them can be fragile. Most pet cats will enjoy human companionship, but every cat is different and interactions with cats should never be forced but offered on their own terms. More information about cat friendly interactions can be found here. It’s important to think about the type of cat or kitten you want and choose one that suits your lifestyle and needs. Asking questions about a potential new kitten or cat’s temperament i.e. is the kitten confident, friendly or quiet and shy with people and/or other animals can help make sure you are matched with the right cat or kitten that suits you and your family.

3. Provide a suitable diet

Cats need to be fed regular meals made up of a complete and balanced diet, which should be suitable for their life stage i.e. kitten, adult or senior. Offering easy access to a constant supply of fresh water is vital.

4. Provide a safe and stimulating home

This includes offering a clean and comfortable bed in places where a cat or kitten can perch, rest and sleep. Cats like to climb and observe their surroundings from elevated places, so offering stable resting areas off the ground can provide exercise through climbing and give cats additional privacy and security whilst resting. Kittens are especially inquisitive and more prone to illness or injury, so care must be taken to make sure their environment is safe. Read our article onhelping your new kitten settle infor more information. Introductions to other cats or dogsalready living in the home need to be carried out in a slow and staged way to maximise the chances of success. Cats or kittens that are not given outdoor access need to have additional environmental provision within the home to meet their needs, including regular scheduled playtimes, opportunities to forage for food (e.g.puzzle feeding) and spaces to explore and climb. More details on how to achieve this and make your home cat friendly can be foundhere

5. Take care of all your cat’s healthcare needs

This includes all their preventative healthcare (neutering at four months old, vaccinating against the major feline diseases regularly, treating for fleas and worms), regular grooming, and taking your cat to the vet when they show signs of illness. Older cats, or those affected by illness or disease will need more frequent veterinary checks. Senior cats can make fantastic companions but will need additional care. For more information about special considerations for senior cats, read our separate articlehere.

You’ll also need to think about how much time, care and attention you can give. Most cats can fit into our busy lifestyles. Cats are quite independent and can usually be left alone for longer periods of time than dogs, however, young kittens, senior cats, those with physical health issues, problem behaviours or even those who have particularly strong bonds with their caregivers may not be suited to long periods alone.

## How do I find the right cat – should I get an adult cat or a kitten?

A kitten allows you to develop a lifelong relationship and to provide for your cat in the best way throughout their entire life. Kittens are only kittens for six months out of a possible 14 years or more, and they need a lot of attention and some forethought to keep them occupied and safe. You’ll have to make sure they’re safe when you’re away from home, and you may also have to arrange neutering and primary vaccinations, depending on where your kitten comes from and how old they are.

When it comes to adult cats, confident ones are likely to move in and settle down much more quickly than a nervous cat which will take a little more time and patience. It’s easier to leave an adult cat alone, and in general, they’re less hard work than a kitten. Most adult cats will also be neutered and vaccinated prior to homing.

Another thing to think about is whether to get a domestic cat or a pedigree, which are cats that are selectively bred for specific looks and/or behaviour traits. There’s more to choosing a pedigree than just liking how they look – some breeds need extra care and attention, some don’t like to be left alone for long periods, and others are predisposed to certain health issues which could even affect their quality of life.

## When should I get a new cat?

Choose a time when it’s quiet in your house and, if possible, when you have a couple of free days to help them settle in. Avoid times when you have large periods of time away from home planned in the coming weeks (eg, holiday or work trips).

Cats and people, and other animals

#### Will your new cat be sharing their home with other people and animals? If so, here are some things to think about

## Can I have a cat with a baby or young children?

There’s no reason not to have a cat or kitten if you have children – in fact, it can help children learn about respecting other creatures and being kind. It’s up to you, as the parent, to teach your child from the beginning how to approach and interact with cats gently and kindly. Simple and basic hygiene and safety precautions while the baby is small will ensure all live happily together. For more information on cats and pregnancy in people, read our advice here. Our advice on cats and babies can be foundhere andadditional information on introducing cats to children ishere.

## Can I have a cat if I have a dog?

Yes, depending on your dog – consider your dog’s temperament and life experiences with cats, and ask if your cat or kitten has had any positive experiences with dogs already, as this will help with smooth introductions. Introductions should be made slowly and carefully, with your dog kept on a lead to start with so that your new cat isn’t chased or injured in the early days. Read our article onintroducing a new cat or kitten to a resident dogfor further advice.

## How many cats can I have together?

Cats originate from a largely solitary species, and for those that do live in groups, they’re usually related or self-selected so that they’re not sharing their space with cats they don’t get on with. Signs that your cat doesn’t get along with other resident cats can be quite subtle, so think carefully before bringing more cats into a home where there may already be some tension between the cats living in that household. Read our article onwhether your current cat will accept a new catfor further advice. If you’re bringing a new cat into your home, read our advice onintroducing catsto help both cats adapt and keep stress levels to a minimum.

## FAQs

## Can I keep a cat indoors?

Being outdoors brings a huge amount of variety and enrichment to a cat’s life and makes it easier for your cat to express natural behaviours. There are, of course, risks to going outdoors, but these can be balanced with the physical and mental wellbeing of your cat. Learn more about the pros and cons of keeping cats indoors or allowing outdoor accesshere.

## Can I feed my cat a vegetarian or vegan diet?

No - cats are obligate carnivores which means they need specific nutrients found in meat. It’s dangerous to your cat’s health to feed it a vegetarian or vegan diet. We’ve got more detailed advice onfeeding your cat or kittenfor those that need extra help.

## Is there a cat which doesn’t hunt?

Hunting is normal behaviour for cats. Older cats are likely to hunt less, and some cats don’t bother at all, but there’s no easy way to know how a cat will behave. Learn more about the hunting behaviours of catshere.

## Will I need to groom my cat?

Long-haired cats will need regular grooming sessions, so time will need to be set aside for this, as well as some initial training so your cat becomes accustomed to being groomed. Read our separate article for more information aboutgrooming cats.

## How much does it cost to keep a cat?

There will usually be costs associated with homing your cat in the first instance – how much will depend on if it’s a pedigree cat from a breeder, or a cat from a homing centre. Neutering, if not already done, is a one-off cost, and you’ll need to pay for primary vaccinations and yearly boosters. You’ll also need to provide preventative healthcare regularly (eg, medication to prevent/treat fleas and worms), ensure your cat is microchipped, and consider pet insurance so that if the unexpected happens, your cat is covered. Finally, you’ll need to think about all the physical resources your cat needs at home – food, bowls, beds, litter trays, litter, toys and grooming equipment.

## What happens if my cat needs emergency veterinary care out of normal working hours?

Emergency situations can occur, so it’s best to be prepared and make plans just in case your cat needs emergency veterinary care. Make sure you have access to your local emergency veterinary clinic’s phone number (this may differ from your usual veterinary clinic’s number) address, and a means of transportation in the event your cat needs emergency veterinary attention out of normal working hours.

## I’m allergic to cats – is there any breed which is better than others?

It’s not actually cat hair that causes us to react to some cats, it’s a protein or allergen called Fd1, present in cat’s saliva. Because cats groom themselves regularly, the saliva dries on their coat and then the dust or dander and hairs which contain the allergen are spread. If you’re allergic to cats, you can try visiting the homes of friends or family who have cats to see if there are certain types that elicit less of a reaction, but you may need to consider a different pet depending on how severe your allergy is.

## What should I do with my cat when I go on holiday?

If you’re going away, it’s important to leave your cat in the care of a trusted friend or family member, or you may want to consider a pet sitter or boarding cattery.
