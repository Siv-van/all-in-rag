# Helping your new cat or kitten settle in

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/helping-your-new-cat-or-kitten-settle-in

---

Welcoming a new cat or kitten into your home is an exciting time. And, as with most things when it comes to your new family member, preparation and patience are key to helping them settle in well. Let’s look at all the things you need to make sure this is as stress-free as possible.

Before your new cat arrives, think about where they’re going to live for the first few days or weeks. Kittens, in particular, should be confined to one room - adult cats may also feel more comfortable in one room to start with (and this will be necessary if you have a cat at home already), or may benefit from more space depending on how confident they are. Fill your cat or kitten’s room with all the items they’ll need: food and water bowls, a comfortable bed, a litter tray, toys, and a scratching post.

Read our article onplaying with your catfor more tips and how it can help develop a bond with your kitten.

## How do I prepare my home for my new cat or kitten’s arrival?

Preparing your home and making it a safe and comfortable space is a similar process whether it’s a kitten or an adult cat you’re bringing home, with just a few small differences.

Helping kittens settle in

Experiencing a brand-new home can be daunting for a tiny kitten. Every kitten has a unique personality - some are shy while others are confident. Whatever their character, this is still likely to be a traumatic period for them as they’re experiencing the world alone for the first time.

#### With this in mind, choose a quiet room for your kitten to live in for the first few weeks. To ensure it’s a safe and welcoming space for your kitten

Avoid a room with full-length curtains - your kitten will likely run up them and perch at the top (and probably need rescuing by you - going up is always easier than coming down!).

Check the room for possible hazards such as fireplaces, exposed cables and poisonous plants. Remember, kittens can squeeze into very small spaces.

Similarly, make sure there are no hazardous substances in the room (human medication, cleaning products etc.), and that your kitten can’t access cupboards where these are stored.

Remove all breakable objects from shelves and windowsills and secure all cupboard doors.

Keep windows securely closed.

Place the litter tray in a discreet corner of the room, with the food bowl on the opposite side and the water bowl away from both the food and litter tray. However, cats do like to drink after eating, so placing the water bowl just a few feet away from your kitten’s food bowl is ideal. Consider using the same litter that was used in your kitten’s previous home and feed them the same diet for the first 1-2 weeks, so that at least initially, they will be familiar.

When the kitten arrives, place them in their safe room whilst inside their carrier, and carefully open the door, allowing them to come out in their own time (never force your kitten out of their carrier). The carrier can be left in this room as a safe place for your kitten to retreat to.

You could also place a cardboard box on its side with a thick fleecy blanket inside so that your kitten has somewhere to hide.

Put a padded, washable cat bed in a quiet area away from the food, water and litter tray areas. Line with washable, comfortable bedding.

Place a kitten scratching post nearby (this should be replaced with a taller one as your kitten grows; your cat should always be able to stretch the length of its body vertically up the scratching post).

Have a couple of toys ready for playtime. Don’t leave any toys out with string attached; only use these under supervision so that your kitten isn’t hurt.

The use of synthetic feline pheromone diffusers may also help your cat or kitten feel safer and secure whilst they settle in.

In some cases of introducing a kitten to an adult cat, a large crate for the kitten may be useful, but we recommend seeking behavioural advice to ensure this set up is right for your situation.

We recommend bringing your kitten home with some bedding, if possible, as this is something familiar when everything else around them is new. For the first twenty-four hours, give your kitten time to settle into a calm, quiet environment - don’t overwhelm them with having to meet lots of new people and children too soon.

They will be experiencing many of the room’s sights, sounds, smells and textures for the first time, so be patient and allow them to investigate in their own time.

Once your kitten has explored and found their food, water and litter tray, leave the room for a while. Don’t worry if they don’t show much interest in food initially. And if you brought a familiar piece of bedding home, place it in your kitten’s bed or cardboard box to help them feel at home.

Getting to know your kitten is important to create a bond. A good time to spend time interacting with your kitten is when they’re more naturally active - don’t wake your kitten up for affection or playtime. If your kitten’s responds, play with them, but if they’re not interested or anxious in any way, leave them alone - remember, there’s a lot to take in at the start! Is your kitten hiding? As tempting as it may be, don’t try to coax them out. Instead, spend time in the same room reading or watching TV, and allow your kitten to come to you when they feel ready. Spending time on the floor at their level can make you more appealing to them, too.

Always interact with your kitten when they initiate it. You can invite them to interact with you by offering an outstretched hand to sniff and rub on.

At this age, your kitten needs plenty of rest so always allow them to sleep uninterrupted when they need to. Your kitten will also need 4-6 small meals at regular intervals throughout the day - this helps to avoid any stomach upsets.

Finally, kittens are very playful. Make sure your kitten has an assortment of toys to keep them occupied and exercised - and don’t forget, every kitten (and cat!) loves a cardboard box to play in.

Helping adult cats settle in

For some cats, being confined can be quite a stressful experience. If you’re adopting an adult cat, they may be quite anxious in the early days of coming home. They may retreat to a hiding place, and this is fine - allow them to decide for themselves when it’s safe to explore. Offering elevated and hidden safe places may help create a feeling of privacy and security. You may find during the first few days (or weeks, if they’re especially shy), that your cat only eats and uses their litter tray in the middle of the night - really anxious cats may not even do that for the first twenty-four hours. Just continue putting down fresh food and checking their litter tray regularly. However, if this carries on beyond this time, speak to your veterinary team.

If your new cat is eager to explore their new home, there’s no reason to confine them to one room (unless you have a resident cat). This is when you’ll get to know your cat’s personality, and it’s when you’ll need to be able to read their body language so you can spot anysign of stresseasily. If your cat is reactive and lashes out when approached, take this as a sign that they’re scared, frustrated or confused, and would prefer to meet you in their own time. It can take some cats several weeks to feel safe in a new home, so patience is key, but if they continue to be reactive when touched, contact your veterinary team as sometimes illness or pain can trigger these behaviours.

## When should I let my cat or kitten outside for the first time?

Letting your kitten outside

If you have chosen outdoor access for your kitten, they shouldn’t be allowed outside until at least a week after they’ve finished their primaryvaccinationcourse at around 13-14 weeks old (depending on the vaccine) - any sooner, and you’re putting them at risk of infectious diseases. Once they’re vaccinated, you can let them outside to explore with your supervision. Before allowing your kitten to have free outdoor access, make sure they areneutered (this can be done at around 4 months of age), have fully healed from their surgery and have become used to life in your house, too. Most kittens will benefit from supervised time outside until they are 6 months old, so you can use this time to play with your kitten (using a wand toy or something similar) and teach them to recall using a reward such as food. You can do this at home first, by calling them and offering their favourite treats when they respond.

Letting your adult cat outside

We recommend keeping your new cat indoors for at least two to three weeks, so that they become used to their new home and are less likely to panic and stray in search of somewhere more familiar.

Additional considerations for kittens

Setting a routine

Set routines with your kitten from day one to make life easier for you in the future. Cats are naturally more active at dawn and dusk, but your kitten can soon learn to adjust their sleeping patterns to fit in with your lifestyle. To help them settle at night, provide a bed with high sides to keep out draughts and a low front for easy access, and make sure the lining of the bed is thick to keep your kitten warm and comfortable.

Feeding your new kitten

When your kitten first comes home, feed them on the same food they’ve been used to - a sudden change, combined with the stress of getting used to a new home, can cause an upset stomach and diarrhoea. If you want to change your kitten’s diet, do so gradually by mixing the old food with the new food, increasing the amount of new food and decreasing the amount of old food over a 7-14 day period. Feed a high-quality commercial kitten food to make sure your kitten is getting all the nutrients needed to develop. If the diet is marked as ‘complete’, it contains everything your kitten needs to stay healthy. If it’s marked as ‘complementary’, it doesn’t contain everything needed and will have to be fed alongside another food.

Both kittens and adult cats (where possible) need to be fed little and often. Feed the correct amount according to the instructions, but then split this into 4-5 meals a day. You may want to offer dry food on an occasional basis – it depends on your lifestyle, what your kitten likes and is used to, and if you have other cats in the house with certain feeding routines and habits.

As tempting as it may be, don’t give your kitten cow’s milk as it can cause diarrhoea. If you’d like to offer milk, use one that’s specially made for cats. Be mindful not to feed your kitten too many treats, as this can also cause vomiting or diarrhoea. Fresh drinking water should always be available.

Toilet training your kitten

Cats are very fussy about their toilet habits and kittens usually learn to use a litter tray by copying their mother. If your kitten hasn’t discovered the litter tray on its own, you can place them in the tray when they wake up and after meals, or if your kitten is sniffing, scratching, or beginning to crouch and looks as if they’re about to go to the toilet!

Identification for your kitten

It’s important that your cat can be identified if they become lost or injured away from home. Microchipping is the best form of permanent identification and it will be a legal requirement in England from 10th June 2024 for cats over 20 weeks of age. Microchips are also used to activate some cat flaps, which can be useful to allow selective entry or resident cats into the home. When your kitten becomes older (over 6 months old), you may choose tofit a collarso that they have some form of identification or to carry a magnet or ‘key’ to an electronic cat flap – never put a collar on just for the sake of wearing one. For a young, rapidly growing cat you will need to remember to check the collar’s fit and increase its size accordingly - you should be able to get one or two fingers under the collar and the collar should always have a breakaway fastening for maximum safety.

Grooming your kitten

It’s a good idea to get your kitten used to being groomed from a young age, especially if they have a long coat which will need daily attention. Grooming removes excess hair which can lead to a build-up of fur balls in the stomach. It also gives you a daily chance to check over your cat and build your bond - just make sure it’s always a rewarding and pleasant experience.

FAQ’s

## When should I introduce my children to our new cat or kitten?

If you have young children, allow them limited supervised contact initially to avoid your kitten or cat being over-handled. Read our advice on introducing children to a new cat or kitten for further guidance.

## When should I introduce my resident cat or dog to the new cat or kitten?

Don’t rush to introduce your new kitten or cat to other cats and dogs in the household – this must be done slowly and carefully.
