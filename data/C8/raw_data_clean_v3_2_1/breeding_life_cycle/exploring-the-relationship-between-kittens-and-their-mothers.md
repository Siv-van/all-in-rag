# Exploring the relationship between kittens and their mothers

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/exploring-the-relationship-between-kittens-and-their-mothers

---

Our mothers are a big influence in our lives, and it’s no different for our cats. In this article, we’ll take a closer look at the relationship between mother cats and their kittens and why it’s so important for a kitten’s normal development.

## What do kittens learn from their mother?

A mother cat has a significant influence over her kittens' development of normal cat behaviours. Like many animals, kittens learn many key skills through observation, and their mother is usually their first teacher. An example of learning this way can be during interactions with people. If the mother cat is calm around people and enjoys interacting with them (ie, approaches calmly, but confidently, with a raised question mark tail and/or starts to face and body rub, wrap her tail around the person’s legs and purr, chirrup or trill, or if she jumps on a person’s lap), her kittens will watch her and often learn to respond to people in the same way.

As well as behavioural development, close contact with their mother also helps a kitten’s normal emotional development. Again, if a mother cat behaves calmly around people and is seen to enjoy interacting with them, this has been found to reduce anxiety in her kittens and encourages them to interact with people.

Such is the bond between a mother and her kittens that when they are separated, young kittens may experience significant distress, regardless of whether they’re in a familiar or unfamiliar place. If they are taken to an unfamiliar place, having their mother or siblings with them helps to reduce their distress. An example of this might be a visit to the veterinary clinic forvaccinations, parasite treatment orneutering (if the kitten is neutered before 12-13 weeks.)

## How can we help reduce kitten distress caused by separation from their mother?

Below are some practical tips to try. If you’re worried about how your kittens may react to certain situations, contact your veterinary team, who can guide you through the steps needed for a positive experience.

When kittens visit the veterinary clinic for health checks and vaccinations, they should be transported together, and, where possible, their mother should go on this trip too, particularly when the kittens are very young. It may be safer to transport her in a separate cat carrier, depending on the age of your kittens and how their mother responds to travelling. Read our article ontaking your cat to the vetfor further advice

If your mother cat gets very distressed when travelling, it may be better to leave her at home, as the kittens may learn from her to be scared of travel, but they can be transported with a piece of bedding in the carrier that smells of her to provide familiarity and reassurance

Make sure no kittens are left on their own in the carrier during the veterinary consultation. Keeping them together is ideal as they will provide each other with reassurance, warmth and comfort

Kittens that have to be hand-reared should be kept with their siblings and not separated from one another or go to different hand-rearers. Providing kittens without their mother or other siblings with something warm to cuddle up to can provide them with comfort. Pet-friendly heat pads, well-covered and leak-tested hot water bottles or cuddly toys make good substitutes. Playingcat-centric musiccan also help reassure kittens

## How can we ease separation distress when rehoming kittens?

Some kittens are re-homed as young as 8 weeks, and this will likely be the first time they are separated from their mother and siblings (unless they are rehomed with one or more of their siblings). Having had their contact for the first 8 weeks of their life, suddenly not having that, coupled with a whole new environment, can be very daunting.

Before going to a new home, it’s a good idea to get each kitten used to having some time alone, away from their mother and siblings, for increasing amounts of time (initially, just 1-2 minutes), while they’re in a familiar location. During this time, ensure your kitten has plenty of positive experiences, so they learn that being away from their mother or siblings is nothing to worry about. For example, positive experiences could be playing with your kitten, feeding them or gently stroking them (if they enjoy this). Making sure they transition to their new home with items that smell of their mother and siblings (eg, bedding that has been slept on) can also help make this transition less stressful.

It’s also important to socialise kittens in their sensitive period of two to eight weeks of age, as this is when their brains are most receptive to learning. Socialisation helps kittens to form positive relationships with people and other species, such as dogs.

Kittens also need to become accustomed to living in a domestic home and learn to accept and ignore the sight, sound, smell (and in some cases, movement) of non-threatening objects, such as vacuum cleaners and televisions – this is a process called habituation. More information can be found on this webpage – bringing up a litter of kittens: behavioural considerations.
