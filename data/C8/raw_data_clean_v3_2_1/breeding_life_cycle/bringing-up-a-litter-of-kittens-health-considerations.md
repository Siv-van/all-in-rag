# Bringing up a litter of kittens: health considerations

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/bringing-up-a-litter-of-kittens-health-considerations

---

Has your cat recently had kittens? Or have you recently taken on a young kitten? It brings a lot of responsibility and can feel a little daunting at first. So, what do you need to think about to ensure the kittens are healthy, friendly, and confident? In this article, we will look at everything you need to consider from a health perspective.

## What physical changes will I see from kittens in the first weeks and months?

On average, kittens weigh around 100 grams at birth. Healthy kittens should double their birth weight in the first two weeks, and then continue to gain weight steadily. Weighing the kittens regularly is a good idea so you can be sure they are thriving and growing as they should. When they are small you can use household scales, but as monitoring weight benefits cats of all ages, investing in scales suitable for adult cats (baby scales are often used) is a good idea.

#### Here are some of the other physical changes that take place during the first few weeks of life

0-2 weeks

For the first two weeks of life kittens are quite immobile

For the first two weeks, kittens’ responses revolve around temperature, touch, and smell. While they are quite immobile, they use slow, paddling movements to move short distances. The kittens are completely dependent on their mother (the queen) for feeding - she will initiate nursing and her milk is their only source of nutrition. Teething starts at around two weeks, and their eyes can open any time between 2-16 days (commonly, it is around 7-10 days).

3-4 weeks

From three to four weeks, kittens will use their sight to guide them towards their mother. They will start walking from around three weeks, and by four weeks, they will be able to move a reasonable distance. From around four weeks, kittens normally start to eat solid food, marking the onset of the weaning process.

5-6 weeks

As weaning progresses, kittens start to become responsible for initiating nursing. They will also be able to toilet for themselves, without relying on their mother to lick their bottoms to stimulate urination.

7-8 weeks

By this point, the kittens will start to show adult-like responses to smell and visual threats from other cats. Weaning should be almost complete by seven weeks, and they will also be able to regulate their own body temperature.

9 weeks onwards

Complex motor abilities, such as walking along a narrow fence or turning on it may not develop fully until 10 to 11 weeks after birth, and until around 12-16 weeks, your kittens’ vision will continue to improve.

Kittens can go downhill very quickly. If you are concerned about any of your litter, contact your veterinary team as soon as possible.

## How do I know if the kittens are healthy?

If you notice the kittens are crying excessively, or failing to suck, they are usually hungry or ill. Common signs of illness include weakness, being cold, or regurgitating milk. You may also notice sick kittens lying separately from the rest of the litter and being ignored by their mother. The main health problems seen in kittens include hypothermia, hypoglycaemia, dehydration, diarrhoea, and constipation.

As kittens can go downhill very quickly, if you are concerned about any of your litter, contact your veterinary team as soon as possible.

Healthy, normal kittens should eat or sleep for 90% of the time for the first two weeks of their lives. Well-fed kittens have round bellies and appear content.

Newborn kittens get all their nutritional needs (including water) from their mother’s milk. The first milk is known as colostrum - it is produced for the first two days after birth and contains antibodies to help protect from common diseases until the kitten’s immunity develops. Queens produce around 125ml of milk per day - if the mother is not providing enough milk, you may need to supplement their feeding. In kittens that have been orphaned, or where the queen is not able to feed, total replacement feeding is necessary. Read our advice onhand-rearing kittensfor more information.

## When do I wean the kittens?

Young kitten being weaned onto a solid wet diet

Weaning (moving on to solid food) should begin at around three to four weeks of age. Earlier than this, or if it is done too suddenly, will cause stress to the mother, at what can be a stressful time for the kittens as they start to spend time apart from their mother and littermates. A slow, steady weaning period will lead to healthier kittens.

From around three to four weeks, start offering your kittens wet or dry kitten food (if using dry, you may want to moisten the biscuits with some water or kitten milk to begin with). Feed at least four times daily, or ideally, ad lib (food available always). Kittens fed ad lib will usually eat every few hours.

Offer food on a very shallow saucer or plate for easy access - be prepared for your kittens to stand in and walk through the food, so it will need changing regularly. Feed small amounts at a time. If the kittens need encouragement, try a little food on your finger or on the tip of a small spoon. Over the course of a few weeks, you will notice the kittens starting to eat more of the kitten food and take less milk from their mother.

Kittens should gain weight steadily and be fully weaned by around six to eight weeks of age. From this point, feed them using the recommendations of the food manufacturer as a guide (you will find this on the food packet). Kittens should always be fed a diet designed for their age as adult food will lack nutrients for growth.

While kittens can be offered milk replacement or commercial cat milk as part of the weaning process, this isn’t necessary, as they will get all the nutrition they need from their mother and their kitten food.

## How do I train kittens to use a litter tray?

Provide the kittens with a low-sided litter tray from around three weeks of age. Start with sand, wood, or paper-based litter, rather than clay, as some kittens may try to eat the litter. Silica gel shouldn’t be used for kittens under eight weeks of age.

Many kittens learn how to use their litter tray simply by watching their mother. However, if they don’t, stand your kitten in the tray (the best time is after sleeping or eating) and use one of their forepaws to scratch at the litter. Then massage your kitten’s ano-genital area (openings under the tail) with a ‘wet wipe’ or damp ball of cotton wool. Leaving a small amount of soiled litter in the tray can act as a useful scent reminder.

Preventative healthcare for kittens

Treating for worms and fleas

As intestinal parasites (roundworms, hookworms, and tapeworms) are common in kittens, all kittens should be treated for worms from three to six weeks of age. Flea infestation can be serious in young kittens, so discuss treatment with your veterinary team. You can find more specific advice by reading our article onworming your catandflea controlin cats.

Vaccination

Your kittens’ vaccinations should start from around 8-9 weeks of age. Read our advice onvaccinating your catfor more information.

Neutering

If you are not planning on breeding from your cat, they should beneuteredat around 4 months old, before they reach sexual maturity.

If you own the mother cat, you will also need to think about neutering her - she can quickly become pregnant again, so unless you are planning on further breeding, speak to your veterinary team about neutering her as soon as the kittens are weaned.

Insurance

It is a good idea to insure kittens at an early age – they can go to new homes with insurance covering the next few weeks or insurance can be arranged promptly on rehoming.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If the kitten (s) or queen are showing any signs of ill health, contact your veterinary team immediately. Your veterinary team will also be happy to provide support and answer any questions you may have as your kittens grow.

Additional Resources and Practical tips

In this article, we’ve looked at the various health considerations involved in bringing up a litter of kittens. Of equal importance, is how well your kittens interact with people, and the responsibility for how your kittens behave towards people for the rest of their lives can depend on how they’re brought up in the first seven to eight weeks.
