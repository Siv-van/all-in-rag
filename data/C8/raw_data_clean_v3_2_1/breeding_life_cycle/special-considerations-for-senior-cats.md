# Special considerations for senior cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/special-considerations-for-senior-cats

---

Cats are living much longer now thanks to better nutrition, veterinary, and home care. Over recent years, cats’ life stages have been redefined - as a result, cats are now considered ‘mature’ once they reach 7 years, ‘senior’ between 11-14 years, and ‘super senior’ when they’re 15 years and older.

#### Understanding a cat’s age in human years can be helpful as it gives us a greater appreciation of the illnesses and difficulties our cats may be facing as they get older. The formula for calculating the equivalent age is simple

The first two years of a cat’s life equate to 24 human years and every year thereafter is equivalent to 4 human years.

For example, a 16-year-old cat would be equivalent to an 80-year-old human.

## What are the effects of ageing in cats?

As your cat ages, expect to see changes to their body function (physiology), behaviour and vulnerability to certain illnesses.

Physiological changes include reduced ability to smell and taste food, reduced ability to digest fat and protein, and reduced hearing, immune function, skin elasticity and stress tolerance.

Your cat’s behaviour will also change as they age, often as a direct result of these physiological changes. Because cats adapt to these changes gradually, it can be easy to miss signs of ageing unless you’re specifically looking out for them. Older cats hunt less, spend less time outside, sleep for longer periods and are generally less active. They may become fussy with their food or have a reduced appetite, be less keen to play, groom themselves less, and be more vocal. You may also find they become more dependent on you, as older cats tend to become more insecure.

## How do I care for my senior cat?

As your cat gets older, they’ll naturally struggle with keeping themselves clean. By helping them and checking them daily, you’re not just improving their wellbeing, it’s also an opportunity to detect any potential problems early and take the necessary action.

#### Here’s what we recommend to help care for your senior cat

### Claw trimming

Senior cats can struggle with retracting their claws, so you may find they get caught in furniture and carpets. Claws can also overgrow, curl around and embed into pads, which is not only painful but can also lead to infection if not treated. If your cat is willing and you’re able, trimming their claws regularly can prevent any issues. Speak to your vet first because sometimes overgrown claws can indicate a medical problem such as arthritis or hyperthyroidism. They can also show you how to safely trim your cat’s claws if you wish to do so at home.

## Grooming

As cats age and become less flexible, they often struggle with maintaining their usual grooming routine. In particular, you may notice some matting over the lower spine and hindquarters. Again, it is important to speak to your vet as decreased grooming behaviours may indicate chronic (long-term) pain from arthritis or dental disease.

An example of a senior cat showing clumped fur on its flanks and hindquarters.

Regular grooming can help keep matts at bay and remove any excess hair, helping to prevent hairballs. We recommend using a soft brush and fine comb, and while gently grooming your cat, you can also check for lumps, bumps, sores, or anything you think may need a veterinary check-up. If you do find any matted fur, always tease out the matts rather than trying to cut them away with scissors as it can be very easy to damage your cat’s skin this way. Longhaired cats may benefit from a trim around the back end to prevent soiling if they’re struggling to clean themselves, and you may also need to regularly clean discharge from eyes, ears, and the back end of your cat using moist cotton pads.

## Toilet habits

We recommend a litter tray for older cats, as even those with outdoor access may get to a point where they don’t want to go outside in cold, damp weather. It also means you can check their urine and faeces for any abnormalities.

A litter tray with lower sides that is easier to get in and out of is ideal.

## Dental checks

Check your cat’s teeth and mouth regularly for signs of growths, reddening of the gums or any other signs ofdental disease. Bad breath, drooling, a ‘chattering’ jaw, reduced appetite and pawing at the mouth may all be signs of dental disease. If you’re worried, always speak to your vet.

Regular health checks

In addition to being extra vigilant at home to any potential health problems, your vet will be able to advise you on how often your cat would benefit from health checks with them, based on your cat’s age and general health.

Encouraging your cat to eat

#### As your cat gets older and their sense of smell and taste diminishes, you may find they lose their appetite slightly. There are several ways you canencourage your cat to eat, for example

Offer food little and often – smaller meals can be easier to digest, so try around four to six meals per day as a starting point. We also recommend finding a quiet area for mealtimes so that your cat isn’t distracted by noise and activity.

Try both familiar and unfamiliar foods to tempt your cat’s appetite.

Think about the type of bowl your cat has - they may prefer a wide, shallow bowl or one with a rim.

Example of a senior cat feeding from a bowl placed in a raised position - in this case, the first step of the stairs.

Offer food at room temperature - gently warming food to just below body temperature can increase palatability.

Experiment with the consistency of the food offered - some senior cats, especially those with dental problems, prefer soft food to chunks or biscuits. You can also try adding a small amount of water to your cat’s food and mashing it with a fork.

Raise your cat’s food bowl - if your cat isarthritic, raising their food bowl (for example, onto a box) can make eating more comfortable as it prevents them from bending down, potentially putting strain on sore joints.

Don’t leave uneaten wet food out for more than an hour - and don’t be tempted to offer a range of foods at once as this can be overwhelming for your cat.

Stroking and talking to your cat can help to increase appetite, and you may also want to try hand feeding.

Drinking

Senior cats are at greater risk of dehydration, especially those suffering from conditions such as chronic kidney disease. Make sure that a variety of water bowls are available around your home which are easily accessible and away from areas where food is eaten. As with food bowls, your cat may find it easier to drink from a raised bowl, and you may want to try different bowls (for example, a ceramic bowl, glass, or drinking fountain) and even different types of water (tap water, boiled, filtered, spring etc.) to find your cat’s preference. ## When should I contact my vet about my senior cat?

#### If your cat is showing any of the signs below, make an appointment with your vet

Loss of appetite

Weight loss

Drinking more often or drinking a larger amount per day

Stiffness, limping or difficulty jumping

Lethargy/tiredness

Lumps or bumps anywhere on the body

Balance problems

Toilet accidents or difficulty passing urine or faeces

Disorientation or distress

Uncharacteristic behaviour, such as hiding, aggression, and excessive vocalisation

Changes in grooming behaviour

Additional considerations

In addition to the extra care we’ve talked about giving your senior cat, there are small changes you can make to your home to make life a little easier for them.

We have specific advice onmaking your home cat-friendly, and many of those recommendations work just as well for older cats, with a few small changes. If your cat is finding stairs difficult, you may find they’re spending more time on one level. If this is the case, make sure all their resources (food and water bowls, beds, litter trays, scratching posts, toys) are on that level to make life easier.

If you have laminate, tiled or wood flooring, it can be slippery for older cats. Place mats and runners around the home to make walking easier. For homes with carpets, use cut pile runners over loop pile carpets if you have them - it prevents your cat from getting their claws caught.

Playtime

Does your cat have a favourite toy? Older cats like to play, too! Larger ‘kick’ toys are a good choice as they can encourage your cat to kick with their back legs, which is a great exercise for those with stiff hindlegs.

As all cat owners know, a simple cardboard box is a real favourite for cats. Older cats may struggle to jump in and out easily, so lie the box on its side or cut an opening in the box to provide easy access (and hours of entertainment!).

Scratching posts

Horizontal scratching posts are easier for older cats to use than tall, vertical posts which can place strain on arthritic joints. Using a scratching post is still important for those who enjoy scratching, it provides good exercise for muscles in the front legs and can prevent nails from thickening and over growing.

Lookouts

Cats love to look outdoors and most enjoy sitting on high windowsills, but jumping up can be difficult for some senior cats. Providing a series of shallow steps, or a gently sloping ramp, can help your older cat to comfortably reach their favourite lookout.

A senior cat enjoying its favourite lookout spot on a sunny windowsill. Consider placing soft objects beneath highspots for senior cats who are losing their balance or agility

Puzzle feeders

For a spot of mental stimulation, some older cats enjoy the challenge ofpuzzle feeders- just make sure it’s not too much effort and stops your cat from eating their required amount.

Litter trays

Position litter trays away from other resources, such as food and water, but for the very senior or those cats suffering fromcognitive dysfunction, keep all resources within easy reach to avoid confusion. Open trays with low sides are ideal for older cats, but trial and error will help you to find your cat’s preference.

Beds

Many cats prefer higher-up places to sleep, such as their owner’s bed, or a windowsill or sofa. As your cat may struggle to jump as they get older, provide ramps and steps where possible so your cat can still reach their favourite sleeping spots easily. Narrow windowsills should have padded objects underneath, as some cats struggle with their balance as they age and can fall easily. Where you can, encourage your cat to sleep on secure, wider surfaces. For ultimate comfort, place a thermal blanket out for your cat that’s easily washable, but that can help keep them warm.

A homemade, carpeted ramp helps this senior cat reach its favourite spot on the sofa

Garden

Some older cats become reluctant to go outside because of other cats and the feeling they’re no longer able to defend their patch. Make sure your garden is secure so that you can prevent other cats entering your garden and keep your cat safe, and still able to enjoy their own outside space. Read our article onfencing your gardenfor further advice.

Holiday care

If your cat has always gone into a cattery when you’re on holiday, there’s no reason to change their routine. However, older cats don’t cope particularly well with changes to their routine, so there may come a time when your cat may prefer to stay at home with someone visiting, or staying over, to provide the necessary care. Ideally, the cat-sitter should be someone your cat’s familiar with.
