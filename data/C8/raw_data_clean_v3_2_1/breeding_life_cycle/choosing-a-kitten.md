# Choosing a kitten

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/choosing-a-kitten

---

If you’re committed to taking on a kitten and sharing your life with them for the next 14 to 15 years (or longer!), it’s important to make the right choice after you’ve decidedwhere to get your kittenfrom.

Often, it’s looks that we go for – a favourite colour or marking. However, there’s much more to it than that – you need to know about the kitten’s personality, needs and health.

## What shapes a kitten’s personality?

Cats have a wide range of personalities. How confident or fearful a cat is as an adult will be affected by what happens when it’s a kitten. Much of this ‘personality’ development has already taken place before we get our kitten. For the cat, learning to enjoy the company of people takes place early in life – somewhere from around 3 weeks to 8 weeks old. During this time, the kitten hasn’t yet learnt to fear everything, and their mind is open to forming bonds with other animals or people and learning how to deal with new experiences without being overwhelmed.

If kittens don’t encounter people in those early weeks, they may never be able to see them as part of ‘normal’ life. A kitten who hasn’t been handled by people, met dogs or experienced everyday things such as vacuum cleaners, doorbells or children laughing, may automatically find them threatening and react accordingly. They may try to avoid interacting, hide away or react if a person gets too close. While kittens continue to learn beyond 8 weeks of age, if the fundamentals are missing there may be little or nothing to build upon. So, a fearful kitten is likely to be a fearful cat and no amount of love from an owner may change this.

Using this information when you’re choosing a kitten, can help you to ask questions to determine whether the kitten has had the right early experiences to fit in with your lifestyle – for example, bringing home a nervous kitten if you want a loving, affectionate adult cat is not going to work.

Research the litter by asking questions before you view any kittens. If you’re not satisfied with any of the answers, then it may be better to look elsewhere for another kitten.

## What questions should I ask before viewing a kitten?

## What have the kittens experienced in their first 8 weeks of life?

This is a sensitive time in kitten learning. The best scenario is that kittens have been kept in a home environment, or moved into the home environment well within the first 8 weeks of life, and are used to all those things associated with a home – people, noises, smells, visitors, children, dogs etc. The worst scenario is that the kittens have been kept away from all the things associated with normal living, so that when they experience it in a new home, it’s frightening, and they have difficulty adjusting to it. This can happen in homing/rescue centres if kittens are kept in a pen and not handled or exposed to everyday living – many good homing/rescue centres ensure kittens are handled regularly by different people and experience everyday sights and sounds. This can also happen with pedigree breeders if too many cats are being bred, often in outside pens, and each litter is not getting the handling and exposure it needs to become confident pet cats.

Kittens kept in a home environment will be used to all those things associated with a typical home, such as people, noises, smells, visitors, children, dogs and so on

What is the temperament of the mother cat (and the father if known?)

‘Friendliness’ can be influenced by genes, and genetics will play a part in how kittens react to he world. Some will be bold and some naturally nervous or shy. For domestic cats, the combination of genes from each parent isn’t usually controlled by people and often the father (tom) of the kittens is never seen. A friendly mother (queen) will pass on friendly genes, as well as being relaxed and interactive with people as an example to her kittens. For pedigree cat breeders who control the mating of their cats, there’s a chance to breed from friendly cats to pass this on to the next generation.

## If I decide on a pedigree breed, is there a breed disposition for a certain type of behaviour?

Cats in general have very individual personalities – some are noisy, some are active and others are very laid back. However, there are some breeds where some aspects of personality are likely to come through – Siamese cats, for example, are known for their ‘talkative’ nature and certain breeds are quite demanding of attention. So, it’s best to know what you might be taking on. There’s no guarantee it will happen, but it’s more likely when this behaviour is seen among this group or breed of cats.

Ask about your requirements

If you have a dog at home, it’s a lot easier to introduce the kitten if they have already met dogs and aren’t frightened. Likewise, if you have children, ask if the kitten has met children. If the kitten has just been around women and not men, it may be fearful of loud deep voices, so again, asking the right questions is important.

The answers to these questions will give you an idea of the quality of care the kittens are receiving. The next step is to visit and view the kittens.

The Kitten Checklisthas been put together by 20 animal welfare and veterinary organisations, to help you make an informed decision when choosing a kitten. It will help you find a healthy, friendly kitten and avoid some of the pitfalls which can occur.

Looking for clues about the kitten’s health

When choosing a kitten, check for signs of ill health, such as runny eyes or nose, dirty ears, and a dirty or sore area under the tail which may indicate the kitten is suffering from diarrhoea. The kitten should look healthy, with bright eyes, a good coat and be able to move easily.

Ask to see the other kittens in the litter and the mother to make sure they’re healthy too.

Follow your instincts. If you’ve arrived at a place that is dirty, smelly and has lots of other cats and kittens, be wary. Good hygiene is essential to keeping kittens healthy.

## What questions should I ask to find out about a kitten’s needs?

## May I handle the kittens?

Ask to handle the kitten to find out how relaxed it is with people. Is it well-socialised and friendly or is it scared? Spend time with the kitten so you can understand if it’s just initially a little wary of new people but soon adapts, or whether it just tries to hide away. Ask to see the whole litter to find out how they react to you, each other and the environment.

## What sex is the kitten and how old is it?

Ask about the kitten’s sex and how old it is. Pedigree kittens are usually over 12 weeks old when they are rehomed but domestic kittens may be around 8 weeks old.

## What type of coat will the kitten have?

It can be hard to tell if a domestic kitten is going to be long-haired unless their mother has a long coat. Certain coats need regular grooming, so only take on a long-haired cat if you’re prepared to make the time for this.

## What other care will the kitten need, both now and as an adult?

Some breeds, such as Persians, have very flat faces, which can cause an overflow of tears. Owners must be able to clean the eyes and face regularly to prevent some health problems.

Ask about vaccinations. Most pedigree kittens will have had their vaccinations before they can be homed, but it’s worth checking. Many kittens from rescue centres may also have had at least one vaccination, depending on the age at which they are homed. Make sure you get the vaccination certificate if undertaken.

Find out if the kittens have been wormed and treated for fleas and if so, which treatment was used.

Ask if any of the kittens have ongoing health issues that require medication. If the kitten is a pedigree, ask about any tests for inherited diseases.

## How much attention will the kitten need, both now and as an adult?

Some breeds need more attention and company than others, some cats will be much more independent. Again, decide what suits you.

## What food, litter etc. is currently being used?

Ask what the kitten is currently eating, what type of litter it is used to using etc. so that you can decide if you want to keep your kitten on the same diet/litter when you take them home, or if you need to gradually change them over.

Ask if the kitten is insured

In countries where it’s available, such as the UK, many breeders, rescue centres and even individuals who’ve bred an ‘accidental’ litter will provide new owners with insurance cover for the kitten which lasts for the first 6 weeks in their new home. This covers health issues and other benefits. The insurance company will then contact the new owner to see if they wish to continue with the insurance. Speak to your veterinary team if you’d like more advice on insurance for your cat.

Additional considerations

Be prepared to walk away

Potential owners must be prepared to walk away and not buy a kitten out of pity because it’s ill or scared, just to ‘save’ it from its current environment. As hard as this can be, it can also be difficult taking on a kitten with health or behaviour problems, if you’re not prepared.

Do your homework

Do your homework first, then go and visit. Most breeders will ask you to come along for a first viewing when the kittens are still a bit too young to home (they usually need to be kept until they’re about 12 weeks old and need to have had at least their first vaccinations to protect them).

A good breeder or rescue centre will want to know that you’ll care for the kitten properly and will give you lots of advice. They should be able to provide help or guidance if you need it and will want to hear from you if you have any problems. They should also be willing to take the kitten back should serious problems arise.
