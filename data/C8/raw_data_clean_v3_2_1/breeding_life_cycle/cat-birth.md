# Cat Birth

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/cat-birth

---

Cats are usually very fertile and generally give birth (also known as ‘parturition’), without any problems - especially domestic cats (non-pedigrees). Your cat will have a nine-week pregnancy, so from around 63 days after mating, you can prepare for your cat to give birth.

Preparation for your cat to give birth should have been made in advance (read our article onpregnancyfor more detailed advice). In this article, we’ll look at what you can expect from a normal cat birth, the causes of birthing difficulty (dystocia) and when you might need to seek veterinary advice.

## What are the signs that my cat is about to give birth?

#### Just before your cat gives birth, she may show some of the following signs

- Purring and panting

- Becoming restless and vocal (meowing)

- Grooming excessively (especially around the birth passage and teats)

- Showing nesting behaviour (looking for somewhere quiet, clean, and warm to give birth)

## How do I know when my cat is giving birth?

A normal cat birth can generally be broken down into three stages. That said, with cats, the second and third stages are repeated with each kitten and the third stage is brief, and almost continuous with the second.

The first stage

Thefirst stageis where the cervix and vagina relax, and the uterus starts to contract.

You won’t be able to see your cat straining at this point, though you may be able to feel the kittens moving through your cat’s belly. There’s not much else to see at this stage, except for your cat making repeated visits to her kittening bed. Cats that are very dependent on their owners may also seek your reassurance.

Your cat may start scratching up and making her bed, and late on in the first stage, she may start to pant. In cats kittening for the first time, this stage can be very prolonged - it may last up to 36 hours, which can be normal.

The second and third stages of birth

As thesecond stagebegins, the uterine contractions become stronger and more frequent. The first kitten, contained within its membranes, is pushed towards and into the pelvic opening. As the first kitten enters the birth canal the outer layer of the membranes appears at the vulva as the ‘water bag’ and bursts, releasing some fluid. This is usually cleared up by your cat. The inner layer retains some of the fluid to lubricate and assist the passage of the kitten.

As your cat pushes to help her kitten through the pelvis, you’ll usually notice straining for the first time, and the kitten will normally be born within 30 minutes.

Thethird stagefollows straight away and is simply where the membranes, complete with the separated placenta (also known as afterbirth) are passed. Usually, this is passed after each kitten - but if, for example, a second kitten follows quickly, the membranes from the first kitten will be trapped temporarily and the two sets will be passed together. If possible, count how many placentas have passed and ensure there’s one per kitten, as any left behind could pose an infection risk. Contact your vet if you have any concerns.

As each kitten is born, your cat will tear open the membranes, clearing the kitten’s mouth and nose so they can breathe. She’ll bite off the umbilical cord and eat the afterbirth. These second and third stages are repeated for each kitten.

The time between the delivery of kittens varies. It can be as little as 10 minutes or up to an hour. On average, cats tend to have around four kittens per litter, but this can range from one to twelve. Larger litter sizes are more commonly seen in pedigree breeds, such as Oriental, Siamese and Burmese.

After one or more kittens have been born, you may notice your cat stops straining and rests happily, suckling her kittens. She’ll be keen to eat and drink and will behave perfectly normally, apart from the fact that it’s obvious from her size and shape, and the presence of movement, that there are still kittens to be born. This resting stage can last up to 24 or even 36 hours, after which she’ll start straining again, and the remainder of the litter is born.

Keeping the kittens warm

Warmth is essential for newborn kittens as they can’t react to cold by shivering, or control their own body temperature. Normally, warmth is provided by direct body contact with the mother. If the mother is not cooperative, keep the kittens warm with a well-covered hot water bottle or heat pad, and conserve heat with a covering blanket.

## When to contact your veterinary team

In cats with normal interrupted labour, it will be obvious that your cat is not in distress, has a normal appetite and is perfectly happy with the kittens already born. Straining during a normal birth, while it may or may not be vigorous, is clearly productive in moving the kitten along and does not appear to cause pain. If you’re worried about your cat in any way while she’s giving birth, always contact your vet for advice.

#### Times to contact the veterinary clinic include

If labour does not start despite your cat having passed the due date

- There is a discoloured discharge present before the birth of a kitten

If there is a very bloody discharge during labour

- Signs of a blockage (obstruction), such as straining without producing any kittens, panting, crying, appearing exhausted, restless and unsettled, and finally, giving up any attempts to gain sufficient strength to push again

If the cat has been straining for 20-30 minutes without delivering a kitten, or if she’s crying out in pain

- When no kittens are born despite signs of labour

## Complications

Most cats (particularly non-pedigrees) give birth without a problem, and a normal birth needs no intervention from you. However, it can be useful to know when your cat may be having difficulty giving birth and what are some of the causes and potential signs that your cat is experiencing a problem. Always consult your veterinary team if you have any concerns about a pregnant or labouring cat.

Dystocia (difficulty in giving birth) can be caused by problems with the queen (mother cat) or kittens (foetus). It can also be classified according to whether it arises from obstruction of the birth canal or a functional deficiency of the uterine muscle. Dystocia can be more likely in some breeds such as British Shorthair, Oriental breeds, Birman, Ragdoll, Abyssinian and Somali cats.

Obstructive dystociais caused by a mismatch between the size of the kittens and the queen’s birth canal. Factors affecting the size of the queen’s birth canal include skeletal disorders (eg, a healed pelvic fracture), or problems with the pelvic soft tissue or the uterus itself (eg, uterine torsion or rupture). Foetal causes of obstructive dystocia may be due to malpresentation (which way round the kitten (foetus) is coming [eg, head or tail first]), severe foetal malformation (eg, hydrocephalus, conjoined twins), foetal oversize or foetal death.

Functional dystociais usually called inertia and can be either primary or secondary. Primary inertia is the most common cause of dystocia in cats. It’s seen when the uterus produces no, or only weak, infrequent contractions, so the kittens aren’t passed through the birth canal. Primary inertia may be caused by stress, old age, obesity, ill health or by certain drugs. Primary inertia due to stress, also known as ‘hysterical inertia’, is not uncommon and can be seen in the Oriental, Siamese and Burmese breeds. Extreme apprehension in the first stage of labour causes any progress to stop, and the cat will be distressed, crying constantly and demanding attention.

Abnormalities of the first stage of labourinclude all forms of primary inertia and occasionally rare disorders, such as torsion (twisting of the uterus, cutting off its blood supply) or rupture of the uterus.

Abnormalities of the second stage of labour. Secondary inertia develops after prolonged second-stage labour and may be caused by obstructive dystocia, muscle fatigue, or excessive pain.

Additional post-birth considerations

In normal situations, the mother cat (queen) will clean and dry her kittens, so don’t interfere unless necessary. If a kitten is not breathing, however, or on those few occasions when the maternal instinct appears to be lacking and the kitten is ignored, reviving the kitten (s) becomes a matter of urgency.

#### If you find yourself in a situation where you need to revive any kittens, follow the steps below

Tear the membranes from the nose, wipe the nose and open the mouth, tilt the kitten’s head down and clear away any fluid

If the cord hasn’t broken on delivery, tear it a good inch from the kitten and remove the membranes. Complicated cutting and tying of the cord aren’t necessary. The cat would chew it through, providing a blunt crushing action to prevent bleeding; tearing it between the first two fingers and thumb does much the same thing. The kitten should be supported, and the cord held at the kitten end so that the risk of pulling on the kitten is minimised

If the kitten isn’t breathing, or if they’ve come out tail first and possibly inhaled fluid, you’ll need to clear debris and fluid from the air passages. One of the traditionally used methods involves swinging the kitten, but newborn kittens are slippery and easy to drop, so rubbing the kitten’s back with a towel with its head lower than its bottom can be tried first. If swinging the kitten, place the kitten in the palm of your hand, its back towards the palm and neck between your forefinger and third finger, its head protruding between your fingers. Enclose the kitten in your fingers and, turning the hand palm downwards with the arm extended, with adequate room around you, give a very gentle swing – don’t be too vigorous, or this can cause serious injury to the kitten. The swing will force fluids out of the air passages – wipe the nose and mouth to clear it away. The swing will also stimulate breathing

Next, stroke and rub the kitten with a clean towel to stimulate breathing. Assuming the kitten is, by now, breathing regularly, this can be followed by a brisk general rub dry

## Are there any potential problems for a queen after giving birth?

#### Some post-kittening complications include

- Retention of foetal membranes - where a cat fails to pass the final set of membranes after she’s finished giving birth

- Metritis (inflammation of the uterus) - occurs occasionally, usually within three days of giving birth

- Uterine prolapse - Where the uterus telescopes and then protrudes from the vulva. It occurs rarely, but when seen it’s an emergency, and your veterinary team must be contacted immediately

- Mastitis (inflammation of the mammary glands)- sometimes occurs during early lactation

- Lactation tetany - While the precise causes of the condition are unknown, lactation tetany involves a sudden drop in the amount of calcium circulating in the bloodstream

If your cat is showing signs of any of the above, or general signs of ill health after giving birth, contact your veterinary team immediately.
