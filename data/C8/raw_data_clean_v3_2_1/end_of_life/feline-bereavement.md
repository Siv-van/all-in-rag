# Feline bereavement

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/feline-bereavement

---

The death of a cat companion has a deep impact on a household due to grieving humans, changes in routine and the absence of a familiar part of the social unit.

The remaining cats in the house can show either positive or negative responses to the loss, often determined by whether the cats were bonded. If the cats are bonded and part of the same social group, then the length of time the cats have lived together, as well as the amount of time they have spent engaging in friendly social activities such as resting close together, grooming each other, nose touching or body rubbing have been reported (by caregivers) to influence the remaining cat’s behaviour more strongly.

## What are the signs my cat is grieving?

If your cat does react to the loss there are often three common stages of grief. The first is quite short and you’ll notice your cat is more vocal, pacing and searching the house. Your cat might be attempting to find the missing cat and may be seen looking out of windows or sniffing as they go from room to room. You may also see an increase in attention-seeking behaviours during this time and your cat may want to spend more time with you.

The second stage is more passive, where your cat may become withdrawn and inactive, avoiding contact with people or other animals in the house. Some cats will even hide. You may see a decrease in certain normal behaviours, such as sleeping properly and playing. More sensitive and social breeds like Siamese and Burmese may eat less and appear quite unwell for several weeks during this part of the process. This stage lessens with time until your cat reaches the third and final stage, of acceptance.

Some cats will become more friendly and attentive towards their owners. Others will appear to ‘blossom’ after losing their companion and become more vocal and active. This is especially true for cats that live together, but do not have a strong bond and are not part of the same social group.

There’s no set period for grieving in cats. Some cats will go through the process in a matter of days, others will skip the withdrawn, depressed phase, and a few may be unsettled for several weeks or months. Some cats will show no response at all.

## How can I help my cat through the grieving process?

Losing a pet can be very distressing, but how it impacts your emotional state and behaviour may, in turn, affect your cat. Try to maintain the general routines of the house as much as possible – predictability is important at any time, but more so now. If possible, don’t be too quick to remove the deceased cat’s favourite bedding – the gradually fading scent will confirm that the individual is no longer around.

While it can feel natural to want to comfort your cat, this may cause them further stress if the attention from you is unusual or unwanted. Instead, make yourself available to your cat, so they can initiate contact with you if that’s what they want.

You may also notice an impact in the territory outside your house. Neighbourhood cats may start to intrude on previously defended areas, which can cause a great deal of stress to your cat if they’d previously relied on their companion to defend their territory. Be vigilant and provide litter trays around the home so that your cat has a safe place to toilet if necessary. Once your remaining cat has settled, you may also find it useful to add some additional shrubs or flowerpots in your garden to provide some additional cover and privacy for your remaining cat.

If you have several cats at home, there can be quite an obvious upheaval as the relationship between them shifts depending on the role played by the deceased cat. This is another complex part of the grieving process, and it’s best to leave them to achieve stability again without your intervention. However, if you can see an obvious problem forming it’s best to contact your vet, who may suggest referral to a clinical animal behaviourist for further support and advice.

## Should I contact my veterinary team when my cat is grieving?

If your cat has stopped eating, or is showing any signs of illness, contact your veterinary team as soon as possible. We shouldn’t assume that any signs are purely due to emotional causes.

## FAQs

## Is it helpful for cats to see the body of the deceased?

If there is no specific risk of disease transmission and the death occurred under circumstances that make it practical then there is no harm in doing so. However, some cats can respond negatively to any foreign or challenging smells on the body.

## Will a new kitten help my cat overcome the loss of a companion?

Some cats take days or even months to adjust to losing a companion. You may be keen to comfort your cat, and a new kitten may seem like a good solution. However, replacing a familiar long-term and predictable companion with a young, boisterous kitten isn’t usually an appropriate substitute. However, some cats may benefit if they’re prone to forming dependent relationships with other cats, although they can reject a newcomer, craving instead the company of the familiar cat. You know your cat best, but don’t be afraid to seek professional advice if you’re not sure how your cat will cope.
