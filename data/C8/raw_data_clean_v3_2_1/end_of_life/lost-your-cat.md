# Lost your cat?

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/lost-your-cat

---

Cats are known for their independent nature, but when your cat is missing it can be an incredibly distressing experience. Whether your cat lives entirely indoors or has free-roaming outdoor access, there are steps you can take to increase the chances of their safe return.

## Is your cat lost?

It's natural to feel anxious when your cat goes missing but try not to panic. Cats are resilient animals and it’s not uncommon for them to wander off for several days, only to return home perfectly healthy. Sometimes your cat may even be inside the house, so it’s a good idea to check thoroughly to ensure they weren’t inadvertently shut in a closet of cupboard, or perhaps they weren’t feeling well so found a new secret place to hide.

## Search your local area

Once you’ve established your cat isn’t inside the house, start a search of your local area. Cats are often found close to home. Speak to your neighbours and local businesses and have everyone check inside and outside of their properties in places the cat may have become stuck (like sheds, cellars, bins, cars, or outbuildings), or places where the cat may be hiding if scared or injured (like under hedges or parked cars).

While you are searching, call out your cat's name and shake a package of their favourite treats, then listen carefully. Do this especially at night when it is quiet so they have more chance of hearing each other.

Remember, if your cat is not used to the outdoors or is lost in an unfamiliar area, it may be frightened and unresponsive to your calls and could take several days for them to feel safe enough to come out of hiding.

## Help your cat find their way home

Encourage your cat home by leaving out items that carry its scent, such as its bedding, or it’s favourite smelly food (like tinned tuna). Cats have a remarkable sense of smell, and familiar scents can guide them back to their territory.

## Spread the word

Notify the relevant people and organisations. If your cat is microchipped, let the microchip database know that your cat is missing. You’ll also want to alert local homing centres, vets, and unowned cat feeders, and provide them with a recent photograph, description of your cat, when and where it was last seen, and your contact details. You may also want to make and distribute a missing cat poster in your area and spread the word on social media and lost and found pet websites.

## Continue the search

Don't give up hope if your cat doesn't return immediately. Keep searching and spreading the word. Cats are resilient animals and can survive outdoors for extended periods, especially if they are used to exploring.

## Prevention

Microchipping is really important so that if your cat does go missing, it can easily be reunited with you.

Collars and tags are sometimes used as an alternative to microchipping, but these come with some issues. Some cats will find wearing a collar unpleasant, and cats who are able to wear one can become seriously injured in trying to remove it should it get caught on an object. If a collar is to be used, it’s important that it has a ‘snap open’ safety mechanism. Even if a cat doesn’t outwardly mind wearing or collar, or it has a safe release mechanism, the risk remains that the information attached can be lost.
