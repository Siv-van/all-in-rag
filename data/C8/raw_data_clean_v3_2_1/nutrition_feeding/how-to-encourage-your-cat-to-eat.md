# How to encourage your cat to eat

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/how-to-encourage-your-cat-to-eat

---

If your cat isn’t eating as they normally would or appears to have become ‘picky’ with their food, contact your veterinary clinic. Even short periods (as little as three days) of eating less than normal can have significant effects on your cat’s health.

Unlike dogs, who can cope with less food for a short time, cats must be treated quickly, and in some cases, a feeding tube may be used while they don’t feel like eating. Medication may also be given to boost their appetite if required.

## What does inappetence mean?

Inappetence is a term used to describe a reduction in appetite, a common sign of illness in cats. You may describe your cat as ‘not eating as well as normal’, having a ‘reduced appetite’, being ‘fussy’ or ‘picky’ or refusing food. Your cat may show some interest in food, sniff at it, but walk away.

If you notice your cat eating less, becoming choosier with food, or even eating differently (dropping food or chewing on one side, for example), contact your veterinary clinic. It’s important to investigate the underlying cause as soon as possible.

## What is causing my cat’s reduced appetite?

There are many causes of inappetence, including any illness making your cat feel sick, pain, dehydration orconstipation. Sudden changes in their food orstressat home (for example, the arrival of a new pet or cat in the neighbourhood) can also put a cat off their food. Read our guide oninappetencefor more information on why a cat may eat less.

As many cats get a lot of their daily water requirement from their food, it’s important toencourageyour cat to drinkduring periods of inappetence so that they don’t become dehydrated.

## How do I tempt my cat to eat?

If an illness has been ruled out by your vet, or your cat is recovering from surgery or has been stressed, there are techniques you can use to tempt them to eat. If these tips don’t work, speak to your veterinary team again for further advice.

#### Our top tips include

- Wide, shallow, ceramic food bowls are generally preferred

- Avoid offering multiple types of food all at one time - this can be overwhelming for your cat

Offer treats or strong-smelling foods, such as fish or chicken, if you’re given the OK to do so by your veterinary team. This can be useful to encourage your cat to start eating again, but shouldn’t be offered long term as these foods won’t provide your cat with all the nutrients they need to stay healthy

Food can be slightly warmed to release its smell. However, if your cat is feeling sick, we don’t recommend this, as strong smells can make them feel worse. Instead, feed small portions of their usual food at room temperature or slightly chilled

- Don’t offer human food such as baby food or gravies, as they may contain onion, which can be toxic to cats

- Feed in quiet areas, away from the hustle and bustle of a busy house. Many cats also don’t like to eat near other cats

- Use shallow food bowls and try different types. Some cats prefer ceramic to reflective metal or plastic, which can taint their food

- Position food bowls away from litter trays and water bowls

- Some cats like to be stroked or fed by hand, but some cats prefer to be left alone and may prefer to eat overnight when the house is quiet

Don’t try to offer a ‘prescription’ type diet when a cat feels unwell and isn’t eating much. If they need to eat this longer term, it can deter them - so change them onto their new diet when they’re feeling better

- Never force food on your cat. Syringe feeding or pushing food into a cat’s mouth is stressful and can risk severe medical complications. It can also put your cat off eating altogether

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat isn’t eating normally or is showing any signs of difficulty eating, contact your veterinary clinic - this should be done urgently if your cat has had a reduced appetite for three days or more.

If your cat has been seen by your vet, and you’ve been asked to encourage your cat to eat at home, if any of the methods we’ve recommended above don’t improve your cat’s appetite, speak with your veterinary team again as further treatment is likely to be needed.

Monitoring your cat’s bodyweight

If your cat has had a reduced appetite, monitoring their weight at regular intervals will help you to track your cat’s progress and keep your veterinary team up to date with any changes.

Weigh your cat as often as your vet recommends, likely weekly

Weighing yourself with and without your cat is an inaccurate method which could miss small weight changes. Electronic paediatric scales, designed for weighing babies and readily available to buy, will give you a more accurate figure
