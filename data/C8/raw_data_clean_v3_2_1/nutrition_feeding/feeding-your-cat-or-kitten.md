# Feeding your cat or kitten

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/feeding-your-cat-or-kitten

---

Like all cats, the domestic cat (Felis catus) is a carnivore (a meat eater) and has developed its abilities as a natural hunter. Cats are what’s known as obligate carnivores, meaning they are dependent on a meat diet and can’t thrive or survive without it.

This adaptation to become strict carnivores means that cats have several special dietary requirements that don’t apply to many other animals. It’s vital, for their health and welfare, that cats are fed a properly balanced diet.

## What does my cat’s food need to contain?

Protein

Proteins are large complex molecules consisting of chains of smaller building blocks called amino acids. Cats, like all animals, need protein in their diet as proteins are used for many different biological processes. However, while people and dogs can adapt to diets that have a fairly low protein content (eg, plant-based diets), cats need much more protein in their diet because they use protein as an energy source.

In addition, cats also need several specific amino acids to be present – these are taurine, arginine, methionine and cysteine, which are not found in plants. Many animals (including dogs and people) can convert and use other amino acids derived from plants, but cats aren’t able to do this and so, without them in their diet, cats become unwell.

Fats

Fat in the diet is a good source of energy, but it also supplies fat-soluble vitamins (A, D and E), enhances the flavour of food, and is a source of essential fatty acids (EFAs). Many animals can convert EFAs found in plants into the EFAs that are needed in the body, but again, cats need a source of animal fat with preformed animal-origin EFAs, as they can’t meet their needs from plant sources.

Carbohydrates

As obligate carnivores, cats are less able to digest and use carbohydrates. In contrast to many other animals, cats will get most of their blood sugar (and so their energy) from the breakdown of protein in their diet rather than carbohydrates. This doesn’t mean that cats can’t use carbohydrates or that they shouldn’t be present in their diet, but the diet needs to be formulated carefully.

Other nutrients

Cats need preformed vitamins in their diet that are present in animals but not in plants – these include vitamin A, vitamin D and vitamin B3 at the correct levels (too much of some of these vitamins can cause problems).

Advice in this article may not suit every cat, and advice should be taken from your veterinary team.

## How do I choose the best food for my cat?

Because of their unique dietary requirements, providing a well-balanced diet for cats with home-prepared foods is extremely difficult. Feeding a good quality commercial cat food (tins, sachets or dry food) is ideal, at least as a major part of their diet. One of the most important things to look out for when choosing food for your cat is that it’s ‘complete’ food (this will be stated on the packaging). This means it’s been developed to meet all your cat’s nutritional needs.

We generally recommend feeding healthy cats a variety of bothwet and dry foodso that they can enjoy the benefits of both food types and don’t become accustomed to only one type of food.

Cats are individuals and will vary in their dietary preferences, lifestyle and behaviour. Feeding advice may change according to a cat’s lifestyle, health and if they are over- or underweight.

Advice in this article may not suit every cat, and advice should be taken from your veterinary team.

## How often should I feed my cat?

In the wild, cats eat frequent small meals throughout the day and night (when their nocturnal prey are active). Feral cats can spend 12+ out of every 24 hours looking for and catching food.

Most cats no longer follow their natural meal pattern of eating several small meals throughout the day and night, as they’re reliant on their owners for their food. Often, we feed cats to fit in with our lifestyles – for example, feeding two larger meals during the day.

Cats given unrestricted access to food, which allows them to eat little and often, may overeat, which can lead toobesity. In addition to this, cats are often fed from a bowl, in the same place, daily, so no effort is required on the cat’s part. This lack of mental stimulation can lead to boredom, anxiety, frustration and stress in cats, particularly if they are indoor cats.

Where possible, it’s best to feed cats little and often, dividing their daily ration into at least five portions and feeding this throughout the full 24-hour period, using timed and puzzle feeders. Puzzle feeders, in particular, help meals last longer, increase the physical effort needed to get food, and provide a fun ‘brainteaser’ for your cat!

Cats prefer to eat from shallow bowls or saucers so that they can see around them at the same time as eating, and so their whiskers are not brushing against the sides of the bowl. It’s also better to feed cats from a glass or ceramic bowl rather than a plastic bowl, as plastic bowls can pick up odours (which may be unpleasant and become tainted).

Energy needs in cats and obesity

Obesityis a growing problem for cats around the world. Many cats only need to visit their bowl to eat, compared to their ancestors in the wild who use up a lot of energy hunting. For these reasons, it’s important to control your cat’s food intake and prevent them from becoming overweight. Follow the guidance on the food packet as a starting point and adjust your cat’s daily ration accordingly to keep them at their ideal weight and body condition. Just like people, their activity levels and needs vary, so regularly weighing your cats (using cat scales or scales designed for babies) can help keep a close eye on their health.

For dry food, weighing the food will add less than a couple of minutes to your feeding routine, but could add years to your cat’s life by preventing extra weight gain. Dry food is very energy-dense, so while the correct amount may look small, it provides all the calories your cat needs to stay fit and healthy.

If you’re not sure what your cat’s ideal weight is, or if you want advice on your cat’s diet, speak to your veterinary team who will be happy to help.

Additional considerations

Palatability of foods

Several factors affect how palatable (tasty) cats find certain foods, and these include texture, smell, taste and temperature. A cat’s preferences will also be affected by their early life exposure to food and can be very fixed to certain flavours or textures.

The smell of food is particularly important, and cats have an extremely well-developed sense of smell. This is also enhanced when food is slightly warmed, so cats tend to prefer food that is around body temperature (around 35°C). The texture of food is also important and in general, cats prefer the texture of meat.

If changing your cat’s food, do so slowly to avoid any digestive upsets. Offer the new food next to the old food, and slowly reduce the amount of the old food as the new food is accepted.

Feeding kittens

Because kittens are growing at such a fast rate, they have higher nutritional needs than adult cats and should be fed food designed for this life stage and not adult cat food. It’s usually possible to start weaning kittens (moving them from milk onto solid food) from around 3-4 weeks of age, at which time, small amounts of good quality kitten food can be offered. It’s usually best to start with wet kitten food, or to soak some dry kibbles designed for kittens in water to thoroughly moisten them. Offering both dry and wet food can help kittens accept different textures in later life. Weaning is usually completed by around 8 weeks of age.

Helping your cat feel comfortable at feeding time

#### It’s important that your cat feels comfortable while eating. To make sure this happens

Provide a safe space– cats should have a quiet, calm part of their home where they can eat out of sight of other cats. This is not always the kitchen, for example, which may be busy

Provide multiple, separate resources– ideally, there should be 2 or 3 feeding and watering areas for each cat in your home and it’s important to feed your cats in separate areas. Cats generally do not like to share food bowls or be seen by other cats when eating. These should be away from litter trays, entry points to the home, and busy areas

Provide a chance for your cat to play and ‘hunt’– cats need the mental and physical stimulation they’d normally get through hunting. Try to replicate this using toys (eg, using a wand toy that when ‘caught’, results in a scatter of biscuits for them to enjoy) and puzzle feeders

Provide your cat with positive, consistent and predictable interactions– learn whether your cat prefers your company while eating, or to be left alone

Provide a feeding area that respects your cat’s sense of smell– avoid strong scents (eg, perfume, plug-in scent diffusers) around your cat’s food to help them feel more relaxed when eating. Plastic bowls can develop an odour that cats don’t like, so should be avoided where possible (use ceramic or glass bowls instead), and warming your cat’s food if they seem to be eating less canencourage eating
