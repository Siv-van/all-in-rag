# Constipation in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/constipation-in-cats

---

When your cat becomes constipated, faeces remain in the colon or rectum for longer than normal. As a result, they tend to become drier and harder, which can worsen the problem.

Your cat may show signs of pain when trying to pass faeces, along with excessive straining. In cases where there’s constipation for a long time, the faeces can become severely impacted or blocked in the colon and rectum (obstipation). Repeated bouts of both constipation and obstipation can cause the colon to become swollen, losing its ability to contract and push faeces towards the rectum. This is a condition called ‘megacolon’, which simply means, a very enlarged colon.

## What are the signs of constipation in cats?

#### Signs that your cat may be constipated include

- Straining and difficulty passing faeces

- Digging around and spending extra time in the litter tray

- Pain when passing faeces (crying out)

- Passing faeces less often

- Producing small, hard dry faeces

You may also notice more visits to the litter tray with non-productive straining. However, it can be difficult to tell whether your cat is straining to urinate or defecate. Note: It is important to distinguish fromstruggling to pass urinewhich can be an emergency.

Occasionally, especially in cats with obstipation, your cat may pass faecal fluid rather than formed faeces during straining, which looks likediarrhoea. In severe cases, cats can also lose their appetite and may be sick (vomit).

## What causes constipation in cats?

Many different factors can cause or contribute to constipation in cats. We’ve listed some of the most common below.

Pain

Some cats may find getting in and out of the litter tray or moving into a position to pass faeces painful. An example of this is in older cats suffering fromarthritis.

Your cat may also be experiencing pain from the colon or rectum. This can happen if there are abnormalities, or if your cat has eaten something that causes trauma to the lining of the colon as it passes through. Abscesses and other painful skin conditions around your cat’s bottom can also cause a reluctance to pass faeces.

Dehydration

Water is absorbed from faeces in the colon and if cats are dehydrated (or not drinking enough), they will try to reabsorb more fluid from the colon, resulting in dry and hard faeces. Chronic kidney diseaseis common in older cats and leads to more urine being produced, so cats tend to become mildly dehydrated and this can cause constipation.

Behaviour and lifestyle

Wood pellet litter substrates can be hard and therefore painful for cats to stand on whilst stooping to toilet and lead to a litter tray aversion

#### Cats can be reluctant to pass faeces in the following situations, and this can lead to constipation

- Their litter tray is dirty

- There’s competition for a litter tray with other cats in the household

- Your cat doesn’t like the type of cat litter used

- Their litter tray is in a noisy or unpleasant place

- Your cat has had a bad experience when using their litter tray

- Metabolic disorders

- Some metabolic disorders, such as low thyroid hormones (hypothyroidism, a rare condition in cats) and low blood potassium or calcium, can prevent the colon from contracting and can lead to constipation.

- Neurological problems

The nerves that control the contraction of the intestines (gut) can be damaged because of trauma (for example, being hit by a car and ‘tail-pull’ injuries affecting the lower spine). This can mean your cat can’t control when they pass faeces (faecal incontinence), or it could cause your cat to become constipated.

Obstruction of the colon

Deformities of the pelvis (for example, a healed fracture of the pelvis after a road accident) can cause narrowing of the pelvic canal. This can cause compression of the colon in the pelvis and a build-up of faeces behind the area that’s constricted. If severe, and if this results in prolonged obstipation, secondary megacolon can develop. Narrowing of the colon can also happen due to scarring, tumours and foreign bodies.

Idiopathic megacolon

Megacolon can result from several months of severe constipation and obstipation, causing a gradual and permanent deterioration in the colon’s ability to contract due to continued enlargement. However, megacolon can also be seen in cats as a primary disease with no obvious underlying cause – known as idiopathic megacolon.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat is struggling to pass faeces, seems to be in any discomfort, or is unable to pass anything, contact your veterinary team for an appointment. Constipation can be very uncomfortable for your cat and can lead to serious issues if not treated.

How your vet will diagnose constipation in your cat

#### Because there can be various causes of constipation, your vet will take a detailed history from you to understand what your cat’s been doing and will examine your cat thoroughly. In addition, they may want to perform further tests, including

- Blood and urine tests

- X-rays or ultrasound

- Radiograph showing megacolon and build-up of faeces in the colon

- Colonoscopy – using a medical ‘camera’ to look at the inside of the colon and potentially take samples (biopsies)

- Careful examination under sedation or anaesthesia

## How is constipation treated?

The treatment your cat receives will depend on the underlying cause, the severity of the condition, and how long it’s been a problem. If your cat is suffering from severe constipation or obstipation, they may need to be hospitalised, placed on a drip, and have the impacted faeces removed under anaesthetic. They may alternatively be given laxatives via a tube into their stomach.

Ongoing care

#### There are several things to think about when it comes to ongoing care for your cat

Maintain good hydration– keep up your cat’s fluid intake by feeding wet food (tin, sachets etc) rather than dry, andencourage them to drinkas much as possible

Manage your cat’s litter tray- look at where the trays are located, their size, number, and type to encourage your cat to use them frequently. Read our article onhow to choose and use a litter tray for your catfor more information. For cats with mobility problems, a low-sided litter tray with soft, sandy litter can make passing faeces more comfortable

Consider your cat’s diet- feeding your cat a diet with a high moisture content (wet food) and a high fibre content can help your cat pass regular, softer faeces. Your vet will be able to recommend the best diet for your cat

Enemas (passing liquid into the colon to soften the faeces) – may sometimes be needed to help relieve constipation

Laxatives- can help manage cats with regular constipation. Different laxatives are available, and your vet will prescribe the most suitable for your cat’s condition

Prokinetic drugs- assist with contractions in the colon

Pain relief medication– for cats with arthritis, pain relief can allow them to access the litter tray and posture to pass faeces more comfortably

Surgery- if it’s not possible to manage your cat’s constipation medically, surgery may be required. An operation called ‘subtotal colectomy’ removes most of the colon, and although it’s major surgery and your cat may have an initial period of diarrhoea, in general, the long-term results are very good

## FAQs

## What’s the outlook for a constipated cat?

Chronic constipation is a fairly frequent problem, especially in older cats. Although mild cases can often be successfully managed with medical and dietary treatment, some cats, particularly those more severely affected, may eventually need surgery.

## How can constipation be prevented?

While it’s not always possible to prevent constipation, encouraging your cat to drink, feeding wet food, and placing multiple litter trays around the house can help. In addition to this, ensure any conditions which may cause your cat discomfort, such as arthritis, are treated, and considerlow-sided litter traysif your cat struggles to get in and out.

## Glossary of Terms

- Colon– large intestine where water is absorbed from faeces

- Rectum– final part of the large intestine, connecting the colon and anus

- Faeces– poo or stool

- Megacolon– a condition where the colon is stretched and does not contract properly

- Obstipation– severe constipation requiring veterinary attention to relieve

- Tenesmus- Straining and difficulty passing faeces
