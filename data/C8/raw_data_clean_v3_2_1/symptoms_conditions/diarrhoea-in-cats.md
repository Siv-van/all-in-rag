# Diarrhoea in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/diarrhoea-in-cats

---

If your cat is suffering from diarrhoea, their faeces will become soft, liquid, or watery. You may also notice a change in colour, and that their faeces are lighter or darker than normal or covered with a jelly-like material.

Diarrhoea occurs when your cat’s faeces move through the intestines faster than normal, and there’s less absorption of water, nutrients, and electrolytes. While not a disease, it is a sign of a disease or another problem with your cat.

## What are the signs of diarrhoea?

#### The signs of diarrhoea can vary, depending on how severe it is, the region of the intestine that’s affected, and the underlying cause. Some of the common signs include

- Soft, liquid, or watery stools

- Stools that are lighter or darker in colour than usual

- Straining to pass faeces

- Passing faeces more often than normal

- Passing mucus and/or fresh blood.

- In some cases, your cat may also vomit, lose their appetite, and become quite tired.

If your cat doesn’t use a litter tray, it can be difficult to know if they have diarrhoea - you may, however, spot other signs such as weight loss, soft faeces stuck to the fur under their tail, having accidents in the house.

## When to contact your veterinary team

It is always important to have your cat checked by your veterinary team whenever diarrhoea is severe, accompanied by other signs of your cat being unwell (for example, not eating normally), or if the diarrhoea continues for more than a few days. Although blood in the faeces may appear alarming, unless there is a large amount, this doesn’t require emergency attention - but your cat should be checked by your veterinary team. Faeces that are black and tarry can indicate bleeding higher up in the intestine (melaena) and should prompt contact with the veterinary team.

We recommend monitoring your cat’s toilet habits and noting the nature of the diarrhoea, its frequency, and whether there is any straining, blood, or mucus present, as these are all things that will help your veterinary team narrow down the potential underlying causes.

How your vet will diagnose the cause of diarrhoea

Your vet will take a detailed history from you and examine your cat before deciding if any further tests are necessary. As there are so many potential causes of diarrhoea, sometimes quite extensive investigations may be needed to get to the root of the problem.

#### If your cat’s diarrhoea has developed recently, is mild, and your cat is still bright, eating and alert, your vet may simply suggest treating the signs and waiting to see if the problem resolves. If not, or if the diarrhoea is more serious, further tests may be needed. This may include

- Blood and urine tests to rule out various diseases, pancreatic insufficiency, and to look for low vitamin B12 levels which may require treatment

- Examination of faecal samples – these can be examined for parasites and tested to check for potentially harmful bacteria or other infectious causes

- X-rays and ultrasound are often used to examine the gastrointestinal tract (stomach and intestines) for physical abnormalities

Endoscopy – this is where a small medical camera is passed into the intestine (either through the mouth and/or through the anus) under a general anaesthetic so that the lining of the intestine can be examined, and biopsies can be collected. This can be useful, especially where tumours, polyps or inflammatory bowel disease are suspected

Exploratory surgery – this is sometimes done as an alternative to endoscopy or if endoscopy has failed to provide an answer. It allows all the intestines to be examined (along with organs such as the liver and pancreas) and is an excellent way of getting biopsies from various parts of the intestine and other organs

## How is diarrhoea treated in cats?

Treatment of diarrhoea can be either specific or supportive. Most cases of moderate to severe diarrhoea will need some form of supportive treatment but specific treatment means identifying the underlying cause and targeting the treatment at this.

Supportive therapy

#### Most cats suffering from diarrhoea for any length of time will benefit from supportive therapy. This may include

- Dietary therapy– switching to a simple, highly digestible diet is often helpful in managing diarrhoea egardless of the underlying cause. Discuss appropriate diet with your veterinary team as many ‘gastrointestinal’ type diets are available

Probiotics and prebiotics– probiotics are live bacteria that can be given with food and prebiotics encourage healthy bacteria in the gut. They can sometimes help in the management of diarrhoea by improving intestine health. They are sometimes combined with medications to improve diarrhoea available from the veterinary clinic

- Fluid intake– with more severe diarrhoea, cats can become dehydrated, so fluid intake is important, and your vet may suggest you encourage your cat to drink an appropriate electrolyte solution rather than plain water to help with this

- Specific therapy

#### Specific therapies will depend on the underlying cause of the diarrhoea, but these may include

- Specific types of diet– some cats can be treated with diets designed to avoid any reaction to the food (hydrolysed diets), more than one diet may need to be trialled to find the most suitable

- Treatment of diseases causing diarrhoea– for example, managing an overactive thyroid (hyperthyroidism)

- Anti-bacterial and anti-parasitic drugs– if infection or parasites are present, relevant medication may be prescribed by your vet to treat your cat accordingly

- Anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive drugs– these are often used in the management of inflammatory bowel disease

- Surgery and/or chemotherapeutic drugs– these can be useful in the management of intestinal tumours

- Vitamin B12– weekly injections of vitamin B12 or B12 tablets/powders are used when B12 deficiency has been diagnosed

## FAQs

## What is the outlook for cats with diarrhoea?

The prognosis depends on the cause of the diarrhoea – diseases which are easily managed with diet and/or drugs carry a good prognosis, and many cases will completely resolve with appropriate therapy. In other cases, ongoing therapy may be necessary.

In all cats, diagnosing the underlying cause of persistent diarrhoea early will help prevent long-term changes, which may be irreversible.
