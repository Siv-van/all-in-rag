# Heart murmurs in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/heart-murmurs-in-cats

---

Heart murmurs are an abnormal sound heard when listening to a cat’s heart. They indicate a turbulent flow of blood through the heart. The murmur may be due to an underlying condition, or it could be what’s known as an ‘innocent’ murmur, present in some young, growing kittens, which disappears as they get older. Stress can also result in a heart murmur that is not heard at lower heart rates when a cat is calmer.

Heart murmurs explained

A heart murmur is caused by turbulent blood flow within the heart or the large vessels exiting from the heart. This causes an abnormal noise (whooshing) which can be heard by your vet when listening to your cat’s chest with a stethoscope. It’s a very distinct noise, heard alongside the normal ‘lub’ and ‘dub’ heart sounds.

## What are the signs of heart murmurs in cats?

Your cat may not show any signs of a heart murmur, and the first you may know of it is when your vet detects it during a routine examination of your cat.

#### Heart murmurs are not always caused by heart disease - there are other reasons for this sound. However, cats with underlying heart problems may show the following signs

- Lethargy/tiredness

- Pale gums

- Abnormal breathing pattern or increased breathing effort

- Panting/open mouth breathing

- Weakness

- Reduced appetite

- Coughing

- Collapse

- Heart murmurs caused by other conditions (eg, anaemia, hyperthyroidism, hypertension) may mean a cat shows additional signs of illness.

## When to contact your veterinary team

Contact your veterinary team if your cat is showing any of the signs listed above, or if you’re worried about their heart in any way.

How your vet will diagnose a heart murmur

A heart murmur can be heard by your vet when they listen to your cat’s heart with a stethoscope.

Heart murmurs are graded I-VI, with a Grade I heart murmur being the mildest and Grade VI the most severe. The grading system is based largely on how loud the murmur is, but other factors are also considered, such as the area over which the murmur can be heard.

The grade of the heart murmur doesn’t necessarily reflect the severity of the underlying heart problem. Some severe heart conditions may not have a heart murmur at all, and some murmurs may occur with relatively small defects.

The only way to determine if heart disease may be causing the murmur, is to perform a detailed ultrasound examination (scan) of the heart (echocardiography). This is a completely painless procedure. Other tests may include x-rays, an ECG (an electrical tracing of the heart) and blood tests to look for any potential underlying causes of heart disease or other reasons for the murmur.

## How are heart murmurs treated?

This very much depends on the underlying cause of the murmur. Cats with heart disease may not need specific treatment, depending on the results of the heart scan, while others may benefit from medication. Sometimes the murmur is documented in the cat’s medical records and monitored without specific treatment.

If the murmur is suspected to be related to stress, then the vet may want to listen again when the cat is more relaxed. If the murmur is caused by an underlying illness like hyperthyroidism or high blood pressure, then these will need to be treated.

## What’s the outlook for cats with a heart murmur?

The outlook will depend on what’s causing the heart murmur and how easy the condition is to treat or manage – your veterinary team will be able to give you specific guidance based on your cat’s condition. If no underlying disease is present, your cat may live a long and happy life without any issues.
