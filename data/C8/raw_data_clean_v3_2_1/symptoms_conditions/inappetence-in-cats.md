# Inappetence in cats

## Source
https://icatcare.org/articles/inappetence-in-cats

---

A common sign of illness in cats is a reluctance to eat, eating less, or refusing to eat at all. A loss of appetite is termed ‘inappetence’. A lack of appetite might also be referred to as ‘anorexia’ when a cat eats nothing at all, and ‘hyporexia’ when food intake is below what’s normal. It’s very stressful for owners when a cat won’t eat, and it requires veterinary attention.

## What causes inappetence in cats?

There are many reasons why your cat may go off their food. Cats can be notoriously fussy eaters, and a change in their diet may lead to food avoidance. Therefore, always introduce a new diet to your cat in gradual stages, adding the new food as a small proportion of their existing diet and increasing this slowly. This may be done by offering the new food next to the old food in a separate bowl, and gradually reducing the amount of the new food, as some cats do not like different types of food mixed together.

Sometimes a cat can be tempted to eat by warming their food or offering tasty treats, such as fish or chicken, but always seek veterinary advice to find the cause of their reduced appetite as every day a cat eats less than normal can be detrimental to their overall health.

Your cat going off their food can indicate that they’re feeling unwell. Many diseases reduce a cat’s desire to eat; therefore, it’s always important to monitor your cat’s eating habits and remember that periods without food can be very serious to cats. As a species, they can develop liver problems if they go without food even for a short time. Conditions such asdental disease, kidneyandliverproblems, intestinal problems, infections, andcancerare just a few that can affect a cat’s appetite. Any disease causing nausea (feeling sick) or pain can affect appetite. Dehydration can be another factor, so make sure your cat has several sources of fresh water in different locations toencourage drinking– you can also consider a water fountain as many cats prefer to drink moving water.

If your cat is eating less than usual, or worse, not eating at all, contact your veterinary team as soon as possible. It’s important to find the underlying cause and get treatment started to minimise weight loss, which can lead to further health problems. Your vet will work with you to make sure your cat receives the care and nutrition they need to return to good health. In some cases, afeeding tubemay need to be placed to give your cat nutrition (and medications) without stress. Cats should never be forced to eat (syringe feeding) as this can cause emotional distress or severe health complications if your cat inhales any food. For cats with longer-term illnesses, or to help with medicating, a feeding tube can allow them to be managed at home with support from your veterinary team.

If no physical health issues causing inappetence can be found, the cause of appetite loss may be psychological. Inappetence can be caused bystress, in which case it’s important to identify the cause of stress and try to reduce it. Speak to your veterinary team, and if necessary, they may be able to arrange a referral to a local clinical animal behaviourist, who will identify and address any stressors which may have triggered or exacerbated the problem.

## When to contact your veterinary team

If your cat isn’t eating normally or is showing any signs of having difficulty eating, contact your veterinary clinic - this should be done urgently if your cat has had a reduced appetite for three days or more.

If your cat has been seen by your vet and you’ve been asked to encourage your cat to eat at home, but none of the methods improve your cat’s appetite has worked, speak with your veterinary team again as further treatment is likely to be needed.

Monitoring your cat’s bodyweight

If your cat has had a reduced appetite, monitoring their weight at regular intervals will help you to track your cat’s progress. Remember to keep your veterinary team up to date with any changes.

Weigh your cat as often as your vet recommends, likely weekly

Weighing yourself with and without your cat is an inaccurate method which could miss small weight changes. Electronic paediatric scales designed for weighing babies are readily available to buy and will give you a more accurate figure
